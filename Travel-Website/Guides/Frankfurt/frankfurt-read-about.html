Frankfurt has been the financial capital of Europe, the birthplace of German democracy, and the cradle of the Holy Roman Empire — and most visitors pass through its airport without ever finding out.

The city that traded everything

Before Frankfurt was a financial center, it was a fair. The Frankfurter Messe — the Frankfurt Trade Fair — has been running since the 12th century, making it the oldest continuous trade fair in the world. By the medieval period, merchants from across Europe converged twice a year on the Main River crossing where Frankfurt sat, and the city grew rich on the commissions, the hospitality, and the credit that moving that much goods required. This is the fundamental fact of Frankfurt: it has always been a node, not a destination. The Romans built a fort here to control the ford across the Main. Charlemagne crossed the river here in 794 and held an imperial synod that named the place — Franconovurd, "ford of the Franks." The trade fair came next. The banks followed the fair. The stock exchange followed the banks. The European Central Bank followed the stock exchange. Two thousand years of infrastructure decisions compounded into one city.

The Jewish community arrived in Frankfurt by the 12th century and eventually became the beating heart of the city's financial life. They were confined by law to the Judengasse — a narrow lane in the Altstadt, walled at both ends and locked at night — and barred from most professions. Finance was one of the few permitted ones, and they excelled at it. By the 17th century, the Judengasse was one of the most densely populated streets in Europe, housing several thousand people in buildings stacked up to five stories on a lane barely two meters wide. Mayer Amschel Rothschild was born there in 1744, built a currency-exchange operation from a shop no larger than a cupboard, and died there in 1812 as one of the wealthiest men on the continent. His five sons fanned out to London, Paris, Vienna, Naples, and Frankfurt, each establishing a bank, and together built the most powerful financial network in 19th-century Europe. The Frankfurt branch occupied the Rothschild Palais on Untermainkai — today it holds Germany's oldest Jewish museum, which opened in 1988 and expanded substantially in 2021.

Where emperors were made

From 1356 to 1792 — for more than four hundred years — Holy Roman Emperors were elected in Frankfurt. The Golden Bull, the constitutional document that fixed the rules for imperial succession, designated the city as the permanent site for elections. Seven prince-electors — archbishops, dukes, and kings — gathered in the Wahlkapelle inside Frankfurt Cathedral and voted. Starting with Matthias in 1612, the emperors were also crowned in the same building. If you stand in the nave of the Kaiserdom today, in what looks like a fairly ordinary Gothic church, you're in the room where the legal fiction of a Christian empire stretching from Poland to Portugal was renewed, generation after generation, for the better part of five centuries.

The Frankfurt that existed then — the Altstadt you'll walk through today — was almost entirely destroyed. In the bombing raids of March 1944, British forces dropped 1,500 tons of incendiary bombs on the medieval city center in a single night. By the time the raids were over, ninety percent of the Altstadt was gone. The 14th-century Cathedral survived, scorched and partially roofless. The Römer's stepped gables survived. Almost nothing else did. Frankfurt faced the question that every bombed German city faced: what to build in place of what the war had taken.

Frankfurt's answer to that question was different from every other German city's, and that difference shaped the skyline you see today.

Other cities — Cologne, Dresden, Nuremberg — rebuilt their historic centers in some approximation of what had stood before. Frankfurt did the opposite. The city committed fully to modernism, cleared the rubble, and rebuilt its center as a new city for a new Germany. The financial institutions that had begun settling here in the 1950s accelerated, and when West Germany needed a central bank, Frankfurt got it — the Bundesbank opened in 1957. The European Central Bank followed, established in Frankfurt in 1998. By the 1980s, Frankfurt had the only true skyscraper skyline in continental Europe: glass towers rising from the Main basin, a profile unlike anything in Paris or Vienna or Rome. Germans gave it a name — Mainhattan — and the nickname stuck.

The democracy that failed and the one that didn't

In May 1848, delegates from every German state assembled in Frankfurt's Paulskirche — an 18th-century circular church just off the Römerberg — to attempt something that had never been done on the continent: a freely elected parliament drafting a constitution for a unified German nation. The Frankfurt Parliament sat for a year, produced a draft constitution that was remarkably liberal for its time (including freedom of the press, freedom of religion, and habeas corpus), offered the crown of a constitutional monarchy to Prussia's King Friedrich Wilhelm IV, and was refused. Friedrich Wilhelm didn't want a crown "picked up from the gutter." The parliament dissolved in failure by 1849. German unification, when it came in 1871, came from Bismarck and Prussian military force, not from a freely elected assembly.

The Paulskirche and its failed parliament became a symbol that Germany carried with it through the Imperial period, through Weimar, through the Nazi years, and into the Federal Republic. When West Germany rebuilt its democratic institutions after 1945, the memory of 1848 was explicitly invoked. Today the Paulskirche hosts the Peace Prize of the German Book Trade each October, awarded in a ceremony that is, in its way, a deliberate continuation of what was attempted there in 1848 — a public space in which ideas, not force, are given the podium.

The reconstruction within the reconstruction

Frankfurt's second transformation — the one that tells you the most about the city — began in 2012 and finished in 2018. The Dom-Römer project rebuilt 35 historic houses on the footprint of the medieval Altstadt, using historical records, old photographs, and architectural surveys to reconstruct buildings that had been destroyed in 1944. Not approximations — specific buildings, with documented facades, rebuilt stone by stone and timber by timber. The project took over a decade, cost roughly €200 million, and produced a neighborhood that looks medieval but is, in almost every case, a 21st-century building behind a historically accurate face.

You walk through it today and the effect is strange. The buildings are real — the materials, the proportions, the details are correct. But the patina is missing. A genuine medieval street has centuries of repair, modification, and decay written into it. The Dom-Römer quarter doesn't. It's the difference between a photograph and a copy of a photograph: technically identical, recognizably different to anyone paying attention. Frankfurt knows this. The city didn't reconstruct the Altstadt because anyone was confused about whether it was real history; it did it because the memory of what had stood there mattered enough to rebuild, even imperfectly. That is, in its way, the most Frankfurt thing imaginable — a financial calculation in favor of culture, executed with German thoroughness.

The museum mile no one tells you about

On the south bank of the Main, along a two-kilometer stretch of the Schaumainkai called the Museumsufer — the Museum Embankment — fifteen world-class museums sit within walking distance of each other. The Städel holds 700 years of European painting, from Botticelli to Gerhard Richter. The Liebieghaus contains 5,000 years of sculpture in a late 19th-century villa. The Deutsches Filmmuseum runs a cinematheque. The Museum Angewandte Kunst, in a Richard Meier building, covers design from Islamic carpets to Braun industrial products. The Museum für Kommunikation traces the history of communication from ancient postal routes to the internet. None of these museums are household names outside Germany. All of them are genuinely excellent.

The Museumsufer didn't happen by accident. Frankfurt assembled it deliberately, beginning in the 1970s and expanding through the 1980s and 1990s, using municipal investment and private foundation money to turn what had been a residential embankment into one of the densest concentrations of museum resources in Europe. Every August, the city shuts the Schaumainkai to traffic for a weekend and the museums open free, the riverbank fills with food stalls and music, and Frankfurt does what Frankfurt almost never does: it shows off. On any other day, you can walk the entire mile in an hour and spend a week in the buildings along it. Start at the Liebieghaus end and walk west into the afternoon sun.

A city that built itself twice — first as a trade fair, then as a bank, then as a medieval fantasy it had already lost — has earned a specific kind of confidence. Frankfurt doesn't compete with Munich or Berlin or Hamburg for the affection of travelers. It doesn't need to. The ECB is here. The stock exchange is here. The Goethe house is here. The airport that connects the continent is here. Frankfurt is infrastructure, in the same way Rome was once infrastructure — the city through which everything passes, the city where the decision about everything gets made. The cathedral and the towers are both correct. The city built both on purpose.
