Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 10 min · 🚕 4 min → Book of Kells at Trinity College
1.Book of Kells at Trinity College
↳ Ireland's most treasured illuminated manuscript — four gospels hand-lettered by monks around 800 AD — lives inside the Old Library's Long Room, lined with 200,000 antique books and marble busts of great thinkers. The exhibition traces the manuscript's creation and survival across twelve centuries.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Book of Kells Experience · 4.7⭐ · visittrinity.ie

⏰ ~90 min
📍 College Green · South-East Inner City
🚶 8 min · 🚕 3 min → National Museum of Archaeology
2.National Museum of Archaeology
↳ Ireland's finest collection of prehistoric gold, Viking artefacts, and Celtic treasures in a Victorian Palladian building with a Pantheon-inspired rotunda. Highlights include the Ardagh Chalice, the Tara Brooch, and one of the world's largest displays of Iron Age bog bodies. All free.
📖 Wikipedia

3.National Gallery of Ireland
↳ Over 2,500 works spanning five centuries of European and Irish painting in sky-lit galleries — all free. The collection includes Caravaggio's only Irish painting, Vermeer and Rembrandt, and an entire room of Jack B. Yeats alongside Ireland's greatest portrait collection.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 25 min · 🚕 9 min → Christ Church Cathedral
1.Christ Church Cathedral
↳ Dublin's oldest building, founded by Viking king Sitric Silkenbeard in 1030 and rebuilt by the Normans after 1170. The atmospheric crypt runs the full length of the nave above and houses a mummified cat and rat frozen mid-chase inside an organ pipe — an unexpectedly memorable detail.
📖 Wikipedia

2.St. Patrick's Cathedral
↳ Ireland's largest church, built where St. Patrick is said to have baptized converts in the 5th century, and home to the tomb of Jonathan Swift. The Gothic nave is lined with the tattered battle flags of Irish regiments and the memorial brasses of medieval knights who shaped the city.

3.Museum of Literature Ireland
↳ Ireland's national museum of literature in Newman House on St. Stephen's Green — where Joyce studied and Hopkins taught. Exhibits trace Ireland's extraordinary literary tradition through rare manuscripts and objects connected to Yeats, Beckett, Joyce, and Ireland's four Nobel laureates in literature.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 14 min → Kilmainham Gaol
1.Kilmainham Gaol
↳ A working prison from 1796 to 1924 where the leaders of the 1916 Easter Rising were executed in a stone courtyard. The guided tour moves through the Victorian wing's iron galleries, dim stone cells, and the execution yard where James Connolly — too wounded to stand — was tied to a chair before being shot.
📖 Wikipedia

2.Guinness Storehouse
↳ Seven floors of brewing history inside the original fermentation plant at St. James's Gate — a glass-floored pint-shaped atrium surrounded by displays on ingredients, craft, and the brand's advertising legacy. Finish at the Gravity Bar on the top floor with a complimentary pint and a 360-degree panorama of Dublin.

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 24 min · 🚕 10 min → 14 Henrietta Street
1.14 Henrietta Street
↳ A single Georgian townhouse telling 300 years of Dublin's social history — built as an aristocratic mansion in the 1740s, divided into cramped tenement flats by the 1880s, housing over 100 people by 1911. The guided tour moves floor by floor as the story shifts from landed gentry to desperate poverty.
📖 Wikipedia

2.GPO Witness History
↳ The General Post Office served as headquarters of the 1916 Easter Rising — Pádraig Pearse read the Proclamation of the Irish Republic from its steps on Easter Monday. The immersive exhibition inside uses touchscreens and reconstructed scenes to bring the rising to life in the building that still bears bullet marks.

3.EPIC The Irish Emigration Museum
↳ Twenty interconnected galleries tracing the 10 million people who left Ireland over the centuries and the outsized mark they made worldwide. Among the most creative museum experiences in Dublin, weaving together individual emigrant stories from famine survivors to Che Guevara's Irish grandfather.
📖 Wikipedia

🚶 3 min · 🚕 1 min → Jeanie Johnston Tall Ship
4.Jeanie Johnston Tall Ship
↳ A full-scale working replica of the 1847 barque that carried 2,500 Irish emigrants to North America and never lost a single passenger to disease or disaster. The guided tour goes below decks into the cramped hold where families huddled for six to eight weeks, bringing individual emigrants' stories to life.
📖 Wikipedia

🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 22 min → James Joyce Tower
1.James Joyce Tower
↳ The Martello tower at Sandycove where Joyce lived briefly in 1904 and set the opening scene of Ulysses — reproduced almost exactly as he described it. The free museum holds Joyce's guitar, waistcoat, cigar case, and death mask. Reached by DART to Sandycove & Glasthule, then a 7-minute coastal walk.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Wednesday - Sunday 10:00am - 4:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday & Tuesday

2.Dalkey Castle
↳ A 15th-century fortified townhouse in one of the most charming coastal villages near Dublin — once a major medieval port. Actors in period costume deliver interactive history performances, and the Writers' Gallery honours the remarkable number of writers, from Shaw to Maeve Binchy, who have lived in Dalkey.

1.Kilkenny Castle
↳ A magnificent Norman fortress on a limestone bluff above the River Nore, built in the 1190s and occupied by the Butler dynasty until 1935. The Victorian Long Gallery's stunning hammerbeam timber roof is hung with Pre-Raphaelite and Flemish portraits. The castle's parkland runs down to the river and is free to enter.
📖 Wikipedia

2.St. Canice's Cathedral
↳ Ireland's second-longest medieval cathedral — a vast Gothic interior of black Kilkenny marble and medieval floor tiles lined with Norman and Viking lords' tombs. Beside it stands a 9th-century round tower, climbable via internal ladders for sweeping views over Kilkenny.

🍮 Local Tastes
Guinness
↳ Ireland's defining stout, brewed at St. James's Gate since 1759 — the creamy nitrogen head and roasted-barley bitterness taste noticeably better poured in Dublin, a fact every local will tell you.
📍 Mulligan's · The Long Hall · Neary's
Dublin Coddle
↳ Dublin's own one-pot dish — pork sausages, back bacon rashers, and sliced potatoes slow-cooked in stock until the liquid turns silky. A distinctly Dubliner recipe; Swift wrote about it and Joyce ate it.
📍 The Brazen Head · Mulligan's
Full Irish Breakfast
↳ Rashers, pork sausages, white pudding, black pudding, fried egg, grilled tomato, and soda bread — Ireland's most important morning meal. Black and white puddings are uniquely Irish; quality varies sharply by producer.
📍 Grogan's Castle Lounge · Bewley's
Irish Soda Bread
↳ Leavened with bicarbonate of soda — Ireland's most distinctive bread with a dense crumb and slightly tangy flavour. The brown wholemeal version and the white spotted variety are both staples at every Irish breakfast.
📍 Bread 41 · Bewley's
Irish Whiskey
↳ Triple-distilled for smoothness — lighter and less peated than Scotch with a characteristic floral and honeyed character. Dublin's craft distillery revival means excellent whiskey bars worth exploring beyond Jameson.
📍 Teeling Distillery · The Dead Rabbit

Maynooth · 35 min

Drogheda · 1 hr
↳ A medieval port town on the Boyne — the Millmount citadel with panoramic river views, St Peter's Church housing the head of St Oliver Plunkett, and the nearby Brú na Bóinne Neolithic tombs at Newgrange. Irish Rail Enterprise service direct.

Wicklow Town · 75 min
↳ A small coastal town serving as a gateway to Wicklow Mountains National Park; buses and taxis connect to Glendalough, the hauntingly beautiful 6th-century monastic ruins set between two dark mountain lakes in a glacial valley. Recommended by Fodor's and Rick Steves as a half-day or full-day excursion.
🎫 book at: thetrainline.com or Omio
Dundalk · 1h 20 min
↳ A border town with a strong medieval heritage — Dundalk Castle remains, the County Museum in the 18th-century distillery, and the traditional market quarter. Enterprise service runs hourly toward Belfast.

🪞 The Thread of 1916

What surprised me building this guide is how clearly a single thread runs through it — the 1916 Easter Rising and the long shadow it casts across the city. At 14 Henrietta Street you see what the tenement city looked like from inside, the hunger and overcrowding that made people willing to fight. At the GPO you stand in the room where Pádraig Pearse read the Proclamation from the steps on Easter Monday. At Kilmainham Gaol you walk into the stone yard where fourteen leaders were shot, one by one, over ten days.

The three stops aren't on the same day — they're spaced across Days 3 and 4 — but they form a single argument about a country that chose its moment and paid the price. Dublin doesn't just remember 1916; it's still partly shaped by it. The bullet marks on the GPO columns are still there. The execution yard at Kilmainham is still exactly as it was. That refusal to smooth things over is one of the things that makes the city genuinely worth taking seriously.
