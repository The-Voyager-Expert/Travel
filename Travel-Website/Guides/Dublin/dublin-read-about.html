A city with eight hundred years of grievance and a gift for turning that grievance into literature, music, and conversation so good you forget you came in for a pint an hour ago.

The Viking foundation

Dublin was founded by Vikings. This is not a metaphor or a loose use of "founded" — the Norse established a longphort, a ship harbor and fortified camp, at the confluence of the Liffey and the Poddle around 841 AD, and it became one of the most important Norse trading towns in the British Isles. The name comes from Dubh Linn, the Black Pool, the tidal pool at the rivers' junction. Underneath the medieval cathedrals and Georgian streets, archaeologists keep finding Viking bones and combs and weapons and evidence of a town that was serious commercial enterprise long before the English arrived.

The Norse were absorbed rather than expelled — converted to Christianity, intermarried, folded into the Gaelic world around them. By the time the Anglo-Norman invasion arrived in 1169, Dublin was already a mixed place, Hiberno-Norse, speaking a language that had been blending for three centuries. The English who came with Strongbow and Henry II added another layer to a city that had always been a place where different people came and worked out arrangements.

The colonial city

For seven hundred years, Dublin was the capital of English rule in Ireland — and the ambivalence of that position runs through everything the city built. The Georgian squares you walk through in the south city — Merrion Square, Fitzwilliam Square, St. Stephen's Green — were built by and for the Anglo-Irish Protestant Ascendancy, the class that controlled Ireland's land and politics under British patronage. These are beautiful squares. They were also built on an economy that depended on the dispossession of the Catholic majority.

The Custom House on the north quays, finished in 1791 and still one of the finest neoclassical buildings in Europe, was built to process the commerce of an empire. Four Custom House heads — the allegorical riverine faces by Edward Smyth on the keystones — represent the Atlantic Ocean, the Liffey, and the other rivers of Ireland. They look like they belong on a public building of the first rank, because they do. The building was burned out by the IRA in 1921 and had to be substantially rebuilt. The walls that survived are Georgian; much of what's inside is twentieth-century reconstruction. Dublin's buildings carry their history this way — often beautiful on the outside, complicated in their interior logic.

Merrion Square is the finest Georgian square in Ireland. Oscar Wilde was born at number 1. His statue — languid, ironic, reclining on a rock in the park — looks across at his birthplace with appropriate detachment.

The Famine and what it made Dublin

Between 1845 and 1852, a potato blight destroyed the staple crop of Ireland's rural poor. One million people died of starvation and disease. Another million emigrated. The population of Ireland fell by twenty to twenty-five percent in seven years. It has never fully recovered — Ireland today has roughly the same population it had in 1800, a fact with no parallel in European history.

Dublin was affected differently than the rural west, where the Famine hit hardest. The city received refugees — the starving poor walking in from the countryside, dying in the streets, crowding the workhouses. But Dublin was also where the British administration that failed to prevent the Famine was headquartered. The question of responsibility — whether British policy constituted negligence or something closer to intentional neglect — is not a settled historical argument in Ireland. It shapes the political consciousness of the country in ways that are still live. When you look at the Famine memorial sculptures by Rowan Gillespie on the north quays — bronze figures, emaciated, walking toward the emigrant ships — you're looking at something Dubliners pass every day and feel something about.

The Famine made Dublin a port city defined by departure. More people of Irish descent live outside Ireland than in it. The Irish diaspora — in America, Australia, Britain, Argentina — is the consequence of a century and a half of emigration that began with the Famine and didn't slow until the 1990s economic boom. When you talk to Dubliners about family, the conversation almost always involves someone who left.

The rising and what came after

On Easter Monday 1916, a force of around 1,200 Irish Volunteers and Irish Citizen Army members seized the General Post Office on O'Connell Street and several other buildings around Dublin, proclaimed an Irish Republic from the GPO steps, and held them against British forces for six days. The Easter Rising was, militarily, a failure — the rebels surrendered, sixteen of their leaders were executed at Kilmainham Gaol, and the city center lay in ruins.

The executions changed everything. Public opinion in Ireland, which had been largely indifferent or hostile to the Rising while it was happening, shifted dramatically when the British executed the leaders one by one over ten days, including James Connolly, who had been wounded and had to be strapped to a chair because he couldn't stand. Within five years of the Rising, Ireland had fought a War of Independence and established a Free State. The GPO is still a working post office. The bullet holes in its columns have been preserved. You can queue to buy stamps twenty feet from where Pádraic Pearse read the proclamation.

Kilmainham Gaol, on the west side of the city, is the place the Rising's history is most viscerally present. The execution yard where the leaders were shot is small and plain. The cells where the leaders spent their last nights before the executions are intact. The guide who takes you through will tell you the story without editorial commentary, and the story is sufficiently powerful that it doesn't need any.

The writers, who were the city's real industry

Dublin is the most literary city of its size in the world, and not by accident. The city produced, in the century between 1850 and 1950, an improbable concentration of writers who changed how fiction and drama and poetry worked: Oscar Wilde, George Bernard Shaw, W.B. Yeats, J.M. Synge, Samuel Beckett, and James Joyce. These are not simply Irish writers — they are foundational figures in the literature of the English language, and several of them are among the most important writers in any language.

Joyce is the one most attached to the city, in the complicated way of someone who left it and couldn't stop writing about it. Ulysses takes place on June 16, 1904, in Dublin — a single day, rendered in extraordinary detail, following Leopold Bloom through streets and pubs and funerals and newspaper offices and beaches. The book is technically about Homer and the human mind and the nature of consciousness, but it is also, practically, a street map of a city at a specific moment. You can walk the route. People do, every year on June 16 (Bloomsday), wearing Edwardian dress and eating the kidneys and gorgonzola sandwiches that Bloom eats, reading passages aloud outside the relevant buildings. This is either a celebration of literature or a very good party or both.

The James Joyce Centre on North Great George's Street has a door from 7 Eccles Street — the fictional home of Leopold Bloom, demolished in 1982 — installed in its wall. It is a door to a house that no longer exists, preserved because of a novel.

Samuel Beckett was born in Foxrock, south Dublin, and educated at Trinity College. He left for Paris in his twenties and barely returned. Waiting for Godot, Endgame, the trilogy of novels — all written in French first, then translated by Beckett himself into English. Dublin is everywhere in Beckett in the way repressed material is everywhere: in the flat vowels, in the rhythm of the speech, in the bleak comedy of people waiting for something that isn't coming. The Samuel Beckett Bridge over the Liffey, shaped like a harp, is a fitting monument to a man who left the city and thought about nothing but.

The pubs, which are the point

Don't skip the pubs. This is not a recommendation to drink heavily — it's a recommendation to understand what the Dublin pub actually is. It is the city's real public space, the room where social class blurs, where strangers talk, where the conversation can range from football to philosophy to the state of your family in about twenty minutes. The pub in Dublin is an institution that has survived every economic transformation the city has been through precisely because it serves a function that nothing else serves as well.

The best pubs are the old ones — Mulligan's on Poolbeg Street, where the Guinness is as good as it gets; the Long Hall on South Great George's Street, with its Victorian mirrors and brass fittings; Kehoe's on South Anne Street, which barely acknowledges that the twentieth century happened. These places are not performing Irishness for tourists. They are Irish pubs because they have always been Irish pubs. The difference is perceptible the moment you walk in.

What changed, and what didn't

The Dublin you're visiting is not the Dublin of twenty years ago. The Celtic Tiger economic boom of the 1990s and 2000s transformed a conservative, religious, relatively poor country into one of the wealthiest in Europe. The Church's influence collapsed after decades of abuse scandals. Ireland legalized gay marriage by referendum in 2015 — the first country in the world to do so by popular vote — and legalized abortion in 2018. The country that drove its writers out for treating sex as a subject, that criminalized homosexuality until 1993, that had a blasphemy law until 2018, rewrote itself in the space of a generation.

Dublin absorbed all of this while staying recognizably Dublin. The Georgian streets are still there. The pubs are still there. The argument about what Ireland means is still ongoing. The city is more expensive than it used to be and faces a serious housing crisis that is shaping its politics in real time — you'll see the evidence in conversations and newspapers. But the quality that makes Dublin Dublin — the willingness to talk to strangers, the refusal to take itself too seriously, the particular pleasure of a good conversation in a warm room — that has survived everything else. Go to a pub. Let someone talk to you.

The thing to carry

Before you board, here is what changes how you see Dublin: this is a city that turned eight hundred years of colonial history and dispossession and emigration and grief into the greatest concentration of literary talent in the modern English-speaking world. That's not a coincidence. The Irish literary tradition is so powerful because the Irish had to become masters of language in a language that wasn't theirs — English was the language of the colonizer, and Irish writers took it and bent it until it said things it hadn't said before. Beckett wrote in French to get out from under both. Joyce turned a single day in Dublin into the longest sustained act of revenge literature has ever attempted. When you walk the city, you're walking through the material these writers worked with — the streets, the pubs, the Georgian facades, the rivers with their complicated names. Once you feel that weight, the city stops being pretty and starts being what it actually is.
