An island that speaks five languages at once, paints its buildings in colors that started as a medical complaint, and turned a failed orange into a famous drink.

The colors that came from a headache

The first thing you notice about Willemstad is the Handelskade — a row of Dutch colonial merchant houses lining Sint Annabaai, painted in deep ochre, terracotta, aquamarine, and canary yellow. They look like they have always been this way, like the Caribbean simply demanded color. They haven't. For most of their history, these buildings were white. The standard colonial whitewash. Then, around 1817, Governor Albert Kikkert declared that the glare of the white buildings was giving him headaches and ordered them painted in other colors. Whether the headaches were real or whether Kikkert simply had opinions about aesthetics is a matter nobody settled. Either way, the order stuck.

The Handelskade is now a UNESCO World Heritage Site, one of the few in the Caribbean, and its painted facades have become the defining image of the island. But the buildings themselves predate the color by two centuries. Dutch traders built them in the late 1600s and early 1700s, copying the tall narrow gable-front houses of Amsterdam but adjusting for the climate — wider windows, deeper overhangs, thicker walls to hold the heat out. You are looking at the Dutch Golden Age rebuilt for the tropics and then accidentally turned into something more interesting than the original.

The sand on the synagogue floor

Mikvé Israel-Emanuel, in the center of Willemstad, is the oldest continuously active synagogue in the Western Hemisphere. It was founded in 1651 by Sephardic Jews who had come to Curaçao from Amsterdam, who had come to Amsterdam from the Iberian Peninsula, who had been expelled from Spain and Portugal during the Inquisition. The current building dates to 1732, and it is beautiful — mahogany pews, brass chandeliers, a simplicity that feels both spare and rich.

The floor is covered in sand. Not tile, not stone — sand, several inches deep, spread across the entire interior. The tradition is usually explained as a reference to the desert wanderings of the Israelites, but the more immediate history is the Inquisition itself. In Portugal and Spain, Jews who practiced in secret — the conversos — held services in hidden rooms and spread sand on the floor to muffle the sound of footsteps. You walked into the room and nobody outside could hear you praying. The sand at Mikvé Israel-Emanuel is a memorial to the centuries when prayer required silence.

The sand on the synagogue floor is not decoration. It is a memory of the centuries when worship had to be silent.

The Jewish community in Curaçao was, for a long time, the largest in the Americas. These were the families who helped establish what would become the Jewish communities of New York, Newport, and Savannah. The congregation at Mikvé Israel-Emanuel predates every synagogue in the United States. When you stand inside this building, you are standing at one of the starting points of Jewish life in the Western Hemisphere.

The museum that doesn't look away

Curaçao was a major hub of the Atlantic slave trade. The Dutch West India Company operated one of the largest slave depots in the Caribbean here, and enslaved people were held in Willemstad before being sold and shipped throughout the Americas. This is not a footnote in the island's history. It is the central fact of it.

The Kura Hulanda Museum, built on the site of a former slave yard in the Otrobanda district, is the most comprehensive museum of the Atlantic slave trade in the Caribbean. It traces the full arc — from West African kingdoms before European contact, through the Middle Passage, to the plantation economies of the Americas. The collection is large, the documentation is thorough, and the museum does not soften what happened. It presents the mechanisms of the trade — the ships, the auctions, the legal frameworks that made human beings into property — with a directness that many museums in former colonial capitals still avoid.

You should go. It is not a comfortable experience and it is not meant to be. What it offers is context — the kind that makes the rest of what you see on the island, from the merchant houses to the fort to the demographic composition of the streets, legible in a way it isn't otherwise.

The Swinging Old Lady

The Queen Emma Bridge is a pontoon bridge that connects the two halves of Willemstad — Punda on the east side of Sint Annabaai and Otrobanda on the west. It floats on sixteen pontoons, and when a ship needs to pass through the harbor, the entire bridge swings open on a hinge at the Otrobanda end, pivoting sideways like a gate. You can stand on it while this happens if you're on the right side. If you're on the wrong side, you wait, or you take the free ferry.

Locals call it the Swinging Old Lady. The original bridge was built in 1888 by a businessman named Leonard Burlington Smith, who charged a toll: two cents for anyone wearing shoes, free for anyone barefoot. This was, in practice, a tax on wealth — the poor walked barefoot and crossed for nothing. The toll is gone, but the story persists, and it captures something real about how the island thinks about itself.

The bridge opens and closes several times a day, and the rhythm of it — the horn blast, the slow pivot, the ship gliding through — becomes part of the city's daily texture within a few hours. You learn to check the bridge before you walk toward it. Or you don't check, and you stand at the railing and watch a container ship pass close enough to read the hull markings.

Five languages in one sentence

The mother tongue of Curaçao is Papiamentu, and it is one of the most remarkable languages in the Americas. It is a creole that braids together Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch, English, and West African languages into something that sounds like none of them and carries pieces of all of them. A single sentence might pull its grammar from Portuguese, its vocabulary from Spanish, and its rhythm from the Akan and Igbo languages that enslaved Africans brought with them.

Papiamentu is what happens when five colonial languages and a dozen African ones share a kitchen for four centuries.

Papiamentu is not a simplified language. It has a full grammar, a written tradition, newspapers, literature, and pop music. It is the language of daily life — the one people use at home, at the market, in arguments, in love. Dutch is the official administrative language and the language of schooling, but Papiamentu is what you hear on the street. Most Curaçaoans also speak Spanish and English with varying degrees of fluency, which means the average person on this island of 150,000 moves between four languages in the course of a day without thinking much about it.

For a visitor, the effect is disorienting in the best way. You are on a Dutch island in the Caribbean where everyone speaks a Portuguese-based creole and switches to Spanish or English depending on who they're talking to. The cultural layers are not stacked neatly. They are woven together, and Papiamentu is the thread that holds them.

The wild side

The north coast of Curaçao looks nothing like the Caribbean you have in your head. The south coast, where Willemstad sits, has the calm turquoise water and the white-sand beaches. The north coast is something else entirely — volcanic rock, crashing surf, and a landscape that is closer to the arid scrubland of Aruba or the dry hills of the Venezuelan coast than to anything you'd call tropical. Christoffel National Park, at the island's northwest tip, is built around Mount Christoffel, the highest point on the island at 375 meters, and the hike to the top crosses terrain that feels more like the Canary Islands than the Caribbean.

The vegetation is adapted to drought. Divi-divi trees bent permanently sideways by the trade winds. Columnar cacti taller than you are. Aloe plants growing wild in rocky soil. Curaçao gets less rainfall than most of the Caribbean — it sits outside the hurricane belt, below the main rain bands — and the landscape reflects this. The island is not lush. It is lean and thorny and windblown, and if you only see the resort beaches on the south coast, you miss the thing that makes Curaçao geographically unusual.

The Shete Boka National Park, also on the north coast, is where the sea meets that volcanic rock. The bokas are inlets — narrow channels carved into the limestone where the waves funnel in and explode upward. Boka Tabla has a cave you can walk into and watch the surf crash through the opening. It is loud and violent and nothing like a beach day.

The orange that failed

Blue curaçao, the liqueur, is made from the laraha orange, a bitter citrus that grows only on this island. The laraha is a descendant of the Valencia orange, brought to Curaçao by Spanish colonists who expected it to thrive. It did not. The soil was too poor, the rainfall too scarce, and the fruit that the trees eventually produced was small and intensely bitter — inedible as a fruit. The colonists considered the trees a failure and abandoned them.

Then someone — the historical record is vague on exactly who — discovered that the dried peel of the laraha, when distilled, produced an aromatic oil with an extraordinary fragrance. The Senior family, who have been on the island since the nineteenth century, built a distillery at the Landhuis Chobolobo and began producing a liqueur from the peel. The blue color, it should be said, is artificial — added for visual distinction. The original liqueur is clear. But the laraha itself is real, and it grows nowhere else, and the fact that it exists at all is a consequence of agricultural failure turned into something worth drinking.

The Chobolobo distillery is still operating, still run by the Senior family, and you can visit it. The laraha trees grow in the yard. The fruit hangs on the branches looking like a small, ugly orange that nobody would eat. That is exactly what it is, and the drink made from it is served in bars on six continents.
