Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 22 min · 🚕 8 min → Teatro Colón
1. Teatro Colón
↳ Opened in 1908 and ranked among the top five opera houses on earth for acoustics. Guided tours thread through the gilded auditorium, rehearsal halls, costume workshops, and the vast stage — a behind-the-scenes look at a house that hosted Callas, Pavarotti, and Nureyev.

2. Palacio Barolo
↳ A 1923 art nouveau tower conceived as a three-dimensional rendering of Dante's Divine Comedy — Hell in the basement, Purgatory in the middle floors, Paradise in the lighthouse top. The spiral staircase is extraordinary, and the rooftop observation deck delivers one of the finest views in the city.

3. Casa Rosada
↳ The bubblegum-pink Presidential Palace faces Plaza de Mayo — where Eva Perón addressed the crowds and where the Mothers circled every Thursday for three decades. The free museum behind the palace tells Argentine political history from the colonial fort to the present.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Wednesday - Sunday 10:00am - 6:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday & Tuesday

🚶 15 min · 🚕 5 min → Puente de la Mujer
4. Puente de la Mujer
↳ Santiago Calatrava's slender white suspension bridge spins 90 degrees to let boats through, its inclined mast designed to evoke a tango couple mid-turn. It is the finest piece of modern architecture in the city — glowing white against the river, best photographed at dusk when the lights come on.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7

1. Recoleta Cemetery
↳ A city within the city — ornate mausoleums, marble angels, and bronze doors crowd paths past presidents, generals, and Eva Perón. The contrast between immaculate family vaults and others surrendering to ivy tells you everything about Argentina's turbulent fortunes.
📖 Wikipedia

🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Junín 1760 · Recoleta
🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → Floralis Genérica
2. Floralis Genérica
↳ Eduardo Catalano's 23-tonne steel-and-aluminum flower stands in a reflecting pool in Plaza de las Naciones Unidas, its six petals opening with the rising sun and folding closed at dusk. A genuinely strange and beautiful object — silvery, monumental, and oddly moving when it moves.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~20 min
📍 Plaza de las Naciones Unidas · Recoleta
🚕 22 min → Caminito
3. Caminito
↳ The 150-metre alley that launched a thousand postcards — corrugated iron tenements painted in clashing reds, yellows, and blues, balconies strung with laundry, murals running the full length. The colours came from surplus dock paint in the 1880s when immigrants built homes from whatever the shipyards discarded.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Caminito S/N · La Boca
🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → Fundación PROA
4. Fundación PROA
↳ Housed in a 19th-century building at the edge of Caminito with the Riachuelo flowing past the back terrace, PROA is Buenos Aires's best contemporary art museum. It consistently pulls major international shows — Duchamp, Warhol, Malevich have all shown here.
📖 Wikipedia

🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 27 min · 🚕 9 min → MALBA
1. MALBA – Museo de Arte Latinoamericano de Buenos Aires
↳ Opened in 2001, MALBA holds over 700 works of Latin American modern and contemporary art — Frida Kahlo, Diego Rivera, Tarsila do Amaral, Xul Solar, and Wifredo Lam among them. The limestone, steel, and glass building was selected by a jury that included Norman Foster, César Pelli, and Mario Botta.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Wednesday 11:00am - 8:00pm · Thursday - Monday 12:00pm - 8:00pm
🚫 Closed Tuesday
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min

🚶 8 min · 🚕 4 min → Jardín Japonés
2. Jardín Japonés
↳ Established in 1967 and expanded in 1979 when Emperor Hirohito donated a stone lantern, the garden covers 5.5 hectares within Parque 3 de Febrero. A wooden Yumendo bridge, tea pavilion, koi ponds, and arch bridge are the main features. One of the largest Japanese gardens outside Japan.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 6:45pm
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Av. Casares 3450 · Palermo
🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → Parque 3 de Febrero
3. Parque 3 de Febrero · Bosques de Palermo
↳ Buenos Aires's main urban park — approximately 400 hectares of lakes, rose gardens, and woodland. Laid out in the 1870s on land belonging to dictator Rosas, it was named for the date of his defeat in 1852. The Rosedal holds 18,000 rose plants; the park also contains a rowing lake and the Galileo Galilei Planetarium.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Av. del Libertador · Palermo
🚶 8 min · 🚕 4 min → Monumento de los Españoles
4. Monumento de los Españoles
↳ A 24.5-metre marble and bronze column commissioned by the Spanish community for the 1910 centennial of the May Revolution. Sculptor Agustín Querol died before completion; it was unveiled in 1927. The four allegorical figures at the base represent the Andes, the Chaco, the Pampas, and the Río de la Plata.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~20 min
📍 Av. del Libertador y Sarmiento · Palermo

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 12 min → Iglesia Nuestra Señora de Belén
1. Iglesia Nuestra Señora de Belén
↳ The oldest colonial church in Buenos Aires, begun by Jesuit architect Andrés Blanqui in 1734 and inaugurated in 1752. The two towers were finished in 1876 by Pedro Benoit. Declared a National Historical Monument in 1942, the interior has a barrel-vaulted central nave and original colonial decorative elements.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 7:00pm
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Humberto Primo 340 · San Telmo
🚶 8 min · 🚕 4 min → Parque Lezama
2. Parque Lezama
↳ The oldest public park in Buenos Aires, traditionally identified as the site of Pedro de Mendoza's 1536 founding. The estate belonged to José Gregorio de Lezama before the city acquired it in 1894. The park contains the Museo Histórico Nacional, a Russian Orthodox Cathedral with blue domes, and an amphitheatre.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Defensa y Brasil · San Telmo
🚶 2 min · 🚕 2 min → Museo Histórico Nacional
3. Museo Histórico Nacional
↳ Founded in 1889 and housed in the former Lezama mansion in Parque Lezama, the museum holds Argentina's primary collection from the May Revolution and War of Independence — portraits, weapons, San Martín's sabre, flags, and documents tracing the country's history from colony to republic. Admission is free.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Wednesday - Sunday 11:00am - 7:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday & Tuesday
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Defensa 1600 · San Telmo
🚶 10 min · 🚕 4 min → Plaza Dorrego
4. Plaza Dorrego
↳ The focal square of San Telmo, renamed in 1905. Every Sunday since 1970, nearly 300 antique vendors line the square and Calle Defensa, selling vintage silver, vinyl records, furniture, and clocks. Tango dancers perform between stalls; the surrounding cafés occupy the same 19th-century buildings.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~45 min

🍮 Local Tastes
Asado
↳ Open-fire beef grilling is Argentina's sacred ritual — bife de chorizo or entraña over wood embers, served with chimichurri and provoleta cheese. Order jugoso for medium-rare and never rush the meal.
📍 Don Julio · La Brigada · El Mirasol
Empanadas
↳ Half-moon pastry pockets filled with spiced ground beef, chicken, corn, or cheese — baked or fried, eaten by hand. The folded edge pattern tells you the filling.
📍 Any traditional corner bakery
Dulce de Leche
↳ Argentina's national obsession — thick amber caramel made from slowly reduced sweetened milk. Spread on toast, dolloped on ice cream, or eaten from the jar. Havanna brand is the gold standard.
📍 Havanna
Malbec
↳ Argentina's signature grape — deep purple, plum-forward, and unlike the same variety made anywhere else. Look for Zuccardi, Catena Zapata, or Achaval Ferrer on wine lists.
📍 Any parrilla or wine shop

✨ Claude Inspiration

Buenos Aires is a city built by people who left somewhere else and never quite stopped mourning it — Italian facades, French boulevards, Spanish language, and a tango that sounds like all of that grief at once.

The mausoleums of Recoleta are the most honest thing in town. A city that builds houses for its dead the size of houses for its living is a city that takes memory seriously. Walk slowly. The sadness is the point.
