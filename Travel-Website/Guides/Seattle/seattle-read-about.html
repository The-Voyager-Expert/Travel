A city that built itself on timber and rain and reinvented itself twice before most cities had finished their first act — and still has a mountain it can't quite see most of the year.

The skid that named a neighborhood

There's a term in American English — "skid row," meaning the disreputable part of town where the destitute congregate — and it comes directly from Seattle. Henry Yesler built a steam sawmill on the waterfront in 1853 and needed to get timber down from the hills above, so he cut a road and greased it so the logs would slide. This was called Yesler's Skid Road. The logging crews who worked it were rough men who spent their time off in the taverns and brothels at the bottom of the hill. When this pattern repeated in other American cities, the disreputable district always got the same name. Seattle gave the language that word.

The city was logging country before it was anything else. The Douglas firs here were extraordinary — some of them two hundred feet tall, eight feet in diameter, a thousand years old — and the Pacific coast had no shortage of ships that needed masts and spars and lumber. Seattle found its early identity and its early money in converting those trees into board-feet, and then into ships, and then into anything else the bay and the railroad could carry. This extractive relationship with the landscape set a pattern: Seattle has always been a city that finds something the world wants, takes it as fast as possible, and then figures out what comes next.

The fire and the city underneath your feet

On June 6, 1889, a cabinetmaker's assistant knocked over a glue pot in a basement workshop on First Avenue. The fire that spread from that pot burned for about eighteen hours and destroyed twenty-five city blocks — the entire commercial center of Seattle. Nobody died, which is notable given the scale, but the city was effectively gone.

What happened next is the part worth knowing. Seattle rebuilt immediately, but this time it made a decision: the new buildings would be raised one to two stories above the original street grade. The old streets were too low, too close to the tideflats, perpetually flooding at high tide. So Seattle rebuilt its downtown on top of its old downtown. The original street-level storefronts, sidewalks, and building entrances still exist underground — sealed off when the elevated city was built above them, forgotten for decades, rediscovered in the 1960s. You can tour them as Bill Speidel's Underground Tour, which has been running since 1965 and is worth doing once. You walk through Victorian-era storefronts and pharmacies and the original sidewalks, all underground, in the dark, under the streets you arrived on. It is genuinely strange.

Seattle literally built a new city on top of its ruined one and sealed the old city beneath the sidewalks. The underground is still there. You walk over it every time you're in Pioneer Square.

Pioneer Square, the neighborhood that grew up in the rebuilt blocks after the fire, is the oldest neighborhood in the city and one of the most undervisited. It has the Romanesque revival brick buildings that went up in the early 1890s, the preserved ironwork pergola at First and Yesler (rebuilt after a truck hit it in 2001), and Occidental Square, which on any given afternoon has either a farmers market or a group playing bocce or nothing at all, depending entirely on the season and the weather. It is a neighborhood that has cycled through boom and bust enough times to have a certain philosophical equanimity about the whole process.

The water is everywhere and it matters

Seattle is not a city that happens to be near water. It is a city defined and confined by water. Elliott Bay lies to the west. Lake Union sits in the center. Lake Washington forms the entire eastern boundary. The Ship Canal connects the lakes to the bay. The city is, in effect, a narrow peninsula and several surrounding islands connected by bridges, and the water that surrounds it is not scenery — it's structural.

This means that getting anywhere in Seattle involves crossing water. The floating bridges on Lake Washington — the Mercer Island and 520 bridges — are the longest floating pontoon bridges in the world, and they flex visibly in storms. The ferries that run across Puget Sound to Bainbridge Island and Vashon and the Kitsap Peninsula are not tourist boats; they are the commuting infrastructure for a significant part of the metropolitan population. Taking the Bainbridge Island ferry from the terminal near Pike Place Market is one of the best things you can do in Seattle — thirty-five minutes each way, the Olympic Mountains out one side, the city coming and going on the other, and Bainbridge itself worth an afternoon.

The fishing culture is real. Pike Place Market, which opened in 1907 and is the oldest continuously operating farmers market in the country, has always been anchored by the fish vendors. The famous fish-throwing is a performance put on for tourists now, but the fish is genuinely good. The Dungeness crab here is some of the best in the world, and the salmon — king, sockeye, coho — is not something you need to leave Seattle to eat better. If you eat fish at all, eat it here.

Boeing and the aerospace years

William Boeing launched his first seaplane from Lake Union in June 1916, and the company he built defined Seattle for the better part of a century. At its peak in the 1960s, one in ten people in the Seattle metropolitan area worked for Boeing or a Boeing supplier. The company built the B-17 bomber here, the B-52, the 707 that opened the jet age, the 747 that made mass international air travel possible. When you fly anywhere on a widebody jet, there is a good chance that the engineering that makes it work was developed in the Puget Sound basin.

The Boeing downturn of the early 1970s was so severe that someone put up a billboard near the airport that read: "Will the last person leaving Seattle turn out the lights?" The city lost a third of its jobs in two years. That moment — the "Boeing Bust" — is still in the city's memory as a lesson about what happens when you build your entire economy around one company, and it is why Seattle was more prepared, emotionally if not economically, for the shifts that came later.

The Museum of Flight, at Boeing Field south of downtown, is one of the best aviation museums in the world. It has a Concorde, an Air Force One that served Eisenhower through Nixon, the prototype for the 747 (the plane that changed everything about long-haul travel), and the restoration of the original Boeing "Red Barn" factory where William Boeing built his first planes. If you have any interest in aviation at all — or in what industrial-scale American ambition looked like at its height — this is worth a half day.

The sound that came from the drizzle

Grunge is usually described as a music genre, which is accurate but misses the point. What happened in Seattle in the late 1980s and early 1990s was a specific conjunction of geography, economics, and attitude. The city was isolated — before the tech boom, it was genuinely far from the cultural centers of America, too far north and too rainy to attract the kind of industry attention that would have smoothed the roughness off things. The clubs were small. The labels were local. The production values were low not as an aesthetic choice but because that was what anyone could afford.

What came out of that isolation was Nirvana and Pearl Jam and Soundgarden and Alice in Chains and dozens of bands that never made it out of the state. The sound was heavy and abrasive and depressive and also, in odd moments, quite beautiful. It was not music designed to be cheerful. The Pacific Northwest in winter — the months of low gray sky and constant drizzle, the darkness that falls at four in the afternoon — produces a particular interior weather, and grunge was the sound of that interior weather given form.

Kurt Cobain grew up in Aberdeen, two hours south of Seattle, and moved to Olympia before the band settled in Seattle. He died in a house on Lake Washington in 1994. The house still exists; people leave flowers at the bench in Viretta Park next to it.

The EMP Museum — now called the Museum of Pop Culture, MoPOP — has an extensive grunge exhibition with instruments and stage clothes and original recordings. But the better grunge education is to go to a show at the Crocodile, the club on Second Avenue where Nirvana played unannounced in 1993. It is a real music venue that still books real bands. The history is in the walls, not on a placard.

What happened when the internet arrived

Amazon moved into South Lake Union in 2010 and proceeded to build a campus that has functionally transformed that neighborhood and significant parts of the neighborhoods around it. Before Amazon, South Lake Union was a light industrial district with auto shops and low-rent warehouses. Now it has the Amazon Spheres — three glass biomes full of tropical plants that function as a living space for Amazon employees — and several million square feet of office space and apartment towers and restaurants catering to tech workers with money and not much time.

This has made Seattle wealthier and more expensive and more culturally homogeneous in some ways, less so in others. The Pike/Pine corridor on Capitol Hill, which was the city's alternative-culture neighborhood, has held on better than many expected — partly because Capitol Hill has a density and a community identity that proved more resistant to the tech monoculture than South Lake Union. The International District, just south of Pioneer Square, has also held on, though the pressures are real and the population has shifted.

Seattle is still adjusting to what it has become. The city that prided itself on its distance from the mainstream is now, in measurable ways, the mainstream. That reckoning is ongoing and visible if you talk to people who've been here long enough to remember both versions. The right response is not nostalgia — the pre-tech Seattle had genuine problems alongside its genuine character — but the question of what the city actually values, and who it makes space for, is live in a way that makes Seattle interesting to watch.

The mountain and what it means

Mount Rainier is 14,411 feet tall, sits about 59 miles southeast of the city, and on clear days appears so close and so enormous that people who see it for the first time often pull their cars over. It is not just a mountain in the landscape — it is a presence that the city organizes around, the way you orient toward a landmark in a room. When locals say "the mountain is out," they don't mean it arrived recently. They mean the clouds have cleared enough to see it, which happens less often than you'd think. Seattle gets around 150 sunny days a year, and many of those days the mountain is visible but the lower flanks are socked in. A clear shot of Rainier from the city — full peak, full volcano silhouette, usually with a lenticular cloud forming on the summit — is something people stop what they're doing to notice.

Rainier is also an active stratovolcano with a large population living on its debris flow paths. The USGS considers it one of the highest-risk volcanoes in the country, not because it's likely to erupt soon but because an eruption would be catastrophic for the river valleys and towns downstream. This fact exists peacefully alongside the hundreds of thousands of people who hike and ski on its slopes every year. Seattle has a specific genius for holding geological reality and human intention in the same frame without either one canceling the other.

Before you leave, here's the thing to carry: Seattle's defining quality is not the coffee or the rain or even the mountains, though all of those are real. It's the city's practiced ability to start over. The fire in 1889, the Boeing bust in 1971, the grunge era ending in tragedy, the dot-com crash — each time, the city rebuilt on top of what it had been. The underground is literal. Every version of Seattle is built on the previous one, and the city is better for having buried its mistakes under a new street grade rather than pretending they didn't happen. Once you understand that, you see it in the way new buildings go up next to the Victorian brick, in the way the fish market and the tech campus sit two blocks apart, in the way the mountain disappears and reappears over the course of a single afternoon.
