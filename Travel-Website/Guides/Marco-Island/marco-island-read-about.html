The largest of Florida's Ten Thousand Islands — built on Calusa shell mounds, shaped by a 1960s resort boom, and edged by one of the least-altered coastal wilderness systems in North America.

The Island That Time Built

Marco Island is the largest of a chain of barrier islands at the southwestern tip of Florida, separated from the mainland by the mangrove waterways of the Ten Thousand Islands. It is a young island in geological terms — formed from the accumulated shell debris of the Calusa people, who lived here for at least 2,000 years before European contact and who built shell mounds large enough to serve as foundations for entire neighborhoods. The ground beneath the island's resort hotels is largely composed of oyster and whelk shells, the compressed residue of one of the most productive pre-Columbian civilizations in North America.

The island is small — about six miles long and four miles wide — and its geography is defined almost entirely by water. The Gulf of Mexico runs along its western edge as a long, flat beach. The eastern and southern edges break into a maze of tidal passes, channels, and mangrove keys that eventually dissolve into the Ten Thousand Islands, a 35-mile stretch of coastal wilderness that is one of the least-altered coastal ecosystems on the Atlantic coast of the United States. Between the Gulf beach and the mangrove edge, the island is dense with canals and waterways that were dredged in the 1960s during the original resort development — there is no interior to speak of, only a grid of waterways and the residential and commercial streets between them.

The Calusa

The Calusa were not an agricultural society. In a pre-Columbian world where civilizational complexity was almost invariably tied to the cultivation of crops, the Calusa built a hierarchical chiefdom of perhaps 50,000 people sustained entirely by the marine resources of the Ten Thousand Islands estuary. They harvested fish, shellfish, manatees, and sea turtles from the same mangrove channels that exist today, developing a sophisticated technology for trapping and processing marine resources at a scale that could support urban densities.

Their capital, Mound Key, sits in Estero Bay about 40 miles north of Marco Island and is made almost entirely of accumulated shell debris — a human-made island constructed over 2,000 years of habitation. Calusa shell middens extend throughout the Ten Thousand Islands, and the largest were substantial enough to raise land above the tidal range and support permanent settlements. Marco Island contains several such mounds; the historical museum sits adjacent to one of the most significant.

The Calusa were also formidable. When the Spanish explorer Ponce de León arrived in 1513, the Calusa chief Carlos refused to submit and, according to Spanish accounts, met the Spanish with hostility. When Ponce de León returned in 1521 to establish a colony, the Calusa attacked the settlement and wounded Ponce de León with an arrow; he died shortly after returning to Cuba. The Spanish never established a colony in Calusa territory. The Calusa eventually collapsed in the early 1700s, reduced by epidemic disease and raids by Creek people armed with English weapons; the survivors are thought to have fled to Cuba. Their culture left no living descendants in Florida.

The Key Marco Cat — a six-inch wooden figure carved around 700 CE — was discovered in 1896 during an archaeological expedition led by Frank Hamilton Cushing. Cushing's team excavated a waterlogged midden on the island and found a remarkable collection of wooden objects preserved by the anaerobic conditions: masks, figurines, tools, and the cat. The cat, which depicts a crouching feline with a human torso, was still painted when it emerged from the midden; the paint faded within hours of exposure to air. It is now in the Smithsonian collection and is considered one of the finest examples of pre-Columbian wood carving in North America.

The Resort Era

Marco Island's modern history begins in 1964, when the Deltona Corporation purchased the island from the Mackle family and began a large-scale planned resort development. The Mackle brothers had bought the island in 1962, and their sale to Deltona triggered one of the most aggressive resort developments in Florida history: 25,000 residential lots platted, 100 miles of canals dredged, the mangrove fringe stripped from much of the eastern shore, and a modern grid of roads, utilities, and commercial infrastructure installed on what had been undeveloped wilderness.

The environmental impact of the Deltona development was significant and became part of the national conversation about coastal development in the early 1970s. The Army Corps of Engineers eventually denied Deltona's permit to continue dredging on the south end of the island, and the resulting legal and regulatory battles helped establish the precedent that wetland destruction required federal permits. The intact mangrove fringe that now protects the southern and eastern shores of Marco Island — the same mangroves that the Ten Thousand Islands kayak tours navigate — survived partly because the 1970s regulatory environment stopped Deltona from finishing the job.

The JW Marriott Marco Island Beach Resort opened in 1964 as the Marco Beach Hotel and has been the island's anchor resort for its entire modern history. The current building dates from a major renovation in the 2010s, but the site has operated continuously as the island's flagship hotel for six decades.

The Ten Thousand Islands

The Ten Thousand Islands is not a precise number — the actual count of distinct keys, mangrove islands, and sand bars that make up the coastal maze between Marco Island and Everglades City varies depending on how you count, and most of the "islands" are uninhabited mangrove tangles barely above the tidal range. What makes the Ten Thousand Islands remarkable is the scale of what remains intact: one of the largest mangrove forest systems in North America, with very little human development, supporting populations of bottlenose dolphins, West Indian manatees, American crocodiles, nesting bald eagles, roseate spoonbills, wood storks, and a fish diversity that reflects the extraordinary productivity of an estuary with almost no agricultural or industrial impact upstream.

The mangroves that line the islands perform a precise ecological function: they filter nutrients from terrestrial runoff, provide nursery habitat for juvenile fish, and protect the coastline from storm surge. The same function that makes them valuable ecologically — their capacity to bind sediment and accumulate organic material — is what made them attractive to the Calusa, who used the midden material to build elevated ground above the tidal zone. The Ten Thousand Islands National Wildlife Refuge was established in 1996 and protects much of the remaining wilderness; the section nearest to Marco Island is accessible by kayak from Caxambas Park.

Cape Romano

The Cape Romano dome houses — six concrete domes built in 1982 by a Missouri businessman named Bob Lee as an off-grid vacation compound — have become Marco Island's most distinctive landmark, partly because of their unusual architecture and partly because they are actively disappearing. Lee built the domes on Cape Romano, a barrier peninsula at the southern tip of the Ten Thousand Islands, and designed them as a self-sufficient solar-powered structure with rainwater collection and a septic system. By 1992, erosion had begun undermining the foundations; Hurricane Andrew in 1992 and subsequent storms moved the domes progressively closer to the water. Hurricane Ian in 2022 significantly accelerated the process. The domes now sit half-submerged in the Gulf at high tide, their interiors flooded, their concrete cracking, surrounded by water on all sides. They are accessible only by boat and are visible from the water during tours of the Ten Thousand Islands.

Goodland

Goodland is not part of Marco Island proper — it is a separate small community at the end of Goodland Road, connected to the main island by a bridge over Goodland Bay. Its roughly 300 year-round residents constitute one of the last Old Florida fishing communities in the state: a village of stilt houses on canals, working shrimp boats, fish houses, and a bar — Stan's Idle Hour — that has hosted the "Buzzard Lope," a participatory dance-and-celebration event, every Sunday afternoon for decades. The Little Bar Restaurant, on the canal, serves stone crab claws and smoked mullet in the kind of fish-camp setting that has largely been replaced across Florida by chain restaurants and resort cuisine.

Goodland sits on the edge of the Marco River, which separates the island from the mangrove maze of the Ten Thousand Islands. The village's population has remained largely stable for decades; its isolation — a single road in, no commercial development to speak of — has protected it from the resort development that transformed the rest of Marco Island. It remains the most direct access point to the working fishing culture of Southwest Florida.

The Wilderness Edge

The experience of Marco Island is the experience of a resort community at the edge of genuine wilderness — a configuration unusual even in Florida, where coastal development has generally proceeded until the wilderness is gone. The Ten Thousand Islands and Big Cypress National Preserve together form one of the largest protected wilderness areas east of the Mississippi; Everglades National Park begins about 30 miles southeast of Marco Island. The ecological systems that support the Ten Thousand Islands — the slow freshwater sheet flow from Lake Okeechobee through the Everglades, the tidal exchange through Caxambas Pass, the mangrove fringe that buffers the estuary from the Gulf — are the same systems that supported the Calusa for 2,000 years. They are also under significant pressure from the altered hydrology of South Florida's water management system and from the cumulative effects of coastal development. What the Calusa built on Marco Island, and what the Ten Thousand Islands still looks like from a kayak, are both part of the same story about the consequences of how humans have chosen to use this coast.
