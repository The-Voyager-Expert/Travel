The former capital of the largest empire in the pre-Columbian Americas — and a city where the Inca didn't disappear so much as go underground, and the Spanish built on top.

The navel of the world

The name Cusco comes from the Quechua word Qusqu, which most scholars translate as "navel of the world." The Inca did not mean this modestly. At its height in the fifteenth century, their empire — Tawantinsuyu, the Four Regions Together — stretched roughly 2,500 miles along the spine of the Andes, from what is now southern Colombia to central Chile, encompassing perhaps twelve million people and dozens of distinct cultures. Cusco was its administrative and spiritual center, the place from which all four quarters of the empire radiated, the highest-altitude major city in the Americas.

You arrive at 3,400 meters above sea level. The first thing the altitude does is slow your blood. Walking uphill feels like walking through water. Breath comes shorter. Some people get headaches; some feel nauseous; most feel vaguely floaty for the first day. This is not a warning — it is the beginning of understanding the Inca. They built their civilization at this altitude, across terrain that would have stopped any sea-level army in its tracks. Every stone you see was moved by people who lived their whole lives at altitude and considered this the normal condition of human existence. The thin air is not a problem to be managed. It is the medium in which everything here was built.

What the Inca actually built

The Spanish who arrived here in 1533 with Francisco Pizarro were literate people who wrote down what they saw, and what they wrote is useful because it tells you what the city looked like before they dismantled most of it. Cusco in the Inca period had a population of perhaps 100,000 — enormous by the standards of pre-Columbian urbanism — organized around a grid of ceremonial spaces and administrative compounds, with the major temples and palaces clad in hammered gold sheeting that made the whole city gleam in the Andean sun.

The masonry is what survives, and it is the thing that will stop you. Inca stonework — the polygonal blocks fitted without mortar, each surface ground to a tolerance that allows no gap between them — is unlike any other masonry tradition in the world. The stones were quarried miles away and moved without wheeled carts or draft animals larger than llamas, shaped with stone tools, and fitted together so precisely that a knife blade cannot be inserted between them. Earthquakes have leveled colonial buildings in Cusco multiple times; the Inca walls beneath them barely move.

The Spanish didn't destroy the Inca walls. They built on top of them. Walk any block in the historic center and you're walking on foundations the Inca laid.

The street called Hatun Rumiyoq — in the San Blas neighborhood, five minutes from the Plaza de Armas — has what's known as the Stone of Twelve Angles, a boulder cut to fit twelve adjoining stones so perfectly that the shape looks artificial, like something generated by software. It is one stone, cut by hand, at altitude, five hundred years ago. People photograph it constantly, and the photograph never captures it. You have to run your fingers along the joins.

The temple that became a church

Qorikancha — the Temple of the Sun — was the most sacred site in the Inca empire. Its walls were lined with gold panels. Its garden contained life-size replicas of Andean plants and animals, cast in gold and silver. Pizarro's men stripped it of all of this within months of their arrival, melted it down, and sent it to Spain. The temple structure itself was given to the Dominican order, who built the church of Santo Domingo directly on top of it.

What you see today is one of the most revealing buildings in the Western Hemisphere: a baroque Spanish colonial church sitting precisely on the foundations and lower walls of the Inca temple, with several of the original Inca chambers still visible and accessible behind the church. Stand in the main courtyard of the complex and look at the seam between the Inca stonework on the lower course and the colonial masonry above it — rougher, mortar-dependent, less precise — and the entire history of the conquest becomes visible in a single wall. The Dominicans built their church the way they built their civilization: on top of what they found, using it as a foundation, trying to erase what was underneath.

An earthquake in 1950 damaged the colonial structure and left the Inca walls intact. The Spanish built churches that cracked; the Inca built temples that didn't.

Sacsayhuamán and the question of labor

Walk uphill from the Plaza de Armas for about twenty minutes and you reach Sacsayhuamán, the massive ceremonial and military complex on the ridge above the city. The scale is genuinely startling. The outer walls are built from limestone blocks, some of them weighing more than 100 tons, arranged in three parallel zigzag tiers that climb the hillside. The largest single stone in the complex weighs an estimated 360 tons.

The question that every visitor eventually asks — how? — has no fully satisfying answer. The Inca had no iron tools, no wheeled vehicles, no draft animals larger than llamas (which can carry about 30 kilograms and are not built for hauling boulders). What they had was mit'a: a labor tax by which every household in the empire owed a certain number of days of work to the state each year. The Inca state fed, housed, and supplied every worker performing mit'a duty. It was not slavery in the way the Spanish later used the word — workers were compensated in kind and rotated regularly — but it was compulsory. Sacsayhuamán was built by an organized labor system of extraordinary sophistication, and it tells you something about the empire that built it: this was a bureaucracy as much as a civilization.

The complex was partially dismantled by the Spanish, who used its stones to build the colonial city below. Even so, what remains is enough to understand the scale of what was here. Stand at the top tier at dusk, with Cusco below you and the Sacred Valley opening to the north, and you are standing at the center of the world the Inca built. They would recognize the view.

The city that emerged from the rubble

The conquest of Cusco was not a clean operation. After Pizarro executed Atahualpa in 1533, a puppet emperor named Manco Inca cooperated with the Spanish for two years before launching a massive rebellion in 1536. The siege of Cusco lasted months. Sacsayhuamán changed hands. The Spanish held on, barely, and when Manco Inca withdrew to establish a successor state in the jungle at Vilcabamba, he took the remaining Inca resistance with him. The city the Spanish rebuilt was already mostly rubble.

What the Spanish created on top of it is the Cusco you walk through: a colonial city of whitewashed walls, baroque churches, and arcaded plazas, all of it organized around the Plaza de Armas that Pizarro laid out in 1534. The churches were built over Inca temples — Qorikancha under Santo Domingo, the palace of Pachacuti under the cathedral, the palace of another emperor under La Compañía de Jesús — in a deliberate policy of substitution. Sacred Inca space became sacred Christian space. The population converted, at least formally. Quechua survived.

Today Cusco has around 450,000 people, a significant portion of whom are Quechua-speaking, and the city operates simultaneously as a Peruvian provincial capital, a major tourist destination, and a living indigenous city. The women you see selling textiles in the Plaza de Armas are wearing traditional Quechua dress — not for tourists, but because that is what they wear. The market at San Pedro sells coca leaves alongside strawberries and phone cases. The city is neither a museum nor a purely modern place. It is both at once, and the friction between those two facts is what makes it worth paying attention to.

San Blas and what the artisans kept

The neighborhood of San Blas, climbing the hillside northeast of the Plaza de Armas in a tangle of narrow lanes, has been the city's artisan quarter since the colonial period. The craftspeople here — woodcarvers, weavers, ceramic artists, silversmiths — maintained pre-Columbian techniques through the colonial period by integrating them into Catholic devotional objects. The Andean imagery the Spanish tried to erase survived by hiding inside it: the sun disk that represents Inti, the Inca sun god, reappeared as the face of Christ in colonial altarpieces. Andean fertility figures were dressed as saints. Indigenous cosmology persisted inside Christian iconography.

The small church of San Blas has the most celebrated example: a pulpit carved from a single cedar trunk in the late seventeenth century, attributed by tradition to a Quechua artist, that is one of the masterpieces of colonial Baroque carving anywhere in the Americas. At its apex is a human skull — said to be the artist's own, though this is legend rather than fact — ringed by cherubs and saints and vines that, if you look closely, have Andean faces. The church holds it as a Christian object. It is also something else.

What Machu Picchu is, and isn't

You are going to Machu Picchu. Everyone goes to Machu Picchu. It is one of the most photographed places on earth and it is, despite all of that, genuinely extraordinary. Nothing really prepares you for the scale and the setting.

But here is what Machu Picchu is not: the heart of the Inca empire. It was an estate — a royal retreat, built by the Inca emperor Pachacuti in the mid-fifteenth century, occupied by perhaps a few hundred people at any given time, abandoned after the Spanish conquest, and unknown to the outside world until Hiram Bingham arrived in 1911. It was never a major city, never a military stronghold, never the kind of place that commanded an empire. Its significance is architectural and environmental — the integration of the buildings with the mountain is an achievement of stunning intelligence — but the meaning that visitors often project onto it (the lost capital, the secret heart of a vanished civilization) is a projection.

The real centers of Inca power were Cusco and the Sacred Valley. Machu Picchu was where the emperor went to be alone.

Go anyway. Go because the Inca chose the most beautiful place they could find and built something worthy of it, and because standing there at dawn before the tour groups arrive, with the mist burning off the peaks and the Urubamba River cutting through the valley far below, you understand something about what it meant to be the most powerful civilization in the hemisphere and to choose this particular spot for your private mountain estate. The scale of ambition is its own answer.

What Cusco carries

Before you walk into the Plaza de Armas for the first time, here is the thing to carry with you: every surface you see in Cusco is a palimpsest. The colonial church was built on an Inca temple. The Inca temple was built on an earlier ceremonial site. The streets follow Inca channels. The plaza fills on feast days with indigenous Peruvians in dress that predates the Spanish by centuries, performing Catholic rituals that contain Andean ceremonies inside them the way nesting dolls contain each other.

The Inca empire lasted less than a hundred years at its height. The Spanish colonial period lasted three centuries. Independent Peru has existed for two hundred years. And Quechua — the language of the Inca, spoken in various forms across the Andes from Colombia to Argentina — is still the first language of millions of people, including many in the city you are walking through right now. Civilizations declared dead have a way of continuing anyway, underground, in the language, in the weaving patterns, in the agricultural terraces that still produce food on the same hillsides they were cut into five centuries ago. Once you see it, you see it in everything. The stones are not ruins. They are the foundation.
