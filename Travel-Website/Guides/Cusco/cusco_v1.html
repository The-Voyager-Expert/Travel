Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 3 min · 🚕 2 min → Plaza de Armas
1. Plaza de Armas
↳ The civic heart of Cusco — a vast colonial plaza built atop the Inca ceremonial center of Huacaypata, where the empire once assembled for processions and festivals. The Cathedral and La Compañía frame two sides; the arcaded portals shelter restaurants and bars with balcony views over the square.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Plaza de Armas · Centro Histórico
🚶 4 min · 🚕 2 min → Cusco Cathedral
2. Cusco Cathedral
↳ Built 1560–1654 on Inca palace foundations using stones from Sacsayhuamán, this is the masterwork of colonial Cusco. The interior holds 400 Cusco School paintings — including Marcos Zapata's Last Supper where Christ dines on guinea pig and chicha — and a silver altar weighing 1,500 kg.
📖 Wikipedia

3. La Compañía de Jesús
↳ Built 1651–1668 by Jesuits on the palace of Huayna Cápac, this Baroque façade was so theatrically overdone the Pope ordered construction halted before it could eclipse the Cathedral. The gilded interior and carved portals are the finest Spanish Baroque work in Cusco.

4. San Blas
↳ Cusco's artisan quarter since colonial times — steep cobblestone lanes and working workshops of weavers, ceramicists, and silversmiths. The church holds a single cedar pulpit carved from one tree by an anonymous Inca craftsman, considered the finest woodcarving in the Americas.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 1 min · 🚕 2 min → Qorikancha
1. Qorikancha
↳ The Inca Temple of the Sun — walls once sheathed in gold sheet. The Spanish built the Convent of Santo Domingo directly atop the Inca foundations. Precision-fitted Inca masonry visible inside the cloister arches, perfectly intact while the colonial church above it sags. The finest stonework in Cusco.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Sacsayhuamán
↳ The Inca Empire's greatest monument — three zigzagging rampart walls of blocks up to 125 tonnes, fitted without mortar at 3,700 m above sea level by tens of thousands of workers over 70 years. The Spanish quarried it for generations and still could not remove all the stones.
📖 Wikipedia

🚶 12 min · 🚕 5 min → Cristo Blanco
3. Cristo Blanco
↳ A 7.5-meter white Christ figure, arms extended, on a hilltop above Cusco — a gift from Palestinian immigrants in 1944. The panoramic view is the reason to come: the city spreads below in terracotta and white, the valley beyond, and Andean peaks on the horizon. The finest free viewpoint in Cusco.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Colina de Cristo Blanco · above Sacsayhuamán
🚶 18 min · 🚕 8 min → Q'enqo
4. Q'enqo
↳ A labyrinthine Inca oracle site — channels, niches, and underground galleries carved into a limestone outcrop. The name means 'zigzag' in Quechua, referring to channels where priests poured chicha and llama blood to read omens. The cave below holds a stone table believed used for mummification.

🍮 Local Tastes
Cuy
↳ Guinea pig roasted whole or fried — a genuine Andean staple for thousands of years, not a tourist novelty. The skin crisps, the dark meat resembles rabbit. Marcos Zapata painted it on the Cathedral's Last Supper. The definitive Cusco dish.
📍 Pachapapa · Campo Cocina Andina
Choclo con Queso · Andean Giant Corn
↳ Giant white Andean corn served with fresh white cheese — kernels four times the size of sweet corn, starchier and less sweet. A staple cultivated here for millennia. Found at vendors and Mercado San Pedro.
📍 Mercado San Pedro
Chicharrón de Cerdo
↳ Pork belly braised then deep-fried until the skin blisters and the fat renders into a crackling shell, served with corn and potatoes. A Cusco breakfast classic sold from dawn at Mercado San Pedro and surrounding stalls.
📍 Mercado San Pedro
Chicha de Jora
↳ Fermented corn beer made from germinated yellow corn, boiled and left to ferment. Slightly sour, mildly alcoholic, and cloudy — an Andean staple predating the Inca. A white flag above a door marks a chichería serving fresh chicha.
📍 Museo del Pisco
Pisco Sour
↳ Peru's national cocktail: pisco grape brandy, fresh lime, simple syrup, egg white shaken to a foam, and Angostura bitters on top. The altitude at 3,400 m means the effect arrives faster than expected. Every bar in Cusco has a version.
📍 Museo del Pisco · Garibaldi Cocktail Bar

🍋 From the Notebook

Cusco is a city that operates on two timelines simultaneously. The calendar you arrive with — the one that includes check-in times and tour reservations and altitude sickness remedies — sits on top of a much older chronology that the city itself has not forgotten. Walk far enough from the Plaza de Armas in any direction and you will find Inca stonework still in place under the foundations of Spanish churches, colonial mansions, and contemporary restaurants. The fit of the stones is precise enough to be unnerving. No mortar. No decay. Five hundred years of earthquakes and the joints remain tight.

The reason the Spanish built on top of rather than next to the Inca structures was not aesthetic — it was practical. The Inca masonry was better than anything the colonists could build. The 1650 earthquake that devastated the city's colonial buildings left the Inca foundations standing. The Qorikancha walls, the curved apse of the Temple of the Sun that the Spanish incorporated into Santo Domingo, have not moved perceptibly in five centuries. Sacsayhuamán's stones are so large that the Spanish quarried them for building material for generations and still could not remove them all. What remains is what was too big to take.

One thing worth knowing before you arrive: Cusco sits at 3,400 meters. The air at that altitude contains roughly 30% less oxygen than at sea level. This is not a metaphor or a polite warning — it is a physical reality that will affect your first day regardless of your fitness level. Walk slowly. Drink water. Accept the coca tea that everyone offers. The altitude taxes your body before you have any sense that it is happening, and then you stand up too quickly and the city tilts slightly. Give yourself 48 hours. The ruins will still be there.
