A railroad terminus with no reason to exist except as a crossing point — burned once, rebuilt twice, and still, underneath the skyline, doing exactly what it was founded to do.

A city that started as a dot on a survey map

Most cities grow up around a river, a harbor, a fort, something the land itself gave them. Atlanta didn't have any of that. In 1837, state surveyors drove a stake into the Georgia red clay to mark the southern end of a planned rail line, the Western and Atlantic, and that was it — that was the whole reason the place existed. They called it Terminus, because that's literally what it was: an endpoint. Not a town, not a settlement, a terminus. A few years later it got renamed Marthasville after a governor's daughter, which never really stuck, and by 1845 it had become Atlanta, a name pulled from "Atlantic," tacked onto the railroad company itself.

When you're downtown near the Five Points area or waiting on a MARTA platform, you're standing more or less where those first rail lines converged — where Georgia's railroads met and the town grew up around the meeting point rather than the other way around. Atlanta is one of the only major American cities whose founding purpose was pure connectivity. No port, no strategic river crossing, no gold rush. Just tracks meeting other tracks. That fact matters more than it sounds like it should, because almost everything the city became afterward is a variation on the same theme: a place built to move things and people through it, fast, in every direction at once.

Burned to make a point

In November 1864, Union General William Tecumseh Sherman ordered Atlanta's warehouses, rail facilities, and much of the business district put to the torch before his army marched to the sea. Atlanta wasn't destroyed because it was symbolically important in some abstract sense — it was destroyed because it was the Confederacy's rail and supply hub, the same terminus function that built the place in the first place, and Sherman understood that breaking the junction broke the war machine behind it. The fire took a huge share of the city's buildings. What was left of downtown Atlanta after the war was mostly cinders and chimneys.

The response is the part worth knowing, because it's still on the city's official seal today: a phoenix rising from flames, with the motto Resurgens — Latin for "rising again." Atlanta didn't memorialize the burning with a museum of ashes. It picked the mythology of rebuilding and wore it as a civic identity, and then it actually did the rebuilding, fast, commercially, without much sentimentality. Within a couple of decades the city had rebuilt its rail network, expanded it, and turned itself into the commercial capital of the postwar South. You'll see the phoenix on manhole covers, on public buildings, on the city flag. It isn't decoration. It's the closest thing Atlanta has to a founding myth, and unlike a lot of civic mottos, this one is actually true.

Atlanta didn't rebuild in spite of the fire. It rebuilt because rebuilding, faster and bigger than before, is what the city has always known how to do.

Auburn Avenue and the making of a movement

A few blocks east of downtown sits Auburn Avenue, and in the first half of the twentieth century this stretch of street was one of the most important Black economic and cultural corridors in the entire country — banks, insurance companies, churches, and businesses built and owned by Black Atlantans in the era of Jim Crow segregation, when white-owned capital simply wasn't available to them. It was often called "the richest Negro street in the world." Martin Luther King Jr. was born on Auburn Avenue in 1929, in a house that still stands, and he was raised inside Ebenezer Baptist Church, where his father and grandfather had both preached before him.

Walk that stretch of Auburn Avenue today — past the King birth home, past Ebenezer Baptist, past the reflecting pool where King and his wife Coretta Scott King are entombed at the King Center — and you're not visiting a monument to something that happened elsewhere and got memorialized here. You're standing in the actual place where the theology, the organizing networks, and the personal formation that produced the Civil Rights Movement's most consequential leader all came from. This wasn't incidental to Atlanta. The city's Black institutions — its churches, its historically Black colleges clustered together as the Atlanta University Center, its Black-owned press — gave the movement infrastructure that most Southern cities simply didn't have.

"The city too busy to hate"

Atlanta's business and political leadership in the mid-twentieth century made a calculated choice that other Southern cities didn't: rather than fight desegregation into a spectacle of violence that would scare off investment, longtime mayor William Hartsfield and the white business establishment worked to manage it, however imperfectly and however slowly, in a way that kept the city's commercial reputation intact. The self-branding that came out of it — "the city too busy to hate" — was partly genuine and partly marketing, and honest histories of the period will tell you both things are true at once. It wasn't racial harmony. But it also wasn't Birmingham.

What it produced was a city that, compared to its regional peers, kept growing through the era other Southern downtowns were hollowing out from white flight and disinvestment. It's also part of why Atlanta became the corporate and financial capital of the modern South, the place multinational companies picked when they wanted a Southern headquarters that wouldn't become a national news story. That calculation, cynical as parts of it were, is baked into why Atlanta looks the way it does today — a city with a Black political establishment (it has elected Black mayors continuously since 1974) sitting inside a metro region built by corporate capital that arrived precisely because the city sold itself as manageable.

Pemberton's syrup and the world's busiest crossroads

In 1886, a pharmacist named John Pemberton mixed up a caramel-colored syrup at a drugstore not far from downtown and started selling it as a tonic. Coca-Cola is now headquartered in Atlanta, still, a century and a half later, and the World of Coca-Cola downtown is a genuinely strange monument to how a patent medicine became one of the most recognized brands on Earth — but the more interesting thing about Coca-Cola's Atlanta roots is what it says about the city's economic personality. Atlanta has never really been a manufacturing town or a natural-resource town. It makes its money moving things — goods, information, capital, people — through a hub, and then attaching a brand or a service to that movement. Coca-Cola is a syrup company that became a logistics and marketing empire. That's an Atlanta story before it's a soft-drink story.

The clearest expression of that hub instinct is the airport. Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International, named for that same Mayor Hartsfield who pushed early aviation investment decades before anyone thought commercial air travel would matter this much, has been the world's busiest airport by passenger traffic for most of the last several decades. That's not an accident of geography so much as a continuation of the original terminus logic — a city built at a rail junction figured out, a century later, how to become the country's best airline connecting point instead, sitting at a latitude and longitude that make it a natural stopover between the Northeast, the Midwest, Florida, and the whole rest of the Southeast. If you've ever connected through Atlanta on your way to somewhere else entirely, you've experienced the terminus function still working exactly as designed.

The Olympics and the reinvention of downtown

Atlanta hosted the 1996 Summer Olympics, and whatever you think about the event itself — the transit was overwhelmed, a bombing during the Games killed one person and injured over a hundred — the Olympics left a permanent mark on the city's geography. Centennial Olympic Park, built as the Games' central gathering space in what had been a rundown warehouse district, is still there downtown, ringed now by the aquarium, the World of Coca-Cola, and the National Center for Civil and Human Rights. The Games gave the city an excuse and a deadline to remake a stretch of downtown that corporate flight to the suburbs had left half-abandoned, and thirty years later that reinvented core is still the tourist and civic heart of the city.

Black Hollywood of the South

What's happened in Atlanta over the last two decades is, in its own way, another version of the terminus story — the city figuring out what it's positioned to funnel through itself next. Georgia's aggressive film tax incentives, introduced in the 2000s, turned Atlanta into one of the largest film and television production centers in the country, rivaling Hollywood itself for certain kinds of production volume. But that infrastructure landed on top of something that already existed: Atlanta's decades-long role as the center of Black entertainment, media, and music in America, a legacy running from the Civil Rights-era institutions on Auburn Avenue through the hip-hop and R&B explosion of the 1990s and 2000s that made Atlanta rap (OutKast, Jermaine Dupri's So So Def, the Dungeon Family, and later the trap sound that dominates mainstream hip-hop today) one of the most influential regional sounds in American music.

The city's massive Black middle and professional class, its historically Black colleges producing generation after generation of graduates who stayed, and its self-image as a place where Black success wasn't an exception but an expectation, made Atlanta the obvious place for a Black entertainment industry to root itself once the money and the soundstages showed up. People call it "Black Hollywood of the South" now, somewhat casually, but it's worth taking literally: nowhere else in America has that combination of production infrastructure and cultural gravity pointed at Black audiences and Black creators at this scale.

What to carry onto the plane

Atlanta doesn't have a single monument or skyline silhouette that explains it the way the Eiffel Tower explains Paris or the Colosseum explains Rome. What it has instead is a pattern, repeating at every scale of its history. It was founded as nothing but a junction — a place with no reason to exist except to connect other places. It was burned to the ground because that junction mattered enough to destroy. It rebuilt, on purpose, under a motto that means rising again, and it did that so completely and so fast that within a generation it was the commercial capital of an entire region. And when the next century needed a place to organize a movement for full citizenship, it happened here too, on a street a few blocks from where the original rail lines crossed, built on institutions Black Atlantans had built for themselves because no one else would let them in.

The busiest airport on Earth and the birthplace of the movement that forced America to reckon with its own founding promises are not two separate Atlanta stories sitting next to each other by coincidence. They come from the same instinct: build the crossing point, and when it's destroyed, or when the country around it refuses to live up to what it claims to believe, don't mourn it — rebuild it bigger, and make it carry more than it did before. That's what Resurgens actually means here. Not just recovery. Escalation. Atlanta doesn't rise back to where it was. It rises past it.
