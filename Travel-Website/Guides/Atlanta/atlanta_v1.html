Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 24 min · 🚕 4 min → World of Coca-Cola
1. World of Coca-Cola
↳ The flagship museum of the drink invented in Atlanta, on a plaza named for the pharmacist who first mixed it. The vault holding the secret formula, a century of advertising art, and a tasting room pouring more than 100 Coca-Cola products from around the world make it a uniquely Atlanta visit.
📖 Wikipedia

📍 121 Baker St NW · Downtown
🚶 6 min · 🚕 2 min → Georgia Aquarium
2. Georgia Aquarium
↳ One of the largest aquariums in the world — ten million gallons and the only whale sharks on display outside Asia. The Ocean Voyager tunnel sends you through a habitat where the giants glide overhead alongside manta rays, an experience no other aquarium in the Western Hemisphere offers.
📖 Wikipedia

📍 225 Baker St NW · Downtown
🚶 1 min · 🚕 1 min → National Center for Civil and Human Rights
3. National Center for Civil and Human Rights
↳ A powerful museum connecting the American civil rights movement to global human rights struggles. The lunch-counter sit-in simulation — headphones on, hands on the counter — is one of the most affecting museum experiences in the country, and the gallery of Dr. King's personal papers grounds it all in Atlanta.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ National Center for Civil and Human Rights · 4.8⭐ · civilandhumanrights.org
🏛️ Monday - Saturday 10:00am - 5:00pm · Sunday 12:00pm - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 100 Ivan Allen Jr Blvd NW · Downtown
🚶 39 min · 🚕 5 min → Martin Luther King Jr. National Historical Park
4. Martin Luther King Jr. National Historical Park
↳ The blocks of Sweet Auburn where Dr. King was born, preached, and now rests. Historic Ebenezer Baptist Church, the King Center with the crypt of Dr. and Mrs. King beside a reflecting pool, and the eternal flame sit within a few steps of each other. Start at the visitor center for the exhibits and the film.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
⚠️ The Birth Home is closed for rehabilitation — church, crypt, and visitor center remain open
📍 450 Auburn Ave NE · Sweet Auburn

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 27 min · 🚕 5 min → High Museum of Art
1. High Museum of Art
↳ The leading art museum of the South, as famous for its architecture as its collection — Richard Meier's white-porcelain building won him the Pritzker Prize, and Renzo Piano later doubled it. Inside: American decorative arts, self-taught Southern artists, and a sunlit atrium ramp worth the visit alone.
📖 Wikipedia

🏛️ Tuesday - Saturday 10:00am - 5:00pm · Sunday 12:00pm - 5:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~2 hr

🚶 17 min · 🚕 4 min → Piedmont Park
2. Piedmont Park
↳ Atlanta's great lawn — 200 rolling acres laid out on the old Gentlemen's Driving Club grounds, where the Midtown skyline rises over Lake Clara Meer in the city's most photographed view. Joggers, picnics, and dog parks fill the meadows, and the Eastside BeltLine and Botanical Garden both touch its edges.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 6:00am - 11:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 1071 Piedmont Ave NE · Midtown
🚶 15 min · 🚕 4 min → Atlanta Botanical Garden
3. Atlanta Botanical Garden
↳ Thirty acres on the edge of Piedmont Park, crowned by the glass Fuqua Conservatory and the Canopy Walk — a 600-foot skywalk through the treetops of an urban hardwood forest. The orchid house holds the largest collection in the country, and summer evenings bring sculpture and light installations to the paths.
📖 Wikipedia

4. Atlanta BeltLine Eastside Trail
↳ Atlanta's signature urban reinvention — a railway corridor reborn as a landscaped trail stitched with murals, sculpture, and skyline views, busy with walkers and cyclists from morning to night. Join it at Ponce City Market, the old Sears building turned food hall, and stroll the street art toward Old Fourth Ward.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 10 min → Jimmy Carter Presidential Library and Museum
1. Jimmy Carter Presidential Library and Museum
↳ Opened in 1986, the museum preserves 27 million documents from Carter's presidency alongside a full-scale Oval Office replica and his Nobel Peace Prize medal. The 35-acre parklike campus — Japanese gardens, two small lakes, and Atlanta skyline views — sits off Freedom Parkway.
📖 Wikipedia

🏛️ Monday - Saturday 9:00am - 4:45pm · Sunday 12:00pm - 4:45pm
⏰ ~2 hr
📍 441 John Lewis Freedom Pkwy NE · Poncey-Highland
🚶 35 min · 🚕 8 min → Historic Oakland Cemetery
2. Historic Oakland Cemetery
↳ Founded in 1850, Oakland is a 48-acre Victorian garden cemetery — final resting place of six Georgia governors, Margaret Mitchell, and Bobby Jones. Ancient oaks, magnolias, and elaborate 19th-century funerary sculpture make it one of the most atmospheric open spaces in Atlanta.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 248 Oakland Ave SE · Grant Park
🚶 25 min · 🚕 5 min → Little Five Points
3. Little Five Points
↳ Atlanta's counterculture hub since the 1980s — built around the intersection of Moreland, Euclid, and McLendon avenues. Independent record stores, vintage clothing, tattoo parlors, and street murals with no major chains in sight.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Moreland Ave NE at Euclid Ave · Inman Park / Candler Park
🚶 8 min · 🚕 3 min → Inman Park
4. Inman Park
↳ Atlanta's first planned residential suburb, laid out in 1890 with curving boulevards, pocket parks, and Queen Anne and Craftsman mansions. The streetcar suburb fell into decay, was saved by early preservationists in the 1970s, and now anchors Atlanta's most walkable historic neighborhood.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Edgewood Ave NE · Inman Park
🚕 8 min → hotel

🍑 Peachtree Notes

The hotel and the Fox Theatre have been staring at each other across Peachtree Street for nearly a century. When Gone with the Wind premiered in Atlanta, the stars screened the film down the street and the celebration gala filled the hotel's ballrooms — while the Fox's own survival later came down to a grassroots campaign of schoolchildren's pennies and benefit concerts that saved it from becoming a parking lot.

Day 1 traces a quieter thread: the Coca-Cola fortune that built Pemberton Place also helped fund the city's civil rights institutions, and Atlanta's old nickname — the city too busy to hate — was coined by the same business establishment whose skyline now forms the backdrop to Dr. King's crypt on Auburn Avenue. Commerce and conscience have always shared this city's streets.
