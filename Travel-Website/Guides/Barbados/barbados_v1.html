Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 30 min → Harrison's Cave
1. Harrison's Cave
↳ A crystallised limestone cave system toured by electric tram — emerald pools, waterfalls and towering stalactites formed over 400,000 years. Discovered in the 1970s, it is one of the Caribbean's only accessible cave systems of this scale.

2. Hunte's Gardens
↳ A lush tropical garden carved into a deep gully by horticulturalist Anthony Hunte — giant heliconias, rare orchids and centuries-old mahogany trees descend in terraces around a small house. A resident parrot and a complimentary rum punch on arrival make this unlike any other garden visit on the island.

3. Barbados Wildlife Reserve
↳ A mahogany forest reserve where Barbados green monkeys roam freely alongside brocket deer, red-footed tortoises, peacocks and caiman — animals walk past visitors on open pathways with no barriers. Feeding time at 2:00pm draws the monkey colony in large numbers.
📖 Wikipedia

🚕 10 min → St Nicholas Abbey
4. St Nicholas Abbey
↳ One of only three Jacobean great houses in the Western Hemisphere — a working rum distillery and sugar estate from 1658, surrounded by formal gardens. A narrow-gauge heritage steam railway departs from Cherry Tree Hill with panoramic views over the Scotland District.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 25 min → Gun Hill Signal Station
1. Gun Hill Signal Station
↳ A 19th-century British military signal tower set on the highest point in St George, with panoramic views across the island to both coasts. A famous lion carved in 1868 from a single piece of coral stone guards the entrance — one of a chain of six signal stations for rapid military communication.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Andromeda Botanic Gardens
↳ Terraced hillside gardens above the Bathsheba coast showcasing tropical plants from across the world — ancient bearded fig trees, heliconia canopies and cactus gardens overlooking Atlantic surf. Operated by the Barbados National Trust, formerly the private collection of botanist Iris Bannochie.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 4:30pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Bathsheba · St Joseph
🚶 5 min · 🚕 5 min → Bathsheba Soup Bowl
3. Bathsheba Soup Bowl
↳ Barbados' wild Atlantic coast — enormous rust-coloured boulders rise from a powerful surf break nicknamed the Soup Bowl, one of the Caribbean's most respected big-wave surf spots. Walk the rock pools at low tide and take in the untamed east-coast panorama.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Bathsheba · St Joseph
🚕 45 min → Animal Flower Cave
4. Animal Flower Cave
↳ The only accessible sea cave in Barbados — carved by Atlantic waves into the island's northernmost cliff, with natural rock pools fed by the sea and sea anemones tucked into the coral walls. Clifftop views stretch across open ocean in three directions. The rock pools are swimmable when sea conditions allow.
📖 Wikipedia

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 25 min → Mount Gay Rum Visitor Centre
1. Mount Gay Rum Visitor Centre
↳ The world's oldest continuously operating rum distillery, with deeds dating to 1703, offers tasting experiences and tours at its Brandons facility. The Signature Tasting Experience walks through the production process and a flight of premium expressions including Eclipse, Black Barrel and XO.

2. Carlisle Bay
↳ A sheltered harbour home to six accessible shipwrecks between 12 and 55 feet deep, teeming with sea turtles, seahorses, rays and tropical fish. The bay is the best shore-snorkeling site on the island and can be explored at a basic level from Pebbles Beach with no rental equipment needed.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2 hr
📍 Pebbles Beach · Bay Street · Saint Michael
🚶 15 min · 🚕 5 min → Garrison Historic Area
3. Garrison Historic Area
↳ Part of the UNESCO World Heritage Site "Historic Bridgetown and its Garrison," this is the most complete surviving British colonial garrison in the world — over 70 listed buildings, a 12-hectare parade ground now used as a racetrack, and a ring of 70 historic iron cannons.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 The Garrison · Saint Michael
🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → George Washington House
4. George Washington House
↳ The only place outside the United States where George Washington lived — a 19-year-old Washington spent two months here in 1751. Fully restored as a museum, it recreates period interiors with artifacts from his Barbadian stay and includes access to a 200-foot section of the Garrison Tunnels.
📖 Wikipedia

🍮 Local Tastes
Flying Fish & Cou-Cou
↳ Barbados' national dish — steamed or fried flying fish laid over cou-cou, a dense cornmeal and okra porridge, served with a rich Creole tomato sauce. Found from roadside vans to fine-dining tables across the island.
📍 Brown Sugar · Bay Street · Bridgetown
Pudding & Souse
↳ A Saturday Barbadian ritual — pickled pork served with sweet potato pudding. Traditionally sold from roadside stalls on Saturday mornings only; finding it on any other day is rare.
📍 Brown Sugar · Bay Street · Bridgetown
Macaroni Pie
↳ Barbados' beloved Sunday side dish — a firm, sliceable baked mac and cheese seasoned with onion, pepper and Bajan spices. Richer and denser than its American cousin and found on every local lunch plate across the island.
📍 Waterfront Café · The Careenage · Bridgetown
Mount Gay Rum
↳ The world's oldest commercial rum brand, distilled continuously in Barbados. The island standard is a rum and ginger beer — order it at any bar; the locals call it a rum swizzle.
📍 Mount Gay Distilleries · Spring Garden Hwy · Saint Michael

🥃 The Island That Invented Rum

Every bottle of rum in the world traces its lineage to Barbados. The island's 17th-century sugar plantation economy produced the first commercial distillation of molasses into a drinkable spirit — and Mount Gay began an unbroken production run that makes it the oldest rum brand on earth. This was no accident. Barbados sits at the eastern edge of the Caribbean, the first landfall after the Atlantic crossing, which meant it received the earliest sugar cultivation techniques from Brazil and had first pick of the trade winds that powered the plantation ships. The monks of St Nicholas Abbey were making their own estate rum before the great house turned twenty years old. When British sailors were given a daily tot of rum in the 1650s, it was Bajan rum filling the barrels. Rum shaped the island's economy, its architecture, its culture and its national identity more completely than any other product of the New World. To visit Barbados without tasting the spirit at its source — at Mount Gay, at Foursquare, at St Nicholas Abbey — is to visit Scotland without tasting whisky.
