The city Hitler called irreplaceable, which is exactly why he spared it — and why what you see here is the real thing.

The city that survived

Most of Central Europe's medieval city centers are, in part, reconstructions. Warsaw's Old Town is a meticulous post-war replica, rebuilt brick by brick from wartime photographs. Dresden's Frauenkirche stood as rubble for fifty years before being reassembled from numbered pieces. Even Prague's famous streetscapes contain more twentieth-century restoration than most visitors suspect. Kraków is different. What you see when you walk into the Rynek Główny — the largest medieval market square in Europe — is not a reconstruction. It is the original, and it survived because someone powerful decided it was worth preserving.

That someone was Hans Frank, the Nazi governor-general of occupied Poland, who made Kraków his headquarters precisely because the city was intact and beautiful. He lived in Wawel Castle, used the city as a showcase, and protected its architecture from the bombing that leveled every other major Polish city. The irony is almost too heavy to carry: Kraków's extraordinary state of preservation is a consequence of Nazi occupation. The beauty you walk through today is inseparable from that fact.

Wawel: the hill everything orbits

Before you understand Kraków, you need to understand Wawel. The limestone hill rising above the Vistula at the city's southern edge has been the center of Polish royal and spiritual power for a thousand years. The castle on top was home to every Polish king from the tenth century until the capital moved to Warsaw in 1596. The cathedral beside it is the burial place of Polish monarchs, military heroes, and national poets — not just religious figures, but everyone the country has decided to canonize as important. If you want to understand what Poland thinks of itself, this is where it keeps the evidence.

The cathedral is the more significant of the two, and most visitors underestimate it. Stand in front of the gilded chapel of the Jagiellonian dynasty — the Kaplica Zygmuntowska, finished in 1533 — and you're looking at what many architectural historians consider the finest Renaissance building north of the Alps. It was built by an Italian, Bartolomeo Berrecci, brought to Kraków by a king who wanted to announce that Poland was not a backwater. The chapel's gold-tiled dome is visible from across the river. When the Zygmunt Bell in the cathedral tower rings — only on major national and religious holidays — it can be heard for miles.

The kings are in the crypt below the cathedral floor. Walk slowly down there. The names on the sarcophagi are the names every Polish schoolchild memorizes.

The dragon is worth mentioning too. At the base of Wawel Hill, at the entrance to the cave called Smocza Jama, stands a bronze sculpture that breathes actual fire — powered by natural gas, lit every few minutes. The Smok Wawelski, the Wawel Dragon, is the founding myth of Kraków: a king's son outwitted the beast by stuffing a sheep with sulfur and setting it as bait. The dragon drank from the Vistula to cool the burning and exploded. The statue was made in 1972. The fire is real. Children love it without knowing they're standing at the source of the city's origin story.

The square at the center

The Rynek Główny has been the heart of Kraków since 1257, when the city was formally chartered and the square was laid out in a single planning decision that has governed the city ever since. It covers about ten acres, which is difficult to grasp until you're standing in the middle of it and realize the buildings on the far side look genuinely distant. The Cloth Hall — the long Renaissance arcade running down the center — has been a market since the fourteenth century and still sells things, though now it's amber and linen and folk art rather than salt and metal. The stalls inside have been occupied, in various forms, for seven hundred years.

St. Mary's Basilica anchors the northeast corner. Its two towers are deliberately different heights — one was a watchtower, one was a bell tower, and they were built by rival brothers in a story that Krakovians tell with varying degrees of belief. What's true is that every hour, a trumpet call issues from the taller tower, breaking off abruptly mid-phrase. The hejnał, as it's called, commemorates a thirteenth-century trumpeter who was shot through the throat by a Mongol archer while sounding the warning call during the invasion of 1241. The broken note is the arrow hitting. It has been played this way, every hour, for roughly six hundred years.

Kazimierz: the world that was

Walk south from the Rynek Główny for about fifteen minutes and you cross an invisible line into Kazimierz, the neighborhood that was Kraków's Jewish quarter from the fifteenth century until the Second World War. Before 1939, roughly sixty-five thousand Jews lived in and around Kraków, in a community that had been here for nearly five centuries. By 1945, fewer than six thousand remained.

Kazimierz is not a museum, and it is not a ruin. It is a lived-in neighborhood where the evidence of what happened is everywhere if you're paying attention: the Old Synagogue on Szeroka Street, the oldest surviving synagogue in Poland, now a museum of Jewish history; the Galicia Jewish Museum, where the photographs insist on specificity rather than abstraction; the Remuh Synagogue, still a functioning place of worship, where the sixteenth-century cemetery crowds right up to the building's walls. The stones in that cemetery have Hebrew inscriptions worn smooth by four centuries of weather. Some of them were smashed during the war and later reassembled from pieces, the cracks still visible.

Kazimierz was not emptied in a single action. It happened in stages, and the neighborhood has the complicated texture of a place where people went on living while something catastrophic was occurring nearby.

The neighborhood today is also where Kraków's creative class lives and eats — the cafés and restaurants on Plac Nowy are full in the evenings, the buildings are being renovated, young people have moved in. This coexistence of memory and daily life is not disrespectful. It is what living cities do. The alternative — leaving it untouched, a frozen zone — would be its own kind of wrong.

Podgórze and Schindler's factory

Cross the river from Kazimierz into the Podgórze district and you're standing where the events of Schindler's List actually happened. The Kraków Ghetto was established here in March 1941, not in Kazimierz where the prewar Jewish community had lived, but in this working-class district across the water. The Nazis forced more than fifteen thousand people into an area that had housed three thousand. Plac Bohaterów Getta — Heroes of the Ghetto Square — is the main plaza of the former ghetto. It is marked now by seventy-two empty metal chairs, one for each thousand people deported or killed. The chairs are life-size. The emptiness is the point.

Oskar Schindler's factory is a ten-minute walk away, on Lipowa Street. The building is now a museum of Kraków under Nazi occupation, and it is one of the finest historical museums in Poland — not just because of the Schindler connection, but because it insists on showing the city as it actually was: the collaborators as well as the resisters, the bystanders as well as the heroes, the full texture of what ordinary life looked like under a regime determined to erase a population. Don't skip it because you've seen the film. The film is one story. The museum is the city's.

Nowa Huta: the city they built next door

Eight kilometers east of the Rynek Główny, connected by tram, is a place that most tourists never see and that Kraków itself has complicated feelings about: Nowa Huta, the socialist realist city built from scratch starting in 1949. The communist government had two goals. One was practical: build a steel works and house its workers. The other was political: dilute the intellectual and religious character of historic Kraków by importing a loyal proletariat. Nowa Huta was intended to transform the city's soul.

What they built is genuinely impressive, in the way that ambitious bad ideas sometimes are. The streets are wide and tree-lined and organized around a central plaza — Plac Centralny, now renamed Ronald Reagan Central Square — that is larger than the Rynek Główny and designed with a grandeur that was meant to make the old city look modest by comparison. The apartment blocks that radiate outward were, by the standards of postwar housing, comfortable and human-scaled. People actually wanted to live here.

What the planners didn't account for was that the people they imported were also Catholic Poles who would immediately begin fighting for the right to build a church. The battle over the church in Nowa Huta — which took decades of resistance before the communist government finally relented — became one of the defining stories of Polish religious and civic identity, and it was won here, in the workers' city that was supposed to be the proof that Poland could be remade. Walk out to the Arka Pana church when you're in Nowa Huta. It looks like a ship. It took twenty-three years to get permission to build it.

What salt built

Thirty kilometers southeast of Kraków, accessible by train or on a day trip, is Wieliczka — a salt mine that has been operating continuously since the thirteenth century and that should, by any reasonable standard, be less interesting than it actually is. Salt mines are industrial facilities. This one contains, at various depths below the ground, a series of chambers that the miners carved over centuries into something that requires the word "cathedral" and then admits that the word isn't quite right. There are chapels down there with altarpieces carved from salt crystal. There is a ballroom with chandeliers. There is a reproduction of Leonardo's Last Supper chiseled into the wall by a single miner working in his off-hours over the course of three years.

The mine powered Kraków's medieval economy. Salt was what money was made of, in the sense that it was scarce and essential and the crown's monopoly on it funded wars and cathedrals and the first Polish university, the Jagiellonian, founded in 1364. When you stand in the Rynek Główny and wonder how a city this size and this grandeur appeared in the middle of the Polish plain, the answer is mostly: salt.

Before you go

Here is the thing to carry with you into Kraków: this is a city that contains more history per square meter than almost anywhere in Europe, and almost all of it is tragedy held in remarkable physical containers. The castle is real. The square is real. The salt mine is real. And the absence of sixty thousand people is also real, felt in the empty chairs in Podgórze and the cracked gravestones in Kazimierz and the museum on Lipowa Street that refuses to let you look away.

The city doesn't ask you to feel guilty. It asks you to pay attention. The trumpeter's call breaks off every hour at the moment the arrow struck. Six hundred years of that broken note, and the city is still here to play it. That's what you're walking through.
