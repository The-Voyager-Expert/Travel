A coastal village that somehow became one of the world's leading centers of oceanographic and biomedical research — La Jolla sits on the San Diego coast looking like a postcard and functioning like a laboratory.

The Name and the Cliffs

La Jolla (pronounced La HOY-a) takes its name most likely from the Spanish la joya — the jewel — though some historians argue for la hoya, the cave or hollow, referring to the sea caves that pierce the sandstone cliffs along the village's northern waterfront. Both interpretations fit. The sea caves are real and explorable, seven of them accessible by kayak and one — Sunny Jim Cave — by a wooden staircase cut through the cliff in 1902. The jewel comparison is the kind of thing that sounds like chamber-of-commerce exaggeration until you stand on the coastal path at Goldfish Point and look north toward the Children's Pool and south toward the cove and understand that whoever named it was being factual.

The coastline here is sandstone — soft, easily eroded, sculpted by wave action into the arches, coves, and caves that define the waterfront. The same geology that makes it beautiful makes it unstable; sections of the coastal bluff periodically collapse, and the path along the top is maintained with the understanding that it moves over time. The cliffs are roughly 100 feet high in places, and the water at their base shifts from deep blue in open stretches to vivid green in the shallower coves where kelp grows close to the surface.

Ellen Browning Scripps and the Foundation of Modern La Jolla

La Jolla in the late 19th and early 20th centuries was a small resort town with a particularly influential benefactress: Ellen Browning Scripps, a newspaper heiress who moved to La Jolla in 1896 and spent the following decades donating to nearly every significant institution the village produced. She funded the Women's Club, the Bishop's School, the Children's Pool seawall, and — most consequentially — the Marine Biological Association of San Diego, which she co-founded with her brother E.W. Scripps and the marine biologist William Ritter in 1903. That organization eventually became the Scripps Institution of Oceanography, now part of UC San Diego and one of the oldest and largest centers of ocean and Earth science research in the world.

Ellen Scripps also funded the La Jolla Woman's Club building, designed by the architect Irving Gill, and worked with Gill on several other projects that gave the village its early architectural character. Gill was designing in a proto-modernist style — flat roofs, plain surfaces, functional forms — before modernism had a name. Several of his La Jolla buildings survive. The combination of Scripps's philanthropy and Gill's architecture in a single decade gave the village a cultural and physical infrastructure that most California beach towns of comparable size never had.

Scripps Institution of Oceanography

The Scripps Institution of Oceanography occupies the coastline at La Jolla Shores, just north of the main village. The campus includes research vessels, the Birch Aquarium (the public outreach arm), and a pier that has been used for oceanographic measurements since 1916 — one of the longest continuous pier records of ocean temperature and other variables in the world. The Scripps pier is a working research platform, not a fishing pier, and the data collected there feeds into climate research that has been cited in IPCC reports.

The institution's history tracks the history of oceanography itself. Roger Revelle, who directed Scripps from 1950 to 1964, was one of the first scientists to argue that the ocean could not absorb all the CO2 being generated by human activity — a finding, published with Hans Suess in 1957, that became a foundational paper in climate science. Revelle later supervised the work of a graduate student named Al Gore, a connection that Gore acknowledged in An Inconvenient Truth. The Scripps pier's continuous temperature record now shows the ocean warming that Revelle's theoretical work predicted. The data and the prediction are both produced by the same institution, in the same place, over seven decades.

La Jolla Cove and the Harbor Seals

La Jolla Cove is a small, protected beach at the base of the coastal bluffs, flanked by the Children's Pool to the north and a rocky promontory to the south. The water here is calm enough for snorkeling even on moderate swell days, and the underwater topography of reefs and kelp forest supports a population of garibaldi (bright orange fish, California's state marine fish), leopard sharks, and bat rays. The cove is part of the San Diego-La Jolla Underwater Park Ecological Reserve, established in 1971 — no fishing, no collecting, no harassment of wildlife.

The Children's Pool, a small cove enclosed by a seawall that Ellen Scripps funded in 1931 to create a safe swimming area for children, has been the subject of sustained community conflict since California harbor seals discovered it as a haul-out site in the early 1990s. The seals like the protected beach because humans can't reach them easily from the water and because the seawall cuts the surf. At peak season (pupping season runs roughly December through May), over 200 seals may occupy the beach. The rope barrier keeping humans back from the seals is the visible record of a legal and political battle that has been ongoing for over two decades, involving the California Coastal Commission, federal marine mammal protection laws, and a San Diego city council that has repeatedly found no good resolution. The seals remain. The rope remains. The argument continues.

UC San Diego and the Research Cluster

The University of California San Diego was founded in 1960, in part because Scripps Institution of Oceanography was already there and provided a natural anchor for a research university. UCSD grew fast — it now enrolls over 40,000 students — and its presence in the La Jolla mesa above the village created one of the highest concentrations of biomedical and scientific research in the world. The Salk Institute for Biological Studies, the Sanford Burnham Prebys Medical Discovery Institute, and the Scripps Research Institute are all within a few miles of the campus. Several of the scientists who worked in these institutions have won Nobel Prizes in Chemistry, Physiology or Medicine, and Physics.

The result is a community that is simultaneously a tourist destination, a wealthy residential enclave, and a working research environment. The demographics are unusual: you will see surf instructors, Nobel laureates, venture capitalists, marine biologists, and retired military officers all using the same coffee shop on Girard Avenue. The density of graduate students keeps rents high and the restaurant scene competitive in ways not typical of beach resorts. The Farmer's Market on Sunday mornings draws a crowd that is more likely to ask where the produce was grown than where the nearest Starbucks is located.

The Art Scene

La Jolla has had a disproportionate visual arts presence since the early 20th century. The Museum of Contemporary Art San Diego has its primary location in La Jolla, in a building originally designed by Irving Gill and subsequently expanded. The permanent collection focuses on work produced after 1950 and has particular strengths in minimalism, conceptual art, and California artists. The location — a building of genuine architectural interest, on a bluff above the Pacific — makes the museum an experience that would work even if the art were less good. The art is not less good.

The village streets around Prospect Street and Girard Avenue hold a concentration of commercial galleries, many of them oriented toward the market for established Western American and California painters. Some are serious and some are tourist-grade, and distinguishing them requires a few minutes of looking. The better ones carry work that represents real regional artists rather than generic coastal prints.

What You're Really Looking At

The thing about La Jolla that changes how you see it is the understanding that the landscape and the science are connected. The kelp forests you snorkel through in La Jolla Cove are studied by researchers at Scripps. The sea surface temperatures recorded on the Scripps pier since 1916 tell the story of a warming ocean in real numbers. The seals at the Children's Pool are protected under laws that are the direct product of the environmental movement that marine biologists and oceanographers helped build. None of this is invisible — the Birch Aquarium makes it explicit, and a walk along the coastal path from the cove to La Jolla Shores takes you past the Scripps pier, past the research campus, past the point where leisure and science share the same address. Most beach towns look like places where nothing much is happening beneath the surface. La Jolla is not that. Pay attention to what's actually here.
