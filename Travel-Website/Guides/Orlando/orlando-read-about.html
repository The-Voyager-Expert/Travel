How a swamp full of citrus groves became the most visited city in America — and what Walt Disney actually had in mind when he bought it.

Before the mouse

Orlando in 1960 was a small inland Florida city surrounded by cattle ranches and orange groves, known mainly for its proximity to Cape Canaveral and the pleasant winters that drew northerners looking for sun. The population was around 88,000. The biggest employer was Martin Marietta, building missiles for the space race. The city had a decent downtown, a few lakes, and no particular reason for a tourist to stop on the way to the coast.

Then, between 1964 and 1965, a series of shell companies with names like "Tomahawk Properties" and "Latin-American Development and Management Corporation" quietly bought up 27,000 acres of swamp and cattle land southwest of the city. The parcels were cheap — much of the land flooded seasonally — and the sellers had no reason to suspect anything unusual. By the time a reporter at the Orlando Sentinel connected the purchases to Walt Disney Productions in October 1965, the deal was already done. Disney had acquired an area roughly twice the size of Manhattan for about five million dollars.

Everything that Orlando is today — the 75 million annual visitors, the second-largest hotel inventory in America, the eight-lane arterials lined with chain restaurants and souvenir shops — follows from that land purchase. The city didn't evolve into a tourist destination. It was built into one, deliberately, by a man with a very specific idea of what he wanted to create. Understanding that idea, and how spectacularly it was both fulfilled and abandoned, is the key to understanding Orlando.

Walt's real plan

Here is what most visitors never learn: Walt Disney did not buy 27,000 acres to build a theme park. He bought 27,000 acres to build a city.

Disneyland in Anaheim had opened in 1955 and was a huge commercial success, but Disney was deeply frustrated by what had happened around it. Motels, souvenir shops, and fast-food restaurants had crowded up to the park's borders, and he had no control over any of it. The experience of arriving at Disneyland was, to Disney, a betrayal of the experience inside it. He wanted total environmental control — not just of the park, but of everything a visitor would see, hear, and encounter from the moment they arrived.

Walt Disney didn't want to build a better theme park. He wanted to build a better city — one where the problems of urban America were solved by design.

The Florida property was the solution. The sheer size of the purchase created a buffer zone. But the theme park — what became the Magic Kingdom — was always meant to be the funding mechanism for a much larger project: the Experimental Prototype Community of Tomorrow, or EPCOT. Not the park you visit today. A real, functioning city.

Disney's EPCOT was going to be a permanent community of 20,000 residents, built under a climate-controlled dome, with a monorail-based transit system and no private automobiles in the residential core. Corporations would test new technologies there — new building materials, new waste management systems, new approaches to urban planning — and the residents would live inside a continuously evolving prototype of the American city of the future. It was part world's fair, part laboratory, part genuine utopian project. Disney sketched it obsessively in the last two years of his life.

He died in December 1966, fourteen months after the Florida land purchase was announced and five years before the Magic Kingdom opened. Without his personal drive, the company's leadership looked at the EPCOT plans and saw something between impossible and terrifying. Running a theme park was one thing. Governing a city — with residents who could vote, sue, and complain — was something else entirely. The residential city was quietly shelved. What opened in 1982 as EPCOT Center was a permanent world's fair, brilliant in its own right but fundamentally different from what Disney had envisioned. The dome became Spaceship Earth. The city became Future World. The residents became tourists.

The kingdom of controlled experience

What Disney's successors did build was, in its own way, just as radical. The Walt Disney World Resort that opened on October 1, 1971 — the Magic Kingdom, initially — was the first destination in America that controlled the visitor's experience from arrival to departure. The property was so large that once you drove through the entrance, you were inside Disney's world. The hotels, the monorail, the landscaping, the sightlines — everything was designed to sustain a single coherent atmosphere.

The Magic Kingdom itself sits on the second floor. The entire park is built on top of a network of underground service tunnels called utilidors — a direct inheritance from Disney's urban planning ambitions. Trash, deliveries, and costumed employees all move through the tunnels so that nothing breaks the surface-level illusion. A cast member in a Frontierland costume never walks through Tomorrowland. The garbage never piles up. The deliveries arrive invisibly. It is the most elaborately maintained artificial environment on the planet, and it works so well that most visitors never think about the infrastructure underneath their feet.

This was the real Disney innovation — not any single ride or character, but the idea that an entire environment could be designed and maintained to produce a specific emotional state. Every theme park that has been built since, anywhere in the world, is working from this premise. Disney didn't invent the amusement park. He invented the concept of the immersive environment as a consumer product.

The competition that changed everything

For two decades, Disney had central Florida essentially to itself. Universal Studios Florida opened in 1990 as a working film studio with ride attractions — a respectable competitor but not a destination that required multiple days. SeaWorld had been around since 1973, drawing visitors who wanted marine life between park days. Neither fundamentally challenged Disney's dominance.

That changed in 1999 when Universal opened Islands of Adventure, and it changed completely in 2010 when the Wizarding World of Harry Potter opened inside it. For the first time, a non-Disney park had an attraction so compelling that families planned entire trips around it. The Hogwarts castle, the Diagon Alley expansion that followed in 2014, and the Hogwarts Express that connected the two parks — these were immersive environments built to Disney's own standard, and in some ways beyond it.

The result was an arms race that has made Orlando's parks collectively better than they have ever been. Disney responded with Pandora at Animal Kingdom and Star Wars: Galaxy's Edge at Hollywood Studios — entire themed lands built with a level of detail that earlier generations of Imagineers could not have attempted. Universal countered with Epic Universe, a full new park that opened in 2025 with themed worlds spanning Nintendo, classic monsters, and astronomical fantasy. The competition is real, and the visitor is the beneficiary.

What you are visiting when you come to Orlando is not one company's vision but the product of two companies pushing each other to build increasingly ambitious things. The quality floor has risen. The mediocre ride that would have survived for decades in a monopoly environment gets replaced in a competitive one. This is the best era in the history of themed entertainment, and it exists because Universal decided to fight.

The space coast next door

An hour east of the theme parks, Cape Canaveral has been launching rockets since 1950. The proximity is not a coincidence — the same flat, undeveloped Florida landscape that attracted Disney also attracted the military and then NASA, and the two industries have coexisted in an unlikely symbiosis ever since. Many of the engineers who built the Saturn V rockets that went to the moon lived in Orlando and commuted to the coast.

The Kennedy Space Center Visitor Complex sits on Merritt Island, adjacent to the launch pads, and it contains the single most impressive artifact in Florida: the actual Saturn V rocket, lying on its side in a building constructed around it, 363 feet long and still the most powerful machine ever built. Standing under it recalibrates your sense of scale. The theme parks are masterpieces of illusion. This is the real thing — the machine that put twelve human beings on the surface of the moon.

An hour from the world's greatest manufactured experiences sits the real thing — a rocket that actually went to the moon.

SpaceX now launches from the same coast, and on clear nights you can sometimes see the booster return and land from Orlando itself — a column of light descending toward the horizon and then, impossibly, stopping. The space industry and the theme park industry don't interact much, but they share a philosophical ancestor: the belief that you can engineer something extraordinary if you commit fully to the vision. Walt Disney and Wernher von Braun were friends. They appeared on television together. The connection is not accidental.

Orlando beyond the parks

Strip away the theme parks and Orlando is still a real city of about 320,000 people — closer to 2.7 million in the metro area — with a downtown that most tourists never see. The city sits on a chain of lakes, and the neighborhoods around them are green and residential in a way that the International Drive corridor does not prepare you for. Winter Park, the old-money suburb just north of downtown, has a walkable main street lined with restaurants, a Rollins College campus shaded by live oaks, and the Charles Hosmer Morse Museum, which holds the world's largest collection of Louis Comfort Tiffany glass — including an entire chapel interior reconstructed inside the museum.

The food scene has grown substantially. The Mills 50 district, Orlando's Vietnamese-American neighborhood along Colonial Drive and Mills Avenue, is one of the best collections of Vietnamese restaurants in the American South. The Milk District, a few blocks south, has become the city's independent bar and restaurant strip. These are not tourist zones. They are where Orlando's residents eat, and they represent the city that exists year-round regardless of park attendance.

Orlando's weather is genuinely subtropical — summer afternoons produce thunderstorms so reliable you can set your watch by them, usually arriving between 2 and 4 PM and clearing within an hour. The locals plan around them. The theme parks plan around them. The afternoon downpour is not a disruption; it is a feature of the climate, and it drops the temperature ten degrees and clears the crowds. Experienced visitors treat the afternoon storm as an opportunity, not an obstacle.

The thing to understand

Orlando is easy to dismiss. The sprawl along International Drive, the endless chain restaurants, the sheer commercial density of the tourist corridor — all of it can feel like the opposite of authentic travel. And in a certain sense it is. Orlando was not built by centuries of human settlement and cultural accumulation. It was built, in its modern form, in about sixty years, by corporations competing to create the most compelling artificial experiences ever constructed.

But that is exactly what makes it interesting. The theme parks are not cynical products. They are the work of thousands of designers, engineers, artists, and storytellers who have spent careers figuring out how to make you feel something in a built environment. The best of what they have produced — the sense of arrival when you first see Hogwarts castle, the emotional gut-punch of a well-designed dark ride, the sheer engineering audacity of a launch coaster — belongs in the conversation about what humans can build when they commit resources and talent to a vision.

Walt Disney wanted to build the city of the future. He didn't get that. What he got — what you're walking into — is something he probably couldn't have predicted: the most visited place in America, built on swampland he bought for pennies an acre, where two corporations spend billions trying to outdo each other in the art of manufactured wonder. It is ridiculous and extraordinary in equal measure, and there is nowhere else like it on earth.
