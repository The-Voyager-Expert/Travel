A city that spent centuries getting rich on other people's wine, other people's labor, and other people's wars — and is only now figuring out what it wants to be on its own terms.

The Crescent on the River

You will notice the shape before you notice anything else. The Garonne bends through Bordeaux in a wide crescent — the Port de la Lune, the port of the moon — and the entire city faces it like an audience in an amphitheater. The limestone facades along the left bank run in an unbroken curve for nearly four kilometers, pale gold in the morning, almost white at noon, honey-colored by evening. UNESCO inscribed the whole ensemble in 2007, calling it "an outstanding urban and architectural ensemble" of the Age of Enlightenment. They were not exaggerating.

What you are looking at is not medieval Bordeaux. Almost nothing medieval survives at the waterfront. What you see is the work of two 18th-century royal intendants — Claude Boucher, who arrived in 1720 and began tearing out the cramped medieval quarter to create Place de la Bourse, and the Marquis de Tourny, who took over in 1743 and unified the entire quayside into the harmonious wall of facades you walk along today. Tourny was so effective that Baron Haussmann later cited his Bordeaux as the model for reshaping Paris. The student, in other words, became more famous than the teacher.

Stand at Place de la Bourse on a still evening, when the Miroir d'eau — 3,450 square meters of shallow water — mirrors the three baroque palaces above it, and you will understand why the intendants bothered. They were building a stage set for commerce. Ships docked directly below these facades, and the city's wealth needed a face worthy of the trade.

Three Hundred Years of England

In 1152, Eleanor of Aquitaine married Henry Plantagenet, who two years later became Henry II of England. With that marriage, Bordeaux and the entire duchy of Aquitaine passed to the English crown. It stayed English for the next three centuries.

This was not a hostile occupation. Bordeaux thrived under English rule because the arrangement was mutually profitable. The English wanted wine — vast quantities of it — and Bordeaux controlled the river mouth through which every barrel from the interior had to pass. The wine the English drank was light, young, red — they called it claret, from the French clairet, and they drank so much of it that by the 13th century the Bordeaux wine fleet was the largest regular trade convoy in the medieval world. Bordeaux grew rich not by making the best wine but by controlling the bottleneck.

The English era ended on July 17, 1453, at the Battle of Castillon — the last battle of the Hundred Years' War. Bordeaux returned to France, and the city mourned. The English had been good customers. You can still feel traces of this long alliance in the city's temperament: a certain reserve, a commercial pragmatism, a preference for understatement over Parisian flash.

Wine Is Not What You Think

Here is the thing visitors get wrong about Bordeaux and wine: the vineyards are not in Bordeaux. The great chateaux — Margaux, Latour, Haut-Brion, Petrus — sit in the countryside, some of them an hour's drive away. Bordeaux the city is a port, not a vineyard. For most of its history, it was the place where wine was blended, aged, taxed, loaded onto ships, and sold. The negociants — the merchant houses of the Chartrons district — made fortunes without owning a single vine.

Bordeaux did not make wine. Bordeaux made wine into money.

The Cite du Vin, which opened in 2016 on the riverfront, is the city's most ambitious attempt to claim the wine story as its own. It is a striking building — all curved golden panels meant to evoke swirling wine in a glass — and it covers wine cultures from every continent. But notice what it does not do: it does not pretend Bordeaux is a vineyard. It positions the city as a place that understands wine intellectually, commercially, globally. The pitch has shifted from "we grow it" to "we know it." Whether that is enough is still an open question.

The Dark Chapter

Bordeaux was France's second-largest slave-trading port, after Nantes. Between the early 18th century and 1793, Bordeaux merchants financed roughly 480 slaving expeditions to West Africa and the Caribbean. The wealth from this trade built many of the elegant townhouses you admire in the city center.

The Hotel Nairac, built between 1775 and 1777 by the architect Victor Louis, is one of the most visible examples. Pierre-Paul Nairac, who commissioned it, personally financed at least 25 slaving expeditions. The building is now an administrative courthouse. There is no plaque explaining its origin.

Bordeaux was slow to confront this history. For most of the 20th century, the city simply did not talk about it. The first significant public acknowledgment came only in the 2000s, when the Musee d'Aquitaine opened a permanent exhibition on the slave trade. In 2019, the city unveiled a statue of Modeste Testas, an enslaved woman, on the left-bank quay. The reckoning is underway, but it is recent, and it is far from complete. You will walk past the evidence of this wealth every day of your visit. Knowing where it came from changes what you see.

The Twenty-Five-Year-Old City

If Bordeaux looks impossibly pristine for a city this old, there is a reason. Until the mid-1990s, it was gray. Literally gray — the limestone facades were black with soot and car exhaust, the riverfront was an industrial wasteland of warehouses and parking lots, and the city center was choked with traffic. Bordeaux was considered provincial, dull, a place people left for Paris.

Alain Juppe became mayor in 1995 and launched a renovation so thorough it amounted to reinventing the city. The facades were cleaned, revealing the golden stone underneath for the first time in a century. The riverfront warehouses were demolished and replaced with parks, promenades, and the Miroir d'eau. A modern tram system, inaugurated in 2003, replaced the car traffic that had been strangling the old streets. The whole project took roughly two decades, culminating in the UNESCO inscription.

The Bordeaux you walk through today is, in a real sense, about twenty-five years old — an 18th-century city that was invisible under grime until someone decided to wash it.

This matters because it changes the atmosphere. Bordeaux does not feel worn in the way that Rome or Lisbon feel worn. The stone is too clean, the tram tracks too precise, the riverfront too perfectly landscaped. There is a slight uncanny-valley quality — everything is genuinely old but looks brand new. Once you know why, the effect makes sense. You are not looking at a preserved city. You are looking at a revealed one.

The Food

Bordeaux food is not Paris food. It is heavier, more direct, less interested in impressing you and more interested in feeding you. Entrecote bordelaise — a thick rib steak with a shallot and red-wine sauce — is the defining dish, and you will find it at every price point from brass-railed brasseries to linen-tablecloth restaurants. The sauce is the thing: reduced Bordeaux wine, bone marrow, shallots cooked until they dissolve.

The canele is the city's signature pastry — a small, caramelized cylinder with a custardy interior, flavored with rum and vanilla. Its origin is tied to the wine trade: the story goes that nuns at the Annonciades convent made them with egg yolks donated by wine merchants, who used the whites to clarify their wine. The historical record is thinner than the legend, but the canele itself is perfect, and you should eat one still warm from a bakery before you try one from a tourist shop.

The Marche des Capucins — the belly of Bordeaux, as the locals call it — is open Tuesday through Sunday and has been the city's central food market for over a century. Go early. The oyster stands open at breakfast, and eating a dozen Arcachon oysters with a glass of cold white wine at nine in the morning is one of those things that sounds wrong and turns out to be exactly right.

The City That Brokered Everything

Here is the idea to carry with you. Bordeaux spent most of its history as a middleman. English wool and wine in the medieval centuries. Colonial sugar, coffee, and enslaved people in the 18th century. Wine from other people's chateaux in the 19th and 20th centuries. The city's genius was always commercial — knowing how to move goods, take a cut, and build a beautiful facade over the transaction.

What Juppe's renovation did, and what the Cite du Vin and the cleaned quays and the tram and the UNESCO designation are all trying to do, is give Bordeaux an identity that does not depend on being a broker for someone else's product. The city is betting that it can be a destination in itself — not a gateway to the vineyards, not a port for someone else's goods, but a place worth visiting for what it is. Walk along the crescent of the Garonne at sunset, with the facades catching the last light, and you will think the bet is working. Whether the city believes it yet is another question.
