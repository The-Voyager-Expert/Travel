Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 12 min → Victoria Clock Tower
01. Victoria Clock Tower
↳ The iconic Little Ben stands at the heart of Victoria, cast in Britain and erected in 1903 to mark the coronation of King Edward VII. A replica of London's Big Ben at one quarter scale, it became a symbol of the island capital and its colonial past.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Clock Tower Roundabout · Victoria
🚶 3 min · 🚕 3 min → National Museum of History
02. National Museum of History
↳ Seychelles' principal heritage museum traces island life from prehistoric ocean crossings to colonial rule and independence. Highlights include a 200-year-old stone of possession — a French colonial boundary marker — and artefacts from the unique Creole culture that fused African, Asian, and European influences.
📖 Wikipedia

03. Botanical Gardens of Victoria
↳ Founded in 1901, the Botanical Gardens spread across 15 acres of carefully tended jungle in the hills above Victoria. A dedicated coco de mer grove holds mature specimens of the world's largest seed; a tortoise pen is home to a breeding group of Aldabra giant tortoises.
📖 Wikipedia

04. Mission Lodge Lookout
↳ At 400 metres above sea level in the heart of Morne Seychellois National Park, the ruins of a 19th-century mission school frame one of the island's most expansive views — sweeping across Beau Vallon Bay and the offshore islands of Silhouette and North.

🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 12 min → Victoria Ferry Terminal · 🚢 1 hr → Grand Anse Praslin · 🚕 8 min → Vallée de Mai Nature Reserve
01. Vallée de Mai Nature Reserve
↳ A UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of only two places on earth where the coco de mer palm grows wild. Six endemic palm species form the canopy; the Black Parrot — found nowhere else — nests in the valley. Marked trails wind past towering palms, waterfalls, and volcanic boulders carpeted in fern.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Vallée de Mai Nature Reserve

02. Fond Ferdinand Nature Reserve
↳ A private reserve on Praslin's northeastern plateau protecting the largest remaining wild coco de mer forest — denser and wilder than Vallée de Mai. Entry is by guided tour only; naturalist guides lead small groups along forest trails past endemic orchids and gecko colonies.
📖 Wikipedia

03. Anse Lazio
↳ Consistently rated among the world's finest beaches, Anse Lazio occupies a protected cove on Praslin's northwest coast where powder-white sand curves between two granite headlands. The water is shallow, calm, and reef-fringed; snorkelling off the south rocks reveals parrotfish and hawksbill turtles.
📖 Wikipedia

04. Curieuse Island
↳ Home to the largest free-roaming population of Aldabra giant tortoises outside Aldabra — over 300 tortoises wander the island freely and approach visitors. A former leper colony and 18th-century doctor's house add history to the afternoon.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 12 min → Victoria Ferry Terminal · 🚢 1 hr 30 min → La Digue Jetty · 🚶 8 min · 🚕 5 min → L'Union Estate
01. L'Union Estate
↳ A working historic estate at the southwest tip of La Digue preserving a colonial-era coconut and vanilla plantation complete with a working copra mill, a traditional ox press, and a boathouse housing two original pirogues. Entry also covers the path to Anse Source d'Argent. Giant tortoises share the grounds freely.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 L'Union Estate · La Digue
🚶 5 min · 🚕 5 min → Anse Source d'Argent
02. Anse Source d'Argent
↳ The most photographed beach on earth according to several surveys — a series of shallow interconnected lagoons separated by monumental pink granite boulders, some the size of houses. The water is knee-deep at low tide and aquamarine at high tide; the rock formations cast dramatic shadows in afternoon light.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~2 hr
📍 Anse Source d'Argent · La Digue

03. Nid d'Aigle
↳ Eagle's Nest is La Digue's highest point at 333 metres. From the rocky summit the view opens in every direction — Praslin and Félicité to the north, Mahé to the southwest, and the island's car-free lanes threading through palms below.
📖 Wikipedia

04. Anse Cocos
↳ One of La Digue's remotest beaches, reached only on foot via the east coast track. An almost always empty stretch of dark granite and pale sand with powerful Indian Ocean swell; a natural lagoon at the north end provides calmer swimming.

01. Sainte Anne Marine National Park
↳ Seychelles' first marine park, established in 1973, encompasses six islands just offshore from Victoria. A half-day boat excursion covers snorkelling over the coral reef, a glass-bottom boat pass, and a beach stop on Moyenne Island.
📖 Wikipedia

02. Copolia Trail
↳ A 1.4-kilometre ascent through Morne Seychellois National Park to a granite plateau at 497 metres. The path climbs through pitcher plant habitat; Nepenthes pervillei is endemic here. The summit gives a 360-degree view over Mahé's east coast and the St Anne archipelago.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1.5 hr
📍 Copolia Trailhead · Sans Souci Road
🚕 25 min → Bel Ombre
03. Anse Major Trail
↳ A coastal trail along Mahé's wild northwest shore, tracing cliff-edge paths above the Indian Ocean to a secluded beach. The 2.5-kilometre one-way trail passes through ancient takamaka forest and open granite faces before descending to the boulder-fringed cove.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2.5 hr
📍 Bel Ombre Trailhead · Bel Ombre · Mahé
🚕 8 min → Baie Ternay Marine National Park
04. Baie Ternay Marine National Park
↳ A sheltered bay on Mahé's northwest tip enclosed between forested headlands — a marine park since 1979. Faces west for excellent late afternoon light; the reef edge begins just beyond the shoreline. Nearly always quiet, with a dive centre nearby.
📖 Wikipedia

🍮 Local Tastes
Bat Curry
↳ Fruit bats braised in coconut milk with cinnamon, cloves, garlic, and chili — a genuine Seychellois delicacy. The flavour is rich and faintly gamey, closer to duck than poultry. Served when in season at dedicated Creole restaurants.
📍 Marie Antoinette · Victoria
Ladob
↳ Plantains or root vegetables simmered in coconut milk with vanilla and nutmeg until soft and fragrant. Savoury versions use breadfruit or cassava without sugar. Found in homes and local cafés island-wide.
📍 Local cafés island-wide · Victoria
Satini Requiem
↳ Raw shark marinated in lime juice with fresh chili, grated coconut, and herbs. The lime cure firms the flesh while coconut softens the heat. Served as a starter in old-school Creole restaurants and at the Victoria Market.
📍 Victoria Market · Marie Antoinette
Cari Koko
↳ A rich coconut curry with freshly grated coconut, turmeric, lemongrass, and your choice of chicken, prawns, or octopus. Spooned over rice with breadfruit on the side — the backbone of Creole home cooking across all three main islands.
📍 Local Creole restaurants island-wide
Pwason Griye
↳ Red snapper marinated in garlic, ginger, and lemon, then char-grilled whole over coconut husks. Fish landed that morning and on the grill by lunch. Beach restaurants on all three main islands serve it with rice and chutney.
📍 Beach restaurants island-wide · Beau Vallon · Anse Lazio
Kat-kat Banane
↳ Unripe green bananas slow-cooked in coconut milk with turmeric and fresh herbs until the flesh breaks down into a dense, aromatic mash. More commonly found in home cooking and local canteens than tourist menus.

✨ A 250-Year Mystery

For over two centuries, the coco de mer — the world's largest seed, weighing up to 25 kilograms — was one of the great botanical mysteries of the age. The nuts appeared floating in the Indian Ocean long before anyone knew where they came from, and no living tree had ever been seen. Medieval Arab sailors were convinced the nuts grew from a vast submarine tree somewhere on the ocean floor, a phantom forest in the deep. The Portuguese called them "coco do mar" — coconut of the sea — and the name stuck.

The mystery persisted until 1768, when French explorers finally reached Praslin and walked into the Vallée de Mai. What they found was a palm unlike anything previously documented: trees reaching 30 metres, the female bearing a bilobed fruit unmistakably shaped like a human pelvis, the male catkin producing pollen for nearly a year. The fruit takes seven years to ripen. The tree lives for centuries. It reproduces only in two places on earth: Praslin and nearby Curieuse. The answer to a 250-year riddle turned out to be an island most Europeans had never heard of.
