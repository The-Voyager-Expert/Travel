Islands nobody wanted

When you fly into Mahé, the granite peaks rising from the ocean look ancient and permanent, like something that has always been inhabited. They haven't. For most of recorded history, the Seychelles existed only as a rumour. Arab navigators knew the islands were out there — they called them the Zarin, the high islands — but they left no settlement and no chart. Vasco da Gama noted the granite outcrops in 1502 on his second voyage to India. He did not land. Portuguese maps marked the cluster as the Seven Sisters, then forgot about it. For a hundred and fifty years after that, the islands appeared on European charts only intermittently, unnamed and unoccupied, a navigational landmark between Africa and Asia and nothing more.

What kept the colonial powers away was the total absence of anything worth taking. No gold, no silver, no agricultural plain, no deepwater harbour large enough for a fleet. The granite core of Mahé, Praslin, and La Digue rises steeply with limited flat land. The Indian Ocean trade routes ran either far north through the Red Sea or far south around the Cape of Good Hope. The Seychelles sat between both, off the main lines of movement — close enough to be known, far enough to be ignored. You are visiting a place that, for centuries, every passing empire looked at and shrugged.

The French were the first to take a formal interest, and only because of a logistics problem. In the 1740s, the French East India Company controlled Mauritius, 1,800 kilometres to the south, and needed a resupply station for the passage from Madagascar to India. Captain Lazare Picault surveyed the islands in 1742, landed on Mahé, and returned with reports of timber, fresh water, and giant tortoises so numerous they covered entire beaches. The tortoises were the selling point: each one provided months of fresh meat for a ship's crew and required no feeding on the voyage. That single detail — free, self-transporting protein — is what finally made the islands worth claiming.

France formally annexed the Seychelles in 1756. A stone of possession was planted on Mahé, inscribed with the king's name, and the islands were named after Jean Moreau de Séchelles, the French finance minister. No colonists followed. The French presence for the next fifteen years was essentially symbolic: an occasional ship stopping to collect tortoises, a boundary marker standing in the jungle. When the first permanent settlement was finally established in 1770 — twenty-eight years after Picault's survey — it consisted of fifteen Europeans, five Indians, and seven enslaved Africans, sent to grow spices on a small patch of flat ground near what is now Victoria. The spice plantation failed within a few years. What grew instead was something nobody planned.

A language born from the mixing

Walk through Victoria's market on a Saturday morning — Sir Selwyn Selwyn-Clarke Market, the social centre of Mahé — and listen. The language you hear is Kreol Seselwa: Seychellois Creole. It sounds like French if French had been taken apart and reassembled by people who also spoke Malagasy, Bantu, Tamil, and half a dozen Indian Ocean trading tongues. That is exactly what happened.

Over the decades after that first failed spice settlement, the Seychelles developed a society shaped by maritime isolation and by forced migration. Enslaved people brought from East Africa and Madagascar outnumbered free settlers by more than three to one. The Creole language that emerged from this mixture became the first language of the islands, and it remains so. This is not a pidgin or a simplified French — it is a fully developed language with its own grammar, its own literature, its own poetry, spoken by virtually everyone in the archipelago. When you hear it in the market, at a bus stop, drifting from a fishing boat off Beau Vallon, you are hearing something that was invented here and exists nowhere else.

The Seychelles was empty when the French arrived. Every person living here descended from someone who came from somewhere else — France, Africa, Madagascar, India, China, Britain. The culture is not a survival. It is an invention.

The British took the islands without a fight in 1810, during the Napoleonic Wars. A Royal Navy squadron sailed into Victoria's harbour and demanded surrender. The French settlers — who had been swapping the colonial flag between French and British depending on which navy's ship appeared — handed over the keys without firing a shot. Britain confirmed sovereignty at the Treaty of Paris in 1814 and named the capital Victoria when the queen took the throne in 1837. But the population kept speaking French Creole, the legal system stayed rooted in the Napoleonic Code, and the cultural character of the islands remained shaped by its French and African roots. If you visit the National History Museum in Victoria — a modest colonial building near the clock tower — you can trace this layering: French colonial records, British administrative stamps, African and Malagasy cultural artefacts, all from the same small population on the same small island.

The granite and the coral

Here is the thing about the Seychelles that most visitors never think about until they arrive: the geology is visible everywhere, and it explains everything. The inner islands — Mahé, Praslin, La Digue, Silhouette — are granitic, meaning they are fragments of the ancient supercontinent Gondwana that broke apart roughly 750 million years ago. They are the only mid-ocean granitic islands on earth. When you stand on Anse Source d'Argent on La Digue and look at those iconic boulders — smooth, enormous, sculpted into shapes that look deliberately arranged — you are looking at some of the oldest exposed rock on the planet. They are not coral formations. They are not volcanic. They are pieces of a continent that sank.

The outer islands are something else entirely: low-lying coral atolls, most of them uninhabited, spread across a maritime zone larger than France. Aldabra, the largest raised coral atoll in the world, sits 1,100 kilometres southwest of Mahé and is home to around 100,000 giant Aldabra tortoises — the largest population of giant tortoises anywhere on earth. You are unlikely to visit Aldabra (access is tightly restricted and expensive), but the tortoises you will see everywhere on the inner islands — in botanical gardens, hotel grounds, conservation enclosures — are their cousins, and understanding Aldabra's scale helps you understand why the Seychelles takes conservation with a seriousness that borders on religion.

In 1965, the British government proposed building a joint Anglo-American military airstrip on Aldabra. Scientists from the Smithsonian and the Royal Society launched a campaign that killed the project — one of the earliest successful international conservation battles. Walk through the Vallee de Mai on Praslin, a UNESCO World Heritage Site where the endemic coco de mer palm grows in a forest that has been largely undisturbed for millennia, and you are walking through the direct result of that impulse. The coco de mer produces the largest seed in the plant kingdom — up to 17 kilograms — and grows wild only on Praslin and nearby Curieuse. When you see one, you will understand why early sailors thought they came from a mythical underwater tree: they are extravagantly, almost absurdly, large.

Independence and the quiet coup

The Seychelles gained independence from Britain on June 29, 1976 — one of the last African nations to do so. If you walk past the courthouse in Victoria, you will see the date commemorated, but what followed is more interesting than the ceremony. The first president, James Mancham, was overthrown in a bloodless coup just one year later by France-Albert René, who governed for twenty-seven years under a one-party socialist system. René nationalised key industries, expanded public health and education to levels remarkable for a nation of 100,000 people, and curtailed political freedoms. Multi-party democracy was restored in 1991 after international pressure, and the Seychelles has held competitive elections since.

What you notice on the ground is the legacy of those socialist-era investments. The roads are good — surprisingly good for a small island nation. Public education is free through university. Healthcare is universal. Literacy is near total. The Seychelles ranks among the highest in Africa by GDP per capita, built on tourism and a growing offshore financial sector. It is a tiny country that punches absurdly above its weight on development indicators, and the reason traces directly back to those post-independence decades when a one-party government poured money into infrastructure and human capital. You can disagree with the politics and still see the results in the clinics, the schools, and the condition of the coast road circling Mahé.

What the beaches are actually telling you

Most visitors come for the beaches, and most visitors never look past them. That is a mistake, because the beaches here are doing something unusual. Anse Lazio on Praslin, Anse Intendance on Mahé, Anse Source d'Argent on La Digue — these are regularly cited among the most beautiful beaches in the world, and in person they earn it. But what makes them different from, say, a Caribbean beach or a Thai beach is the granite. Those boulders framing every cove are not decoration. They are the exposed bones of a lost continent, and the contrast between warm turquoise water and cool grey-pink rock that predates complex life on earth is what gives these beaches their particular, almost unsettling beauty.

The boulders at Anse Source d'Argent are not coral, not volcanic, not decorative. They are fragments of Gondwana — pieces of a continent that sank 750 million years ago, still standing in the surf.

The water clarity matters too, and it is not accidental. The Seychelles has designated roughly half its territorial waters as marine protected areas — one of the most ambitious ocean conservation commitments any nation has made. When you snorkel off Sainte Anne Marine National Park, just a short boat ride from Victoria, and the visibility extends thirty metres through water so clear you can count individual coral polyps, that is a policy outcome, not just a geographic gift. The reefs are healthy because the government decided they would be, and enforced it.

La Digue deserves a particular note. There are almost no cars on the island. You get around by bicycle or ox cart. The pace is so slow it feels performed, but it is not — it is simply what happens when an island of 3,000 people has no particular reason to hurry. Ride a bicycle from La Passe to Grand Anse, past vanilla plantations and colonial-era copra sheds, and you will understand something about the Seychelles that resorts on Mahé cannot teach you: the tempo of daily life here is not a tourism product. It is the actual speed at which these islands have always moved.

The Creole heart

If you are here in late October, you will land in the middle of the Festival Kreol — a week-long celebration of Creole language, music, food, and identity that draws Creole-speaking communities from across the Indian Ocean. Sega music fills the streets of Victoria: a percussion-driven, hip-swaying dance form that traces back to enslaved communities on Mauritius and Reunion and took its own shape here. The food is the other anchor — a Creole cuisine built on fresh-caught fish, coconut milk, breadfruit, and a set of spice combinations (curry, chilli, ginger, lemongrass) that reflect the Indian, African, and Malagasy roots of the population. Order a fish curry at a local restaurant on Mahé — not a resort restaurant, a local one — and you are eating something that has been refined on these specific islands for two hundred and fifty years.

What the Seychelles does not have, and has never had, is a history of ethnic violence. There was no indigenous population to displace. There were no rival colonial settlements to fight over. Every person here arrived from somewhere else, and the resulting culture is neither indigenous nor colonial in any simple sense but something that grew out of that particular mixing and became entirely its own. A grandmother on La Digue makes ladob — green bananas simmered in sweetened coconut milk — the way her grandmother made it, using a recipe that did not exist anywhere in the world before these islands were settled. That dish is not a survival from some prior civilisation. It is an original, invented here, by these people, in this place.

The one thing to carry with you

Before you go, here is the thing that changes how you see everything once you notice it: the Seychelles is not a natural paradise that people moved into. It is a place that was genuinely empty — no human being had ever lived here — and everything you experience that feels cultural, traditional, or rooted was built from scratch in the last two hundred and fifty years. The language, the food, the music, the architecture, the way people greet each other, the particular rhythm of a Saturday market — all of it was invented by people who arrived with nothing from half a dozen different civilisations and had to figure out, together, what life on these islands would be.

That is why the Seychelles feels different from other beach destinations, and it is not the granite or the water clarity or the tortoises, though all of those help. It is the fact that you are standing in a culture that is genuinely young — younger than the United States, younger than most European cities — and yet feels complete, settled, and entirely sure of itself. The mixing worked. The thing they built holds together. And the beaches, spectacular as they are, are the least interesting part of the story.
