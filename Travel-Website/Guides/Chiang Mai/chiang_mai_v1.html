Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 12 min · 🚕 5 min → Tha Phae Gate
1. Tha Phae Gate
↳ The eastern gate of Chiang Mai's 1296 walled city. The sandstone archway at the end of Tha Phae Road flanks an intact moat wall — the most complete surviving section of the original Lanna fortification. The gate name recalls the teak rafts once floated down from the northern forests and unloaded at the pier below.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Tha Phae Road · Old City
🚶 10 min · 🚕 4 min → Three Kings Monument
2. Three Kings Monument
↳ A bronze monument depicting the three rulers who jointly chose this valley as the site for Chiang Mai in 1296 — King Mangrai of Lanna, King Ngam Muang of Phayao, and King Ramkhamhaeng of Sukhothai. The figures stand on a raised plinth in front of the Old City's civic buildings.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~20 min
📍 Phra Pokklao Road · Old City
🚶 2 min · 🚕 2 min → Chiang Mai City Arts & Cultural Center
3. Chiang Mai City Arts & Cultural Center
↳ Chiang Mai's foremost civic museum, housed in the 1924 former Provincial Hall directly opposite the Three Kings Monument. Fifteen themed rooms trace Lanna civilisation from origins through craft, religion, and folk life. Among the finest colonial civic structures in northern Thailand.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Sunday 8:30am - 5:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min

4. Mae Ping River Cruise
↳ A longtail boat cruise on the Ping River — the principal waterway through Chiang Mai. Cruises pass orchid gardens, riverside temples, and traditional teak houses on the banks south of the Old City, offering a perspective on the city rarely available from the streets.

1. Doi Inthanon National Park
↳ Thailand's highest peak at 2,565 metres, 75 km southwest. The park spans cloud forest, twin Royal Chedis, and the Wachirathan and Siriphum waterfalls. The park holds the highest recorded bird diversity of any protected area in Thailand — over 390 species.

1. Elephant Nature Park
↳ A rescue and rehabilitation sanctuary in the Mae Taeng valley. Operating since 1996, the park cares for elephants rescued from logging operations and tourist riding. Visitors walk alongside the herd in open pasture — no riding, no performance shows.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 5:00pm

🚕 40 min → Bua Tong Sticky Waterfall
2. Bua Tong Sticky Waterfall
↳ A series of limestone terraces in Mae Taeng where calcium carbonate deposits coat the riverbed in a travertine-like layer — creating enough grip to walk directly up the cascades barefoot. Unlike any conventional waterfall, the falls can be climbed. The surrounding secondary forest is dense.

🍮 Local Tastes
Khao Soi
↳ Northern Thailand's defining dish — a rich coconut curry broth with egg noodles, topped with crispy fried noodles and braised meat. The Lanna version runs heavier on spice paste and subtler on coconut sweetness than southern Thai curries.
📍 Khao Soi Islam · Lamduan Faham
Sai Oua
↳ Northern Thai sausage — coarsely ground pork packed with lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime leaf, dried chilli, and shrimp paste, grilled over charcoal. Sold by the link at market stalls across the city.

🌿 The City in the Valley

Chiang Mai sits at 310 metres in the valley of the Ping River, ringed by highlands that reach 2,565 metres at Doi Inthanon — the same mountain reachable in under two hours from the Old City moat. That vertical difference of 2,255 metres in a single drive is one of the most dramatic elevation gradients accessible from any major Thai city.

The Bua Tong waterfall's grip comes from the mineral content of the springs feeding it. The water carries dissolved calcium carbonate; as it flows over limestone and meets the air, it precipitates as travertine — building up layer by layer into a rough coating. The same chemistry that builds stalagmites in caves is what lets bare feet walk straight up a waterfall.
