A city that most travelers treat as a layover on the way to Machu Picchu — and that quietly became one of the best places to eat on earth.

The city the desert built

Lima is a coastal desert city, and this is the first thing that will surprise you. Peru means the Andes to most people — the highlands, the ruins, the thin air — but the capital sits at sea level, on a series of cliffs above the Pacific, in one of the driest inhabited places on earth. The Atacama Desert extends up from Chile and turns the land around Lima into a fog-blanketed gray-brown for much of the year. It almost never rains. The famous garúa — the coastal fog that blankets the city between May and November — is not quite rain and not quite mist but something in between, a permanent dampness that never becomes a downpour.

This matters for how you experience the city. Lima is not a sun-and-light Mediterranean-feeling place. Its neighborhoods — Miraflores on the clifftops, Barranco sloping toward the sea, the historic center laid out on the flat valley floor — have a particular gray quality, softened by bougainvillea and the constant roar of the Pacific below the cliffs. In the garúa season, the light is diffuse and flat and everything looks slightly underwater. Some people find this melancholy. After a few days you may find it beautiful.

The pre-Columbian cultures understood this landscape in a way the Spanish largely ignored. The Huaca Pucllana — a massive stepped pyramid of adobe bricks in the middle of what is now the Miraflores district — was built by the Lima culture around 400 CE, a full thousand years before the Inca arrived in this valley. Walking around it now, flanked by apartment towers on every side, you are looking at a sacred administrative center that served the coastal plain while the Andes loomed behind it. The desert cities of Peru's coast are as sophisticated as anything the highlands produced. Lima has always been here; the name you know it by just came later.

The conquest, in one square

Francisco Pizarro founded Lima in 1535, three years after capturing and later executing the Inca emperor Atahualpa. He chose this valley because of the river — the Río Rímac — and laid out the city in the Spanish colonial grid that still defines the historic center: the Plaza Mayor at the center, the cathedral on one side, the archbishop's palace on another, the government palace where the viceroy ruled on a third.

Stand in the Plaza Mayor on any weekday morning and you are standing in the administrative center of an empire that once stretched from Colombia to Argentina. The cathedral took 150 years to build, was damaged by earthquakes multiple times, and contains — in a glass case in the nave — what are claimed to be the remains of Pizarro himself, though this has been disputed by historians since the nineteenth century. The presidential palace across the square stages a changing of the guard every day at noon, with considerable pomp, in front of a building that sits on the exact site of the palace Pizarro built for himself.

The Spanish built Lima directly on top of what they found. The traces underneath keep surfacing.

The Convento San Francisco, one block from the Plaza Mayor, goes further underground than most visitors expect. The catacombs beneath it served as Lima's primary cemetery from the seventeenth century until 1821 and hold the bones of an estimated 25,000 people — Franciscan monks and ordinary parishioners, arranged in the circular ossuaries that were standard practice at the time. The catacombs were only rediscovered in 1943, during renovation work. Walking through them is not morbid in the way you might expect. It is more like an accounting: this many people lived and died in this city before the streets above were paved.

The museum that changed how the world understands Peru

The Larco Museum is one of the best archaeological museums in the Americas, and it is housed in an eighteenth-century vice-regal mansion in the Pueblo Libre neighborhood — not in the historic center, which means many visitors miss it in favor of the easier cluster of tourist sites downtown. Go anyway. The collection spans five thousand years of pre-Columbian cultures across the coast, highlands, and jungle of what is now Peru, and it is displayed with a clarity and curatorial intelligence that makes everything else feel like a warehouse.

Rafael Larco Herrera began collecting in the 1920s, when serious archaeological excavation of Peru's coastal cultures was just beginning. What his family assembled is remarkable: Moche portrait vessels so individuated they feel like photographs, Nazca textiles with geometric patterns that still feel contemporary, Chimú metalwork in gold and silver that the Spanish melted down by the ton and that survives here only because some of it was buried rather than displayed. The famous "erotic gallery" — a separate room with explicit Moche ceramics that the museum's prudish early administrators tried to suppress — is worth seeing not for the obvious reason but because the vessels document a sexual frankness that colonial culture spent centuries trying to erase.

The museum's storage vault, visible through glass walls, holds forty-five thousand additional pieces in organized rows. Looking at it, you start to understand the scale of what the Spanish found here — and what they broke.

Why Lima's food is what it is

Lima is, by almost any serious measure, one of the great food cities in the world. This fact became publicly acknowledged after a series of Peruvian restaurants began appearing at the top of the World's 50 Best Restaurants list in the 2010s, but the food itself is not a recent invention. It is the product of five centuries of layered migration and exchange that happened nowhere else in quite this way.

The base is Andean and coastal indigenous: potatoes (Peru has more than three thousand native varieties), corn, quinoa, ají chili peppers in dozens of forms, freshwater fish from the highlands, ceviche from the coast. The Spanish added wheat, beef, olives, and onions. African slaves, brought to Peru to work the coastal plantations, contributed cooking techniques and ingredients — the anticucho, the beef-heart skewer sold at street stalls across Lima, comes from this tradition. Chinese workers arrived in the mid-nineteenth century to build railways and work the sugar fields, and their food merged with the local in ways that produced chifa — the Peruvian-Chinese hybrid cuisine, named from the Cantonese for "have you eaten?" — that is now its own fully realized thing, as distinct from either parent as Tex-Mex is from either Texas or Mexico. Japanese immigrants followed, and their influence on how Lima handles raw fish is everywhere.

Ceviche is not just a dish in Lima. It is a daily institution, eaten at midday, never at dinner, always made with fish that arrived this morning.

The result is a food culture that is genuinely impossible to replicate anywhere else because its ingredients are specific to this latitude and these ecosystems. The cold Humboldt Current that runs up the Pacific coast makes the ocean off Lima extraordinarily productive — the sea bass, the lenguado (sole), the corvina that appear in the city's cevicherías come from some of the most biologically rich water on earth. The ají amarillo pepper, the yellow chili that forms the flavor base of almost everything, grows nowhere else with quite the same heat and fruity brightness. You can cook Peruvian recipes in your home kitchen but you cannot make them taste like Lima. The place is an ingredient.

Miraflores and Barranco: two ways to live by the Pacific

Miraflores is the prosperous clifftop district where most visitors stay — green parks, contemporary restaurants, the Malecón walkway above the ocean, paragliders launching from the cliffs on weekday afternoons. It is easy and comfortable and a bit antiseptic, built for people who want Lima's pleasures without Lima's friction.

Barranco, twenty minutes south along the cliffs, is the version of the city that stays with you. It was Lima's beach resort in the late nineteenth century, built by families who wanted to escape the summer heat of the historic center, and the wooden Victorian mansions that remain give it a slightly faded grandeur. The neighborhood became the city's bohemian quarter in the twentieth century — artists, musicians, the counterculture — and it retains that energy even as the restaurants have gotten expensive and the weekend crowds have gotten large. The Puente de los Suspiros — the Bridge of Sighs, a wooden footbridge over a ravine leading to the ocean — is one of those small pieces of urban engineering that exist in every old city but that Lima has somehow made into something genuinely moving.

What Barranco has that Miraflores lacks is the feeling that the city was lived in before you arrived. The streets are narrower, the houses are older, the ocean is closer, and the Bajada de Baños — the path that descends from the bridge to the water — leads to a beach that Limeños use as a beach rather than as a backdrop for photographs.

What Lima asks of you

Before you board to Cusco, here is the thing that changes how you see Lima: this city has been rebuilt, layered over, renamed, and reinvented multiple times, and it has never fully erased what came before. The Inca empire built on top of coastal cultures that are still visible in the huacas scattered across the modern city. The Spanish built on top of the Inca. The waves of immigration — African, Chinese, Japanese, Lebanese, German — built on top of the Spanish. Each layer absorbed what it found and produced something that wasn't quite either parent.

When you eat ceviche in a Miraflores cevichería and the leche de tigre in the bottom of the bowl is bright with ají amarillo and the corvina is from the Humboldt Current and the corn is a variety the Inca domesticated — you are eating five thousand years of cultural synthesis. Lima doesn't advertise this. It just serves it to you for lunch.
