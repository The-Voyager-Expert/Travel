The UAE's capital has more money, more oil, and more Emiratis than Dubai — and has spent two decades asking what that wealth is actually for.

The island that became a capital

Abu Dhabi is built on an island. A nearly flat, roughly rectangular slab of land about 12 kilometers long, connected to the mainland by bridges, sitting in the Persian Gulf about 140 kilometers from Dubai. The Bani Yas tribe reached this island in the 1760s when a gazelle hunt led them to a freshwater spring — the name Abu Dhabi translates roughly as "father of the gazelle" — and the spring was rare enough in the surrounding desert that they stayed. For most of the next two centuries, this was a small fishing and pearling settlement, modest even by the standards of the Trucial Coast.

The island's flatness, which looks from the air like a deliberate constraint, is actually what made it buildable. When oil was confirmed in enormous quantities offshore in 1958 and onshore in the 1960s, Abu Dhabi had land that could be developed at speed. Within fifteen years, the mud-brick settlement had become a city. Within thirty, it had become one of the wealthiest cities on earth. Walk the Corniche today — the 8-kilometer promenade along the island's western shore, backed by gleaming towers and fronted by a clean Gulf beach — and there is almost nothing left of what was here before. The spring that brought the Bani Yas to the island was built over decades ago.

What replaced it is instructive: Abu Dhabi now has one of the highest per-capita incomes in the world, a sovereign wealth fund that manages well over a trillion dollars in assets, and a government that has spent the past twenty years thinking hard about what a country does when it has run out of things to want and must decide what it wants to become.

Sheikh Zayed and the idea of the UAE

The United Arab Emirates came into existence on December 2, 1971 — a date celebrated annually as National Day — when seven Trucial States (Abu Dhabi, Dubai, Sharjah, Ajman, Umm Al Quwain, Fujairah, and Ras Al Khaimah) formed a federation upon British withdrawal from the Gulf. The architect of that federation, more than any other single person, was Sheikh Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan, who had become ruler of Abu Dhabi in 1966 and served as the UAE's first president until his death in 2004.

Zayed's project was not primarily political — it was human. He came from a people who had survived for centuries in one of the most hostile environments on earth through the careful husbanding of resources, hospitality toward travelers, and a tribal code that made the desert navigable. His approach to the oil wealth that arrived during his reign was shaped by that background: he distributed it widely, built schools and hospitals before palaces, and insisted that the physical infrastructure of the new country be accompanied by a social compact that Emiratis would recognize as theirs.

His name is now on the airport, the main road, the grand mosque, the national museum, and dozens of other institutions across the UAE. This is not the personality cult of a dictator — Emiratis genuinely revered him — but it is worth pausing at the scale of the naming. When you walk into the Sheikh Zayed Grand Mosque, his tomb is in the courtyard to your right, marked by white marble and visited constantly by Emiratis who come not because they are required to but because they want to be near him. That relationship between a ruler and a people is rarer than most political systems suggest.

The mosque that changed the scale of things

The Sheikh Zayed Grand Mosque is the third-largest mosque in the world, and it is the building in Abu Dhabi that most resists being described accurately. The numbers exist: 82 domes, 1,000 columns, a chandelier in the main prayer hall made from Swarovski crystals, a carpet that took 1,200 artisans two years to weave. These statistics are accurate and completely insufficient.

What the mosque actually does is redefine the default scale of things. You arrive thinking you understand how large a religious building can be, and then you walk through the entrance and the courtyard opens ahead of you and every prior calibration is wrong. The white marble underfoot reflects the sky. The minarets at the corners reach 107 meters. The main dome is large enough to contain an entire church without touching the walls.

The mosque was designed to be the house of God in the most literal architectural sense — a building at the scale of aspiration rather than human use.

Non-Muslims can visit, provided they dress appropriately (abayas are available to borrow at the entrance). Go at dusk, when the exterior turns from white to a warm gold and the interior lights come up. Go again at night if you can, when the white marble glows blue under the moon and the reflection pool on the forecourt doubles everything. The mosque is open to visitors until 10pm most evenings. Most people see it at midday because it appears on the schedules. Go later.

Why Abu Dhabi built the Louvre

In 2007, France and Abu Dhabi signed an agreement that gave Abu Dhabi the right to use the Louvre name and borrow works from French national collections for thirty years, in exchange for roughly a billion euros in fees and support for French cultural institutions. The Louvre Abu Dhabi opened on Saadiyat Island in 2017. It was designed by Jean Nouvel and covered by a perforated lattice dome that filters desert light into "a rain of light" across the galleries below — a direct architectural reference to the patterns of a date-palm oasis.

The deal was criticized in France on the grounds that French cultural heritage was being rented to a petrostate. It was criticized in the broader art world on the grounds that the cultural legitimacy of an institution shouldn't be franchisable. These are reasonable objections, and the Louvre Abu Dhabi has lived with them from the beginning.

What it has done with that criticism is more interesting than the criticism itself. The museum's permanent collection is organized not by culture or period — the usual museum logic — but by themes that cut across civilizations: the first cities, the first empires, the first world religions, the age of encounters, the modern world. A Jain manuscript sits near a Byzantine icon near a Japanese screen. The argument the museum makes is that human creativity has always moved across borders, and that organizing art by national school is a nineteenth-century European convention that doesn't describe how culture actually works.

You can disagree with the argument. But it's a real argument, and it makes the Louvre Abu Dhabi one of the few new museums built in the last two decades that is doing something conceptually original rather than replicating an existing template. Walk through it without the audio guide. Let the juxtapositions do the work.

The Emirati question

Abu Dhabi feels different from Dubai, and the difference is demographic as much as architectural. The capital has more Emiratis — citizens of the UAE — than Dubai, which is a city of almost entirely migrant residents. This doesn't mean Abu Dhabi is Emirati in the way that Paris is French: expatriates still make up roughly 80 percent of the population. But 20 percent versus 12 percent is a real difference in how a city feels, in whether you encounter citizens going about their lives rather than just at ceremonial occasions.

Emirati culture is not easy to observe as a visitor, partly because Emiratis guard it deliberately. The social life of Emirati families happens in private homes, in the majlis (the traditional reception room), not in restaurants or public spaces. What you see in public — the national dress, the cars, the manner of moving through the city — is the outer surface of a culture that has spent sixty years negotiating its relationship with enormous wealth and enormous change while trying to keep something intact that it can't always name.

The question Abu Dhabi is actually asking itself — and has been asking since oil — is: what does Emirati mean when almost everything Emiratis do is done by someone else? When the hospital, the school, the government office, and the company are all staffed primarily by foreigners? The investments in culture — the Louvre, the coming Guggenheim on Saadiyat, the Zayed National Museum under construction — are one answer to that question. They are attempts to create institutions that belong to Emiratis in a way that a financial sector staffed by British and American bankers does not.

Saadiyat Island and the bet on art

Saadiyat Island sits just off the northeastern coast of Abu Dhabi's main island, connected by a causeway, and it is where the government has placed most of its cultural ambitions. The Louvre is already open. The Guggenheim Abu Dhabi, designed by Frank Gehry, is in construction. The Zayed National Museum, designed by Norman Foster, is planned. There will eventually be a performing arts center by Zaha Hadid. The island also has the Manarat Al Saadiyat cultural center, a beach resort, and the NYU Abu Dhabi campus.

The scale of the bet — the sheer number of starchitect buildings concentrated in a single district — is either magnificent or absurd depending on your prior relationship with institutional ambition. What is not in doubt is that Abu Dhabi has put serious resources into creating a genuine cultural district rather than a single monument, which is the more defensible long-term strategy. A cluster of institutions that talk to each other across disciplines is more likely to generate a cultural life than a single spectacular building.

Go to the Louvre. Walk the beach afterward. Note that Saadiyat's beach is one of the last nesting sites in the UAE for hawksbill sea turtles — the development was deliberately designed around their nesting zones, and conservation teams mark and protect the nests each season. The turtles predate everything else on the island by several million years.

What Abu Dhabi is slowly becoming

Abu Dhabi is more conservative than Dubai — more Islamic in its public culture, slower to adopt the anything-goes liberalism that Dubai has deployed as a branding strategy. The alcohol laws are more restrictive, the dress expectations more present, the pace of life deliberately less frenetic. Many visitors treat Abu Dhabi as a day trip from Dubai and miss the texture of this. Stay at least two nights. Walk the Corniche at 6am, when the city is at its most itself: joggers, dhows on the water, the towers still in early shadow. Go to the central market for vegetables and spices. Eat at a Yemeni restaurant in the older neighborhoods.

Dubai performs its ambition in public. Abu Dhabi tends to mean it quietly, which is a different thing altogether.

Here is what to carry into Abu Dhabi: this is a city built on oil money that is seriously trying to spend that money into something that outlasts the oil — into culture, into institutions, into the kind of soft power that makes countries matter after their natural resources are gone. You can question the methods, the labor conditions, the political system. All of those questions are legitimate and open. But the project itself is not cynical. It is the attempt of a young nation with very particular resources to become something worth remembering. The mosque and the museum are evidence of that attempt. Whether it works is a question that won't be answered for another generation.
