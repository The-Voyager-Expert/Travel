Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 20 min → Mistico Arenal Hanging Bridges Park
1. Mistico Arenal Hanging Bridges Park
↳ A private reserve with 16 bridges — six suspended steel-cable spans up to 98 m long — strung through primary rainforest above the Arenal foothills. The trail crosses 3 km of canopy and river-level forest, putting visitors at eye height with toucans, howler monkeys, and tree frogs.

🚕 10 min → Arenal Volcano National Park
2. Arenal Volcano National Park
↳ The park protects the 1,657m cone of Arenal Volcano. The Las Coladas trail crosses the solidified 1992 lava field at the base, threading through secondary forest reclaiming the hardened rock. The Mirador viewpoint faces the summit directly. Arrive early for the best odds of an unobstructed view.

🚕 25 min → La Fortuna Waterfall
3. La Fortuna Waterfall
↳ A 75-metre free-fall waterfall dropping into a pool at the base of a forested canyon 3 km south of La Fortuna. The descent to the pool is about 500 steps, steep; the ascent is the harder half. Steps can be slippery. Swimming in the pool is permitted. Fed by streams draining Chato Volcano.

4. Tabacón Thermal Resort & Spa
↳ A hot-springs complex fed by geothermal water heated by Arenal Volcano, channelled into terraced pools, rivers, and waterfalls framed by jungle. The grounds include a cold river, a waterfall to stand under, and a swim-up bar.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 30 min → Lake Arenal
1. Lake Arenal
↳ Costa Rica's largest lake — an 85 km² reservoir created in 1979 when a dam flooded the original town of Arenal. The northern shore road passes through cattle-ranch country with Arenal Volcano framing the eastern horizon. The western shore ranks among the world's top windsurfing venues.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Route 142 · La Fortuna de San Carlos
🚕 45 min → Balsa River
2. Balsa River White-water Rafting
↳ Class II–III white-water rafting on the Río Balsa through primary rainforest south of La Fortuna. Fast and continuous rapids suitable for first-timers and experienced paddlers alike. The riverbanks are dense jungle — sloths, toucans, and iguanas are commonly spotted from the water. All gear included.

🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 2h → Caño Negro National Wildlife Refuge
1. Caño Negro National Wildlife Refuge
↳ A flooded lowland refuge in the northern plains near the Nicaraguan border, centred on the Río Frío. Boat tours move at walking pace through channels packed with wildlife: caiman, jabiru storks, roseate spoonbills, anhingas, river turtles, howler monkeys, and freshwater bull sharks. Over 300 bird species recorded.

🍮 Local Tastes
Gallo Pinto
↳ Rice and black beans cooked together, seasoned with Salsa Lizano, served with eggs, tortillas, and fried plantains at breakfast. The national dish — every soda in La Fortuna serves it.
📍 Soda Víquez · La Fortuna de San Carlos
Casado
↳ The standard Costa Rican lunch plate: rice and beans side by side, a protein — grilled chicken, beef, or fish — and plantains or salad. Best-value eating at any local soda.
📍 Soda Víquez · La Fortuna de San Carlos
Chifrijo
↳ A layered bowl of frijoles, white rice, chicharrón, pico de gallo, and avocado served with tortilla chips. Invented in San José in the 1990s and now a staple bar food across Costa Rica.
📍 Don Rufino · La Fortuna de San Carlos
Café Chorreado
↳ Coffee brewed through a cloth sock filter — the traditional Costa Rican method. San Carlos region Arabica brewed this way is clean and full. Ask for negro or con leche.
📍 Red Frog Coffee Roaster · La Fortuna de San Carlos
Agua Dulce
↳ A hot morning drink made by dissolving raw cane sugar in hot water or milk. Common in the cooler mountain region around Arenal — lighter and sweeter than coffee.

🌋 Locals Called It Just a Mountain — Then It Erupted Without Warning

For centuries before 1968, the people living at the foot of Arenal called it Cerro Arenal — Arenal Hill — because nothing in living memory suggested it was anything other than a quiet, forested mountain. On the morning of July 29, 1968, it erupted with no warning at all, hurling rocks weighing several tons more than a kilometer at extreme speed and burying three villages — Tabacón, Pueblo Nuevo, and San Luís — under lava, ash, and debris. Eighty-seven people died, some swept away trying to cross a stream that the eruption had turned into a torrent within minutes. The Las Coladas trail that visitors now walk across at the base of the volcano runs directly over that same hardened 1968 flow.

Lake Arenal hides an entire drowned town beneath its surface. When the dam was built in 1979, it flooded the original settlement of Arenal along with the nearby town of Tronadora, and in dry years when the water drops low enough, the old church steeple still breaks the surface near the shore. The dam that submerged those towns now generates a large share of the country's electricity — the lake framing the volcano views on the Day 2 shore drive is, underneath, a working power plant.
