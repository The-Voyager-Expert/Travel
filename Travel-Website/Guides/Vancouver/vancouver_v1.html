Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 15 min · 🚕 7 min → Stanley Park
1.Stanley Park
↳ A 405-hectare old-growth rainforest peninsula — 10 First Nations totem poles at Brockton Point, a 22 km seawall, Lost Lagoon, and unbroken mountain views. Walk the waterfront from Coal Harbour to the totems and continue as far as time allows.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~3 hr
📍 Stanley Park · West End
🚶 20 min · 🚕 8 min → Vancouver Lookout
2.Vancouver Lookout
↳ Observation deck at the top of Harbour Centre, 169 m above street level. A glass-sided elevator rises in 40 seconds; the deck offers unobstructed 360° views of downtown, Stanley Park, Burrard Inlet, and the North Shore mountains.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ All-Day Admission · 4.5⭐ · 41+ reviews — GetYourGuide
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~45 min
📍 555 W Hastings Street · Downtown
🚶 5 min · 🚕 5 min → Gastown
3.Gastown
↳ Vancouver's oldest neighbourhood — 14 cobblestone blocks of Victorian red-brick buildings, boutiques, and the Steam Clock at Water and Cambie streets that chimes every 15 minutes. Named after "Gassy Jack" Deighton, who set up a saloon here.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 305 Water Street · Gastown
🚶 20 min · 🚕 8 min → hotel
Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 25 min → Capilano Suspension Bridge Park
1.Capilano Suspension Bridge Park
↳ A 140 m suspension bridge spanning 70 m above the Capilano River. The park also includes Cliffwalk — cantilevered platforms along a granite cliff — and Treetops Adventure, seven platforms linked by walkways through ancient Douglas firs.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ General Admission · 4.5⭐ — capbridge.com
🏛️ Daily 8:30am - 8:00pm
⏰ ~2 hr
⚠️ Timed entry required — book online in advance. Sells out on summer weekends.
📍 3735 Capilano Road · North Shore
🚶 20 min · 🚕 5 min → Grouse Mountain
2.Grouse Mountain
↳ The Skyride gondola climbs 1,100 m to 1,231 m above sea level. From the summit: panoramic views across downtown, the Strait of Georgia, and on clear days Mount Baker. Two resident grizzly bears — Grinder and Coola — live on the summit alongside hiking trails and a lumberjack show.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Skyride Mountain Admission · 4.6⭐ — grousemountain.com
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 10:00pm
⏰ ~3 hr
⚠️ Grouse Grind trail is one-way uphill only — take the paid gondola down after hiking.
📍 6400 Nancy Greene Way · North Shore
🚕 25 min → hotel
Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 50 min → Shannon Falls Provincial Park
1.Shannon Falls Provincial Park
↳ British Columbia's third-highest waterfall at 335 m — a short 5-minute flat walk from the car park. Immediately adjacent to the Sea to Sky Gondola, making both a natural pairing on the same day.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 7:00am - 10:00pm
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Highway 99 · Squamish
🚶 10 min · 🚕 3 min → Sea to Sky Gondola
2.Sea to Sky Gondola
↳ An 820 m gondola climb above Howe Sound fjord to Summit Lodge at 885 m, where a suspension bridge crosses a canyon and trails fan out above treeline. The view — water, fjord walls, and peaks in every direction — ranks among the most dramatic in the Sea to Sky corridor.
📖 Wikipedia

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 12 min → VanDusen Botanical Garden
1.VanDusen Botanical Garden
↳ Twenty-two hectares of botanic garden on a former golf course — over 7,500 plant species from around the world, an Elizabethan hedge maze, a rhododendron walk, and a Korean Pavilion. Saved from residential development by public campaign.
📖 Wikipedia

2.Bloedel Conservatory
↳ A triodetic dome 35 m wide atop Queen Elizabeth Park at the city's highest point — three climate zones with 100+ free-flying tropical birds and 500+ exotic plants. The park itself has a sweeping 360° view of the city skyline from 152 m.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ General Admission · 4.5⭐ · 40+ reviews — TripAdvisor
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 7:00pm

🚕 15 min → Dr. Sun Yat-Sen Classical Chinese Garden
3.Dr. Sun Yat-Sen Classical Chinese Garden
↳ The first full-scale classical Chinese garden built outside China. Completed by 52 artisans from Suzhou using no power tools, it recreates a Ming Dynasty scholar's garden — Taihu limestone, black pine, bamboo, and pavilions reflected in a jade-green pool.
📖 Wikipedia

Day 5
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 25 min · 🚕 7 min → Granville Island
1.Granville Island
↳ A former industrial peninsula tucked under the Granville Bridge, reinvented as Vancouver's arts-and-makers district — artist studios, a working foundry, theatres, galleries, and waterfront boardwalks along False Creek, with seaplanes overhead and little Aquabus ferries criss-crossing the water.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2 hrs
📍 Granville Island · False Creek
🚕 20 min → Museum of Anthropology at UBC
2.Museum of Anthropology at UBC
↳ Arthur Erickson's 1976 building on the UBC cliffs holds the world's finest Northwest Coast First Nations collection — massive carved house poles in the Great Hall against floor-to-ceiling glass, Bill Reid's cedar masterpiece The Raven and the First Men, and 9,000 objects in the open-storage Multiversity Galleries.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Sunday 10:00am - 5:00pm · Thursday 10:00am - 9:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~2 hrs
📍 6393 NW Marine Drive · UBC
🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → Nitobe Memorial Garden
3.Nitobe Memorial Garden
↳ One of the most authentic Japanese stroll gardens outside Japan — stone lanterns, koi ponds, a traditional tea garden, and maple-lined paths designed by Kannosuke Mori. Named for Inazo Nitobe, Japan's leading internationalist. A two-minute walk from the Museum of Anthropology.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 4:30pm
⏰ ~45 min
⚠️ Garden closes December to February — verify open dates before visiting
📍 1895 Lower Mall · UBC
🚕 25 min → hotel

🌊 Claude Inspiration

Vancouver is the only major city in the world where you can ski a glaciated mountain in the morning and kayak an ocean fjord in the afternoon — and both are within 30 minutes of the downtown towers. What makes it remarkable isn't any single attraction but the improbability of the combination: a genuinely great city pressed right up against genuinely wild nature, with no buffer between them. The seawall wraps around a rainforest. The gondola summit looks down on a financial district. The suspension bridge crosses a canyon you can't see the bottom of. Everywhere you go, the scale of the landscape makes the city feel provisional — like it arrived recently and hasn't fully decided whether to stay. That tension is Vancouver's real quality.
