A city barely 140 years old, wedged between mountains and ocean, still figuring out what it wants to be — and already one of the most beautiful places on earth.

The setting

Vancouver has the most dramatic physical setting of any major city in North America, and it is not close. To the north, the Coast Mountains rise directly from the harbor — not in the distance, not as a backdrop, but right there, close enough that their snowline determines the color of the city's light on any given afternoon. To the west, the Pacific. To the south, the wide mud-brown mouth of the Fraser River. The city occupies a peninsula between all of this, and on clear days the effect is of standing inside a landscape painting that someone has accidentally filled with glass towers and coffee shops.

You can ski Grouse Mountain in the morning and swim in the ocean in the afternoon. People say this about Vancouver the way they say things about places that sound too good to be true, except in this case it is literally true and locals actually do it. The North Shore mountains are a twenty-minute drive from downtown. English Bay is a ten-minute walk. The transition from alpine to urban to marine happens at a speed that your brain doesn't quite process the first time.

And then there is Stanley Park — a thousand acres of old-growth temperate rainforest on a peninsula that juts into Burrard Inlet, surrounded by water on three sides. It is larger than Central Park. It is older than the city itself. The trees here — western red cedar, Douglas fir, Sitka spruce — were already centuries old when the first sawmill opened on the inlet. A 22-kilometer seawall rings the park's perimeter, and walking it is one of those experiences that makes you briefly angry at wherever you actually live.

The first peoples

The land Vancouver occupies has been home to the Musqueam, Squamish, and Tsleil-Waututh peoples for thousands of years before any European arrival, and their presence here is not historical. It is current. This is one of the things that distinguishes Vancouver from most North American cities: the Indigenous nations whose territory the city sits on are not absent from it. They are here, visibly, in the governance and the art and the daily texture of the place.

The totem poles at Brockton Point in Stanley Park are among the most visited attractions in British Columbia. They are not artifacts. They are not relics. Several are recent commissions by living carvers from the Squamish and other Coast Salish and Kwakwaka'wakw nations. They stand at the edge of the water and they look out at the mountains and the harbor, and if you arrive early in the morning before the tour buses, you can stand among them in something close to silence and begin to understand why this particular place was chosen, over and over, for thousands of years.

The Museum of Anthropology at the University of British Columbia is the building where this understanding deepens. Arthur Erickson designed it in 1976 — a concrete-and-glass structure set on the cliffs above the ocean, its Great Hall framed by floor-to-ceiling windows that look out at the water and the mountains beyond. Inside, the museum holds one of the world's great collections of Northwest Coast Indigenous art: massive carved house posts, feast dishes, ceremonial regalia, and Bill Reid's The Raven and the First Men, a monumental yellow cedar sculpture depicting a Haida creation story. Reid carved a raven perched on a clamshell, coaxing the first humans into the world. It is four and a half feet tall and it is one of the most important works of art in Canada.

Vancouver is not built on top of Indigenous history. It is built inside it. The difference matters.

The railroad city

Vancouver exists because of a corporate decision. In 1884, the Canadian Pacific Railway chose this small lumber settlement on Burrard Inlet as the western terminus of the transcontinental railroad, and the city — which had been incorporated only the year before — went from a population of roughly a thousand to a continental gateway almost overnight. The first transcontinental train arrived on May 23, 1887. The city is barely 140 years old.

Before the railroad, there was Gastown — a sawmill settlement on the inlet's south shore, named not for any industrial process but for a talkative English saloon keeper named John "Gassy Jack" Deighton, who arrived in 1867 with a barrel of whiskey and an understanding that sawmill workers would want somewhere to drink. The settlement that grew around his saloon became the nucleus of the city. Today Gastown is fourteen blocks of Victorian red-brick, cobblestone streets, and a steam clock at Water and Cambie that chimes every fifteen minutes. It is the oldest neighborhood in the city, and it is younger than most American gas stations.

This youth is not a deficiency. It is the thing that makes Vancouver feel different from every other major city on the Pacific Rim. There is no medieval quarter. There is no colonial old town slowly calcifying into a heritage district. The oldest European-built structures in the city date from the 1890s, and many of them are still in active commercial use. Vancouver has no ruins because it hasn't been around long enough to produce any.

The Asian Pacific city

Vancouver's relationship with Asia is not an add-on, not a recent development, not a demographic footnote. It is the city's economic and cultural center of gravity, and it has been for longer than most people realize.

The Chinese community in Vancouver dates to the construction of the Canadian Pacific Railway in the 1880s, when thousands of Chinese workers were brought in to build the most dangerous sections of track through the Rocky Mountains. They were paid a fraction of what white workers earned. When the railroad was finished, the Canadian government imposed a head tax on Chinese immigrants — first fifty dollars, then five hundred, the equivalent of two years' wages — and then banned Chinese immigration entirely from 1923 to 1947. Chinatown survived all of this. It contracted, it endured decades of official hostility and social exclusion, and it is still here, centered on Pender Street, one of the oldest Chinatowns in North America.

But the Chinese-Canadian presence in Vancouver now extends far beyond Chinatown. Richmond, the suburb immediately south of the city across the Fraser River, is majority Chinese-Canadian, and its food — particularly along Alexandra Road and in the Aberdeen Centre mall — is routinely cited as some of the best Chinese food outside of Asia. This is not an exaggeration designed to flatter a diaspora community. The dim sum in Richmond is genuinely extraordinary. The ingredients are fresh, the cooks are skilled, and the diners are demanding, because many of them grew up eating this food in Guangdong or Hong Kong or Taipei and they know exactly what it should taste like.

The same applies to the Japanese food. Vancouver's sushi and ramen are the best in North America outside of a handful of restaurants in New York and Los Angeles, and the reason is the same: the cooks and the audience are here because this is a Pacific city, not a city that happens to face the Pacific.

The price of beauty

Vancouver is one of the most expensive housing markets in the world relative to local income, and this fact shapes the city as profoundly as the mountains and the water do. The glass condo towers along False Creek and Coal Harbour are beautiful objects — slender, reflective, catching the mountain light in ways that make the skyline look like a rendering of itself — and they represent a housing market that has priced out much of the working and middle class.

The city's beauty and livability created a demand that has made it unaffordable for many of the people who make it livable.

The causes are debated endlessly and bitterly. Foreign investment, particularly from mainland China, drove prices up sharply in the 2010s. The provincial and federal governments responded with foreign buyer taxes and vacancy taxes and speculation taxes, and the prices paused and then resumed climbing. The geography plays a role too: Vancouver is hemmed in by mountains, water, the American border, and the Agricultural Land Reserve, which protects farmland from development. There is not much room to sprawl, so the pressure goes upward.

What this means, practically, is that the young people who work in the restaurants and the shops and the studios that make Vancouver the kind of city people want to live in increasingly cannot afford to live in it. They commute from Surrey and Langley and Maple Ridge, an hour or more each way, and the city they serve becomes incrementally less theirs. This is not unique to Vancouver — it is the story of every desirable city in the developed world — but the contrast here between the physical beauty of the place and the economic stress of living in it is particularly sharp.

The rain

Vancouver's reputation for rain is accurate but misunderstood. It does not rain hard. It mists. It drizzles. It stays gray and damp and low-ceilinged from roughly November through March, and the mountains that are so spectacular on clear days simply vanish behind a wall of cloud that descends to about the elevation of the Starbucks signs. Locals do not use umbrellas. This is a shibboleth — a genuine cultural marker. If you see someone walking through the rain with an umbrella in Vancouver, that person is a tourist or has recently arrived from somewhere drier. Vancouverites wear waterproof shells and accept the moisture as a fact of life, the way New Yorkers accept noise.

But when the sun comes out — and it does, spectacularly, from May through September — the city transforms so completely that you wonder if you are in the same place. The mountains emerge. The water turns from gray to a deep cold blue. The snow on the peaks catches the light. People pour out of their apartments and their offices and onto the seawall and the beaches and the patios, and the collective relief is almost physical. You understand, in those moments, why people pay what they pay. You understand why they endure the five months of drizzle without complaint. The deal is this: you get a summer that feels like a reward for something you didn't know you'd earned.

A city still becoming

Vancouver is the youngest major city you are likely to visit, and it shows — not in immaturity, not in any lack of sophistication, but in the absence of accumulated historical weight. There are no medieval quarters to preserve, no ancient grudges to maintain, no centuries of architectural argument to relitigate. The oldest building in the city would be a recent addition in most European towns. The cultural institutions are still being built. The neighborhoods are still sorting themselves out.

This gives Vancouver a quality that is hard to name but easy to feel. The city is not finished. It is not settled. It is still becoming whatever it is going to be, and the decisions it makes now — about housing, about transit, about its relationship with the First Nations whose land it occupies, about how much of its character it is willing to trade for growth — will determine what kind of city it is in fifty years. The mountains and the ocean, of course, don't care what it decides. They were here before the sawmills and they will be here after whatever comes next. That indifference is part of the beauty. The setting does not need the city. The city needs the setting, and it knows it.
