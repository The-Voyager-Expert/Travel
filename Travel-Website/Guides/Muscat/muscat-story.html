A harbor city where Portuguese cannon emplacements guard the same waterfront as a ceremonial palace with a blue-and-gold facade — and where a country rebuilt itself from near-zero in a single generation.

The Shape of the City

Muscat does not announce itself. There is no skyline in the conventional sense — no cluster of towers visible from twenty kilometers away, no single image that resolves into the place when you think of it. Instead, Muscat is a series of districts threaded along 60 kilometers of Gulf of Oman coastline, separated by ridges of the Hajar Mountains that push down to the water and force the city to split around them. Old Muscat, Muttrah, Ruwi, Qurum, Shati Al-Qurm: each district has its own center of gravity, its own texture, and the drives between them cut through bare limestone rock before the city reassembles on the other side.

Old Muscat is the original settlement — a pocket of harbor behind two volcanic headlands, used by Indian Ocean traders for centuries before the Portuguese arrived in 1507. The twin forts they built, Al Jalali and Al Mirani, still stand on those headlands, flanking the ceremonial palace that Sultan Qaboos built in 1972. The juxtaposition is Muscat in a frame: four centuries of layered authority, and the place still feels quiet enough to hear the water.

Muscat still has the calm and openness of somewhere that hasn't quite caught up to being famous yet.

Before 1970

The modern history of Oman begins with a date: 23 July 1970. On that day, Sultan Qaboos bin Said deposed his father Said bin Taimur in a palace coup and began what became one of the most compressed national transformations of the twentieth century. The country he inherited had fewer than ten kilometers of paved road, one civilian hospital, three formal schools, and a population largely kept in poverty under a regime that banned radios, sunglasses, and the wearing of trousers in public.

Said bin Taimur had governed in isolation since 1932. Oman's oil had been discovered in 1964, but the revenues were hoarded rather than deployed. By 1970, neighboring Gulf states were building universities and highways while Oman remained effectively medieval. The sultan lived in Salalah, in the south, and rarely left. Muscat — the nominal capital — had no electricity grid.

Qaboos was 29 when he took power. He had been educated at Sandhurst and had spent his early adult years under a form of house arrest in Salalah, studying Islam and music. The first public statement of his reign promised schools, hospitals, roads, and the lifting of the restrictions his father had imposed. It was not rhetorical.

The Transformation

Between 1970 and 2000, Oman went from near-zero infrastructure to a functional modern state. The statistics are difficult to contextualize: from 3 schools to over 1,000; from 1 hospital to hundreds of clinics and hospitals; from 10 kilometers of paved road to a national highway network. Muscat acquired electricity, water systems, a port, an international airport, a university, and eventually a Royal Opera House — the first on the Arabian Peninsula, opened in 2011.

The Sultan Qaboos Grand Mosque, which opened in 2001, encodes the scale of this ambition in a single building. The main prayer hall holds a hand-knotted Persian carpet woven over four years by 600 craftspeople in Iran — 4,343 square meters, 1.7 billion knots, still the largest hand-knotted carpet in the world. The chandelier above it is 14 meters tall and holds 1,122 lamps. The mosque took six years to build and was inaugurated as a gift from the sultan to the nation. Visiting it as a non-Muslim — which is permitted every morning except Friday — is one of the stranger and more affecting experiences Muscat offers: the scale of the space, the carpet underfoot, and the fact that it is simply open.

Old Town and Muttrah

Old Muscat and Muttrah sit four kilometers apart around a mountain ridge, and the distinction between them matters. Old Muscat is where the administrative and ceremonial core of the old city was: Al Alam Palace, the Portuguese forts, the national museum, Bait Al Zubair. It is small, well-maintained, and oriented toward history. The streets are quiet in the heat of the day and the harbor has the scale of a town, not a city.

Muttrah is older in feel, more commercial, and more immediately alive. The Corniche along the harbor is where Muscat's residents actually walk — date palms, dhows, the mountains on the far side of the water. The Muttrah Souq behind it is one of the Gulf's oldest covered markets, a labyrinth of silver, frankincense, khanjar daggers, and textiles that has been operating in some form for centuries. Frankincense — Oman's ancient export commodity, traded before oil was imaginable — is still sold here by weight, in resin chunks that range from pale green to amber.

Frankincense and the Indian Ocean World

Oman's position at the mouth of the Arabian Gulf, with its natural harbor at Muscat and its command of the trade winds, made it one of the defining nodes of the Indian Ocean trading world for over two thousand years. Omani sailors navigated to East Africa, India, and Southeast Asia using monsoon winds before the compass was in common use. The dhow — the shallow-drafted, lateen-rigged wooden vessel still visible in Muttrah harbor — was the instrument of this commerce.

Frankincense was the first commodity. The Dhofar region of southern Oman produces Boswellia sacra, the tree whose resin is the source of the highest-grade frankincense in the world. It was traded to Egypt, Rome, and Mesopotamia across overland and sea routes for thousands of years. By the medieval period, Muscat had become a critical entrepôt for the wider trade — spices from India, silk from China, gold and ivory from East Africa. The Portuguese understood this when they took Muscat by force in 1507 and held it for 143 years.

The National Museum of Oman devotes substantial space to this maritime history, and the objects it holds — navigation instruments, trading contracts, carved ivory, ancient coins from across the trade network — make the abstraction of "Indian Ocean commerce" concrete and specific. It is one of the better history museums in the region precisely because it has a subject: not just Oman's history, but Oman's role in a larger world that most people in the Gulf never think about.

The Omani Approach

Muscat carries itself differently from Dubai or Abu Dhabi. There is no ambition visible in the architecture — no tower competing to be tallest, no development project announced to reshape the skyline. What there is instead is a kind of careful restraint that runs through the built environment: most buildings are white or cream, height limits have been enforced, the old coastline has not been reclaimed into artificial islands. The Royal Opera House, which could easily have been a maximalist statement, is instead a building of disciplined Omani-Islamic architecture — arches, carved screens, muted color, craft visible in the stonework rather than broadcast in scale.

This restraint has something to do with Omani foreign policy — a country that has maintained neutrality and diplomatic access across ideological lines that divide the rest of the Gulf — and something to do with Sultan Qaboos himself, who died in January 2020 after fifty years in power. Qaboos was unusual among Gulf monarchs: publicly committed to education, to women in the workforce, to religious tolerance, and to a foreign policy that kept Oman out of the conflicts that drew in its neighbors. The transformation he presided over was extraordinary. The question Muscat now poses is what happens to a country built around the will and vision of a single person when that person is gone.
