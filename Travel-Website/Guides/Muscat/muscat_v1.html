Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 25 min → Sultan Qaboos Grand Mosque
1. Sultan Qaboos Grand Mosque
↳ The largest mosque in Oman — one of the few on the Arabian Peninsula open to non-Muslim visitors. The main prayer hall holds a hand-knotted Persian carpet: 4,343 m², woven by 600 craftspeople over four years, 1.7 billion knots — still the largest hand-knotted carpet in the world.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Saturday - Thursday 8:00am - 11:00am (non-Muslim visitors)
🚫 Closed Friday

🚕 15 min → National Museum of Oman
2. National Museum of Oman
↳ Oman's flagship state museum — 14 galleries across 13,700 m² tracing Omani civilization from prehistoric times to the modern era. Bronze Age metalwork, a maritime history gallery on Oman's Indian Ocean trade routes, and the Aflaj gallery on the underground irrigation systems still in use today.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ National Museum of Oman
🏛️ Saturday - Thursday 10:00am - 5:00pm · Friday 2:00pm - 6:00pm
⏰ ~2 hr
📍 Al Saidiya Street · Old Town
🚶 5 min · 🚕 2 min → Al Alam Palace
3. Al Alam Palace
↳ The Sultan of Oman's ceremonial palace, built in 1972, positioned between the twin Portuguese forts on Old Town's natural harbor. The striking blue-and-gold facade is the defining image of Old Town. Viewed from the courtyard and adjacent promenade — the interior is not open to the public.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Al Alam Road · Old Town
🚶 5 min · 🚕 2 min → Bait Al Zubair Museum
4. Bait Al Zubair Museum
↳ Muscat's finest private heritage museum in a restored Omani house complex. The Zubair family collection spans khanjar daggers, silver jewelry, traditional costumes, and manuscripts across five interconnected buildings around a garden courtyard. Received Sultan Qaboos' Award for Architectural Excellence in 1999.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Bait Al Zubair Museum
🏛️ Saturday - Thursday 9:00am - 5:00pm
🚫 Closed Friday
⏰ ~1.5 hr
📍 Al Saidiya Street · Old Town
🚶 10 min · 🚕 3 min → Al Jalali & Al Mirani Fort Viewpoints
5. Al Jalali & Al Mirani Fort Viewpoints
↳ Twin Portuguese forts built in 1587 to guard Old Town's natural harbor. Al Jalali on the east promontory, Al Mirani on the west — both closed to the public, but viewed from the promenade with Al Alam Palace between them, they form the most photographed panorama in Muscat.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 15 min → Muttrah Corniche
1. Muttrah Corniche
↳ A 3-kilometer waterfront promenade along Muttrah's natural harbor, lined with date palms and moored wooden dhows against the Hajar Mountains. One of the most photogenic waterfronts in the Gulf — best in the early morning and at dusk when fishing boats return.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Muttrah Corniche Road · Muttrah
🚶 8 min · 🚕 3 min → Muttrah Fort
2. Muttrah Fort
↳ A 16th-century Portuguese fort on the hill above Muttrah, part of the ring of fortifications built after the Portuguese took Muscat in 1507. The fort overlooks the Corniche and harbor, giving a panoramic view of the dhow-lined bay. The battlements and exterior are accessible.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~30 min
🆓 Free
📍 Above Muttrah Corniche · Muttrah
🚶 8 min · 🚕 3 min → Bait Al Baranda Museum
3. Bait Al Baranda Museum
↳ The city museum of Muscat, in a restored 1920s merchant house on the Muttrah Corniche. Covers Muscat's geological formation, prehistoric settlement, and maritime trade history from the Bronze Age through the Portuguese era. One of the most focused city history museums in Oman.
🏛️ Sunday - Thursday 9:00am - 5:00pm · Saturday 9:00am - 1:00pm
🚫 Closed Friday
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Muttrah Corniche · Muttrah
🚕 10 min → Al Riyam Park & Incense Burner Monument
4. Al Riyam Park & Incense Burner Monument
↳ A hilltop park at the eastern end of the Muttrah Corniche, anchored by a giant stone incense burner — a Mabkhara — built for Oman's 20th National Day in 1990. Panoramic views across Muttrah harbor and the Hajar Mountains. Best at golden hour when the light catches the white stone.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 11:30pm
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Eastern end of Muttrah Corniche · Muttrah

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 20 min → Qurum Natural Park & Nature Reserve
1. Qurum Natural Park & Nature Reserve
↳ Muscat's largest urban park with spacious lawns, a lake, and direct access to 4 km of public beach on the Gulf of Oman. The adjacent Qurum Nature Reserve is a protected mangrove estuary with boardwalk access to grey herons, egrets, and kingfishers — one of the best urban birdwatching spots in Oman.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 11:00pm
⏰ ~1.5 hr
📍 Al Qurm Street · Qurum
🚕 12 min → Royal Opera House Muscat
2. Royal Opera House Muscat
↳ The first opera house on the Arabian Peninsula, opened October 2011. The building blends contemporary Omani architecture with Italian craftsmanship — the same artisans who carved the mahogany interior ceilings also designed the adjacent Al Angham restaurant. Season October–May; building tours year-round.
📖 Wikipedia

🍮 Local Tastes
Shuwa
↳ Oman's national dish — whole lamb marinated in spices, wrapped in banana leaves, and slow-cooked in an underground earth oven for 24 to 48 hours. Served on Fridays.
📍 Bait Al Luban · Al Angham
Majboos
↳ Oman's most common daily rice dish — spiced basmati cooked in a rich broth with chicken or lamb, flavored with dried limes, saffron, turmeric, and rose water. Found at every local restaurant.
📍 Al Hawas Restaurant · Bait Al Luban
Omani Halwa
↳ The defining Omani sweet — a dense, gelatinous confection of sugar, ghee, cornstarch, saffron, rose water, and cardamom, often studded with nuts. Served with qahwa coffee at every traditional gathering.
📍 Kesar Restaurant & Sweets · Muttrah Souq
Omani Qahwa
↳ Ceremonial Arabic coffee — lightly roasted beans steeped with cardamom and saffron, served in handle-less cups called finjan. Poured continuously until the guest shakes the cup to signal enough. Always served with dates and halwa.
📍 Bait Al Luban · Al Angham

✨ The Rug and the Road

The main prayer hall of the Sultan Qaboos Grand Mosque is anchored by a single hand-knotted Persian carpet measuring 4,343 square meters — woven over four years by 600 craftspeople in Iran, shipped in sections, assembled on-site, and still the largest hand-knotted carpet in the world. The math alone stops you: 1.7 billion knots. 28 tonnes. One room.

What the carpet symbolizes is as striking as the carpet itself. Sultan Qaboos came to power in 1970 in a country with fewer than 10 kilometers of paved road, one hospital, and three schools. The mosque opened in 2001. A civilization-scale transformation in the lifetime of people who remember the before — and a city that still has the calm and openness of somewhere that hasn't quite caught up to being famous yet.
