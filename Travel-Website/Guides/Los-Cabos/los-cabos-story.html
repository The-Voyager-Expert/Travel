At the exact southern tip of the Baja Peninsula, where the Pacific Ocean meets the Sea of Cortez across a single visible line, Cabo San Lucas has been shaped by geography more than by history.

Land's End

The Arch of Cabo San Lucas — the granite formation at Land's End that every boat photograph captures — is not simply a scenic landmark. It is the precise geological dividing point between two bodies of water with different temperatures, different current patterns, and different marine populations. Jacques Cousteau called the Sea of Cortez "the world's aquarium." The Pacific side of the same headland is cold, rough, and largely empty of the reef life that characterizes the other sea just around the corner.

Standing at the Arch by water taxi, you can watch the water change color from slate grey-green to luminous turquoise across a visible line. On calm days, the transition is sharp enough to photograph. Most visitors spend their whole trip on the Sea of Cortez side — the snorkeling bays, the marina, the beach strip — without realizing the Pacific is a five-minute boat ride in the opposite direction with a completely different character.

The whole arc of a Los Cabos trip is shaped by that boundary between two oceans compressed into thirty kilometers of desert peninsula.

The Corridor and the Two Towns

Los Cabos is not a single city. It is two distinct towns — Cabo San Lucas and San José del Cabo — connected by a 30-kilometer stretch of highway called the Tourist Corridor, lined with resort hotels and punctuated by the snorkeling bays of Santa Maria and Chileno. The two towns have almost no resemblance to each other. Cabo San Lucas grew from a fishing village into a resort and sport-fishing hub; San José del Cabo retained its colonial grid, its church plaza, its gallery district, and a functioning estuary where birds feed at the edge of hotel territory.

Most visitors stay in one town and never reach the other, or use the Corridor bays only as beach stops between hotels. The estuary at San José — Estero San José, a federal protected zone where the Río San José meets the sea — is five minutes from the hotel zone and almost entirely absent from standard itineraries. It is one of the few places in the entire Los Cabos municipality where the land looks as it did before the resort development arrived.

Todos Santos and the Desert Interior

Eighty kilometers north of Cabo San Lucas on Mexico Highway 19, the town of Todos Santos sits in a narrow band of agricultural land watered by underground springs descending from the Sierra de la Laguna. The surrounding desert grows nothing; the town grows mangoes, sugarcane, and avocados. It has also attracted artists, boutique hotels, and one of the more durable myths in Baja travel — the claim that the Eagles song "Hotel California" was written about the Hotel California on Juárez Street.

The hotel existed before the song. The song has no verified connection to the building. The owners have leaned into the association for decades. The hotel is genuinely worth seeing regardless — a 19th-century hacienda with thick adobe walls, painted rooms, and a courtyard with a mango tree — but the mythology around it tells you something accurate about Todos Santos more broadly: a small town that has been successfully marketed to travelers without entirely losing the texture that made it interesting in the first place.

Cabo Pulmo and the East Cape

The road to Cabo Pulmo runs northeast from Cabo San Lucas along the East Cape — a coastal corridor that has no resort hotels, almost no services, and a reef system that has been a UNESCO-recognized marine national park since 1995. Cabo Pulmo's coral reef is one of only three living coral reefs in North America. After a community-led ban on fishing in the early 1990s, the reef biomass increased by 463 percent in ten years — one of the most documented marine recovery stories on record.

The dive sites and snorkeling spots at Cabo Pulmo are accessed by a handful of licensed local operators who know the reef. The town of Cabo Pulmo itself is a cluster of small houses, a few guesthouses, and a dive shop. The contrast with the Corridor hotels — visible from the highway 105 kilometers west — is complete and deliberate. The communities that live along the East Cape have repeatedly declined development proposals to preserve the reef conditions that sustain the marine park.

Water and Scarcity

Everything in Los Cabos depends on water that is not naturally there. The southern Baja Peninsula receives less than 250mm of annual rainfall. The resort development that began in earnest in the 1980s required desalination plants, water trucking, and aquifer draws that have been studied for sustainability concerns ever since. The golf courses along the Corridor — there are more than a dozen — each require millions of liters of water per year in a desert landscape.

This is not unique to Los Cabos; it is the calculus of desert resort development everywhere. But it is worth understanding as context for what the landscape actually is. The sugarcane fields of Todos Santos and the mangrove estuary of San José exist only where fresh water surfaces naturally. The rest of the peninsula is Sonoran desert scrub, cardón cactus, and rock. The resort hotels are engineering projects as much as they are hospitality projects, maintaining conditions that the landscape itself does not provide.
