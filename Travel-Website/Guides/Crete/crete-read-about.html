Crete housed the oldest advanced civilization in Europe — a Bronze Age palace culture that built five-story complexes, developed writing systems that remain undeciphered, and collapsed suddenly around 1450 BC, leaving ruins that weren't excavated until 1900.

The Minoans and what they actually built

The Minoan civilization took its name from Minos, the legendary king of Crete, but the people themselves left no written record of what they called themselves. What they left instead was structural: five major palace complexes — Knossos, Phaistos, Malia, Zakros, and Kydonia — built and rebuilt between approximately 2000 and 1450 BC, each with multi-story rooms, paved courtyards, extensive storage capacity, and sophisticated drainage systems. The palaces weren't fortified. No walls, no defensive architecture. That absence is still debated.

Knossos is the largest and most visited, but it's also the most altered. British archaeologist Arthur Evans excavated the site starting in 1900 and made a decision that continues to generate argument: he reconstructed major sections using reinforced concrete and painted the reconstructed walls with frescoes derived from fragments found on site. What you walk through today is partly Evans's interpretation of what the palace might have looked like — the North Entrance with the bull relief, the Throne Room, the Grand Staircase. Some of the reconstruction matches the evidence closely; other sections go considerably further than the fragments warranted.

Phaistos, by contrast, was not reconstructed. The site sits on a ridge above the Mesara Plain, 62km southwest of Heraklion, and what remains is original stonework from two distinct phases: a First Palace destroyed around 1700 BC (probably by earthquake) and a Second Palace built on top of it. The Phaistos Disc was found here in 1908 — a baked clay disc about 15cm in diameter, covered on both sides with a spiral sequence of stamped symbols that have never been deciphered. It's in the Heraklion Archaeological Museum.

Two writing systems, neither fully decoded

The Minoans developed at least two distinct writing systems. Linear A, used from roughly 1800 to 1450 BC, appears on clay tablets, pottery, and stone at sites across Crete and the Aegean. It has not been deciphered. Approximately 1,500 inscriptions survive, not enough (and not diverse enough in content) to allow translation without a bilingual key that doesn't exist.

Linear B, the second system, was adapted from Linear A and used by Mycenaean Greeks who occupied Crete after the Minoan collapse. Linear B was deciphered in 1952 by Michael Ventris, a British architect with no formal training in classics, who recognized it as an early form of Greek. The tablets turned out to be administrative records: inventories of livestock, grain, textiles, and military equipment. There are no Linear B literary texts. The decipherment confirmed that Mycenaeans were on Crete but told us nothing about what happened to the Minoans or what language Linear A encodes.

The collapse

Around 1450 BC, almost every Minoan palace site was destroyed. Knossos survived and continued to function, but under Mycenaean administration rather than Minoan. The cause of the destructions is unresolved. The eruption of the Thera volcano (modern Santorini), approximately 100km north of Crete, had been proposed as a cause, but the chronology is disputed — most current dating puts the Thera eruption decades before the destructions. Other theories involve Mycenaean invasion, internal revolt, drought, or a combination. The evidence doesn't settle it.

What's certain is that the palace culture that had dominated the Aegean for five centuries ended abruptly. The Linear A writing system disappeared. The palace complexes were never rebuilt to their pre-collapse scale. The Mycenaeans at Knossos continued for another century or two before that site was also abandoned, probably around 1300 BC.

What followed

After the Mycenaean period, Crete was populated by Dorian Greeks — the same migration that reshaped much of Greece in the centuries after the Bronze Age collapse. The island fragmented into dozens of independent city-states. Gortyna, in the Mesara Plain, eventually became the dominant city and, under Roman rule, the capital of the province of Crete and Cyrenaica. The Code of Gortyna — 12 limestone blocks carved with civil law governing marriage, divorce, inheritance, property, and the status of slaves — dates to approximately 480 BC and is the most complete surviving Greek legal code.

Rome held Crete from 67 BC. Byzantium held it from the 4th century until 824 AD, when Arab forces from Al-Andalus seized the island and held it for over a century. Byzantine forces retook it in 961. The Venetians acquired Crete in 1204, as part of the partition after the Fourth Crusade, and held it for over four centuries — the longest single period of external rule in the island's recorded history. Ottoman forces besieged Heraklion from 1648 to 1669 in the longest siege in recorded history; the city fell after 21 years. Crete joined Greece in 1913, after a series of revolts and autonomous periods following the Greco-Turkish War of 1897.

The Battle of Crete

In May 1941, Crete was the site of the first large-scale airborne invasion in military history. Germany deployed approximately 22,000 paratroopers and glider-borne troops in Operation Mercury. Allied forces — British, Australian, New Zealand, and Greek — numbered around 40,000. The Allied force outnumbered the attackers, had prior intelligence of the invasion from Ultra intercepts, and held the high ground. They lost anyway, largely due to failures in communication, the destruction of the Maleme airfield on the first day, and the decision not to counterattack immediately when the outcome was still in question. The Historical Museum in Heraklion covers the battle in detail; so does the War Cemetery at Souda Bay, where 1,527 Commonwealth soldiers are buried.
