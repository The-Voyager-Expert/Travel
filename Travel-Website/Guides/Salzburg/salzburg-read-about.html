A city the size of a small American suburb that produced Mozart, hosted Europe's most prestigious music festival, and was ruled for eight centuries by archbishops who thought they were kings — because, legally, they were.

The Church-State on the River

Salzburg was not part of Austria for most of its history. This is the single most important thing to understand before you walk through it, because it explains nearly everything you see. For over eight hundred years — from 798 to 1803 — Salzburg was an independent prince-archbishopric, a sovereign state ruled by Catholic archbishops who held both spiritual and temporal power. They answered to the Pope and the Holy Roman Emperor, not to the Habsburgs in Vienna. They collected taxes, maintained armies, minted coins, and built a city to match their ambitions.

It was Napoleon who ended it. When his armies redrew the map of Europe, the old ecclesiastical principalities were dissolved in the Reichsdeputationshauptschluss of 1803. Salzburg was secularized, bounced between Bavaria and Austria, and finally became a permanent Austrian province in 1816. By then, the archbishops had already left their mark on every square meter of the old town. The Baroque churches, the grand residences, the fountains, the gardens — all of it was built by men who governed a city-state and wanted it to look like one.

The Fortress Above

Hohensalzburg Fortress sits on the Festungsberg hill above the old town, and it has been sitting there since 1077. Archbishop Gebhard I built the original structure during the Investiture Controversy — a power struggle between the Pope and the Holy Roman Emperor over who got to appoint bishops. Gebhard backed the Pope and needed somewhere to retreat if the Emperor's forces came knocking. They did. The fortress was expanded over the next five centuries by successive archbishops, each one adding walls, towers, and rooms until it became the largest fully preserved castle in central Europe.

You can see it from almost everywhere in the city, and that is not an accident. The fortress was designed to be visible — a physical reminder that the archbishop's authority was not abstract. It was stone, it was armed, and it was watching. Prince-Archbishop Leonhard von Keutschach, who completed the fortress's final major expansion around 1500, lived up there in rooms that included a Gothic chapel, a torture chamber, and elaborate ceramic stoves decorated with saints. Spiritual and secular power shared the same floor plan.

Salzburg's archbishops built a fortress that could be seen from every street in town. The message was not subtle, and it was not meant to be.

Today you ride a funicular to the top, and the view from the ramparts explains the city's geography: the Salzach River dividing the old town from the new, the domes of the cathedral and the Franciscan church clustered below, and the Alps beginning immediately to the south. Untersberg, the nearest peak, is fifteen minutes away by car. The mountain wall is so close that Salzburg feels less like a city at the edge of the Alps and more like a city the Alps decided to allow.

Mozart: Born Here, Gone at Twenty-Five

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart was born in Salzburg on January 27, 1756, in a third-floor apartment on Getreidegasse. His father, Leopold, was a court musician for the prince-archbishop. The young Mozart was a prodigy whose talent was obvious before he turned six, and Leopold spent years touring him across the courts of Europe — Vienna, Paris, London, Rome — like a walking advertisement for what Salzburg could produce.

Mozart's relationship with Salzburg was complicated, and it ended badly. He worked for Prince-Archbishop Hieronymus Colloredo, who treated him as a servant — which, by the social rules of the time, he technically was. Court musicians ate with the valets. Mozart found this intolerable, and after years of friction, he was dismissed in 1781 with what he described in a letter as a literal kick. He moved to Vienna, never returned to Salzburg to live, and died there in 1791 at thirty-five.

Salzburg's relationship with its most famous native is the kind of love story where one party only realized what they had after the other was gone. The city ignored Mozart for decades after his death. It was not until the mid-nineteenth century that Salzburg began building the Mozart mythology — the museums, the statues, the Mozartkugel chocolates that fill every shop window on Getreidegasse. His birthplace is now a museum. His family's later residence across the river is another museum. The city that could not keep him alive has made a permanent industry of keeping him present.

The Festival and the Americans' Movie

The Salzburger Festspiele — the Salzburg Festival — was founded in 1920 by the theater director Max Reinhardt, the poet Hugo von Hofmannsthal, and the composer Richard Strauss. Their timing was deliberate. Austria had just lost World War I, the Habsburg Empire had dissolved, and the country was in economic and psychological freefall. The founders wanted to prove that Austrian culture was not dead — that it could still command the world's attention. They staged Hofmannsthal's Jedermann (Everyman) on the cathedral square that first summer, and the festival has opened with that same play on that same square every year since, even during the years of Nazi occupation.

The festival now runs for six weeks each summer and stages opera, theater, and concerts across multiple venues, including the carved-from-rock Felsenreitschule. It draws the highest tier of performers and the highest tier of ticket prices. Salzburg in July and August is a different city — formal, crowded, and expensive, filled with people who flew in for a single evening of Strauss or Verdi.

And then there is The Sound of Music. The 1965 film starring Julie Andrews was shot on location in Salzburg and its surroundings — the Mirabell Gardens, the Nonnberg Abbey, the lake district in the Salzkammergut. It became one of the highest-grossing films of all time in the English-speaking world, and it turned Salzburg into a pilgrimage site for American and British tourists. The twist is that Austrians barely knew the film existed for decades. It was never a hit in the German-speaking market. The Sound of Music tour, which takes busloads of visitors to the filming locations, is an enterprise built almost entirely for English-speaking visitors. Locals are bemused by it, occasionally baffled, and perfectly happy to sell you a ticket.

Salt, Gardens, and the Archbishop's Mistress

The name says it. Salzburg means "salt castle," and the salt mines in the surrounding mountains were the source of the city's wealth for centuries. Salt was medieval Europe's most essential commodity — it preserved food, it was taxable, and whoever controlled the mines controlled the treasury. The prince-archbishops controlled the mines. This is why a small Alpine city has a cathedral the size of something you would find in Rome, and why the archbishops could afford to import Italian architects to build it.

That wealth funded the Baroque transformation of the city in the seventeenth century, and no single project captures the era's spirit quite like the Mirabell Palace and Gardens. Prince-Archbishop Wolf Dietrich von Raitenau built the palace in 1606 for his mistress, Salome Alt, with whom he had fifteen children. An archbishop with a mistress and fifteen children was scandalous even by the relaxed standards of the Counter-Reformation, and Wolf Dietrich's enemies eventually used it against him — he was deposed, imprisoned in the fortress, and died there in 1617.

An archbishop built Salzburg's most beautiful garden for his mistress and their fifteen children. His successor locked him in the fortress for it. Both buildings are still standing.

His successor, Markus Sittikus, promptly moved into the palace, renamed it Mirabell, and continued the garden project. The gardens you walk through today — the geometric parterre, the marble statues, the dwarf garden — are the legacy of men who combined religious authority with the tastes of Renaissance princes. The grand staircase inside the palace, lined with marble cherubs, is now one of the most popular wedding venues in Austria. Wolf Dietrich would appreciate the irony.

Baroque as a Power Statement

The old town of Salzburg is a Baroque stage set, and it was built to function as one. After the Thirty Years' War ended in 1648, the prince-archbishops embarked on a building campaign that remade the medieval city in the image of Counter-Reformation confidence. The Salzburg Cathedral, rebuilt by Archbishop Paris von Lodron with an Italian architect's plans, seats ten thousand people and has a facade modeled on Roman churches. The Kollegienkirche (University Church), designed by Johann Bernhard Fischer von Erlach — Austria's most important Baroque architect — uses curves and light the way a composer uses dynamics, pulling your eye upward toward the dome.

None of this was accidental. Baroque architecture was the visual language of Catholic power in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. It was designed to overwhelm — to make the viewer feel small in the presence of something vast, luminous, and theologically certain. The prince-archbishops of Salzburg used it the way a corporation uses a headquarters building: to project authority and permanence. The old town, now a UNESCO World Heritage site, is the physical record of that campaign.

Walk through the Residenzplatz — the main square — and you are standing in the space the archbishops designed as the center of their power. The Residenz palace is on one side, the cathedral on the other, and the fountain in the center is the largest Baroque fountain in central Europe. It was all built within a few decades, by rulers who had salt money, Italian architects, and the conviction that their small city deserved a capital's architecture.

Small City, Outsized Ambitions

Salzburg's population today is around 155,000 — smaller than most American cities you have never heard of. It has always been small. And it has always acted as though that were irrelevant. The prince-archbishops governed as though they ruled a major European power. Mozart composed as though he were writing for the ages, which he was. The festival founders launched an event in 1920 that now ranks alongside Bayreuth and La Scala. The city's entire history is a case study in refusing to accept the limitations of scale.

This is what you feel walking through the Altstadt on a quiet morning before the tour buses arrive. The streets are narrow, the buildings are close, and the fortress watches from above. Everything is compressed and intensified — as though someone took the ambitions of a city five times this size and packed them into a river valley between two mountains. The Alps start where the city ends. There is nowhere to sprawl, so Salzburg went vertical and went deep instead.

That compression is Salzburg's defining quality. It is a small city that has always punched absurdly above its weight, because the people who controlled it — archbishops with the budgets of kings, composers with the talent of a generation, festival founders with a vision for a broken postwar Austria — had outsized ambitions and the Alps as a backdrop. The mountains did not limit them. The mountains made the stage.
