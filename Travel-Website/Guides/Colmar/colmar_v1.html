Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 13 min · 🚕 5 min → Unterlinden Museum
1. Unterlinden Museum
↳ One of France's great regional museums, home to the Isenheim Altarpiece — a 16th-century polyptych painted for a hospital treating ergotism. Grünewald depicted Christ's body covered in ergotism sores so patients could see their suffering in the Passion. Among the most powerful paintings in European art.

2. Dominican Church
↳ A 14th-century Gothic church housing Martin Schongauer's Virgin in the Rose Bower, 1473 — a panel painting of extraordinary refinement. Michelangelo traveled to Colmar to study under Schongauer, arriving only to learn he had died. What remained was this painting and his legacy across the city.

3. Petite Venise
↳ The most photographed quarter — half-timbered houses in Alsatian pastels reflected in the Lauch river, with flower-draped balconies leaning over the water. Built by tanners and fishmongers who backed their houses onto the canal; what survived is one of the most intact medieval waterfronts in France.
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min

4. Maison Pfister
↳ Colmar's most celebrated house — a 1537 merchant's residence with an oriel tower, loggia arcade and exterior roundels painted with German emperors and biblical scenes. A masterwork of German Renaissance civic architecture, it sits at a bend in the street that makes the full facade reveal itself as a theatrical event.
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~20 min

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 12 min · 🚕 4 min → Collégiale Saint-Martin
1. Collégiale Saint-Martin
↳ Colmar's great Gothic church, begun in the 13th century and built over two hundred years. The west façade carries some of the finest Rhineland Gothic sculpture in Alsace; the roofline features polychrome glazed-tile patterns that define Alsatian church architecture. Known informally as Colmar's Cathedral.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Koïfhus
↳ The old customs house of Colmar — a 15th-century arcaded loggia building in late-Gothic style that once controlled all goods traded in the city. Its ground-floor arches were open for merchants; the upper floor housed the city's tax administration. Among the best-preserved customs houses on the Upper Rhine.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~20 min
📍 29 Grand-Rue · Old Town
🚶 2 min · 🚕 2 min → Bartholdi Museum
3. Bartholdi Museum
↳ The birthplace and museum of Frédéric Auguste Bartholdi, the sculptor who designed the Statue of Liberty. The 17th-century townhouse contains models, drawings and personal effects tracing the Liberty statue from its earliest sketches through its 1886 installation in New York Harbour.
📖 Wikipedia

4. La Krutenau
↳ The former tanners' district — narrow lanes of half-timbered houses whose upper floors were oversized to allow hides to dry indoors. The buildings list into the street, wooden frames leaning over cobbled alleys barely touched since the 17th century. Immediately east of Petite Venise and far less visited.

🍮 Local Tastes
Tarte Flambée
↳ The emblematic Alsatian dish — thin unleavened dough spread with crème fraîche, sliced onions and smoked lardons, baked in a wood-fired oven. Light, crisp and gone in minutes. Order it at any winstub.
📍 1 rue Turenne · Petite Venise
Choucroute Garnie
↳ Braised sauerkraut piled with various pork cuts — sausages, knuckle, lard, ham — and boiled potatoes. A dish made for cold Alsatian winters; the definitive regional main course.
📍 12 rue des Tanneurs · La Krutenau
Alsatian Wine
↳ Colmar sits at the heart of the Alsace wine route. Local varieties — Riesling, Gewurztraminer, Pinot Gris, Muscat — grown on slopes immediately west of the city. Order by the glass in any winstub.
📍 Marché Couvert · Place de l'Ancienne Douane
Kougelhopf
↳ The domed Alsatian yeast cake baked in a fluted ring mould — enriched with eggs and butter, studded with raisins and almonds. Found in every bakery window. A sweet version with kirsch is traditional; a savoury version with bacon exists too.
📍 Marché Couvert · Place de l'Ancienne Douane
Munster Cheese
↳ Pungent washed-rind cheese from the Munster valley in the Vosges, barely 30 km west. Soft and creamy when young; intensely aromatic when aged. Buy it from producers at the Saturday market.

🎨 The Hospital Painting and the Artist Who Arrived Too Late

The Isenheim Altarpiece was commissioned not for a church but for a hospital chapel run by the Antonite monks, who treated a disfiguring skin disease called St Anthony's Fire — ergotism, caused by a fungus in contaminated grain. Matthias Grünewald painted Christ's body literally covered in the disease's characteristic pustules and sores. The intention was clinical as much as devotional: patients in extremity would look at the altarpiece and see their suffering accurately mirrored in the Passion. The painting was a form of therapy. It remains, five hundred years later, one of the most viscerally powerful works in Western art, and it has never left Colmar.

In the Dominican Church a few streets away hangs Martin Schongauer's Virgin in the Rose Bower, 1473 — a small panel painting of extraordinary refinement, one of the finest works of the northern Renaissance. When Michelangelo was still a teenager, he reportedly made the journey to Colmar specifically to study under Schongauer. He arrived to find him recently dead. Schongauer's influence reached Michelangelo anyway — through the city's works and through Schongauer's brother in Basel — but the detail of the missed encounter lingers. Colmar was once a place that artists traveled toward. Considering what remains, it is not hard to see why.
