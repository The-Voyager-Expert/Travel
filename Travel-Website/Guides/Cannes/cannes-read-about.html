A fishing village that became a stage — and why the twelve days everyone knows about are the least interesting thing about it.

The lord who got stuck here

Cannes exists, in its modern form, because of a quarantine line. In 1834, Henry Brougham — Britain's former Lord Chancellor, one of the most powerful lawyers in Europe — was traveling south with his sick daughter, headed for Nice and then Italy, when a cholera outbreak closed the border at the Var river. He couldn't cross. So he stopped at the nearest place that would have him: a fishing village of a few hundred people called Cannes, with no real harbor, no real road, and nothing to recommend it except the weather and, as it turned out, a bowl of fish soup at the local inn that he apparently could not stop thinking about.

He liked it enough to stay. He liked it enough to come back the next winter, and the one after that, and eventually to buy land and build an Italianate villa he named Éléonore-Louise, after the daughter who'd been sick on that first trip. Brougham had the kind of social weight that turns a personal preference into a migration: where a former Lord Chancellor built a winter villa, the British aristocracy followed. Within a few decades a village of fishermen had fifty hotels, an Anglican church, a yacht club, and a stretch of waterfront being paved into what would become La Croisette. There's a statue of Brougham near the harbor today — not a founding father in the usual sense, just a stranded traveler who liked the soup and never really left.

The other city, up the hill

Almost none of what Brougham built is the oldest part of Cannes. That's Le Suquet, the hill above the modern seafront, which was a fortified settlement centuries before anyone thought to winter here. Climb it — it's a real climb, narrow stepped streets, laundry overhead, the kind of gradient that discourages rolling suitcases — and you reach the old watchtower and the fragments of a medieval fort that has nothing to do with film premieres or beach clubs. This was Cannes before Cannes was a resort: a Provençal fishing town, Ligurian in its bones, that happened to get discovered by exactly the right sick aristocrat at exactly the right moment.

The two Canneses still sit next to each other and mostly ignore one another. Down on La Croisette, the hotels face the water. Up in Le Suquet, the streets face each other, tight and shaded, selling the same fish that's been sold there for a thousand years. Most visitors see one or the other. The trick is that the town only makes sense as both — the postcard version built by outsiders and the working town it grew out of.

An island prison for a man nobody could name

Twenty minutes offshore by boat, Île Sainte-Marguerite holds Fort Royal, a pentagonal stone fortress built in the 1620s and later reworked by Vauban. For eleven years, from 1687 to 1698, one of its cells held a prisoner whose face nobody was allowed to see — a man transferred under guard, kept masked, whose real identity was never recorded. Alexandre Dumas turned him into the Man in the Iron Mask a century and a half later and gave him a name, Eustache Dauger, that historians still argue about without resolving. The cell is open now, with its single window looking back across the water toward the town, which is the closest the prisoner ever got to Cannes itself.

The prisoner never saw Cannes. He only ever saw it from that one window, looking back at a shoreline he couldn't reach.

The island is worth the crossing for reasons that have nothing to do with the legend — pine forest, clear water, a coastline with no hotels on it — but the fort earns its place in the story. Cannes has always had this dual character: a glamorous shoreline with a harder, older history sitting just out of view, whether that's a hilltop watchtower or an island prison holding a man the state wanted erased.

A festival built to spite Mussolini

The reason the world knows Cannes at all has a specific, almost petty origin. In 1938, the Venice Film Festival — by then five years old and effectively a propaganda arm of Mussolini's government — handed its top prizes to a film supervised by Mussolini's own son and to Leni Riefenstahl's Olympia, a documentary commissioned by Hitler's regime. Democratic juries walked out. French officials, particularly the historian Philippe Erlanger, decided the solution wasn't to keep protesting Venice — it was to build a rival festival where fascist governments had no say over who won.

They chose Cannes for the climate and the light, announced the first edition for September 1939, booked Hollywood stars to attend, and scheduled the opening film — fittingly, The Hunchback of Notre Dame — for September 1st. Germany invaded Poland that same day. The festival opened for one night and then the war shut it down entirely; it didn't actually run until 1946. The event now synonymous with glamour and red carpets began as a direct, deliberate rebuke to a fascist propaganda machine, and it took a world war to actually get off the ground.

Twelve days, then an ordinary town

Here's the thing almost nobody arriving for the first time expects: the festival is twelve days a year. The Palais des Festivals, that slab of a building at the end of La Croisette where the red-carpet photos get taken, spends the other 353 days hosting trade conventions — advertising, real estate, television — because a building built for one glamorous event a year doesn't pay for itself on residual fame. Walk the Croisette in October and you'll find a perfectly nice, perfectly ordinary mid-sized French resort town: retirees, regional families on a beach weekend, a few yachts, none of the density of stars or paparazzi that the name conjures.

This isn't a bait-and-switch. It's the actual character of the place. Cannes was never a city that generated its own fame organically, the way Paris or Rome did over centuries — it was a stopover that got lucky twice: once with a stranded lord in 1834, once with a diplomatic grudge against Mussolini in 1939. Both times, something arrived from outside and reshaped the town around it. The festival crowd doesn't live here. The people who actually live in Cannes are dealing with rent, traffic, and the fishing boats that still go out from the Vieux Port every morning, mostly unbothered by what happened on the red carpet the week before.

Cannes was never a city that made itself famous. Twice, something arrived from the outside — a stranded aristocrat, a political grudge — and the town simply rearranged itself around what showed up.

What to actually take from this

The useful frame for Cannes isn't glamour, and it isn't authenticity either — it's opportunism, in the least cynical sense of the word. This is a town whose entire identity was built by saying yes to whoever turned up with money, prestige, or a reason to be here, then building infrastructure around them fast enough to keep them coming back. A cholera quarantine built the aristocratic resort. A political snub built the film festival. Neither was planned by Cannes itself; both were absorbed and monetized within a generation.

That's not a complaint. It's the reason the town works, and the reason it's worth understanding before you arrive: don't expect Cannes to hand you deep local culture the way Aix or Arles will. Expect a town built to accommodate whoever's important this week, wearing that role so comfortably now that it looks native. Go up to Le Suquet for the version of Cannes that predates all of it. Go out to the island for the version that got locked away from it entirely. And go to La Croisette knowing that the glamour you're walking through was, in both of its founding moments, an accident that the town simply decided to keep.
