1. The Sixth Floor Museum
↳ Occupying the former Texas School Book Depository, the museum chronicles the life, death and legacy of President John F. Kennedy through 40,000 artifacts and archival materials. The sixth floor preserves the southeast corner sniper's nest and overlooks Dealey Plaza, the exact site of the 1963 assassination.

4. Perot Museum of Nature and Science
↳ A Thom Mayne–designed cube of concrete and glass rising from Victory Park, housing 11 permanent exhibit halls. Dinosaur fossils, a world-class gems and minerals collection, energy science, space exploration and a dedicated children's discovery hall fill the interior across 180,000 square feet.

🏛️ The Window on the Sixth Floor

On November 22, 1963, a motorcade turned left on Houston Street and then left again onto Elm Street in a place called Dealey Plaza. Within seconds, everything changed. The assassination of President John F. Kennedy in Dallas was one of those events that fractures time into before and after — and the city has spent the decades since carrying that weight, sometimes uneasily, sometimes with genuine civic seriousness.

The Sixth Floor Museum does something genuinely difficult: it documents a national trauma without exploitation. The southeast corner window is preserved exactly as it appeared in the Warren Commission photographs — stacked boxes, the sightline to the street below — while the broader exhibition places that moment inside the arc of Kennedy's presidency, his Cold War decisions, the civil rights era, and the conspiracy theories that have never fully receded. Dallas itself is mostly absent from the surrounding streets as a living referent to 1963. The Texas School Book Depository is an office building now. The grassy knoll is a patch of lawn. The city moved on, built the Arts District a few blocks away, and grew into something new. But the museum remains — and the view from the sixth floor is still one of the most loaded perspectives in American history.
