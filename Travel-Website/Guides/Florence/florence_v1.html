Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 2 min · 🚕 5 min → Florence Cathedral & Brunelleschi's Dome
1. Florence Cathedral & Brunelleschi's Dome
↳ Brunelleschi's brick dome crowns Florence Cathedral and is still the largest masonry dome ever built — the feat that announced the Renaissance. The climb threads between the dome's two shells past Vasari's Last Judgment frescoes to the lantern's 360° view. It is 463 steps with no elevator.

2. Piazza della Signoria
↳ Florence's civic heart fronts the crenellated Palazzo Vecchio and works as an open-air sculpture gallery: Cellini's Perseus under the Loggia dei Lanzi, Ammannati's Neptune Fountain, and a replica of Michelangelo's David where the original long stood. Savonarola's Bonfire of the Vanities happened here.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min

🚶 1 min · 🚕 8 min → Uffizi Gallery
3. Uffizi Gallery
↳ Built by Vasari as Medici administrative offices, the Uffizi holds the world's premier collection of Italian Renaissance painting. Botticelli's Birth of Venus and Primavera, Leonardo's Annunciation, Michelangelo's Doni Tondo and Titian's Venus of Urbino anchor a chronological sweep from Giotto to Caravaggio.

4. Ponte Vecchio
↳ Florence's oldest bridge crosses the Arno at its narrowest point and has been lined with goldsmiths' and jewellers' shops since the Medici evicted the butchers. Their private elevated passage — the Vasari Corridor — runs above the shops, and this was the only Florence bridge spared in the wartime retreat.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min

1. Galleria dell'Accademia
↳ The Accademia is visited overwhelmingly for one thing: Michelangelo's original David, moved here from Piazza della Signoria and displayed under a skylit tribune. The approach corridor holds Michelangelo's unfinished Prisoners, plus Florentine gold-ground paintings and a musical-instruments collection.

2. Medici Chapels
↳ The Medici burial complex at San Lorenzo pairs the marble-encrusted Chapel of the Princes with Michelangelo's New Sacristy, where his Medici tombs carry the allegories of Day, Night, Dawn and Dusk. The Secret Room of charcoal drawings attributed to Michelangelo hides beneath.

3. Basilica di Santa Maria Novella
↳ Florence's principal Dominican church pairs Alberti's geometric green-and-white marble facade with frescoed cloisters. One ticket covers Masaccio's Trinity — the first rigorous one-point perspective in painting — Giotto's Crucifix, Ghirlandaio's Tornabuoni Chapel, and the Green Cloister with its Spanish Chapel.

1. Palazzo Pitti
↳ A colossal Renaissance palace built for the banker Luca Pitti, later home to the Medici grand dukes and briefly the kings of Italy. One ticket covers the Palatine Gallery — state rooms holding a famous concentration of Raphael and Titian — plus the Treasury of the Grand Dukes and the Museum of Costume and Fashion.

2. Boboli Gardens
↳ The Medici's vast terraced park behind Palazzo Pitti is the prototype of the formal Italian garden copied by courts across Europe. It works as an open-air museum: Buontalenti's Grotto, the Fountain of Neptune, Renaissance statuary and the Rococo Kaffeehaus pavilion, with terrace views over the city.

3. Piazzale Michelangelo
↳ A panoramic terrace laid out by Giuseppe Poggi when Florence was briefly the capital of Italy. Its bronze replica of David presides over the classic postcard view of the whole city — Duomo, Palazzo Vecchio, the Arno and Ponte Vecchio — with the Fiesole hills beyond. It is the standard sunset spot.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7

🚶 9 min · 🚕 2 min → San Miniato al Monte
4. San Miniato al Monte
↳ A Romanesque basilica and working Benedictine abbey on the highest point above Florence, ten minutes uphill from Piazzale Michelangelo. Its green-and-white marble facade, glittering apse mosaic and intact crypt make it one of Tuscany's finest Romanesque buildings; the monks still sing Vespers daily.

🍮 Local Tastes
Bistecca alla Fiorentina
↳ Thick-cut T-bone of Chianina beef, flame-grilled rare over coals, seasoned only with salt and olive oil and served on the bone — Florence's defining dish.
📍 Regina Bistecca · Buca Lapi · Trattoria Sostanza
Lampredotto
↳ Florence's iconic street food — the cow's fourth stomach simmered in broth, stuffed into a crusty roll with salsa verde or spicy red sauce, eaten standing at the trippai stands.
📍 Da Nerbone · L'Antico Trippaio
Ribollita
↳ Reboiled peasant soup of stale unsalted Tuscan bread, cannellini beans, black cabbage and savoy cabbage — a cornerstone of Florentine home cooking.

↳ Florence's olive-oil flatbread — crispier and thinner than focaccia — split and stuffed with Tuscan cured meats, cheeses and creams; now world-famous as a sandwich.

🎨 The City as a Workshop

Brunelleschi won the dome commission without ever revealing how he would build it — no plans survive because he kept them in his head, wary of rivals. The answer turned out to be herringbone brickwork that locks each course in place as it rises, letting the dome hold itself up during construction with no scaffolding from the ground. The climb on Day 1 walks you straight through the gap between his two shells — you are inside the solution.

Keep an eye out for the buchette del vino — small arched hatches cut into palazzo walls at shoulder height. Renaissance wine producers used them to sell directly to the street, and they became contactless service windows during plague outbreaks. Dozens survive around the center and the Oltrarno, and a handful have reopened to pour wine through the wall again; the sunset tour in this guide stops at one.

Three stops in this guide are really one statue's biography. David was carved for the cathedral's roofline, stood guard outside Palazzo Vecchio in Piazza della Signoria for nearly four centuries, and now lives a five-minute walk from the hotel in the Accademia — with his replicas holding both of his old posts, one in the square and one in bronze above the city at Piazzale Michelangelo.
