A city of lakes and Himalayan panoramas, Pokhara is Nepal's gateway to the Annapurna range — and one of the most dramatic backdrops on earth.

Gateway City

Pokhara sits at roughly 820 metres above sea level in the Gandaki Province of central Nepal, about 200 kilometres west of Kathmandu by road — a journey of five hours or more, often replaced by a 25-minute domestic flight. The city occupies a wide valley carved by the Seti Gandaki River, with the Annapurna massif rising so close to the north that on clear mornings the snow-capped peaks appear to hover directly above the lakefront. Machhapuchhre — the fishtail mountain, famously never summited — catches the first light before any other landmark in the valley.

For most of its modern history Pokhara was a trading post on the salt route between the Tibetan plateau and the Indian plains. Traders from Mustang and the high valleys would spend nights here before the route climbed back into the mountains. The town was small, the lake central to daily life, and the mountains simply the walls of the world. Mass tourism arrived in the early 1970s, when the Prithvi Highway connected Pokhara to Kathmandu for the first time and the city became the launching pad for the newly opened trekking regions to the north.

On clear mornings the Annapurna range appears to hover directly above the lakefront — a view so outsized it takes several days to stop feeling surreal.

Phewa Lake and the Lakeside Quarter

Phewa Tal is the second-largest lake in Nepal and the gravitational centre of visitor Pokhara. Roughly five kilometres long, it reflects the mountains on still mornings in one of the country's most photographed images. The small island temple of Tal Barahi sits about 200 metres from the eastern shore, reached by wooden rowboat — a pilgrimage site devoted to Ajima, a goddess of lakes and water. The boats are rented by the hour from the Lakeside ghats and the crossing takes about five minutes.

The Lakeside quarter — known locally as Baidam — developed along the eastern shore from the 1970s onward as guesthouses, restaurants, and shops catered to the trekking trade. The street running parallel to the water is dense with gear shops, bakeries, and cafes that have been serving variations of the same apple pie and dal bhat for half a century. It is not a polished resort district but a genuinely lived-in place: hotels range from simple budget guesthouses to newer lakefront properties with mountain-view terraces, and the evenings fill with the particular energy of travellers who have just come down from altitude or are about to go up.

Annapurna and the Trekking Economy

The Annapurna Conservation Area, protecting roughly 7,629 square kilometres of high mountain terrain north of Pokhara, was established in 1986 and remains one of the most visited trekking destinations in the world. The Annapurna Circuit — a classic route circumnavigating the massif via the Thorong La pass at 5,416 metres — brought thousands of trekkers through Pokhara each season at its peak in the 1990s. The circuit has since been partly motorised by road construction, but its inner sections and side valleys remain on foot only, and Pokhara is still the arrival and departure point for the vast majority of Annapurna trekkers.

The Annapurna Base Camp trek — shorter than the circuit, leading up the Modi Khola valley to a cirque of giants at 4,130 metres — draws significant numbers in its own right. Pokhara handles permits, licensed guides, and the last hot shower for most of them. The city's economy has been shaped by this relationship for fifty years: the trekking industry sustains the gear shops, the guide training centres, the guesthouses in every price range, and the restaurants that know to serve large portions to people who have been walking all day.

ZipFlyer Nepal, launched from a ridge north of the city, claims one of the longest and steepest zip lines in the world — 1.8 kilometres at gradients that drop riders from mountain forest to valley floor.

Adventure Beyond the Trail

Pokhara has diversified its adventure offerings well beyond trekking. Paragliding from the ridge at Sarangkot became commercially established in the 1990s and is now one of the city's signature experiences — the thermals above the lake and the unobstructed view of the Annapurna range make the launch site one of the most scenic in Asia. Flights last between 20 and 45 minutes depending on conditions. The morning window, when the mountains are sharpest and the air still cool from altitude, is the preferred time for most operators.

Ultralight flights from the Pokhara airstrip offer a motorised alternative to paragliding, allowing passengers to circle the foothills at low altitude for a view of the valley from above. Kayaking and white-water rafting on the Seti and Kali Gandaki rivers serve visitors who want moving water rather than still. The Seti River Gorge — carving through the city itself in a narrow canyon up to 80 metres deep and sometimes only a few metres wide — is an unexpected geological spectacle that can be viewed from bridges and vantage points within the urban area.

The Mountains as a Daily Presence

What separates Pokhara from most cities that market themselves on scenery is the proximity of the mountains. The Annapurna range is not a distant horizon but a wall occupying the entire northern sky at distances of 25 to 40 kilometres. Dhaulagiri, one of the 8,000-metre peaks, is visible to the west. The views are not guaranteed — the valley has a monsoon season from June through August that brings heavy rainfall and sustained cloud cover — but during the dry season from October through May, the mornings are often crystal clear, and the light on the snow-covered faces in the first hour after sunrise is the reason for every dawn alarm set in every guesthouse in the city.

Sarangkot, a ridge roughly 1,600 metres above sea level accessible by road and a short walk, is the standard sunrise viewpoint. The panorama from there takes in Annapurna I (8,091m), Annapurna II, Annapurna III, Annapurna South, Machhapuchhre, and Hiunchuli across a single horizon. It is not a remote location — the access road is paved and the ridge has small guesthouses and chai stalls — but the view at dawn from a ridge above a city of lakes with those peaks catching the first sunlight is something that requires no embellishment.

City of Gorges

The Seti Gandaki River runs through Pokhara in a series of narrow gorges that cut through the city without being visible from street level until you are at the rim. Prithvi Narayan Campus Bridge, the K.I. Singh Bridge, and several other crossing points offer views down into the white river churning 50 to 80 metres below — a river so powerful it remains milky white from the glacial flour in suspension year-round, earning it the local name that translates as White River. Mahendra Cave, a limestone cavern about 4 kilometres north of the lakefront, and the adjacent Bat Cave provide underground access to the karst geology that underlies much of the valley.

The International Mountain Museum, opened in 2002, is the most serious cultural institution in the city. Its collection covers Himalayan geology, the history of high-altitude mountaineering in Nepal, the cultures of the mountain peoples — Gurung, Magar, Thakali, Sherpa — and profiles of first ascents of the world's 8,000-metre peaks. It is a proper museum with serious content, not a tourist attraction, and it anchors the Ratna Chowk area north of the old bazaar.

Begnas Lake and the Quieter East

About 15 kilometres east of the city centre, Begnas Lake and the smaller Rupa Lake occupy a quieter valley away from the trekking infrastructure. The area around Begnas Tal is farmed: the hills between the two lakes are terraced with rice paddies and vegetable plots, and the few guesthouses on the lakefront serve a clientele looking for a few days away from the Lakeside energy rather than the adventure market. Boats are available for hire; the surrounding trails pass through villages that see relatively few visitors compared to the Annapurna approaches. On clear days the mountains to the north are visible from the eastern shore — a different perspective than the iconic Lakeside panorama, more intimate and less photographed.
