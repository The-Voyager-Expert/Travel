Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 5 min · 🚕 5 min → Cathedral of Saint Tryphon
1. Cathedral of Saint Tryphon
↳ Kotor's most iconic landmark — a Romanesque Catholic cathedral with twin bell towers dating to 1166, housing a treasury of Byzantine silver reliefs and the relics of the city's patron saint. The towers offer sweeping views across the terracotta rooftops.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Cathedral Admission
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 7:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Trg od Sv. Tripuna · Old Town
🚶 4 min · 🚕 4 min → Church of Saint Luke
2. Church of Saint Luke
↳ A rare medieval church that served both Catholic and Orthodox congregations simultaneously for over a century — two altars, two faiths, one 12th-century Romanesque structure. A quiet counterpoint to the Cathedral, with fragments of original frescoes still visible inside.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~20 min
📍 Trg Sv. Luke · Old Town
🚶 2 min · 🚕 2 min → Maritime Museum of Montenegro
3. Maritime Museum of Montenegro
↳ Housed in the 18th-century Grgurina Palace, this museum chronicles the seafaring heritage of the Boka Kotorska through model ships, navigational instruments, uniforms, and archival charts — spanning Venetian rule to the Austro-Hungarian era.
🎟️ Museum Admission
🏛️ Monday - Friday 9:00am - 6:00pm · Saturday 9:00am - 1:00pm
🚫 Closed Sunday
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Trg Boke Kotorske 391 · Old Town
🚶 3 min · 🚕 3 min → Square of Arms & Clock Tower
4. Square of Arms & Clock Tower
↳ The main square of the old town, ringed by Venetian palaces and the 1602 Clock Tower — the most photographed structure in the city. Built as the administrative heart of Venetian-era Kotor; the stone pillar at the tower's base was once a public pillory. Linger for the street life and golden-hour light.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Trg od Oružja · Old Town
🚶 5 min · 🚕 5 min → hotel
Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 8 min · 🚕 5 min → San Giovanni Fortress & City Walls
1. San Giovanni Fortress & City Walls
↳ Kotor's 4.5 km of medieval fortifications climb from sea level to San Giovanni at 260 m — a UNESCO World Heritage rampart two millennia in the making. The ascent of 1,350 steps passes three churches and towers from Byzantine, Venetian, and Austro-Hungarian eras.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ City Walls Admission
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 8:00pm
⏰ ~3 hr
⚠️ Reduced hours Oct - Apr
📍 Stari Grad · northeast gate near River Škurda
🚕 15 min → Kotor Serpentine Viewpoint
2. Kotor Serpentine Viewpoint
↳ The road from the city to Lovćen climbs 25 hairpin turns in under 6 km, rising from the bay to over 900 m. Pull over at the upper bends for an aerial panorama of the old town, the winding bay, and the Adriatic beyond — one of the most photographed views in Montenegro.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 R1 Serpentine Road · above the old town
🚕 20 min → hotel
Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 35 min → Perast
1. Perast
↳ A perfectly preserved Baroque town of 17 palaces and 16 churches on a narrow strip between the bay and the mountain — built by Venetian merchant captains at the height of Kotor Bay's maritime prosperity. Stroll the stone promenade and look out at two tiny islands set improbably in the still water offshore.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Perast · waterfront
🚕 5 min → Our Lady of the Rocks
2. Our Lady of the Rocks
↳ An artificial island church built stone by stone by sailors since 1452 — the Baroque interior glitters with 68 silver votive plaques and a ceiling painting by Tripo Kokolja. Boat taxis depart on demand from the Perast jetty, 10 minutes each way.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~45 min
⚠️ Boat taxi from Perast jetty · departs on demand · pay fisherman directly
📍 Island of Gospa od Škrpjela · Perast Bay
🚕 40 min → Bay of Kotor Group Cruise
3. Bay of Kotor Group Cruise
↳ An afternoon boat tour of the full bay circuit: the turquoise Blue Cave swim stop, the Mamula Island Yugoslav-era submarine tunnels, and the church islet of Our Lady of the Rocks seen from the water. Small-group boats depart from the harbour; the standard tour runs 3-4 hours.

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 40 min → Lovćen National Park
1. Lovćen National Park
↳ Montenegro's spiritual heartland — a mountain massif rising to 1,749 m above the coast, covering 6,220 hectares of black beech forest and limestone karst. The R1 road from the old town winds through the lower slopes with repeated viewpoints before opening onto the plateau above Njeguši.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Park Entry
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
⚠️ Entry fee collected at staffed gate during daytime hours
📍 Lovćen National Park · Cetinje Municipality
🚕 15 min → Njegoš Mausoleum
2. Njegoš Mausoleum
↳ Neoclassical granite monument at 1,660 m housing the tomb of Montenegro's greatest poet and prince-bishop. The final 461 steps cut into the mountain lead to a chamber of golden mosaics and an oversized Njegoš effigy. The terrace panorama takes in the Adriatic, the Bay of Kotor, and Montenegro's interior ranges.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Mausoleum Ticket
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
⚠️ Hours reduce to 9:00am - 5:00pm in winter
📍 Jezerski Vrh · Lovćen National Park
🚕 10 min → Njeguši Village
3. Njeguši Village
↳ The mountain village at 860 m where Montenegro's signature cured products are made: Njeguški pršut — air-dried prosciutto smoked over beechwood — and Njeguški sir, a hard mountain cheese. Small family shops along the single road sell both; the smokehouse air is unmistakable on approach.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~45 min

🍮 Local Tastes
Njeguški Pršut
↳ Air-dried prosciutto from Njeguši village, cold-smoked over beechwood and mountain herbs. Darker and more intensely flavored than Italian prosciutto.
📍 Konoba Scala Santa · Old Town
Njeguški Sir
↳ Hard mountain cheese from the same highland village, aged in olive oil or brine. Sharp, crumbly, and rich — sold paired with pršut at most traditional restaurants.
📍 Available at source in Njeguši village
Rakija
↳ Montenegrin grape or plum brandy, served as a welcome drink at traditional restaurants. Loza is the dominant local style; šljivovica is common inland.

✨ Why Kotor Feels Impossible

There's a moment halfway up the city walls — somewhere around the Church of Our Lady of Health, thighs burning, August sun full in the face — when you look down at the terracotta rooftops squeezed inside the perfectly intact medieval circuit of stone, the bay absolutely flat and silver beyond, and you think: this can't be real. It looks like a model. Kotor is genuinely difficult to comprehend as a place people actually live and always have, because nothing this preserved and this dramatic is supposed to exist at the end of a Montenegrin coastal road. The Venetians ran it for 400 years. The Austro-Hungarians left their baroque fingerprints. The cats — dozens of them, the city's unofficial mascots — move between centuries without concern. Go early before the cruise ships dock. The hour between 7am and 8am, when light fills the alleys and the square belongs entirely to the old men drinking coffee, is the best argument for staying.
