A walled city at the bottom of a fjord, older than most of the nations that have fought over it — and why it still belongs to itself.

The fjord that isn't a fjord

The Bay of Kotor is the only fjord in the Mediterranean — or so the tour guides say. Geologists are more precise: it is a drowned river canyon, carved by a now-vanished river during the last ice age when the sea level sat much lower than it does today, then flooded by the Adriatic as the ice retreated. Whether you call it a fjord or a ria depends on your definitions and your patience for the distinction. What is not in dispute is that it looks like a fjord, behaves like a fjord, and produces the same dramatic compression of mountain and water that makes Norway's western coast so disorienting to anyone arriving for the first time.

The bay is not a single opening but a series of them, linked by narrows that the sea pushes through on the tide. You enter from the Adriatic past the Verige Strait, where the water pinches to half a kilometer, and then the bay opens again into the inner sea. Kotor sits at the innermost point, at the back of everything, where the Lovćen massif drops almost straight into the water and the town has been compressed between cliff and shore for fifteen hundred years.

The setting explains the history. A city this enclosed — ringed by mountains, accessible only by a single coastal road or by water — is either a trap or a fortress, depending on which side of the walls you are standing on. For most of its existence, Kotor chose to treat it as a fortress.

Who built what, and when

The walls enclosing Kotor's old town are Venetian. Or mostly Venetian. They were built and rebuilt and reinforced over four and a half centuries of Venetian rule, beginning in 1420 and ending when Napoleon dissolved the Republic of Venice in 1797 and handed the Adriatic coast — along with everything else — to Austria. But the city inside those walls is older.

There was a Roman settlement here, in the place the Romans called Acruvium, that served the coastal trade. After Rome, the Byzantines held it. Then a succession of medieval Serbian rulers claimed it as part of various kingdoms that rose and fell across the western Balkans. The Bosnian king Stefan Vukčić Kosača controlled it briefly in the mid-fifteenth century, which is when the Venetians negotiated its voluntary submission — Kotor's merchant elite, calculating that Venice offered better protection than the fragile Bosnian state, essentially switched sovereigns of their own accord. Venice accepted, and the arrangement lasted until Napoleon.

What the Venetians did with the city was what they did with every city they controlled on the eastern Adriatic: they built walls, they regulated trade, they appointed rectors, and they brought their particular aesthetic with them. The stone carving above the Sea Gate bears the lion of Saint Mark, the emblem of the Venetian Republic, dated 1555. It has been there for nearly five centuries and has outlasted every subsequent power that claimed the city.

The cathedral and what it contains

The Cathedral of Saint Tryphon is the oldest and most important building inside the walls, and it holds the relics of its patron saint in a Gothic-Byzantine reliquary behind the altar. Saint Tryphon was a third-century martyr from Phrygia in Asia Minor who had no particular connection to Kotor — his remains arrived here by trade, purchased by Kotor merchants in the ninth century, which was the standard medieval method of acquiring a patron saint. The transaction worked: Tryphon became the protector of the city, his feast day the most important holiday in the Kotor calendar, and his image appeared on the city's coins for centuries.

The cathedral itself was consecrated in 1166, though the twin Romanesque bell towers that now frame its facade are later additions, rebuilt after an earthquake in the seventeenth century. The interior is austere in the way Adriatic Romanesque tends to be — stone walls, measured light, carved capitals — with a silver altarpiece completed in 1440 that depicts scenes from Tryphon's life in relief panels of considerable skill. The reliquary above it, gilded and set with enamel and gems, is the visible repository of the city's spiritual claim: that it is protected, that it has always been protected, that the saint in his case behind the altar has kept watch over these streets for twelve hundred years.

Kotor has been claimed by Rome, Byzantium, Serbia, Bosnia, Venice, France, Austria-Hungary, Yugoslavia, and Montenegro. Its cathedral has been there for most of it.

The walls as infrastructure

The walls of Kotor are not a monument. Or rather, they are a monument now, because they no longer serve a defensive function, but for most of their existence they were infrastructure — as practical and utilitarian as a water system or a road. They exist because the city needed them to exist. Without them, Kotor would not be Kotor.

The circuit runs for nearly five kilometers, enclosing the town and climbing the hillside behind it to San Giovanni Fortress at the peak, four hundred meters above sea level. This was not an architectural flourish. The walls ran up the mountain because attackers could approach from above, and a fortification that controlled only the shoreline was half a fortification. The climb to San Giovanni follows the original wall line — fourteen hundred steps that the Venetian garrison had to be able to defend under conditions that did not include handrails.

The walls were tested. The Ottomans tried to take Kotor in 1539 and again in 1657. Neither siege succeeded. Venice reinforced the walls after each attempt, adding bastions and towers calibrated to the evolving capabilities of siege artillery. When the threat finally passed — not through conquest but through the slow withdrawal of Ottoman power from the western Balkans — the walls had become so embedded in the city's identity that no subsequent ruler thought to dismantle them. They survived Napoleon, Austria-Hungary, Yugoslavia, and the 1979 earthquake. UNESCO added them to the World Heritage list in 1979.

On April 15, 1979, an earthquake measuring 6.9 on the Richter scale struck the coastal region of Montenegro. The epicenter was near Bar, to the south, but Kotor absorbed serious damage. Buildings collapsed inside the old town. The cathedral lost portions of its facade. The surrounding villages were devastated. The total death toll across the region was over a hundred people.

The reconstruction took years and required a coordinated international effort — UNESCO, various European governments, and Yugoslav federal resources all contributed. The goal was not simply to repair the buildings but to restore them as accurately as possible to their pre-earthquake state, using original materials and traditional techniques where available. The effort produced one of the more thorough historic preservation projects in the former Yugoslavia, and it is the primary reason the old town looks today as it does: not merely surviving, but genuinely intact.

The earthquake is still within living memory. Residents who were children in 1979 remember the collapse, the dust, the weeks of aftershocks. The old town they live in now is a rebuilt version of the one they grew up in, and they know this in a way that visitors looking at the apparently ancient stones cannot see. The walls, in this sense, are doubly reconstructed: first by the Venetians from whatever came before, and then again by the preservationists of the 1980s from the rubble of 1979.

Perast and the island in the bay

Twelve kilometers up the coast from Kotor, at a bend in the bay where the mountains press close to the water on both sides, the small town of Perast faces an island that shouldn't exist. Our Lady of the Rocks is an artificial island, constructed over centuries by Kotor-area sailors who, by tradition, threw a rock into the bay on each return from a voyage. The legend holds that two brothers fishing in the bay in 1452 found a painting of the Madonna on a reef, and the tradition of adding rocks — and eventually deliberately sunken ships — built the island up around that original find.

The church on the island, completed in its current form in 1725, contains the painting, along with a collection of votive offerings from sailors asking or giving thanks for protection at sea. These are not art objects in any conventional sense — they are tools of petition, accumulated over centuries from people for whom the sea was where you could easily die. The embroidered altar cloth was made by a local woman who worked for twenty-five years using her own hair as thread, a needle drawing from her head what devotion required from her hands.

Perast itself was once a significant naval power — significant enough to train the first Russian naval officers at Peter the Great's request in the late seventeenth century. The town produced eighteen admirals in the service of Venice, a remarkable concentration of naval talent from a settlement of perhaps a few thousand people. The palaces that line the waterfront were built with maritime earnings and speak, even now, of a commercial and strategic confidence that the physical size of the town does not quite explain.

Montenegro as new country

Montenegro declared independence from the state union with Serbia in June 2006, following a referendum in which 55.5 percent of voters backed separation — just barely clearing the 55 percent threshold the European Union had set as a condition for recognizing the result. The country is small by any measure: around 625,000 people, an area slightly smaller than Connecticut, a coastline of 294 kilometers along the Adriatic. It joined NATO in 2017 and began EU accession negotiations, though the process has moved slowly.

For most visitors, the political history is background. What registers is the physical scale of the country — that you can drive from the Croatian border at Herceg Novi to the Albanian border near Ulcinj in a few hours, passing through dramatically different terrain at each turn, from coastal baroque towns to Ottoman-influenced mountain villages to the deep lake at Skadar. The country is not large enough to lose yourself in, but it is varied enough to surprise you.

Kotor itself is Montenegrin but feels Venetian, which is the correct reading of both facts. The Venetian centuries left their mark in the architecture, the street plan, the stone and the light. Montenegro's existence as a sovereign state is recent; the city's identity is not. The Lion of Saint Mark looks down from the Sea Gate with the patience of something that has seen many flags go up and come down, and has learned to wait.

The cats

Kotor has cats. This is not incidental. The city is known for its cats in the way that Istanbul is known for its cats, which is to say that the cats are so numerous and so integrated into the urban fabric that they function as a kind of ambient presence — always somewhere underfoot or overhead, on a wall, in a doorway, asleep in a patch of afternoon sun on the paving stones. There is a cat museum in the old town, small and earnest. The souvenir shops sell cat postcards, cat magnets, cat figurines. The cats themselves are largely indifferent to all of this.

The standard explanation for the cats is nautical: sailors brought them aboard ships to control rats, cats escaped or were left behind in port cities, and over centuries the coastal towns of the Adriatic accumulated the descendants of a thousand such arrivals. This explanation is probably correct in its broad outlines and impossible to verify in its specifics. What can be said is that the cats of Kotor have been living in the old town long enough that they seem to belong to it in the way the cathedral belongs to it — as though the city would not quite be itself without them, which may or may not be true but is the kind of thing that feels true when you are sitting in the Square of Arms at evening with a coffee and a cat on the chair beside you.

What endures

Kotor is a small city that has survived by being useful to larger powers, and by being difficult to take by force when useful wasn't enough. The walls kept the Ottomans out. The merchants kept Venice interested. The sailors kept the republic supplied with admirals. When Venice fell, the Austrians took over and kept the walls standing. When Austria-Hungary dissolved, Yugoslavia inherited the city. When Yugoslavia broke apart, Montenegro became a country and Kotor became a UNESCO site and a cruise ship destination.

None of these transitions destroyed what was here. The cathedral is still here. The streets still follow the medieval plan. The Lion of Saint Mark still watches from the gate. The earthquake came and was repaired. The walls were climbed by conquerors and tourists alike and remain.

What survives in Kotor is the structure of a city that learned very early how to outlast the people who thought they owned it. That is not a small thing.
