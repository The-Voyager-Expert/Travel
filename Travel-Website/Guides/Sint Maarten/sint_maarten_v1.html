Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 20 min → Pic du Paradis
1. Pic du Paradis
↳ At 424 metres, Pic du Paradis is the highest point on the island, rising above the French interior. The summit sweeps across both coastlines — Anguilla, St. Barthélemy and a scatter of uninhabited islets visible on clear days.
↳ Drive to the end of Rue du Pic Paradis and walk 10–15 minutes to the summit; the public trail is free and well-worn. Loterie Farm runs guided rainforest walks Tuesday through Sunday.

2. Concordia Monument
↳ This white obelisk marks the Dutch-French border — one of the world's most open international boundaries, with no checkpoint, customs booth or passport control. A Dutch and French flagpole flank the monument on either side of an invisible line, and the obelisk appears on the Sint Maarten coat of arms.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~15 min
📍 Union Road · Cole Bay border area
🚕 10 min → Fort Amsterdam
3. Fort Amsterdam
↳ The first Dutch fort in the Caribbean, Fort Amsterdam's crumbling coral-stone walls sit above Little Bay and Great Bay, commanding wide views of the harbour and cruise-ship anchorage. The island changed hands between the Dutch, French and English no fewer than sixteen times before its current arrangement.
↳ Access via the Divi Little Bay Beach Resort — inform the front desk you are visiting the fort; a signed path leads to the ruins in about five minutes.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Little Bay · Philipsburg area
🚕 15 min → Maho Beach
4. Maho Beach
↳ Maho Beach sits directly below the approach path to Princess Juliana International Airport, with the runway threshold just metres from the shoreline. Arriving aircraft pass low enough overhead to read the airline livery.
↳ Departing jets blast the crowd at the perimeter fence with powerful jet wash. A real-time flight board outside Sunset Beach Bar displays incoming arrivals and departure times.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2 hr
⚠️ Best viewing 11:00am - 4:00pm for afternoon arrivals and departures

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 15 min → Philipsburg
1. Philipsburg
↳ The Dutch capital of Sint Maarten, stretched along a narrow sandbar between Great Bay and the Simpson Bay Lagoon. Front Street's Dutch colonial facades house duty-free shops and cafés; the boardwalk behind them runs the length of Great Bay beach — the island's main cruise-ship anchorage.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Front Street · Philipsburg
🚕 25 min → Marigot
2. Marigot
↳ The French capital of Saint Martin — a relaxed port town built around a waterfront market selling produce, spices, and crafts. Fort Louis stands on a hill above the bay, and the marina fills with sailboats from the Caribbean circuit; bistros and French patisseries line the quayside.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
⚠️ Waterfront market busiest Wednesday and Saturday mornings
📍 Waterfront Market · Marigot
🚕 20 min → Orient Bay
3. Orient Bay
↳ A 2-km crescent beach on the northeast coast of French Saint Martin, regarded as the Caribbean's premier beach for turquoise water and consistent trade winds. The bay faces the small island of Caye Verte; beach clubs and watersports — kite-surfing, paddleboarding, sailing — line the shore.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2 hr
📍 Orient Bay · French Saint Martin
🚕 10 min → Grand Case
4. Grand Case
↳ A single beachfront strip on the north coast of French Saint Martin holding more restaurants per metre than almost anywhere in the Caribbean. The lolos — local charcoal barbecue stalls — open for lunch alongside French Creole kitchens in converted West Indian houses facing the beach.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min

🍮 Local Tastes
Johnny Cakes
↳ The island's beloved fried bread — dense golden rounds made from flour · butter · coconut milk · eaten for breakfast or as a side with saltfish. Found at every local bakery and morning roadside stall across both sides of the island.
📍 Local bakeries · Philipsburg
Conch and Dumplings
↳ The national dish — fresh conch slow-cooked with dumplings · herbs · Caribbean spices into a hearty stew. No visitor should leave without trying it at a local rum shop or roadside cookout.
📍 Local rum shops · Philipsburg
Conch Fritters
↳ Crispy battered conch pieces deep-fried and served with a spicy dipping sauce — one of the Caribbean's great bar snacks · available at nearly every beach bar on the island.
📍 Sunset Beach Bar · Maho Beach
Callaloo
↳ A thick soup made from dasheen leaves · okra · crab · coconut milk · island herbs — common on the French side especially in Grand Case and Marigot.
📍 Restaurants · Grand Case · French side

🏝️ The Island That Agreed to Disagree

A Dutch soldier and a French soldier once settled a land dispute the way Caribbean people have traditionally preferred to settle things: with a walk. According to the legend both sides still tell, they were placed back-to-back at Oyster Pond and walked in opposite directions around the island's perimeter. Wherever they met — some hours later — became the border. The French soldier allegedly walked further and so France got the larger share: 53 km² to the Netherlands' 34 km². The story is almost certainly apocryphal. The actual Treaty of Concordia was a pragmatic negotiation between two small colonial garrisons with no ability to wage a meaningful war. But the legend endures because something true lives inside it: Sint Maarten and Saint Martin really have maintained one of the world's most open international borders for nearly four centuries.

There is no passport control between the Dutch and French sides. No customs checkpoint, no gate, no fence — not even a sign that says "now entering France." You drive across an invisible line and the road signs switch from English to French, the restaurants shift from Dutch-Caribbean to French Creole, and the currency changes from US dollars to euros. Fort Amsterdam was the first Dutch fort in the entire Caribbean — and then changed hands sixteen times between the Dutch, French and English before both nations eventually agreed to share the island and move on. The Concordia Monument, a modest white obelisk at the border road, marks the point of agreement so durably that it appears on the Sint Maarten flag and coat of arms: one of the very few border monuments in the world to earn that distinction. The whole island is 87 km². It is divided between two sovereign nations, governed in three languages, uses two currencies, and sends representatives to two national parliaments on two separate continents. It has never had a wall.
