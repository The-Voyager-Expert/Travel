The emperor who left and never came back

In 27 AD, the second emperor of Rome sailed out to a small limestone island off the Bay of Naples and stopped coming back. Tiberius was in his late sixties, tired of the plotting and flattery of the capital, and he built a palace on the island's northeastern spur, on a cliff so sheer that it drops straight into the sea. He called it Villa Jovis, the Villa of Jupiter, and he ran the Roman Empire from up there for the last decade of his life — dispatches carried by boat and courier down a mule track that switchbacks for nearly an hour from the town below. He never set foot in Rome again. When you stand at the top of that climb today, looking at the collapsed cisterns and the drop to the sea, you're standing in the actual seat of imperial power at its furthest possible remove from anyone who might have wanted it. Ancient writers loved to fill in the blanks of what Tiberius did up there alone, and the stories got lurid fast, most of them unprovable and clearly colored by resentment back in Rome at being governed by absence. What's certain is simpler and stranger: the most powerful man alive chose this rock over his own city, and stayed.

Tiberius wasn't the first emperor to want it. His predecessor Augustus had taken Capri from Naples years earlier, trading it for Ischia, the much larger and more fertile island next door — a straight swap, small barren rock for something worth far more in farmland, because Augustus wanted this particular piece of geography and didn't especially care what it cost him. When workers broke ground on his own villa here, they turned up fossilized bones of animals that have no business on a Mediterranean island — mammoth, hippopotamus, cave bear — and Augustus, fascinated, kept them on display in his garden, in what may be the first paleontology collection in the Western world. Wanting things because they're rare and pleasing to control turns out to be the oldest instinct this island has ever hosted.

The cave that was cursed, then forgotten, then sold

On the island's northern coast there's a sea cave you enter lying flat in a rowboat, ducking under a mouth barely a meter high, and once inside the water glows an electric, otherworldly blue. It's a real optical effect, not a trick: sunlight enters through a submerged opening below the cave mouth, passes through the water, and the blue wavelengths scatter back up through the surface while everything above the waterline stays dim, so a hand dipped in the water turns silver and the whole chamber looks lit from below. The Romans knew about it — statues and the remains of a small landing stage show Tiberius used it as a private grotto, and those statues now sit in a museum in Anacapri.

Then, for centuries, nobody important went in. Local fishermen avoided it, believing it was haunted, home to witches and sea monsters — not a place you took a boat on purpose. It sat there, glowing, unvisited by anyone who wrote things down, until 1826, when a German writer named August Kopisch got a local fisherman to row him in anyway. Kopisch published what he saw, European travelers started arriving to see it for themselves, and within a generation the Blue Grotto had become the single image most people associated with Capri — the thing you'd heard of before you'd heard of anything else on the island. A natural phenomenon that had existed since before Rome was founded needed one write-up by one romantic-era tourist to become an industry. It's worth remembering, floating in that boat with a dozen other tourists doing the same thing, that the light show is ancient and real, and the fame is barely two hundred years old and entirely a product of demand.

Two islands, one funicular ride apart

Capri Town and Anacapri are a few minutes apart by funicular and they are not remotely the same place. Capri Town, down at the harbor and up around the Piazzetta, is the polished version — the boutiques, the tight little café-lined square, the yachts stacked in Marina Grande. Anacapri, higher up and cut off from easy road access until the cliffside route was carved into the mountainside in the 1870s, was for centuries the poorer, more isolated half of the island, terraced with vines and olive trees because there wasn't much else to do with a mountainside. It stayed that way long enough that the Swedish physician Axel Munthe could buy up a hillside of ruins there around the turn of the twentieth century and spend decades building his villa on the site of an old Roman-era ruin, filling it with fragments he dug up himself.

The gap between the two towns never fully closed. Anacapri still feels like an island behind the island — quieter, more residential, with real vegetable gardens between the walls, and views from Monte Solaro that most people who take a photo in the Piazzetta never bother to go see. If Capri Town is what the island decided to become for other people, Anacapri is closer to what it was for itself.

The sandal that outlived the trend

In 1935, a Vogue editor named Diana Vreeland was on the island and noticed a fresco showing a man in nearly bare sandals — flat leather sole, a couple of thin straps — and asked a local craftsman to recreate them. That style, made by hand on the island for the ninety years since, became the Capri sandal, and it's a small, exact case study in how this place turns attention into craft. After the war, a shoemaker named Amedeo Canfora set up shop across from the Grand Hotel Quisisana, and by the early 1960s Jackie Kennedy was having the store opened after midnight so she could pick sandals in private — a request that produced a model still sold today. You can still walk into a workshop in Capri Town and watch someone cut and stitch a pair to your foot, using a technique that traces back through that 1935 fresco to whatever people on this island were wearing to walk its rocky, uneven ground long before anyone photographed it. Almost nothing about 1950s Capri chic survives as more than nostalgia. This does, because the leather and the fit were always the actual point — the fame just attached itself for a while.

The decade the whole island became a stage

Capri had been a retreat for European aristocracy since the late 1800s, but the 1950s turned it into something else: a name. Audrey Hepburn, Sophia Loren, Grace Kelly and the wider Hollywood and European jet set spent summers there, photographed constantly, and the island's light and its narrow lemon-scented lanes became shorthand for a specific kind of leisure — so much so that a style of cropped trouser invented nowhere near the Mediterranean got named after it anyway, purely by association. Emilio Pucci opened his first couture salon on the island in 1957, betting that Capri's colors and patterns would sell better with the island's name attached to them. None of this required Capri to build anything new. The Piazzetta had already been the town's social center for a century; it just got a lot more famous overnight, and the café tables around it have been charging accordingly ever since.

That transformation never really reversed. The Piazzetta still runs on the same principle — a tiny square, expensive seats, the performance of being seen — and the shops along Via Camerelle sell the same aspirational glamour that Pucci was selling in 1957, just with different labels on the awnings now. Capri Town on a summer evening is essentially still auditioning for the cameras that showed up seventy years ago and never entirely left.

What the day-trip skips

Most visitors arrive on a day trip from Naples or Sorrento, do the Blue Grotto, walk the Piazzetta, ride the chairlift up Monte Solaro, buy something lemon-scented, and leave on the last boat back. It's a genuinely spectacular way to spend a day, and the views from the Faraglioni sea stacks or the Arco Naturale earn every bit of their reputation. But the day-trip version skips the two things that actually explain the island: the climb up to Villa Jovis, which almost nobody bothers with because it's uphill and there's no grotto waiting at the end of it, just ruins and a sheer drop to the sea; and Anacapri once the tour groups have thinned out, when the terraces and gardens go quiet and it looks, for a while, like the farming island it was for most of its history. The postcard version of Capri is real. It's just the smaller of the two islands you can actually visit.

Before you go

Here's the one thing worth carrying onto the boat: Capri has never really been one place. It's always been at least two things stacked on top of each other — the emperor's private hideout and the empire's business address, the cursed cave and the tourist landmark, the farming village and the film set, Anacapri and Capri Town separated by a funicular and a completely different temperament. Every era that's passed through here has added its layer without erasing the one underneath, which is why a fisherman's descendant can row tourists into a cave the family once thought was full of witches, and why you can buy a handmade sandal a short walk from the path an emperor's household once wore something very like it to climb. The island has never resolved these layers into one coherent story, and it isn't trying to now. It just keeps being whatever the moment wants, on top of everything it already was. Once you notice that, you stop expecting Capri to be simple, and it gets a great deal more interesting.
