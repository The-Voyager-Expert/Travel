Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 9 min · 🚕 3 min → CN Tower
1. CN Tower
↳ Toronto's defining silhouette — a 553-metre concrete spire that held the title of world's tallest free-standing structure for over three decades. Glass-fronted elevators climb to observation levels with a vertigo-inducing glass floor and views across Lake Ontario to the mist of Niagara on clear days.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ CN Tower · 4.6⭐ · cntower.ca
🏛️ Daily 9:30am - 11:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
⚠️ Last entry to the observation levels at 9:00pm
📍 290 Bremner Blvd · Entertainment District
🚶 2 min · 🚕 1 min → Ripley's Aquarium of Canada
2. Ripley's Aquarium of Canada
↳ Canada's largest aquarium, sitting at the foot of the CN Tower with 20,000 aquatic animals. The showpiece is the Dangerous Lagoon — a moving glidepath through North America's longest underwater viewing tunnel, where sand tiger sharks and sawfish cruise overhead.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Ripley's Aquarium of Canada · 4.6⭐ · ripleys.com
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 11:00pm
⏰ ~2 hr
📍 288 Bremner Blvd · Entertainment District
🚶 15 min · 🚕 5 min → Jack Layton Ferry Terminal · 🚢 15 min → Centre Island
3. Toronto Islands
↳ A car-free archipelago fifteen minutes off the downtown shore, with the best skyline panorama in the city. Lawns, beaches, gardens and a boardwalk spread across the lagoons; rent a bike or simply wander before catching the ferry back as the towers light up.
📖 Wikipedia

4. Toronto Music Garden
↳ A waterfront garden that translates Bach's first Cello Suite into landscape — cellist Yo-Yo Ma and designer Julie Moir Messervy shaped each of its six sections after a movement of the music, from the swirling river of the Prélude to the grass-step amphitheatre of the Gigue.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~45 min

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 18 min → Casa Loma
1. Casa Loma
↳ A Gothic Revival castle on a hill above downtown — financier Sir Henry Pellatt's 98-room folly, completed in 1914 and lost to taxes within a decade. Secret passages, an 800-foot tunnel to the stables, conservatory glass domes and tower-top skyline views; the audio guide is included.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Casa Loma · 4.7⭐ · 2800+ reviews — GetYourGuide
🏛️ Daily 9:30am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~2 hr
⚠️ Last admission 4:30pm · Scottish Tower closes 3:00pm Wednesday - Friday and 1:00pm Saturday - Sunday
📍 1 Austin Terrace · Casa Loma
🚕 16 min → Nathan Phillips Square
2. Nathan Phillips Square
↳ The civic heart of the city — Viljo Revell's space-age twin-towered City Hall curving over a reflecting pool, the Freedom Arches, and the 3D TORONTO sign that became the city's favourite photo stop. The pool becomes a skating rink in winter; Old City Hall's clock tower watches across Bay Street.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 100 Queen St W · Downtown
🚶 23 min · 🚕 7 min → Allan Gardens Conservatory
3. Allan Gardens Conservatory
↳ A glass Palm House from 1910 anchoring one of Toronto's oldest parks — six greenhouses of tropical palms, orchids, cacti and seasonal flower shows, free to wander year-round. A warm, fragrant pocket of the Victorian city hiding in plain sight east of Yonge Street.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~45 min
🆓 Free

4. Distillery District
↳ The Victorian industrial core of the Gooderham & Worts whisky empire — forty brick-and-stone buildings on cobbled lanes, now a pedestrian-only quarter of galleries, distillers, chocolate makers and patios. The best-preserved collection of Victorian industrial architecture in North America.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 18 min → Royal Ontario Museum
1. Royal Ontario Museum
↳ Canada's largest museum, with 13 million objects across art, world culture and natural history in 40 galleries. The 2007 Michael Lee-Chin Crystal addition — a jagged, angular extension designed by Daniel Libeskind — protrudes from the original Edwardian building onto Bloor Street.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Art Gallery of Ontario
↳ One of the largest art museums in North America, with 95,000+ works spanning five centuries — from Group of Seven landscapes to European Old Masters and contemporary installations. The 2008 Frank Gehry expansion added the Galleria Italia, a glass-and-wood volume along the full Dundas Street facade.

3. Bata Shoe Museum
↳ The world's largest footwear collection — 13,000 specimens spanning 4,500 years, from Ancient Egyptian sandals to Elton John's platform boots. Raymond Moriyama designed the 1995 building to resemble a hatbox, with a lid-like angled roof above four floors of galleries tracing the history of human footwear.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Bata Shoe Museum · 4.6⭐ · batashoemuseum.ca

4. Hockey Hall of Fame
↳ Housed inside an 1885 Beaux-Arts bank building at the corner of Yonge and Front Streets, this is where the Stanley Cup lives. The collection spans over 3,000 artefacts, including game-worn jerseys, championship rings and a full-replica ice rink where visitors can face NHL-calibre virtual shooters.

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 35 min → Scarborough Bluffs
1. Scarborough Bluffs
↳ A 90-metre-high white clay escarpment carved by post-glacial Lake Iroquois, stretching 15 kilometres along the Lake Ontario shoreline. Bluffer's Park at the base offers beach access and unobstructed views back up at the sheer chalk-white cliffs — a landscape unlike anything else in the Toronto metro area.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 1 Brimley Road South · Scarborough
🚕 40 min → High Park
2. High Park
↳ Toronto's largest park at 161 hectares, bequeathed to the city by architect John George Howard in 1876. Grenadier Pond reflects the skyline of mature oaks and maples; the free High Park Zoo is tucked into the southern section, and the meadow trails make it a genuine half-day destination outside the urban core.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 6:00am - 10:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 1873 Bloor Street West · High Park
🚕 28 min → Harbourfront Centre
3. Harbourfront Centre
↳ A 10-acre lakeside cultural campus on Queens Quay West, with free galleries, an outdoor amphitheatre, a craft and visual arts studio, and a marina. The Centre hosts over 4,000 events a year — from literary festivals to open-air concerts — and the waterfront promenade offers unobstructed views across Lake Ontario.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Monday - Friday 10:00am - 6:00pm · Saturday - Sunday 10:00am - 9:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
🆓 Free
📍 235 Queens Quay West · Harbourfront
🚶 25 min · 🚕 10 min → Fort York National Historic Site
4. Fort York National Historic Site
↳ Founded in 1793 to guard the town of York, Fort York holds eleven original War of 1812 buildings — blockhouses, barracks and a powder magazine. American troops occupied the garrison in 1813; the British destroyed the magazine on departure, leaving a crater still visible in the grounds today.
📖 Wikipedia

🍮 Local Tastes
Peameal Bacon Sandwich
↳ Cornmeal-rolled back bacon piled on a soft kaiser bun — born in Toronto's pork-packing Hogtown era and named the city's signature sandwich. Mustard at the counter is the only accepted dressing.
📍 Carousel Bakery · Paddington's Pump
Butter Tart
↳ Ontario's gooey pre-1900 original — flaky pastry filled with butter, sugar and egg, runny or firm, plain or with raisins and pecans. Torontonians argue about the ideal wobble the way others argue about pizza.
📍 Gerrard Street Bakery · Sweet Bliss Bakery · Harbord Bakery
Jamaican Beef Patty
↳ Flaky golden turmeric pastry around spiced beef — a lunch-counter staple of Toronto's Caribbean community since the 1970s, sold hot in Kensington Market bakery windows for pocket change.

🍁 Hogtown Notes

The Toronto Music Garden exists because Boston said no. Yo-Yo Ma and garden designer Julie Moir Messervy first proposed their Bach garden for Boston's waterfront; when the project fell through, Toronto offered its harbour instead, and the First Cello Suite took root beside Lake Ontario. Stand in the spiralling Courante section and you are walking a line of music.

Day 2 ends where Toronto's fortunes began: Gooderham & Worts was once the largest distillery in the British Empire, and the whisky money built much of the Victorian city you cross to reach it. The same 504 King streetcar threads the whole guide together, running past the theatres beside the hotel all the way to its terminus inside the Distillery District.
