A garrison town that spent two centuries being careful, then stitched six cities into one and let half the planet move in — and the seams are the best part.

A fort that became a capital

When you stand at Fort York, a low huddle of whitewashed blockhouses a few blocks from the condo towers of the CN Tower, you are standing where Toronto actually starts. Lieutenant-Governor John Graves Simcoe founded the settlement in 1793, naming it York after a British prince and choosing the site for the same reason Fort York exists at all: a deep, sheltered harbor protected by a long sandbar, a place he judged easier to defend than the alternative he'd been sent to govern from. It was a garrison first and a town second — a scatter of ten blocks near the mouth of the Don River, laid out along two roads, Yonge Street and today's Queen Street, that still anchor the downtown grid.

York was burned by American troops in 1813, rebuilt, and renamed Toronto in 1834 when the city finally shed the colonial name nobody had asked for. What it did not shed for another century and a half was the temperament that founding produced — cautious, orderly, Protestant, British in aspiration. That temperament earned Toronto its most famous historical nickname, and understanding the nickname is the fastest way to understand what the city has since become.

Toronto the Good, and what happened to it

"Toronto the Good" was coined in the 1880s, during the mayoralty of William Howland, a moral reformer who ran on shutting down unlicensed drinking, gambling, and prostitution and spent his term tightening Sunday closing laws. The name stuck for decades because it was accurate: this was a city of churches, a city that voted down Sunday streetcars in 1897 and banned tobogganing on Sundays in 1912, a city whose bars closed early and whose cultural establishment looked to London rather than to itself. Montreal, French-speaking and unapologetically looser, was the Canadian city that mattered. Toronto was the one that behaved.

What ended that version of Toronto was not a single event but a policy: the overhaul of Canadian immigration law in the 1960s and '70s, which replaced a system that had favored British and northern European arrivals with a points-based model open to the entire world. The people who came under it — from Italy, Portugal, Greece, the Caribbean, Hong Kong, India, the Philippines, Vietnam, Somalia, Latin America, and dozens of other places — landed disproportionately in Toronto, wave after wave, for fifty straight years. Today just over half of the city's residents were born outside Canada, one of the highest shares of any major city on earth, trailing only Miami by that measure. The nickname survives now mostly as a joke Torontonians tell about their own reputation. The city it described is gone.

Nobody planned Toronto's diversity as policy. The doors opened, the people came, and fifty years later the city had rebuilt itself from the inside without anyone deciding to.

The neighborhoods that prove it

You will feel this history most directly by walking, not by reading a plaque about it. Kensington Market — a few blocks of Victorian rowhouses west of Chinatown — is not a curated food hall dressed up as a market; it is a working one, where Jamaican patty shops, Portuguese fishmongers, Hong Kong bakeries, and secondhand clothing stalls sit shoulder to shoulder because successive waves of arrivals simply moved in next to whoever was already there. Chinatown on Spadina Avenue has done business continuously since the early twentieth century and still does. Greektown, along the Danforth, is the largest Greek community outside Astoria, Queens, and its souvlaki is made by people whose grandparents made the same dish the same way in Thessaloniki. Little Italy on College Street, Little India on Gerrard Street East, Koreatown on Bloor — none of these are theme districts. They are where those communities actually live, shop, and eat, and the food reads as sincere because it is: cooked by people who grew up cooking it, for customers who know exactly what it's supposed to taste like.

This is the part of Toronto that most visitors underrate on arrival and can't stop talking about on departure. There is no single culinary landmark to point a camera at — no Toronto dish, no Toronto restaurant everyone agrees is the one. The food scene itself is the attraction, distributed across two dozen neighborhoods instead of concentrated in one, and it rewards exactly the kind of aimless, hungry walking that guidebooks rarely recommend and locals do constantly.

Six cities become one

The patchwork quality of Toronto's neighborhoods isn't only immigration — it's also municipal history. Until 1998, "Toronto" as you now experience it didn't exist as a single government. It was a federation: the old City of Toronto plus five surrounding municipalities — Etobicoke, York, North York, East York, and Scarborough — each with its own mayor, its own council, its own downtown-scaled main street. The Ontario government, under Premier Mike Harris, forced them together into one "megacity" on January 1, 1998, over the loud objection of a referendum in which more than three-quarters of voters across the region said no.

The amalgamation happened anyway, and it explains something that puzzles first-time visitors: why Toronto feels less like one city than like six cities wearing a single name tag. North York has its own dense highrise core around Yonge and Sheppard that functions like a satellite downtown. Scarborough, the most ethnically diverse of the former municipalities, has its own commercial spine along Kingston Road with a character entirely distinct from the Entertainment District thirty minutes west. Etobicoke still feels like the postwar suburb it was built as. None of them merged into a single texture, because nothing about the merger touched the street level — it consolidated city halls and budgets, not identities. When you cross from one former municipality into another today, the change in architecture and signage is often more obvious than the change in ZIP code would suggest, because there's no ZIP code to mark it — just a seam nobody quite smoothed over.

A tower built to solve a signal problem

Nothing signals mid-century Toronto's ambivalence about its own importance quite like the reason the CN Tower exists. It was not commissioned to be a monument. Canadian National Railway built it, starting in 1973, because Toronto's new wave of skyscrapers was bouncing broadcast signals around the city and TV reception was suffering. The fix an engineer proposed was simple in concept and absurd in scale: build something taller than the problem. What went up between 1973 and 1976 — 553 meters including its antenna, using 1,500 workers over 40 months — was, for the next three decades, the tallest freestanding structure on the planet, a record it held until the Burj Khalifa passed it in 2007.

The timing mattered as much as the height. The tower rose during the same decade Toronto was quietly overtaking Montreal as the country's financial and demographic capital — a shift driven partly by the same immigration wave reshaping the neighborhoods, partly by the political uncertainty around Quebec sovereignty sending head offices east to Bay Street. A city used to being the polite understudy suddenly had the tallest building on earth and the country's largest population, and didn't entirely know what to do with either fact except keep building. The tower answered a plumbing problem and became, almost by accident, the exclamation point on a decade when Toronto stopped being a big provincial town and became something else.

The city that went underground

Winter is the other force that shaped Toronto, and its answer to winter is mostly invisible from the street: PATH, a network of underground and elevated walkways connecting more than 70 buildings across roughly 30 kilometers beneath the financial core. It began small — a single tunnel Eaton's department store dug in 1900 so shoppers could cross under James Street without going outside, followed by a 1927 link between Union Station and the Royal York Hotel. City planners deliberately expanded it through the 1960s, convincing developers of the new bank towers going up downtown to build underground retail with the promise it would eventually all connect.

It did, and the result is a genuinely strange urban experience: you can walk from Union Station to the Eaton Centre to a half-dozen bank towers, stopping for lunch and doing your banking along the way, without ever putting on a coat, in a city where January regularly drops well below freezing with a lake-effect wind that makes it feel worse than the number on the thermometer. Tourists rarely find PATH on purpose. Locals treat it as the obvious answer to a climate problem nobody had to explain to them.

The water the city turned its back on, then didn't

Toronto sits on Lake Ontario, and for most of the twentieth century it treated that fact as an inconvenience. The Gardiner Expressway, built through the 1950s, runs along the shoreline on elevated concrete and cuts downtown off from its own waterfront about as completely as an urban design decision can. For decades the harborfront was warehouses, rail yards, and parking — the water was there, but nobody planned a city toward it.

That's slowly reversing: converted grain silos and rail sheds now hold restaurants and galleries along Queens Quay, and the Port Lands to the east are mid-way through one of the largest urban redevelopment projects in North America. But the real escape valve has been there the whole time, a fifteen-minute ferry ride from the foot of Bay Street: the Toronto Islands, a car-free archipelago of beaches, bike paths, and a small cottage community that has spent decades periodically fighting the city for the right to keep living there. From the south shore of the Islands, the lake runs to the horizon in one direction and the skyline — CN Tower included — rises behind you in the other, looking like a postcard of the place you just left. It is the fastest way to feel like you've left Toronto without actually leaving it.

What to carry with you

Every old city sells you its cathedrals and its wars. Toronto doesn't really have those, and it knows it, and it has stopped apologizing for the fact. What it has instead is a five-decade experiment that mostly worked: a garrison town that spent 150 years being careful, then opened its doors to the entire world within a single generation and let the newcomers build the place over from the inside — one neighborhood, one restaurant, one grocery store at a time — while its own municipal government was still busy stitching six separate cities into one.

That's the idea to hold onto as you walk it: Toronto isn't hiding its main attraction behind a ticket booth. It's cooking dinner three blocks from your hotel, in a language that isn't English, made by someone whose family has been in the city for two years or fifty. The skyline and the tower and the underground tunnels are all real and all worth seeing, but they're the frame. The picture is the food, the languages overlapping on the same block, and a city that built its whole identity by continuously admitting people who didn't grow up being told what Toronto was supposed to be — and then simply became what they made of it.
