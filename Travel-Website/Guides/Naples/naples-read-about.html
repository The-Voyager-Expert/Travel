A city that has been invaded, buried, looted, misunderstood, and underestimated for three thousand years — and has outlasted every verdict passed on it.

The city that never quite belongs to itself

Naples has been governed by outsiders for most of its existence. Greeks founded it around 470 BC and named it Neapolis — new city — on a site already occupied by an older settlement called Parthenope. Romans absorbed it and used it as a resort town; emperors kept villas on the bay. After the fall of Rome it passed through Byzantine hands, then Norman hands, then the Holy Roman Empire, then the Angevins from France, then the Aragonese from Spain, then the Spanish Habsburgs, then the Bourbons, then Napoleon's brother, then the Bourbons again, and finally unified Italy in 1861. It spent less than two centuries in that entire span under Italian administration of any kind.

What this produces is a city with an unusually fractured relationship with authority. Neapolitans have had good reason, over three millennia, to conclude that whoever is nominally in charge is probably not looking out for them. This is not cynicism in the modern sense — it is a rational response to a particular history. The street-level resourcefulness that foreign visitors sometimes read as chaos is what you develop when the institutions above you have been, with notable exceptions, extractive rather than protective. You learn to solve problems yourself, at the neighborhood level, with the people you know.

This also explains why Naples is one of the most intensely local cities in Europe. People here are from specific neighborhoods — Spaccanapoli, Posillipo, Quartieri Spagnoli — in a way that residents of most European cities are no longer from specific neighborhoods. The city identity is nested inside the neighborhood identity. A Neapolitan from Mergellina and a Neapolitan from Forcella might as well be from different towns.

Vesuvius and the logic of living here

The volcano is always there. From the seafront, from the hill of Posillipo, from the terrace of the Castel Sant'Elmo, Vesuvius sits across the bay and makes itself visible. It is one of the most dangerous active volcanoes in the world — three million people live in its potential eruption zone — and the city has been living with this fact since before it was a city.

The eruption of 79 AD that buried Pompeii and Herculaneum also destroyed the settlements on the slopes of the volcano and sent ash across the bay to Naples itself. Vesuvius has erupted dozens of times since. The last major eruption was in 1944, during the Allied advance through Italy, when lava flows destroyed several towns on the slopes and the volcano put on a pyrotechnic display visible from fifty miles. Allied military photographers documented it. You can find the footage.

People live here not despite the volcano but in a specific negotiation with it — the soil is extraordinarily fertile, the views are exceptional, and the city has been here long enough that leaving feels like the strange option.

The fertility is real. The volcanic soil of Campania produces tomatoes — the San Marzano variety, grown in the ash plains south of Vesuvius — that are genuinely different from tomatoes grown anywhere else. This is not marketing. The mineral content of the soil, the particular quality of the sun, and centuries of agricultural refinement produce a fruit that is less acidic and more intensely flavored than most tomatoes available outside the region. This is why Neapolitan pizza sauce tastes the way it does. The flour matters, the water matters, the wood-fired temperature matters, but the tomatoes are the foundation.

The underground city

Naples is built on tufa — a soft volcanic rock that is easy to cut and hardens when exposed to air. The Greeks began quarrying it when they built the city, tunneling beneath the surface for building material and then, because the tunnels were there, for water channels. Over the next two thousand years the tunnels were extended, connected, repurposed, forgotten, rediscovered, and extended again. The result is a subterranean network beneath the historic center that is approximately forty kilometers long and sits between fifteen and forty meters below street level.

During World War II the tunnels became shelters. Naples was bombed repeatedly by the Allies before and during the Italian campaign — the city changed hands in September 1943 after a four-day uprising, the first city in occupied Europe to liberate itself — and the population sheltered underground during the raids. Graffiti from that period is still visible on the tunnel walls: names, dates, small drawings. The air-raid shelter sections of the Napoli Sotterranea tour are the part that stays with you longest.

The tunnels also preserved Greek and Roman ruins that are otherwise inaccessible. A Greek theater from the third century BC sits beneath a palazzo in the Spaccanapoli neighborhood — you can descend into it through the building's basement. The theater where the emperor Nero performed (he considered himself a serious artist and made his subjects watch) is down there. The ancient city is a layer, not a memory.

What the Spanish left behind

The Spanish ruled Naples from 1503 to 1707, and the physical fabric of the city center is largely their inheritance. The Quartieri Spagnoli — the Spanish Quarters — was built in the sixteenth century to house the Spanish garrison, laid out in a strict grid of parallel lanes running perpendicular to the hill. The grid is still there: Via Toledo on one side, the hill on the other, and between them a labyrinth of narrow streets where laundry hangs between buildings and the ground floors are occupied by pizza shops and fruit vendors and small shrines.

The shrines — the edicole votive, small illuminated niches set into the walls at head height — are one of the most distinctive visual features of Naples and one of the least noticed. There are about a thousand of them in the city, dedicated to the Madonna or to local saints. They are maintained by the residents of the buildings they're attached to, decorated with fresh flowers and candles and photographs and small objects. They are not tourist attractions. They are a living religious practice at the scale of the building, not the institution.

The Spanish also brought the Bourbon dynasty, eventually. Charles of Bourbon became King of Naples in 1734 and began the most sustained period of monumental construction the city has seen. The Palazzo Reale di Capodimonte — the royal palace and art museum on the hill above the city — was his project, as was the Royal Palace at Caserta, which is the largest royal residence in the world by volume and was modeled on Versailles. The Capodimonte collection, assembled over centuries by the Farnese family and then expanded by the Bourbons, contains Titians and Caravaggios and El Grecos in rooms that are rarely crowded. It is one of the great undervisited art museums in Europe.

The art that will stop you

The Cappella Sansevero is a small private chapel near Piazza San Domenico Maggiore that contains what may be the most technically astonishing piece of sculpture in existence: Giuseppe Sanmartino's Cristo Velato, the Veiled Christ, completed in 1753. It is a figure of the dead Christ lying on a stone slab, covered by a thin shroud. The shroud is marble. It is difficult to stand in front of it without reaching out to touch it, to confirm with your fingers what your eyes are refusing to accept. The transparency of the cloth, the way it clings to the face and the body, the folds around the feet — these are not things marble does. And yet.

The chapel was the private project of Raimondo di Sangro, Prince of Sansevero, an eighteenth-century polymath with interests in chemistry, philosophy, Freemasonry, and what we would now call materials science. The room contains other sculptures of equal technical ambition — a figure of Modesty by Antonio Corradini, her face covered by a transparent veil also in marble — and two anatomical machines in the basement that di Sangro commissioned and that remain unexplained: human skeletal systems with intact arterial networks, preserved in a way that no one has fully accounted for. The chapel is small and requires a timed entry and is worth building your entire day around.

The Veiled Christ is the kind of thing that makes you question what craft actually means — how far human skill can be pushed before it stops being skill and starts being something else.

Pizza as a civic document

Neapolitan pizza was added to UNESCO's list of Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2017. This sounds like bureaucratic overreach until you understand what's actually being preserved: a specific set of techniques, ingredients, and standards codified by the Associazione Verace Pizza Napoletana, which has been policing the definition of authentic Neapolitan pizza since 1984. The dough must rise for at least eight hours. The tomatoes must be San Marzano. The mozzarella must be buffalo mozzarella from Campania or fior di latte from the Apennines. The oven must be wood-fired at 485 degrees Celsius. The pizza must cook in sixty to ninety seconds. The result must be soft and pliable in the center, not crisp, with a slightly charred and puffy crust called the cornicione.

Naples is also where pizza margherita was formalized, though the story is probably apocryphal: in 1889, according to tradition, the pizzaiolo Raffaele Esposito made a pizza for Queen Margherita of Savoy decorated in the colors of the Italian flag — tomato, mozzarella, basil. The name stuck. The combination predates the story by decades, but the story attached itself to the pizza and now they are inseparable.

Go to a place that is not famous on the internet. Order a margherita. Eat it immediately. This is not complicated advice and it is the most reliable way to understand what the fuss is about.

What to carry with you

Before you go, here is the thing that makes Naples legible: this city has absorbed every civilization that tried to claim it, and absorbed them on its own terms. The Greeks are in the underground theater. The Romans are in the museum in Pompeii and in the bones of the street grid. The Spanish are in the Quartieri. The Bourbons are on the hill at Capodimonte. And none of these layers have replaced each other — they are present simultaneously, with the Neapolitans moving through all of them as through rooms in a house they have always occupied. The city is not a museum of its past. It is a continuous negotiation between three thousand years of accumulated presence and the people living there right now, selling pizza at midnight outside a church built on a Greek foundation. Once you see that — that the depth is everywhere, not cordoned off — you stop treating Naples as difficult and start treating it as what it actually is: the oldest living city in the Western world, still refusing to simplify itself for anyone.
