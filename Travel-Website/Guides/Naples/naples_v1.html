1. MANN – Museo Archeologico Nazionale
↳ The world's finest collection of Graeco-Roman antiquities — Pompeian frescoes, the Farnese Bull, the Alexander Mosaic, and the Secret Room — all inside a 16th-century palazzo. The treasures of Pompeii and Herculaneum, pulled from the ash and preserved here for centuries.

2. Cappella Sansevero
↳ A single chapel holding one of the most astonishing sculptures in existence: the Veiled Christ, carved from a single block of marble with a veil so fine it seems to defy the material entirely. The alchemical machines in the crypt — anatomical studies preserved in wax — make it stranger still.

4. Piazza del Plebiscito
↳ Naples' grandest public space — a sweeping semicircle of Doric columns flanking the Basilica di San Francesco di Paola, the Royal Palace closing one end, Vesuvius visible at the far end of the seafront. The scale of the city becomes legible from here.

5. Castel Nuovo – Maschio Angioino
↳ The 13th-century Angevin fortress that dominates the port — five cylindrical towers and a white triumphal Aragonese arch wedged between them as an afterthought from a different dynasty. The Civic Museum inside holds Neapolitan art from the medieval period to the 20th century.

1. San Lorenzo Maggiore
↳ A 13th-century basilica over the ancient Greek agora and Roman macellum of Neapolis — descend below to a preserved Roman street, tabernae, and a bakery identifiable from its ovens. Boccaccio met Fiammetta here in 1336; Petrarch lodged in the convent next door.

2. Palazzo Reale di Napoli
↳ The Bourbons' royal palace on Piazza del Plebiscito — 30 frescoed rooms of Flemish tapestries and Neapolitan Baroque furniture arranged as they were in 1800. Eight bronze dynastic statues line the facade; the Royal Apartments are an intact record of 19th-century court life.

1. Certosa e Museo di San Martino
↳ A 14th-century Carthusian monastery at the Vomero summit, rebuilt in the 17th century into a showcase of Neapolitan Baroque. The museum holds the city's finest presepe collection, historical prints, and decorative arts; the cloister terrace looks straight down to the sea.

↳ The 2nd-century catacombs beneath Rione Sanità — the largest underground burial site in southern Italy, four levels deep with frescoed chambers and the original tomb of Naples' patron saint. Tours run by a cooperative from the neighborhood above.

🍮 Local Tastes
Pizza Napoletana
↳ The original — charred crust, risen cornicione, San Marzano tomatoes, and fior di latte or buffalo mozzarella, baked in a wood-fired oven in 90 seconds. Margherita and Marinara are the canonical versions; UNESCO-listed craft.
📍 L'Antica da Michele · Gino Sorbillo · Trianon da Ciro
Sfogliatella
↳ Naples' signature pastry in two forms — riccia · flaky shell-shaped · and frolla · smooth shortcrust. Both filled with semolina cream, ricotta, and citrus peel. Best eaten hot from the oven.
📍 Pasticceria Capparelli · Scaturchio · Attanasio
Pizza Fritta
↳ Deep-fried pizza folded over ricotta, cicoli · cured pork scraps · and provola — the street food that Neapolitans ate when wood-fired ovens were a luxury. Still sold from counter windows across the city; arrive hot.
📍 La Masardona · Starita a Materdei · Di Matteo
Ragù Napoletano
↳ The Sunday sauce — beef braised for six to eight hours in tomato and wine until it collapses into a deep, sweet ragù. Served over rigatoni or ziti spezzati. Nothing like Bolognese; a different logic entirely.
📍 Osteria della Mattonella · Da Nennella
Babà al Rum
↳ A light, yeast-risen cake soaked in rum syrup until it glistens — the French baba arrived in Naples via the Bourbon court and never left. Sold in every pasticceria, from pocket-size to pillar-shaped display versions.
📍 Scaturchio · Capparelli
Mozzarella di Bufala
↳ Made from water buffalo milk in the Campania plain south of the city, with a thin skin that splits to release a milky whey. The DOP version from Caserta or Battipaglia is a different thing from anything sold outside this region.
📍 Cantina di Via Sapienza · L'Antica da Michele

🌋 The City That Never Closed

Every city has layers. Naples has more of them than anywhere — and they refuse to be neatly separated. The Via dei Tribunali you walk along today is the ancient Greek Decumanus Maximus of Neapolis, unchanged in its course for 2,500 years. The cistern you descend into at Napoli Sotterranea is the same cistern that fed water to a Greek settlement in 470 BC, then to a Roman port, then to a medieval capital, then to a Spanish colony. The tufa stone beneath the streets is volcanic material that Vesuvius has been depositing here for tens of thousands of years — the same geology that made Naples fragile and Naples great.

What makes this city unusual is that nothing was ever properly razed and rebuilt. New rulers arrived — Greek, Roman, Norman, Angevin, Aragonese, Spanish, Bourbon, French — and each one built on top of or next to what was already there, rather than clearing it. Baroque churches sit over Roman temples. Medieval streets follow Greek grids. The Cappella Sansevero is a family chapel above a Mithraic altar. Castel Nuovo was built in 1279 and still has its 15th-century triumphal arch slotted between the towers like an afterthought from a different dynasty. MANN holds the art that Pompeii hid under ash for 1,700 years. The hotel address — Via Duomo 319 — is on a Roman street grid drawn in the 4th century BC. One day is not enough. But one day aimed in the right direction shows you more of human time than most cities reveal in a week.
