A Roman amphitheater still selling tickets, a dynasty that built a city in its own image, and wine country that starts where the sidewalk ends.

The Arena That Never Closed

The Arena di Verona was already four hundred years old when the Colosseum in Rome stopped hosting spectacles. Built in the first century, it seated thirty thousand in a city that probably held fewer people than that. The Romans overbuilt it on purpose. Verona sat at the junction of two major roads heading north toward the Alps, and the Arena was a statement: this is not a waypoint, this is a destination.

What sets the Arena apart from every other Roman amphitheater in Italy is that it never really stopped being used. The outer ring collapsed in a twelfth-century earthquake, but the interior survived almost intact. By the Renaissance, tournaments and fairs filled the oval. In 1913, tenor Giovanni Zenatello staged Aida inside it, and summer opera has run there nearly every year since. You can sit on the same limestone tiers where Romans watched gladiators and listen to Nabucco under open sky. The acoustics are imperfect in the way that outdoor stone is always imperfect, and that roughness is part of the point. This is not a museum piece. It is a building that has been in continuous public use for two thousand years because the people who live around it keep finding reasons to fill it.

The Arena is not preserved. It is occupied. That is a different thing entirely.

The Scaligeri and the City They Shaped

If you walk through Verona and wonder why the medieval center feels so deliberately built, so fortified and ornamented at the same time, the answer is a single family. The della Scala dynasty, known as the Scaligeri, ruled Verona from 1262 to 1387. They were not gentle rulers. They consolidated power through assassination, alliance, and expansion, and they poured the proceeds into the city itself.

Cangrande I, the most famous of the line, turned Verona into one of northern Italy's most powerful city-states during the early fourteenth century. He expanded the walls, fortified the bridges, and patronized Dante during the poet's exile. The Arche Scaligere, the family's Gothic funerary monuments clustered next to their private church, are among the most elaborate medieval tombs in Europe. Cangrande sits on horseback atop his own sarcophagus, grinning. It is not subtle. The Scaligeri wanted you to remember them, and the city they built makes sure you do.

Castelvecchio, the fortress they raised on the Adige in 1354, still anchors the western edge of the old city. Its crenellated bridge was designed to provide an escape route north if the city fell. It never needed to serve that purpose for the Scaligeri, but the bridge survived Allied bombing in 1945 and was rebuilt stone by stone. It remains one of the most striking crossings on any Italian river.

The River and the Walls

The Adige bends sharply around Verona's historic core, forming a natural moat on three sides. This is not an accident of urban planning. The Romans chose this site precisely because the river loop created a defensible peninsula. Every power that held Verona afterward, from the Ostrogoths to the Venetians to the Austrians, reinforced that geography with walls, gates, and watchtowers. The city's shape today, that tight cluster of streets inside the river bend, is a direct inheritance of two thousand years of military thinking.

Stand on Ponte Pietra, the partly Roman bridge on the north side, and look south. The rooftops compress into a mass of terracotta and stone that looks too dense to navigate. That density is the old city doing what it was designed to do: pack as much life as possible into the space the river protects.

Piazza delle Erbe and the Roman Bones Underneath

Piazza delle Erbe is Verona's central square, and it has been the central square since the city was Roman. It sits directly on the footprint of the ancient forum. The long, narrow shape is not medieval improvisation. It is the shape of a Roman civic rectangle, preserved through fifteen centuries of rebuilding because every generation kept using the same ground for the same purpose: commerce, argument, spectacle.

Fifteen centuries of market stalls, and the shape of the square has not moved an inch from the Roman plan.

The buildings lining it now are mostly fourteenth to sixteenth century, layered with frescoes that have faded to the color of old paper. The Torre dei Lamberti rises above the northeast corner, offering the best aerial view of how the city fits together. At ground level, the piazza still operates as a market. Vendors sell fruit and trinkets under canvas awnings in the same elongated rectangle where Roman merchants once did the same. The continuity is not curated. Nobody decided to preserve it as a heritage site. People just kept showing up.

The Balcony, the Myth, and What Is Actually There

Shakespeare never visited Verona. The story of Romeo and Juliet was borrowed from an Italian novella, and the "Juliet's House" on Via Cappello is a thirteenth-century building that the city purchased in 1905 and retrofitted with a balcony in the 1930s to give tourists something to photograph. The courtyard below it is packed wall to wall with visitors sticking chewing gum to the stonework and rubbing a bronze statue for luck. It is, by a wide margin, the least interesting thing in the city.

This matters because the myth is so dominant that first-time visitors sometimes treat Verona as a one-stop Shakespeare pilgrimage and miss everything else. The actual city, the one built by Romans and Scaligeri and Venetians, the one where opera echoes off ancient stone on summer nights, does not need a fictional love story to justify the visit. The balcony is a detour. The Arena, the river, the piazzas, the wine: that is the city.

Four Centuries Under Venice

The Scaligeri lost Verona in 1387, and after a brief interval under Milan's Visconti, the city passed to Venice in 1405. It stayed a Venetian possession for nearly four hundred years, longer than it had ever been independent. This is not the version of Verona most visitors picture, but it is written into the city everywhere you look. The winged lion of Saint Mark, Venice's civic emblem, sits carved into the column at the center of Piazza delle Erbe, planted there in 1523 as a deliberate marker of whose territory this was.

Venetian rule meant Verona became a garrison city on the Republic's western frontier, the buffer between the lagoon and whatever army happened to be marching down from the Alps that century. The Venetians rebuilt the fortifications the Scaligeri had started, and the star-shaped bastions ringing the old town's outer edge, visible today as the green belt of parkland that circles the historic center, are Venetian military engineering, not medieval. Verona paid taxes to the Doge and sent grain and soldiers east, and in exchange it got four centuries without the kind of destructive regime change that leveled other Italian cities. Napoleon ended the arrangement in 1797, but by then the Venetian imprint on Verona's institutions, architecture, and self-image was permanent.

Pastissada and a City That Cooks Its Own Way

Verona's food does not look like the rest of Veneto's, and there is a specific, slightly grim reason why. Local tradition traces the city's signature dish, pastissada de caval, a horse meat stew slow-braised for hours in wine and spices, to the aftermath of a sixth-century battle outside the city walls, when the defeated Ostrogothic king's cavalry was left dead on the field and the Veronese, following a custom common to war-scarred medieval towns, found a use for the meat rather than let it rot. Whether the origin story is literal history or civic folklore polished over fifteen centuries, the dish is real, still served in trattorias across the city, and it tells you something true about Verona: it has always been a garrison and crossroads town first, a place that adapted to what war left behind rather than a city that grew up in isolation from conflict.

The gnocchi tradition runs just as deep and is considerably less dark. On the Friday before Lent, Verona holds the Venerdì Gnocolar, a centuries-old carnival procession led by a costumed figure called the Papà del Gnoco, wielding an oversized fork, celebrating a sixteenth-century famine relief effort in which a local nobleman distributed flour and grain to the poor. The city still eats gnocchi that day by the tradition of it, not because a restaurant marketing team invented the occasion.

Valpolicella and the Wine at the Doorstep

Drive twenty minutes northwest of Verona and you are in Valpolicella, one of the most important wine regions in the Veneto. The hills start gently and climb into terraced vineyards producing Corvina, Rondinella, and Molinara grapes. This is where Amarone comes from, the rich, raisined red made from grapes dried on straw mats before pressing. It is also where Ripasso is made, a Valpolicella re-fermented over Amarone grape skins to pick up depth and weight.

Verona's relationship to this wine country is not incidental. The city has been a trading hub for Veneto wines since the Roman period, and the annual Vinitaly fair, held every April, is the largest wine exposition in Italy. The proximity means that restaurants in Verona pour local Valpolicella and Amarone the way restaurants in Lyon pour Beaujolais: casually, affordably, as the obvious choice. You do not need to rent a car or book a wine tour to drink well in Verona. You just need to sit down.

When you leave Verona, carry this with you: every city in Italy claims deep history. Verona is one of the few where the history is not behind glass. The Arena sells tickets. The forum sells vegetables. The river still dictates the street plan. The wine country starts at the city limits. What you see is not a reconstruction. It is a city that kept going.
