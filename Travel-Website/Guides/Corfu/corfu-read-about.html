Every empire that mattered in the Mediterranean left something on Corfu — Venetians built the old town, the French laid the arcade, the British introduced cricket and ginger beer — and the island wears all of it at once, without apology.

Why every empire wanted it

Stand on the ramparts of the Old Fortress — the Paleo Frourio, the massive Venetian fortification on the promontory jutting east of the old town into the sea — and look out at the channel between Corfu and the Albanian coast. On a clear day you can see the Albanian mountains from here, close enough to make the crossing in a few hours. What you're looking at is why Corfu mattered: the narrowest point of the passage between the Adriatic and the Ionian, the gateway that whoever held it could tax and control.

The Greeks colonized the island in the 8th century BCE. The Romans took it. The Byzantines held it for centuries. Then came the Normans, the Angevins, the Venetians, the French under Napoleon, the British under the Treaty of Paris, and finally the Greeks in 1864. No other Greek island has been shaped by so many distinct administrations, and no other Greek island looks quite like Corfu Town as a result. The UNESCO designation the old town received in 2007 recognized it as an outstanding example of a fortified Mediterranean city. The more useful framing is simpler: the place looks like nowhere else in Greece because it spent most of its history not being ruled by Greeks.

Four centuries of Venice

Venice held Corfu from 1386 to 1797 — over four centuries, long enough to fundamentally reshape the island's architecture, agriculture, and social organization. Walk the old town and you're walking a Venetian city that happens to have Greek inhabitants: the campaniles, the loggia, the narrow lanes organized around wells and courtyards, the fortress system that absorbed successive Ottoman attacks without falling. The Venetians built for permanence because Corfu was too valuable to lose.

They also planted olive trees — systematically, with a subsidy of four gold ducats for every tree planted and maintained. That policy transformed the island's agricultural landscape in a way that outlasted Venetian rule by centuries. Corfu now has over three million olive trees, more per square kilometer than almost anywhere in Greece. Many of the gnarled, silver-leafed trees you see along the roadsides were planted under that incentive. When you taste Corfiot olive oil, you're tasting a Venetian economic policy that has been producing returns for six hundred years.

The Venetians built for permanence and the British built for comfort. Both of them left the island more interesting than they found it — which is not what either of them came here to do.

Napoleon's arcade

Venice fell to Napoleon in 1797, and with it Corfu. The French stayed only a few years, but they left something specific and lasting: the Liston. Walk to the Esplanade — the large open green at the edge of the old town — and you'll find a colonnaded promenade modeled on the Rue de Rivoli in Paris, at Napoleon's direct suggestion or so the story goes. The arcade gives the cafés covered outdoor seating, and the tradition of sitting under those arches with a coffee or a glass of wine has continued uninterrupted ever since. You should do it. Order slowly. The Liston exists for exactly this.

The French also abolished the Venetian aristocratic registry — the Libro d'Oro, the Golden Book that listed the island's noble families — and with it the formal caste distinctions that Venetian rule had maintained. This was welcomed by everyone not in the book, which was most people, and deeply resented by those who were. The French period was too short to leave much else physically, but the abolition of the nobility registry had effects on Corfiot social structure that outlasted the French themselves by generations.

The British and the things they left behind

The British took control in 1815 following Napoleon's defeat and held the Ionian Islands as a protectorate until 1864. They left four things that are still visibly in use. The Ionian Academy — the first university in modern Greece — was founded in Corfu in 1824 under British patronage. The island's road network, built for administrative and military movement, forms the basis of the road system you're driving on. The game of cricket was introduced by British soldiers and has been played continuously on the Esplanade ever since — there are still regular matches, making Corfu the only place in Greece where the sport is played with any seriousness. Watch a game if you can; it's surreal in the best way, village cricketers in whites on a pitch bordered by Venetian arcades and Byzantine churches.

The British also introduced ginger beer, which the Corfiots adapted into tsitsibíra — a locally produced ginger-flavored soft drink still made on the island and nowhere else in Greece. The fact that a colonial administration's drink preference persists as a local specialty two centuries later tells you something about the depth of these imprints. These weren't brief occupations. Four centuries of Venetian rule and fifty years of British administration leave marks that outlast the administrators by a long stretch.

The Durrells, and the Corfu they saw

In 1935, the Durrell family — a widowed English mother and her four children, including the ten-year-old Gerald and the twenty-three-year-old Lawrence, already writing — moved to Corfu for several years. Gerald Durrell's memoir My Family and My Others describes the experience with such warmth and precision that it became one of the best-selling travel books of the 20th century and has sent readers to the island ever since. Lawrence Durrell's Prospero's Cell, written in 1945 after the family had left, reads as a sustained argument for why Corfu specifically, and Greece generally, contains something the northern European mind needs and can't find at home.

The houses the family lived in — the Pink Villa, the Strawberry-Pink Villa, and the Snow-White Villa, as Gerald named them — are scattered across the island's north and northeast coast. Finding them requires some effort, which is exactly how the Durrells found Corfu in the first place: by going slightly further than was necessary and paying attention to what was there. If you've read either book before arriving, you'll find the island changed and unchanged in the ways both brothers predicted it would be.

Paleokastritsa, and the west coast

Go to Paleokastritsa on the northwest coast, but go early — by ten in the morning the parking is already difficult and the coves are filling. What you want is the water before the crowd: a series of small bays separated by forested headlands, with water that shifts from emerald in the shallows to deep blue where it drops off, and a 13th-century monastery on the clifftop that has been continuously occupied ever since. Swim in the first cove, then take the path up to the monastery. The view from the terrace — the arc of the bay below, the Ionian going west to Italy — makes clear why this position was chosen and why it has held.

The interior of the island is greener than you expect from a Greek island: Corfu's northern position in the Ionian means higher rainfall than the Aegean, and the result is a hilly, forested landscape of Aleppo pine and ancient olive groves that looks more Italian than the rest of Greece. Rent a car for at least one day and drive north. Leave the coast road. The village roads are narrow enough to require patience and deliver the version of the island that the brochures are picturing when they say "unspoiled."

What the layers add up to

Before you go, here's the thing worth carrying with you: Corfu is the proof that being strategically important is not always a disaster. Every empire that came here — the Venetians, the French, the British — left something that the island absorbed and kept. The fortress that protected Corfu under Venetian rule still stands. The arcade where Napoleon wanted his officers to promenade still serves coffee. The university the British founded still exists. The cricket is still played. Most places that spent five centuries being owned by someone else look like they were depleted by the experience. Corfu looks like it collected something from each of them. Walk the old town with that in mind and it stops being pretty streets and starts being a record of everyone who thought this island was worth having.
