Every empire that mattered in the Mediterranean left something on Corfu — Venetians built the old town, the French laid the arcade, the British paved the roads and invented ginger beer — and the island wears all of it without embarrassment.

The Island at the Crossroads

Corfu sits at the mouth of the Adriatic, close enough to Albania that you can see the Albanian mountains from the island's northeast coast on a clear day. This position — between the Ionian and the Adriatic, on the sea route connecting Greece to Italy and the wider Mediterranean to the north — made Corfu strategically valuable to every naval power that ever operated in the region. The Greeks colonized it in the 8th century BCE. The Romans took it. The Byzantines held it for centuries. Then came the Normans, the Angevins, the Venetians (who proved the most consequential), the French under Napoleon, the British under the Treaty of Paris, and finally the Greeks in 1864. No other Greek island has been shaped by so many distinct colonial administrations, and no other Greek island looks quite like Corfu Town as a result.

The old town — Kerkyra — is the physical record of that sequence. Walk its narrow lanes and you move through compressed history: Byzantine churches alongside Venetian campaniles, a French-built arcade modeled on the Rue de Rivoli in Paris, British neoclassical buildings from the 19th century, and Orthodox churches that have been rebuilt and reconfigured so many times their original date is genuinely uncertain. The UNESCO designation (2007) recognized the old town as an outstanding example of a fortified Mediterranean city, but the more useful framing is simpler: the place looks like nowhere else in Greece because it's spent most of its history not being ruled by Greeks.

The Venetians and the Old Town

Venice held Corfu from 1386 to 1797 — over four centuries, long enough to fundamentally restructure the island's architecture, agriculture, and social organization. The Venetians' primary motivation was strategic and commercial: Corfu was the gateway to the Adriatic, and whoever held it could tax and control the sea route between Venice and the eastern Mediterranean. They fortified it accordingly. The Old Fortress — the Paleo Frourio — sits on a promontory jutting into the sea east of the old town. The New Fortress — the Neo Frourio — was built in the 16th century to cover the town's landward approaches. Both are massive, layered with successive building campaigns, and both remain standing in forms you can walk through.

The Venetians also planted olive trees. A lot of them. They paid Corfiots four gold ducats for every olive tree planted and maintained, a subsidy that transformed the island's agricultural landscape. Corfu now has over three million olive trees — more per square kilometer than almost anywhere in Greece. Many of the gnarled, silver-leafed trees you see along the roadsides are centuries old. The olive oil they produce is consumed locally and exported in modest quantities, and it tastes like it's been absorbing the Ionian climate for generations, which it has.

"The Venetians built for permanence and the British built for comfort, and both of them left the island more interesting than they found it."

Napoleon, the French, and the Liston

Venice fell to Napoleon in 1797 and ceded its Ionian territories to France under the Treaty of Campo Formio. The French held Corfu for only a few years, but they left something specific and lasting: the Liston. Modeled on the Rue de Rivoli arcade in Paris — at Napoleon's direct suggestion, or so the story goes — the Liston is a colonnaded promenade facing the Esplanade, the large open green space at the edge of the old town. The arcade provided covered outdoor seating for Corfu's cafes, and the tradition of sitting under those arches with a coffee or a glass of wine has continued essentially uninterrupted ever since.

The French also abolished the Venetian aristocratic registry — the Libro d'Oro, the Golden Book that listed the island's noble families — and with it the formal caste distinctions that Venetian rule had maintained. This was welcomed by those not in the book and deeply resented by those who were. The French period was short enough that its physical legacy is largely the Liston, but the abolition of the nobility registry had effects on Corfiot social structure that outlasted the occupation itself.

The British Protectorate

The British took control of Corfu and the other Ionian Islands in 1815 following Napoleon's defeat, establishing the United States of the Ionian Islands as a British protectorate under a Lord High Commissioner. The British period lasted until 1864 and produced a set of contributions that are still visible and in use. The Ionian Academy — the first university in modern Greece — was founded in Corfu in 1824 under British patronage. The island's road network, built by the British for administrative and military purposes, forms the basis of the road system still in use. The game of cricket was introduced to the island by British soldiers and has been played continuously in Corfu since; there are still regular cricket matches on the Esplanade, making Corfu the only place in Greece where the sport is played with any seriousness.

The British also introduced ginger beer, which the Corfiots adapted into tsitsibíra — a locally produced ginger-flavored soft drink that is still made and sold on the island and nowhere else in Greece. The fact that a British colonial administration's drink preference persists as a local specialty two centuries later says something about the depth of the Venetian and British cultural imprint on Corfu: these weren't brief occupations. Four centuries of Venetian rule and fifty years of British administration leave marks that outlast the administrators by a long stretch.

Gerald and Lawrence Durrell

In 1935, the Durrell family — a widowed English mother and her four children, including the young Gerald (age 10) and the already literary Lawrence (age 23) — moved to Corfu and stayed for several years. Gerald Durrell's memoir My Family and My Others (1956) describes the experience with such warmth and comic precision that it became one of the best-selling travel memoirs of the 20th century and has sent readers to Corfu ever since. The book's Corfu is a place of gentle eccentrics, abundant wildlife, and a pace of life so different from England that the family's presence there feels like an experiment in how to live.

Lawrence Durrell's time on Corfu produced Prospero's Cell (1945), a lyrical account of the island that reads as a sustained argument for why Corfu specifically, and Greece generally, contains something the northern European mind needs. The two brothers' books, arriving a decade apart, created a literary image of the island that attracted subsequent generations of writers and readers. The Durrell family's various houses on the island — particularly the Pink Villa, the Strawberry-Pink Villa, and the Snow-White Villa, as Gerald called them — have become informal pilgrimage sites for readers of the memoir.

The Beaches and the Landscape

Corfu is greener than most Greek islands — a consequence of its northern position in the Ionian, where rainfall is higher than in the Aegean. The interior is hilly and forested with Aleppo pine and olive groves. The coastline varies: the east coast facing Albania is calmer, with a gentler drop into the water; the west coast faces open sea and catches the prevailing winds, producing dramatic rock formations, sea caves, and beaches backed by low cliffs. Paleokastritsa, on the northwest coast, is the island's most photographed spot — a series of small coves separated by forested headlands, with water that shifts through colors from emerald to deep blue depending on depth and light.

Canal d'Amour, near Sidari on the north coast, is a narrow sea channel cut through pale sandstone cliffs by centuries of wave action. The cliffs are riddled with natural arches, caves, and tunnels, some of which you can swim through. The local legend holds that couples who swim through the main channel will marry — a story that exists in approximately the same form at several coastal rock formations around the Mediterranean, which doesn't make it less pleasant to hear.

What the Layers Add Up To

The thing about Corfu that stays with you after you leave isn't any single thing — not the Liston, not the Old Fortress, not the cricket on the Esplanade. It's the sense that the island has absorbed so many different influences without being destabilized by any of them. The Greek Orthodox church stands next to the Venetian campanile. The British-era road takes you to a Byzantine monastery. The Liston serves Greek coffee under a French arcade. These aren't contradictions; they're accumulations. Corfu spent most of its history as a possession of someone else's empire, and the result is an island that looks like a collaboration rather than a product — layered, hybrid, and more interesting than any single version of it would have been. Walk the old town with that in mind and it becomes legible in a way it isn't when you just see it as pretty streets.
