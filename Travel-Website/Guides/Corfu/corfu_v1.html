Day 1

1. New Fortress
↳ The Venetians' second great citadel, raised to seal the landward approach the Old Fortress left exposed. Ramparts, dry moats, and tunnelled galleries climb to a bastion carved with the Lion of Saint Mark and the widest rooftop view over Corfu's roofs and harbour.

2. Church of Saint Spyridon
↳ The spiritual heart of Corfu, built to shelter the relics of the island's patron saint, still carried through the streets in procession four times a year. Its red-domed campanile is the tallest in the Ionian Islands, and the interior glows with silver lamps and votive offerings.

3. Spianada & Liston
↳ The largest square in Greece, cleared by the Venetians as a firing range before the fortress. Along its western edge runs the Liston, an arcaded café promenade built under French rule and modelled on the Rue de Rivoli. Cricket pitches and bandstands fill the green.

🚶 6 min · 🚕 3 min → Old Fortress
4. Old Fortress
↳ A rocky islet bristling with Venetian fortifications, cut off from the town by the sea-filled Contrafossa moat. Built on Byzantine foundations, it held Corfu through three Ottoman sieges. A climb past the British-built church of St George reaches the lighthouse summit and a sweeping Ionian panorama.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 34 min · 🚕 4 min → Mon Repos
1. Mon Repos
↳ A wooded estate above the bay where ancient Palaiopolis stood, its Regency villa built for a British High Commissioner and later the birthplace of Prince Philip. Shaded paths pass Doric temple ruins on the way to the neoclassical house, now a small museum of British-era memorabilia and antiquities.

2. Vlacherna Monastery & Pontikonisi
↳ Corfu's most photographed scene: a tiny whitewashed convent on its own islet, reached by a slender stone causeway, with the cypress-covered Mouse Island and its Byzantine chapel floating beyond. The viewpoint sits on the Kanoni headland at the tip of the airport runway, so jets skim low overhead.

📍 Kanoni · Kanoni

3. Achilleion Palace
↳ A neoclassical villa built for Empress Elisabeth of Austria — Sisi — and themed on the hero Achilles. Terraced gardens descend toward Kanoni past colonnades and mythological statues, from the colossal Victorious Achilles to the marble Dying Achilles. The hilltop gives one of the island's finest views.

🍮 Local Tastes
Kumquat Liqueur
↳ A bright orange citrus liqueur made from the kumquat, a miniature fruit the British introduced to Corfu and now grown almost nowhere else in Greece. Sold island-wide as a sweet digestif and in spoon-sweet form.
📍 Mavromatis · Lazaris
Pastitsada
↳ Corfu's Sunday dish: rooster or veal braised in a spiced tomato sauce of cinnamon and cloves, served over thick hollow pasta — the island's clearest example of Venetian-Greek cooking.
📍 Rex Restaurant
Sofrito
↳ Thin slices of veal slow-braised in a white wine, garlic, and parsley sauce — milder than pastitsada and a Corfiot home-table staple rarely found elsewhere in Greece.

❗️ Heads Up
❗️ Achilleion Palace — Interior Closed for Restoration
↳ The palace interior is closed for restoration; only the gardens, peristyle, and sculptures are open.
Workaround: Go for the gardens, the Achilles statues, and the Kanoni view — do not expect the furnished rooms.
❗️ Greek State Sites — Closed Tuesdays
↳ Most Greek state sites close on Tuesday — on Corfu the New Fortress and Mon Repos; the Old Fortress is the Tuesday exception.
Workaround: Do the New Fortress and Mon Repos on Wednesday to Monday; save the Old Fortress, Spianada, Liston, and Kanoni for a Tuesday.
🍊 Layers of Empire

What makes Corfu Town feel unlike anywhere else in Greece is that almost no one tore down what came before. The Venetians left two fortresses and a warren of tall, shuttered alleys; the French laid the arcaded Liston over the old firing range; the British added cricket on the Spianada, a Regency villa at Mon Repos, and a taste for ginger beer that survives as the local tsitsibira. Each ruler left a flavour rather than erasing the last.

The kumquat is the neatest example — a tiny ornamental citrus a British botanist planted here that took to the island so well it became Corfu's signature liqueur. Four rulers, and the souvenir you carry home is a fruit from China by way of Victorian England, grown in a Venetian-Greek town.
