Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 22 min → Grand Palace
1. Grand Palace
↳ The ceremonial heart of the Thai kingdom, built in 1782 as the seat of the Chakri dynasty. The walled compound covers 218,000 sq m and holds Wat Phra Kaew — home of the sacred Emerald Buddha — alongside the Grand Throne Hall and the Royal Reception Halls used for coronations and state ceremonies.

2. National Museum Bangkok
↳ The largest museum in Southeast Asia, in an 18th-century palace complex. The collection spans 5,000 years of Thai civilisation — from Dvaravati stone reliefs and Khmer bronzes to gold regalia, royal funeral chariots, and the finest Sukhothai-era Buddhas anywhere.

🚶 35 min · 🚕 12 min → Jim Thompson House
3. Jim Thompson House
↳ Six traditional Thai teak houses assembled by American silk entrepreneur Jim Thompson, who revived the Thai silk trade before mysteriously vanishing in 1967. The interiors preserve his personal collection — Burmese woodcarvings, Ayutthaya-era Buddhas, Chinese porcelain — arranged exactly as he left them.

2. King Power MahaNakhon SkyWalk
↳ Bangkok's highest observation point at 314 m, perched atop the nation's tallest skyscraper. A glass-floored skydeck looks straight down 78 floors to street level. Inside, the SkyVerse immersive experience recreates Bangkok's history through room-scale projections from the capital's founding to its future.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 18 min → Wat Pho
1. Wat Pho
↳ A royal temple complex on Rattanakosin Island dating from the 16th century, expanded by King Rama I in 1788. The complex houses a 46-metre-long Reclining Buddha covered in gold leaf, and is recognised as the birthplace of traditional Thai massage — massage pavilions operate within the grounds.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 7:30pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 2 Sanam Chai Road · Phra Nakhon
🚶 10 min · 🚕 5 min → Wat Arun
2. Wat Arun
↳ The Temple of Dawn occupies the west bank of the Chao Phraya, reached by a short cross-river ferry from Tha Tien Pier. Its 70-metre central prang is decorated with Chinese porcelain fragments and seashells set into plaster — work carried out during the reign of King Rama II in the early 19th century.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 158 Wang Doem Road · Thonburi
🚶 15 min · 🚕 5 min → Wat Ratchanatdaram Worawihan
3. Wat Ratchanatdaram Worawihan · Loha Prasat
↳ A royal temple built in 1846 under King Rama III, housing the Loha Prasat — an iron-spired multi-tiered tower with 37 metal spires representing the 37 virtues of enlightenment. One of only three Loha Prasat temples ever built in the world, the others being in India and Sri Lanka.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 2 Maha Chai Road · Rattanakosin
🚶 10 min · 🚕 5 min → Wat Suthat & Giant Swing
4. Wat Suthat & Giant Swing
↳ A first-class royal temple completed in 1847, containing the 8-metre-tall Phra Si Sakayamuni Buddha — one of the largest bronze Buddha images in Thailand. Directly in front stands the 21-metre-tall red teak Giant Swing, erected in 1784 and used in Brahmin ceremonies until 1935.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~1 hr

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 22 min → Lumphini Park
1. Lumphini Park
↳ A 57.6-hectare public park in central Bangkok, opened in 1925 as Thailand's first public park. The grounds contain an artificial lake for paddle boats, tree-lined paths, and an outdoor gym. Monitor lizards — up to 2 metres long — inhabit the lake margins and are regularly visible from the pathways.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 4:30am - 9:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Rama IV Road · Lumphini · Pathum Wan
🚶 12 min · 🚕 5 min → Erawan Shrine
2. Erawan Shrine
↳ A public Brahmin shrine at the Ratchaprasong intersection housing a gold statue of Phra Phrom, the Thai representation of the Hindu deity Brahma. Built in 1956 after construction accidents at an adjacent hotel prompted spiritual intervention. Devotees commission resident classical dancers to perform as offerings.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~30 min

🚕 12 min → Ananta Samakhom Throne Hall
3. Ananta Samakhom Throne Hall
↳ A royal reception hall in Dusit Palace commissioned by King Chulalongkorn and completed in 1915. The Italian Renaissance building — clad in Carrara marble with a central dome — was designed by Annibale Rigotti and Mario Tamagno. The interior is open as a museum of Thai arts and crafts.

4. Vimanmek Mansion
↳ The world's largest golden teak wood building, constructed 1900–1901 as a royal residence for King Rama V within the Dusit Palace complex. Now a museum displaying royal household artefacts, personal effects, and early 20th-century furnishings from the reign of Rama V.

Pad Thai
↳ Stir-fried rice noodles with egg, bean sprouts, and dried shrimp, tossed in a tamarind-fish sauce glaze and finished with peanuts and fresh lime. Bangkok's most famous street dish, refined to an art form at old riverside noodle stalls.
📍 Thip Samai · Pad Thai Fawng
Tom Yum Goong
↳ Bangkok's signature hot-and-sour prawn soup — a powerful broth of lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime leaf, chilli, and fish sauce. Invented in the Chao Phraya basin and still best eaten along the riverside.
📍 Tom Yum Kung Noodle · Baan Ying
Mango Sticky Rice
↳ Glutinous rice steamed in sweetened coconut milk, served warm alongside slices of ripe Nam Dok Mai mango with a drizzle of salted coconut cream. The perfect Bangkok dessert, seasonal but found year-round at dedicated stalls.
📍 Mae Varee · Srinagarindra Train Night Market
Boat Noodles
↳ Intensely dark, thin broth enriched with pork blood, herbs, and crispy garlic — ladled in small bowls to be eaten by the dozen. A holdover from when vendors paddled through Bangkok's canals selling food from boats.
📍 Victory Monument Boat Noodle Alley

↳ Poached chicken sliced over rice cooked in the same broth, served with a bowl of clear soup and a fermented soybean dipping sauce. Bangkok's quintessential everyday meal — morning or midnight, the best spots are always packed.

The River That Built a Capital

Bangkok exists because of the Chao Phraya. The Chakri kings chose this bend of the river to build a new capital — defensible, connected to the Gulf of Siam, and alive with commerce. For 200 years the river was Bangkok's highway: produce floated from the north, teak came from the forests, and entire floating markets sold goods from boats.

Today the same river carries the Chao Phraya Express ferries past the spires of the Grand Palace, past Lhong 1919's restored jetty, past the glass towers of Silom. Every stop on this guide touches it. Take the boat at least once — you'll understand the city differently from the water.
