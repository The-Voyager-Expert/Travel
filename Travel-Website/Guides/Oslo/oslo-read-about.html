A small city at the end of a fjord that spent most of its history in someone else's shadow — and used the oil money to build its way out.

The city that kept changing its name

Oslo has been called Oslo, then Christiania, then Kristiania, and then Oslo again. The changes track the city's history of subordination and recovery better than any map could. It was founded around 1000 CE, became the capital of a reasonably powerful medieval Norwegian kingdom, and then gradually lost status as Norway fell first under Danish rule (1380) and then into a union with Sweden (1814). The city renamed itself Christiania in 1624 after a fire — the new name honoring the Danish king who rebuilt it — and kept that name through the Danish period and most of the Swedish one, only reclaiming Oslo in 1925, four years after independence and a long political argument about national identity that the name change finally settled.

The history of that subjugation is not ancient. Norway was part of Sweden until 1905. That is within living memory of people who are now grandparents. The Norwegian insistence on its own language, its own culture, and its own way of doing things — which visitors sometimes experience as a mild stubbornness — is not a personality trait. It is the residue of a long effort to exist distinctly in the shadow of larger neighbors.

The fjord at its door

Oslo sits at the end of the Oslofjord — a 100-kilometer arm of the sea that cuts inland from the Skagerrak and narrows, at the city's doorstep, to a bay ringed by forested hills. This geography defines everything. The city is compact because the terrain forces it to be, pinched between the fjord and the Nordmarka forest to the north. The water is immediately accessible from the city center in a way that is unusual even for Scandinavian capitals: you can take a ferry from the city hall pier to the Bygdøy peninsula in ten minutes, or to any of the fjord islands that dot the bay.

In summer, Oslo moves to the fjord. The locals swim in it, sail on it, picnic at its edges. The Aker Brygge waterfront, which was a shipyard until the 1980s, is now restaurants and apartments and a continuous public promenade. The new Munch Museum on the Bjørvika waterfront opened in 2021 and leans out over the water like a building that wants to be in it. The Opera House, completed in 2008, slopes down to the fjord at water level and lets you walk up its white marble roof to stand at the apex and look out over the city — which is an extraordinary thing for a major cultural institution to do, and very Norwegian.

Edvard Munch and the scream that wasn't a scream

The most famous image in Norwegian art is The Scream, and the most common misreading of it is that the figure at its center is the one screaming. Munch wrote about the moment it depicts in his diary: he was walking with friends along the Ekeberg ridge south of the city when he felt suddenly anxious and overwhelmed, and the sky turned "blood and tongues of fire" while his friends walked on ahead. The figure in the painting is not screaming. It is the landscape that is screaming, or rather the figure is hearing the scream of the world and pressing its hands to its ears against the sound.

You can walk to the Ekeberg ridge. It is a short tram ride from the center, a fifteen-minute walk up through the park, and the view from the ridge is recognizable — the fjord below, the city spread along its edge, the sky doing what it does in that latitude. Munch stood here and heard something. You may not hear the same thing. But it is a useful place to stand before you go to the Munch Museum to see the painting.

The figure in The Scream is not screaming. It is covering its ears. The landscape is the one in anguish.

Munch spent much of his adult life in Germany and France, kept returning to Norway, had a nervous breakdown in 1908, and spent the last 27 years of his life at Ekely, his estate outside Oslo, producing an enormous body of work in something close to isolation. He left all of it to the city of Oslo when he died in 1944 — twenty-eight thousand works, the largest bequest any artist has ever made to a single institution. The Munch Museum on the Bjørvika waterfront holds all of it. The building is contentious — large and angular in a way that Oslo's older waterfront wasn't — but what is inside makes the argument for itself.

The Viking ships and what they actually were

The Viking Ship Museum on the Bygdøy peninsula holds three burial ships — the Oseberg, the Gokstad, and the Tune — that were discovered in burial mounds across southern Norway between 1867 and 1904. They are the best-preserved Viking ships in the world, and seeing them is one of those museum experiences that reorganizes what you thought you knew.

The ships are large. This is the first surprise — the Oseberg ship is 22 meters long, with room for thirty rowers, built from oak with a craftsmanship that modern shipwrights still analyze. The second surprise is what the ships were for. These were not longships that crossed the North Sea to England (though very similar ships did that). These were burial ships for people of enormous status, sunk into the earth with everything they would need in the afterlife: sleds, beds, farm equipment, textile tools, a cart of a quality that suggests it was ceremonial, and in the Oseberg ship, two women whose identities are still debated — one may have been a queen, the other her servant or companion.

The Viking age lasted roughly from 793 to 1066. During those 273 years, Norse sailors reached North America (Leif Eriksson landed in Newfoundland around 1000 CE, five centuries before Columbus), colonized Iceland and Greenland, traded as far as Constantinople and Baghdad, and established the Varangian Guard — the Norse mercenary corps that served as the Byzantine emperor's personal bodyguard. Oslo was a minor settlement during most of this period. The center of the Viking world was further west and south, in the fjord regions of western Norway. But the ships are here, and they are extraordinary.

The oil and what it built

In 1969, oil was discovered in the Norwegian sector of the North Sea. What happened next was not inevitable — it was a choice. Norway decided to treat the oil revenue not as an income stream to be spent but as a national inheritance to be invested, and created the Government Pension Fund Global — the sovereign wealth fund — to hold the proceeds. The fund now owns approximately 1.5% of all listed shares in the world. It is the largest sovereign wealth fund on the planet.

The visible effects in Oslo are everywhere and nowhere simultaneously. The public transport is excellent. The museums are free or nearly free. The waterfront was rebuilt from industrial wreckage into public space. The Opera House is clad in Italian marble. The new Munch Museum cost several hundred million dollars and nobody much complained about the expense. The welfare state — universal healthcare, free university education, paid parental leave that fathers are legally entitled to and socially expected to use — is funded in part by the returns on those oil investments.

What this means for you as a visitor is that Oslo is expensive in the way that well-maintained things are expensive: because the city takes care of itself at a level that requires money. The tradeoff is a city where things work, where public space is genuinely good, and where the gap between how ordinary people live and how wealthy people live is narrower than almost anywhere else on earth.

Frogner Park and the Vigeland problem

Gustav Vigeland spent forty years — from 1907 to his death in 1943 — producing the sculpture installation that fills Frogner Park. It is the largest sculpture park made by a single artist in the world: 212 bronze and granite sculptures depicting the human life cycle, arranged along a central axis that runs from the gate to the Monolith — a 14-meter column of 121 intertwined human figures — to the Wheel of Life beyond. The city of Oslo gave him a studio, a house, and a stipend, in exchange for which he gave them all his work.

The Vigeland sculptures are deeply strange. The figures are naked, expressive, and technically skilled, but they exist in a world without context — no specific time, no specific people, just the universal human experiences of birth and childhood and love and age and death. A man hurling a baby over his shoulder. An old man and a young woman embracing. A circle of children dancing. Tourists photograph the Angry Boy — a small bronze of a furious toddler, fists clenched — and it is funny, but it is also a genuine and somewhat unsettling expression of that particular human state.

Go on a Tuesday morning in spring or fall, when the tour buses are elsewhere. Walk the whole axis from the gate to the Wheel of Life without stopping to take photographs. Let it work on you the way Vigeland intended — as a continuous argument about what it means to be alive in a body for a limited amount of time.

Vigeland got forty years and a studio. Oslo got 212 sculptures and a park. It remains unclear who got the better deal.

The forest inside the city

Oslo's northern edge dissolves into the Nordmarka — 1,700 square kilometers of forest that begins within the city limits and extends north for hours. You can take the Holmenkollbanen metro line from the city center, ride forty minutes through increasingly suburban and then forested stations, and arrive at the edge of genuine wilderness. Norwegians call the right to roam across uncultivated land allemannsretten — the everyman's right — and it is codified in law. You can walk, ski, camp, and pick berries anywhere in the Nordmarka that is not private farmland or a garden.

This matters for understanding Oslo. The city's relationship to the outdoors is not recreational in the American sense — not something you do on a designated trail on weekends. It is simply part of how Norwegians move through the world. Families ski to school in winter. People eat lunch in the forest. The distinction between "city" and "nature" is less absolute here than in cities that are more thoroughly separated from the land around them. Holmenkollen, the ski jump that rises above the city on its ridge, is both a world-class competition venue and a place where Oslo residents bring their children on Sunday afternoons.

The thing to carry with you

Here is what changes how you see Oslo: this is a city that could have squandered its oil wealth — and many countries in the same position did exactly that — and instead made a different bet. It decided that the money belonged to the future, not the present, and invested it in what a society actually needs to function well: infrastructure, education, healthcare, public space, cultural institutions. Not because Norwegians are inherently virtuous, but because they made specific political choices at a specific moment and then sustained those choices across decades.

The result is a small city that punches well above its weight — in museums, in food, in public design, in how it manages the water at its doorstep. It is not flashy. It does not try to be Paris or London. It is trying to be the best version of itself, in the latitude it occupies, with the resources it has. That turns out to be something worth seeing.
