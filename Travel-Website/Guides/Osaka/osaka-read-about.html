The city that was Japan's commercial capital for a thousand years, raised an identity around eating rather than elegance, and has never been particularly interested in what Tokyo thinks of it.

The city that refused to be the capital

Osaka has been the economic center of Japan since at least the seventh century, when Naniwa — the city's original name — served as the country's de facto capital before Nara and then Kyoto took the role. It lost the capital and kept the money. This was not an accident. The city sits at the mouth of the Yodo River, where goods moving between the interior and the sea had to pass through, and the merchants who controlled that passage understood very early that commercial position could matter more than political position.

By the Edo period (1603–1868), Osaka was the undisputed commercial hub of Japan. Rice brokers in the Dojima district were trading futures contracts — the earliest such market in the world, predating the Amsterdam exchanges — while Kyoto remained the imperial capital and Edo (now Tokyo) was the seat of the shogunate. Osaka ran neither. It ran commerce, and it did so with a directness that still shows in how the city talks about itself.

The Osaka greeting — mōkarimakka? — translates roughly as "are you making money?" It's a joke, mostly. But it contains something real. The question presupposes that commerce is a legitimate thing to ask about, a normal unit of social exchange, rather than something to be embarrassed about or discussed only obliquely. Kyoto had refinement. Edo had power. Osaka had trade, and trade required talking plainly about what things were worth.

The castle Hideyoshi built

Osaka Castle — the white-walled, gold-leafed fortress that anchors the northeastern quarter of the city — was built between 1583 and 1597 by Toyotomi Hideyoshi, the general who unified Japan after a century of civil war. It was deliberately the largest castle in the country at the time: five floors visible above the moat, eight floors in total, surrounded by stone walls that used the biggest individual stones of any castle in Japan. The largest, the Octopus Stone on the outer wall, weighs 130 tons. It is one of the most impressive pieces of construction in Edo-era Japan, and it was built explicitly to say: look what I can move.

Hideyoshi is important not just as a historical figure but as an emblem of Osaka's self-image. He was not born into the ruling class. He was the son of a peasant foot soldier, and he rose through ability and relentless work to become the most powerful man in Japan. Osaka was his city because he chose it — a commercial port he saw as a base for national power, rather than the imperial center of Kyoto. The castle was the symbol of that choice.

Hideyoshi built the biggest castle in Japan not in the imperial city but in the merchant city. That tells you what he thought was actually powerful.

The castle you see today is a 1931 concrete reconstruction. The original burned in 1615 when Tokugawa forces besieged it after Hideyoshi's death and the succession dispute that followed. A second version was struck by lightning and burned in 1665. The current version has an elevator to the top floor, which feels wrong until you realize that Osaka has never been much interested in putting form above function. The museum inside is excellent.

Kuidaore — eating until you fall down

Osaka has an expression: Kyoto no kidaore, Osaka no kuidaore. It means "Kyoto people ruin themselves buying clothes; Osaka people ruin themselves buying food." Kyoto, as the old imperial capital, valued appearance. Osaka valued appetite. The expression is affectionate self-deprecation, but it contains a genuine cultural distinction.

The Dotonbori canal district — the neon-lit stretch of restaurants and street food stalls that runs along the south bank of the Dotonbori canal — is the most visible expression of this. The food here is not refined in any Michelin sense. It is takoyaki (octopus balls, invented in Osaka in the 1930s by a street vendor named Tomekichi Endo), okonomiyaki (savory pancake, which has its own Osakan variant distinct from the Hiroshima version), kushikatsu (skewered and breaded meat and vegetables, deep-fried), ramen, gyoza, and ice cream in flavors that should not work and do. You eat standing at counters. You eat while walking. The food is good and cheap and honest and there is a great deal of it.

The Kuromon Ichiba market — the city's main indoor food market, running for 150 years — is a better version of the same energy. The stalls here sell to restaurants and to individuals, and the quality of what's available reflects the fact that the buyers know what they're looking at. Fresh tuna, live sea urchin, Matsusaka beef, produce from across western Japan. You can eat a serious meal without sitting down by walking the length of the market and buying a few things at each stall.

The dialect as attitude

Kansai-ben — the Osakan dialect — is immediately recognizable to any Japanese speaker, and the city is openly proud of it. Standard Japanese (Hyojungo) developed from the Tokyo-area dialect after the Meiji Restoration shifted national power east, and many Japanese people associate standard speech with formal registers: news broadcasting, official settings, polite company. Kansai-ben sounds warmer, faster, more informal by comparison — and Osakans use it everywhere, in meetings, on television, with strangers.

The language difference is partly political. Osaka has historically maintained a distinct identity from Tokyo — economically, culturally, temperamentally — and the preservation of the dialect is part of that. Osakans are known across Japan for two things that are related: their directness (which can read as rudeness to people from elsewhere) and their humor. Osaka is the home of manzai, the traditional Japanese form of stand-up comedy performed by duo partnerships. The Yoshimoto comedy agency, founded in Osaka in 1912, is still the country's largest comedy production company. There is a connection between a culture that talks plainly about money, maintains its own dialect, and produces more comedians per capita than anywhere else in the country.

What Shintenno-ji tells you

Shitennō-ji temple in the Tennoji district was founded in 593 CE by Prince Shotoku, making it one of the oldest Buddhist temples in Japan — roughly contemporary with the founding of the city itself. It's not particularly famous internationally, and it's not as visually striking as some of the temple complexes in Nara or Kyoto. This is what makes it worth visiting.

The temple holds its outdoor flea market on the 21st and 22nd of every month. Vendors sell antiques, secondhand kimono, pottery, tools, and general household goods in the precincts around the main buildings. Elderly Osakans pick through boxes of ceramics while tourists photograph the central pagoda. Monks go about their business. The relationship between the sacred and the commercial here is not tense — it is simply what the place has been for 1,400 years, a civic institution that practices religion and runs a market simultaneously.

This coexistence — serious Buddhism, active commerce, afternoon crowds eating takoyaki from a nearby stall — is the Osaka temperament in concentrated form. The city does not feel the need to separate these things.

The underground city

Osaka has more underground shopping area than any city in Japan — hundreds of kilometers of tunnels connecting train stations, office towers, and shopping malls in a subterranean network that operates in parallel to the city above. The area around Umeda station alone contains several distinct underground malls, most of them connected to each other and to at least five different train and subway lines. During the rainy season and the humid Osaka summer, the underground network means you can cross significant distances without stepping outside.

It's easy to get lost in. This is not a flaw. The underground malls have restaurants and food courts at levels that range from excellent ramen shops to serious Japanese cuisine, and the experience of discovering a good restaurant in a basement tunnel under a train station — with no natural light, no Instagram presence, no reason to be there except that someone recommended it — is one of the more specifically Osakan pleasures available.

The thing to carry with you

Here's what changes how you see Osaka: the city has spent over a thousand years being economically essential and culturally overlooked. It was never the capital. It was never the cultural arbiter (that was Kyoto). It was never the seat of power (that was Edo and then Tokyo). What it was — consistently, for a millennium — was the place where things were bought and sold and made and moved. And out of that position it developed something that the capital cities didn't have: an identity that wasn't borrowed from authority.

Osaka knows what it is. It is a city of merchants and comedians and people who eat seriously and talk plainly and maintain their own dialect because the dialect is theirs. The directness that can feel abrupt is the directness of a city that never needed to perform deference. The food culture that looks like gluttony is actually a form of connoisseurship — the same discernment that made the city's rice traders the best in the country, applied to octopus balls.

Walk the Dotonbori on a weekday evening, when the crowds are manageable and the neon is starting to come on over the canal, and eat something that costs almost nothing and tastes better than it should. That's what a thousand years of caring about food produces. Once you understand it as craft rather than indulgence, the whole city looks different.
