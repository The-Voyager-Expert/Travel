A cattle crossing that outlasted an empire of scholars — and the town that never quite agreed to become one of them.

The ford before the scholars

Start with the name, because it tells you what the place was before anyone here had heard of a lecture. Oxford means exactly what it sounds like: a ford for oxen, a shallow crossing point on the river the locals still call the Isis (it's the same Thames that runs through London, splitting into channels here that made it fordable). Around the year 900, this was a place where a north-south cattle route needed to cross water, and here the water happened to be shallow enough to walk livestock through. That's it. That's the founding idea. No monastery, no royal charter, no ambition — just mud, oxen, and a river doing what rivers do.

When you cross Folly Bridge today, a few hundred yards from where that original ford sat, you're tracing a route that predates the university by two centuries. Oxford was a working Anglo-Saxon town — a market, a mint, a fortified burh against the Vikings — for a very long time before it became a byword for learning. It's worth holding onto that fact, because almost everything a visitor sees today conspires to make you forget it. The city wants to show you spires. The ford is the reason any of it is here at all.

An argument in Paris that built a university

There's evidence of teaching in Oxford as far back as 1096, but nobody can point to a founding date, a charter, or a first professor, because that's not how it happened. What actually built the university was a diplomatic falling-out four thousand miles from where you're standing. In 1167, Henry II — furious with the Archbishop of Canterbury and generally at odds with the French crown — banned English students from studying at the University of Paris, which up to that point had been the obvious place for an ambitious English clerk to go.

Cut off from Paris, those clerks needed somewhere else, and Oxford already had the ingredients: a town large enough to house them, churches willing to let them study, and a tradition of teaching already underway. The university didn't arrive by design. It arrived because a king had a grudge, and a market town with a Roman road and a river crossing was the nearest place able to absorb several hundred restless young men with nowhere else to be.

The year the scholars walked out

The town did not take kindly to hosting them. In December 1209, an Oxford clerk killed a woman, apparently by accident, and fled. The town's response was to seize three of his fellow students — men who had nothing to do with the killing — and hang them, over the university's furious objections that clerks answered to church law, not the town's rope. The chancellor suspended lectures. Within days, most of the scholars had packed their books and left Oxford entirely.

Some went to Reading. Some went to Paris, now that the ban had lifted. A cluster of roughly three hundred walked east to a small market town on the River Cam and started teaching there instead. That town became Cambridge. Every joke you've heard about the Oxford-Cambridge rivalry — the boat race, the "other place," the centuries of one-upmanship — traces back to a hanging that had nothing to do with academics and everything to do with a town that resented the scholars living in it.

Cambridge exists because Oxford, once, was a town that hanged the wrong students and the university simply left.

The rivalry never really cooled, and the underlying tension — town versus gown, literally: the people who lived here against the people who wore the university's robes — kept boiling over for centuries. The worst of it came in 1355, on St Scholastica's Day, when a dispute over the quality of wine at a tavern escalated into three days of open street fighting. Bells were rung to call the town to arms. By the time it ended, dozens of students and townspeople were dead, and the city was forced into an annual ritual of public penance to the university that lasted more than 450 years. Walk through the town center today and none of that violence is visible. It's worth remembering it happened, because the friendliness of modern "town and gown" relations is a truce, not an absence of history.

Thirty-nine republics, not one campus

Here's the thing that trips up most first-time visitors: there is no University of Oxford campus. When you walk between Christ Church, Magdalen, and Merton, you're not crossing one institution's quad system — you're crossing the borders of self-governing, financially independent colleges, each with its own charter, its own endowment, its own dining hall, its own gate that locks at night. The university itself — the body that runs the Bodleian, sets the exams, awards the degrees — is more like a federal government sitting on top of 39 college-republics that jealously guard their own identities.

This is why the colleges look and feel so different from each other, and why students at Oxford will tell you their college name before they tell you they go to Oxford at all. Tutorials, the one-on-one or small-group teaching Oxford is famous for, happen inside the colleges. Lectures, exams, and the big research libraries belong to the university. Almost every gate you pass on your walk through town is the entrance to a small, ancient, largely self-contained nation with its own rules.

The library that never has to ask

The Bodleian Library, which you'll see from the Radcliffe Camera's viewing gallery or from Broad Street, has a right that almost no other library on earth holds: since 1662, it has been legally entitled to a free copy of essentially every book published in the United Kingdom. It's one of only six legal deposit libraries in Britain and Ireland — a list that also includes the British Library and Cambridge — meaning that somewhere in the Bodleian's stacks (which run for miles, much of it underground and off-site) sits a copy of nearly every book your own country has ever legally published.

The building most people photograph — the Radcliffe Camera, that round, honey-stone drum you'll see on every second postcard — isn't the Bodleian itself. It's a reading room, built to house the library's science collection when the older buildings ran out of shelf space. That's the practical logic behind one of the most photographed buildings in England: it exists because a library was, quite literally, running out of room for all the books it was legally owed.

A cathedral that's also a dorm's chapel

Christ Church is the one college in the world that doubles as a cathedral. When Henry VIII dissolved the monasteries and reorganized the English church in the 1540s, he took the old priory church here and gave it two jobs at once: it became both the seat of the Bishop of Oxford and the everyday chapel for Christ Church's own students. Nowhere else in the Church of England does one building carry both roles — you can walk into evensong and be sitting in a working diocesan cathedral and a college chapel in the same breath.

It's also, by pure accident of medieval stonework, one of the most recognizable film sets in the world — the dining hall and the staircase leading up to it were part of what inspired the look of Hogwarts. Nobody built Christ Church to be a movie backdrop; they built it as a cathedral-college hybrid four centuries before anyone thought about it that way. The tourists photographing the staircase are, mostly, standing in a functioning religious and academic institution that predates the thing they came to see it for by five hundred years.

Where the hobbits and the lion were born

If you walk up St Giles', you'll pass a pub called the Eagle and Child, nicknamed the "Bird and Baby" by the people who actually drank there. From the 1930s through the late 1940s, a loose group of Oxford dons and writers who called themselves the Inklings met here on weekday lunchtimes in a back room they called the Rabbit Room. J.R.R. Tolkien and C.S. Lewis were the core of it, along with a handful of others, and what they did there was read their unfinished manuscripts aloud to each other and argue about them over beer.

The Lord of the Rings was read in that room in fragments, chapter by chapter, years before it existed as a published book. So were the proofs of The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe. Neither Middle-earth nor Narnia was dreamed up in a study in isolation — both were workshopped, critiqued, and occasionally mocked by friends over pints in an Oxford pub, the same kind of pub you can still get a drink in today. It's a useful corrective to the idea of Oxford as a solitary, cloistered place. Its most enduring stories came out of a group of friends talking loudly in a bar.

What Oxford actually is

It's easy, especially with the spires and the gowns and the Latin over the gates, to mistake Oxford for a university with a town attached. It's the other way around. Oxford is a real, working English city of roughly 150,000 people — with its own industry, its own suburbs, its own residents who have never set foot inside a college and never will — and the university is a nine-century-old guest that arrived because of a king's quarrel in Paris, got run out once by an angry mob, came back, and never fully assimilated into the town that hosts it.

That's the tension worth carrying with you as you walk the streets: gowned and un-gowned Oxford still sit slightly apart, the way they always have, one federation of self-governing colleges layered over an ordinary market town that happens to have thirty-nine small nations tucked inside it. The scholars could always, in theory, do what they did in 1209 and simply leave. The town would still be here, fording the river, long after they'd gone.
