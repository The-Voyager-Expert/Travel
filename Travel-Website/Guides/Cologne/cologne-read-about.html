Cologne is one of the oldest cities in the world where the original layout is still lived in — the same streets, the same river crossing, the same ridge above the Rhine that the Romans chose two thousand years ago.

The Roman foundation

In 38 BC, Roman general Marcus Vipsanius Agrippa established a military settlement on the west bank of the Rhine at a point where the river was wide, navigable, and crossable. He settled a Germanic tribe there — the Ubii, allies who had asked for Roman protection — and named the place Oppidum Ubiorum. In 50 AD, Emperor Claudius elevated it to a colonia: Colonia Claudia Ara Agrippinensium. The emperor’s wife, Agrippina the Younger, had been born here, and she lobbied for the promotion. The short form of the Latin name — Colonia — became Köln in German, Cologne in French and English, and gave its name to the modern city and to the Eau de Cologne fragrance that would be invented here seventeen centuries later.

The Roman city was a grid of streets enclosed by walls roughly three kilometers long. The Praetorium — the governor’s palace — stood near the center. Parts of it are still visible underground, below the floor of the old city hall. The Capitoline temple, the forum, the bathhouses, the amphitheater — Cologne was a proper Roman city of perhaps fifteen to twenty thousand people, the administrative capital of the province of Germania Inferior. The street grid it established is still partially legible in modern Cologne, particularly around the Hohe Straße and Schildergasse shopping streets, which follow the Roman decumanus maximus.

When Roman authority collapsed in the fifth century, the Franks took the city without apparent destruction. Unlike many Roman provincial cities, Cologne continued to function. The walls stayed up. The streets stayed in use. The bishop — a role established in the third century — became the effective power, and Cologne entered the early medieval period as a significant ecclesiastical center rather than a ruin.

The twelve Romanesque churches

Cologne’s twelve Romanesque churches are among the densest concentration of pre-Gothic ecclesiastical architecture in Europe, built between the tenth and the thirteenth centuries when Cologne was one of the largest and wealthiest cities north of the Alps. They are not relics or curiosities — they are the physical trace of a specific historical moment when this city was rich enough, ambitious enough, and sufficiently connected to the networks of medieval Christendom to build on this scale.

St. Gereon’s is the strangest of them. The decagonal nave is built over a late-Roman mausoleum, probably from the fourth century, which itself replaced an even earlier structure. The medieval builders didn’t demolish the Roman walls — they incorporated them, adding a dome of unusual size over the ancient foundations. The result is a building that represents four distinct architectural eras in a single structure: late Roman, early Christian, Romanesque, and Gothic, all compressed into one body. The Romanesque crossing tower and the Roman foundation below it communicate directly through the stone, which is why the building feels unlike anything else in the city.

Gross St. Martin, at the edge of the Old Town above the Rhine, served as the landmark for river traffic approaching from the south for eight centuries. Its trefoil tower — three apses intersecting at the base — is visible from the water long before the Cathedral comes into view, and in the medieval period it was the first Cologne skyline element that arriving merchants would see.

The Cathedral and the relics

Construction on Cologne Cathedral began in 1248. It did not end until 1880. For 632 years, the building was continuously under construction, partially under construction, or simply stopped — a construction halt from 1473 lasted over three centuries. By the nineteenth century, the unfinished south tower, capped with a medieval crane that had been sitting in place since the fifteenth century, had become a symbol of German incompletion, a recurring motif in German Romantic literature, and eventually a rallying point for the unified German nationalist movement. The completion of the Cathedral in 1880 was a statement about the German Empire that had been declared in 1871. The building was finished in sixty years of coordinated effort after standing incomplete for four hundred. It opened as the tallest building in the world, a title it held for four years until the Washington Monument surpassed it.

The Cathedral was built to hold the Shrine of the Three Kings — a gilded reliquary containing what the church claimed were the bones of the biblical Magi, the Three Wise Men who attended the birth of Christ. Archbishop Rainald of Dassel acquired the relics in Milan in 1164 and brought them to Cologne, understanding immediately that possessing the remains of the Magi would make Cologne a pilgrimage destination of the first order. He was right. Within decades, pilgrims were arriving from across Europe, and the income from the pilgrim trade funded the construction that would eventually produce one of the largest Gothic cathedrals in the world. The reliquary itself — a triple-arched golden box roughly two meters long, covered in enamel, gemstones, and figures of the Apostles — is the most elaborate medieval goldsmith’s work in existence. It is displayed inside the Cathedral behind the high altar.

The Hanseatic city

Cologne was one of the founding members of the Hanseatic League, the commercial and defensive alliance of northern European trading cities that dominated Baltic and North Sea trade from the thirteenth to the seventeenth century. Its position on the Rhine gave it natural advantages: the river ran to the North Sea, connecting Cologne to the English wool trade, the Flemish cloth industry, and the Rhine wine trade simultaneously. Cologne merchants controlled significant portions of the trade flowing between England and the continent, and the city maintained a permanent trading post in London — the Steelyard, the Stalhof — from the thirteenth century until 1598, when Queen Elizabeth I expelled the Hanseatic merchants.

The wealth of the Hanseatic period produced the civic institutions that still define the city. The city hall — the Rathaus — was built above the Roman Praetorium, a deliberate act of continuity with ancient civic authority. The guild system organized the trades. The city negotiated a degree of political independence from the Archbishop that made it effectively a free imperial city from 1475, despite the Archbishop remaining nominally in authority. When the Archbishop finally moved his seat to Bonn in 1288 after the Battle of Worringen — in which the citizens of Cologne supported the Count of Berg against the Archbishop — the city’s autonomy was established in practice for the next five centuries.

The water

Eau de Cologne was created in Cologne by Johann Maria Farina in 1709. Farina was an Italian-born merchant who had settled in the city and named his fragrance after his adopted home. The formula — a blend of citrus oils, herbs, and alcohol — was originally sold as a medicine, a general-purpose remedy for headaches, digestion, and skin complaints. It became famous throughout Europe when Napoleon’s troops discovered it during the French occupation of the city in the early nineteenth century and sent bottles home. The name became a generic term for a style of light fragrance, and Cologne’s connection to the perfume industry has persisted: the city still has multiple Farina family enterprises and competing heritage claims, and the Kölnisch Wasser tradition remains commercially active three centuries after its invention.

The firebombing

Between May 1942 and March 1945, Cologne was bombed 262 times by the Royal Air Force and the United States Army Air Forces. The raid of May 30–31, 1942 — Operation Millennium — was the first thousand-bomber raid in history, intended by Arthur Harris to demonstrate to the British War Cabinet that strategic bombing could destroy a German city. Cologne was chosen because it was large, close to England, and navigable by following the Rhine at night. Approximately 15,000 houses were destroyed, 469 people were killed, and 45,000 were left homeless in a single night. By the end of the war, estimates suggest that 90 percent of the city center had been destroyed. The population had fallen from 770,000 in 1939 to under 40,000 in 1945 — the rest had fled, been killed, or been taken prisoner.

The Cathedral survived. It was hit repeatedly — fourteen times by aerial bombs — but the structure did not collapse. The twin spires were visible from the air and helped bomber crews orient themselves on the Rhine, so the Cathedral became a navigational landmark even as the city around it was destroyed. The spires were left standing because destroying them would have made the river harder to find. In the postwar period, the ruined city was deliberately rebuilt with the Cathedral at its center, and the clearing of rubble was organized around preserving the cathedral precincts first. The current streetscape of central Cologne — wide pedestrian zones, low-rise surroundings, the cathedral occupying an unusually large plaza — is in part a consequence of rebuilding after near-total destruction.

Kölsch and the Köbes

Kölsch is a pale, top-fermented ale brewed exclusively in the Cologne region under a geographical indication that has been protected since 1997. By EU law, Kölsch can only be produced within 50 kilometers of Cologne’s city hall. It is served in 200-milliliter cylindrical glasses called Stangen — the smallest beer format in Germany — and brought to tables in wooden circular trays called Kransen by waiters called Köbes. The Köbes tradition — white jacket, blue apron, money pouch — has been documented in Cologne since at least the nineteenth century, and the service ritual around Kölsch is specific: the Köbes replaces an empty glass without being asked, and the signal that you are finished is placing your beer mat on top of the glass. The city has a running argument with Düsseldorf, 45 kilometers to the north, over the relative merits of Kölsch versus Altbier, the darker Düsseldorf style, that has been conducted in bars and newspaper columns for at least a century without resolution.
