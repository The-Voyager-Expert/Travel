The last island on the road, ninety miles from Cuba and closer to Havana than to Miami — a place that has always attracted people running toward something or away from it.

The end of the road

US Route 1 begins in Fort Kent, Maine, on the Canadian border, and runs 2,369 miles south through every version of the American East Coast — industrial New England, the mid-Atlantic sprawl, the Carolinas, the long Florida peninsula — until the land runs out. The road doesn't end at the tip of Florida. It keeps going, hopping from island to island across forty-two bridges and 113 miles of open water until it reaches a small coral island that sits closer to a foreign capital than to the nearest American city of any size. Mile Marker Zero is on the corner of Whitehead and Fleming Streets. There is a sign. Tourists photograph it. And then the continent is done.

Key West has always been defined by this position — at the edge, at the end, hanging off the bottom of the country like a loose button. The island is two miles wide and four miles long. You can walk across it in forty minutes. You can bike around it in an hour. It sits in the Gulf of Mexico in water so shallow that the reef offshore was once the most dangerous stretch of coastline in the Western Hemisphere for shipping. That danger made the island rich.

The wrecking industry

Before Key West was a tourist destination, before it was a naval base, before Hemingway or Truman or Jimmy Buffett, it was a wrecking town. The reef that runs along the Florida Keys tore the hulls out of ships that were carrying cargo between New Orleans, Havana, and the ports of the eastern seaboard. When a ship went down, the wreckers of Key West raced out in small boats to salvage whatever they could — cotton, timber, spices, furniture, sometimes entire cargoes of manufactured goods from Europe.

This was legal. It was also extraordinarily profitable. By the 1850s, Key West was the wealthiest city per capita in the United States, and the source of that wealth was other people's misfortune. The grand houses along Caroline and Simonton Streets were built with wrecking money. The captains who built them furnished them with salvaged mahogany and crystal and silverware that had been bound for New Orleans drawing rooms. The architecture you see in Old Town — those high-ceilinged wooden houses with widow's walks and deep porches — is wrecking-era architecture, built by men who watched the horizon for sails in distress.

By the 1850s, Key West was the wealthiest city per capita in the United States, and the source of that wealth was other people's misfortune.

When the federal government built lighthouses along the reef, the wrecking industry died. Key West pivoted to cigar manufacturing — Cuban tobacco rollers emigrated across the strait and established factories that employed thousands — and then to sponge diving, and then to shrimping, and then to the Navy, and then to tourism. The island has always been good at reinvention, mostly because it has had no choice.

Hemingway's island

Ernest Hemingway arrived in Key West in 1928 and stayed, on and off, until 1939. The house on Whitehead Street where he lived with his second wife Pauline is now a museum, and it is one of those rare literary landmarks that actually tells you something about the writer. The house is handsome — Spanish Colonial, with deep shuttered windows and a second-floor writing studio above the carriage house — and it sits behind a brick wall in a garden full of tropical plants and roughly sixty cats.

The cats are the famous part. Hemingway was given a white six-toed cat by a ship's captain, and the descendants of that cat still live on the property. About half of them carry the polydactyl gene. They sleep on the furniture, sprawl across the pathways, and ignore visitors with the practiced indifference of animals who have been photographed ten thousand times. The museum protects them by name, and each one is named after a famous person. You may find Truman Capote asleep on Hemingway's bed.

What matters more than the cats is what Hemingway wrote here. A Farewell to Arms was finished in this house. To Have and Have Not — his only novel set in the United States — takes place in Key West and draws directly from the island's Depression-era desperation and its proximity to Cuba. For Whom the Bell Tolls was written in the upstairs studio. The Snows of Kilimanjaro and The Short Happy Life of Francis Macomber — two of the best short stories in American literature — were written here too. The decade Hemingway spent on this island was the most productive of his life.

The Conch Republic

On April 23, 1982, the mayor of Key West declared the island's independence from the United States, announced the formation of the Conch Republic, symbolically declared war on the US by breaking a loaf of Cuban bread over the head of a man in a Navy uniform, surrendered one minute later, and applied for one billion dollars in foreign aid.

The background is less absurd than the event. The US Border Patrol had set up a checkpoint on US 1 at Florida City — the last point on the mainland before the Keys — and was stopping every car leaving the Keys to check for drugs and undocumented immigrants. The checkpoint created seventeen-mile traffic jams. Tourists stopped coming. The local economy, which runs on tourism, started to collapse. The city filed an injunction. The courts refused it. So Key West seceded.

The stunt worked. The checkpoint came down. The Conch Republic remains a running joke with real teeth — the island still celebrates Independence Day on April 23, issues its own passports (which have reportedly been accepted at some border crossings, though this is disputed), and flies the Conch Republic flag alongside the American one. The message underneath the comedy is genuine: Key West has always considered itself a place apart, and it does not appreciate being told otherwise by people who live on the mainland.

The cemetery in the middle of town

Most cities put their cemeteries on the outskirts. Key West put its cemetery in the center of the island, on the highest ground available, and even that wasn't enough. The water table in Key West sits about eighteen inches below the surface. You cannot dig a conventional grave here without hitting water. So the dead are buried above ground, in concrete vaults and coral-stone crypts that stack along narrow lanes like a miniature city of their own.

The Key West Cemetery is not a somber place. It is, like the island itself, irreverent. One headstone reads "I Told You I Was Sick." Another says "I'm Just Resting My Eyes." The monument to the sailors who died on the USS Maine — the explosion that triggered the Spanish-American War in 1898 — stands near the center, a reminder that Key West was the nearest American port to Havana and played a direct role in that conflict. The cemetery is free, open during daylight, and almost entirely unvisited by the crowds on Duval Street three blocks away.

Truman's retreat

Harry Truman first came to Key West in 1946 on the advice of his doctor, who told the president he needed rest. Truman came back ten more times during his presidency and spent a total of 175 days on the island — nearly six months of his administration conducted from a converted officer's quarters on the naval base. He called it the Little White House, and he meant it. He made decisions here. He held meetings. He played poker with his staff on the south porch in the evenings and swam at the naval beach in the mornings.

The Little White House is now a museum, and it is one of the better presidential historic sites in the country because it feels lived-in rather than enshrined. Truman's poker table is still set up. His piano is in the living room. The place gives you a sense of a man who genuinely liked being here — who chose this island, repeatedly, over Camp David and every other retreat available to a sitting president. Eisenhower used it too, recovering here after a heart attack. Kennedy held meetings here during the Cuban Missile Crisis, which makes geographic sense — Cuba is visible from Key West on a clear day, or close to it.

Fort Zachary Taylor and the war that didn't reach here

Fort Zachary Taylor sits at the southwestern tip of the island, behind what is generally considered the best beach in Key West. The fort was built in the 1840s and finished just before the Civil War, and its position in that war is one of the stranger facts in American military history. Key West is in Florida. Florida seceded. But the Union held Fort Taylor throughout the entire war, and the Confederacy never took it.

This mattered enormously. Fort Taylor gave the Union a deep-water port at the southern end of the Confederate coastline, and the Navy used it to intercept blockade runners — the fast ships that carried cotton out of Southern ports and brought European weapons and supplies back in. The fort's guns controlled the shipping lanes between the Gulf of Mexico and the Atlantic. Holding Key West didn't win the war, but losing it would have made the Union blockade far less effective.

Today the fort is a state park, and most visitors come for the beach rather than the history. The moat is still there. The walls are still there. The casemates where Union soldiers watched the water for Confederate sails are cool and dark and smell like old coral. The beach on the other side is a narrow crescent of coarse sand with water so clear you can see the bottom at twenty feet.

Sunset at Mallory Square

Every evening, about two hours before sunset, the western waterfront at Mallory Square fills with people. This has been happening since the 1960s, when the hippie counterculture drifted south and found the end of the road. Street performers set up — jugglers, fire eaters, tightrope walkers, a man who has trained housecats to jump through hoops. Vendors sell conch fritters and key lime pie on sticks. And then the crowd faces west and watches the sun drop into the Gulf of Mexico.

The sunset celebration is not a formal event. Nobody organizes it. There is no start time and no end time. People show up, the performers do their acts, the sun goes down, and everyone applauds. It is one of those civic rituals that sounds unbearable in description and turns out to be genuinely pleasant in practice — partly because the sunset over open water from this angle is reliably spectacular, and partly because the crowd is in a good mood. You are at the end of the continent. The day is over. The water is warm. There is nothing south of here but ocean and Cuba.

That proximity to Cuba has shaped everything about Key West — its food, its architecture, its cigar industry, its politics, its sense of itself as a border town between the United States and the Caribbean. The island is American, but it is not entirely mainland American. The rhythms are different. The vegetation is tropical. The roosters that wander Old Town crowing at all hours are descended from Cuban fighting birds. The Cuban coffee at the windows on Duval Street is strong enough to stand a spoon in.

You reach Key West by driving over water for two hours, and when you arrive you understand why the drive exists — not as an obstacle but as a transition. The Overseas Highway strips away the mainland by degrees, island by island, bridge by bridge, until the place you arrive at feels genuinely different from the place you left. That is the point. It has always been the point.
