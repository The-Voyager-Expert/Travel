A city that tore the pictures off its own church walls five hundred years ago and never really put the decoration back — and that plainness, not the money, is the real story.

The sermon that emptied a church

Stand in the Grossmünster, the twin-towered church on the east bank of the Limmat, and the first thing you notice is what isn't there. No gold, no frescoes, no crowd of saints looking down at you — just whitewashed stone, clear glass, a few austere modern windows, and a lot of quiet daylight. That emptiness is not neglect. It's a decision, and it was made on purpose almost exactly five hundred years ago by a priest named Huldrych Zwingli, who arrived here on New Year's Day 1519 and started, of all things, by refusing to preach the assigned Sunday readings. Instead he began reading straight through the Gospel of Matthew from the beginning, chapter by chapter, explaining it as he went — a small act of defiance that turned out to be the opening move of the Swiss Reformation.

The moment that actually broke things open, though, is almost too good to be a real historical event. During Lent in 1522, a group of Zwingli's supporters gathered at a printer's house and, in open violation of the Church's fasting rules, cut up and ate two smoked sausages. Zwingli didn't eat one himself, but he stood up for the people who did, in a sermon that argued Scripture nowhere commands fasting and the Church had no business inventing rules God never gave. Historians call it, without irony, the Affair of the Sausages. It is genuinely considered the starting gun of the Reformation in Switzerland — proof that this whole civilization-altering rupture in European Christianity can trace its local ignition to a plate of cold cuts.

What followed was fast and thorough. By 1524, Zwingli's reformers had stripped the Grossmünster's organ out, removed the statuary, whitewashed the walls, and banned music from services entirely — a purge so complete that Zurich's version of Protestantism ended up more austere than Luther's in Germany. That's the wall you're looking at when you walk into the Grossmünster today. It isn't unfinished. The plainness itself is the monument.

Guilds instead of nobles

Walk the Limmatquai, the row of buildings on the river's edge just across from the Grossmünster, and you're looking at Zurich's other founding decision: in 1336, a guildmaster named Rudolf Brun led what's remembered as the Guild Revolution, restructuring city government so political power ran through the trade guilds — the carpenters, the blacksmiths, the vintners, the merchants — rather than through hereditary nobility. Fourteen guildhalls eventually lined this stretch of riverbank, and several still stand: the Zunfthaus zur Zimmerleuten (carpenters), the Zunfthaus zur Meisen, the Zunfthaus zur Saffran. Today most of them are restaurants, which means you can order dinner in a building whose entire reason for existing was to keep an aristocracy from forming.

Zurich never had a royal court to build a palace for. It had guildhalls, and it still eats dinner in them.

This matters more than it sounds like it should, because it explains something about how the city carries itself now. There's no ruling dynasty's monument at the center of Zurich, no palace anyone's meant to be impressed by. The old town's grandest buildings are trade halls. Swiss identity generally, and Zurich's in particular, runs on the idea that status comes from what you do and what you're good at, not from what family you were born into — and that idea is six hundred years old and built directly into the architecture in front of you.

Money that doesn't announce itself

Walk down Bahnhofstrasse — the wide, tram-lined shopping boulevard that runs from the main station to the lake — and you will not see much that looks like what "the wealthiest street in the world" is supposed to look like. There's no soaring skyline, no gilded facades. The banking is happening below your feet: the private bank vaults are said to run in tiers beneath the street, and the storefronts above them are restrained almost to the point of anonymity. James Joyce, who lived in Zurich for years, is supposed to have said the street was clean enough to lick vegetable soup off. He wasn't wrong, and the cleanliness and the money are the same phenomenon.

Swiss bank secrecy has real roots — as far back as 1713 in Geneva, formalized into federal criminal law in 1934 — and for most of the twentieth century it made Zurich one of the few places on earth where money could sit without anyone asking where it came from. That protection sheltered refugees hiding savings from the Nazis and, less admirably, Nazi loot and dictators' fortunes, in the same vaults, under the same law. Client confidentiality as an absolute was only formally retired in 2018, when Switzerland joined the international automatic-exchange-of-information standard. But the culture the secrecy built — wealth that prefers to be invisible rather than displayed — didn't retire with the law. That's why Bahnhofstrasse still reads as understated rather than showy: the whole point, for three hundred years, was never to look like anything at all.

The neutral country's escape hatch

Switzerland stayed out of the First World War, and that neutrality made Zurich, almost by accident, one of the only places in Europe where refugees, dissidents, and deserters from every side of the fighting could simply show up and be left alone. Lenin lived in exile a few streets from where you're probably standing in the old town, plotting a revolution he'd take back to Russia in 1917. And on February 5, 1916, at Spiegelgasse 1 — a five-minute walk from the Grossmünster — a handful of those refugee artists, led by the poet Hugo Ball and performer Emmy Hennings, opened a small nightclub called Cabaret Voltaire.

It only ran for a few months, but what happened inside it was Dada: sound poetry, simultaneous readings in three languages at once, masks, noise, deliberate nonsense, all of it aimed at a Europe that had just spent two years proving its "rational" civilization could produce industrialized slaughter. Ball read an early version of the Dada Manifesto aloud there in July 1916. If reasoned, orderly society had led straight to the trenches, Dada's answer was to refuse reason altogether. It is genuinely strange that this happened in Zurich of all cities — the most buttoned-up, guild-governed, bank-vaulted town on the continent — but that's exactly the point. Dada needed a safe, quiet, neutral place to detonate from, and Zurich was the only one available. The building is still there, now a small museum, and it's worth the five minutes even though there's not much to see: the idea is the whole exhibit.

Water, and what the Swiss do with it

Follow the Limmat south out of the old town and it opens into Lake Zurich, and the lake is not scenery here — it's closer to a civic religion. On a warm day the lakeside promenades and the Seebad bathing platforms fill with people who work in banks and insurance towers on their lunch break, in the water, no fuss made about it. The Swiss relationship to nature isn't wilderness worship; it's closer to maintenance. The lake is clean enough to swim in because keeping it that way was treated as an engineering problem worth solving, the same unglamorous competence that keeps the trains on time and the streets spotless. There's no separation here between "the pretty part of the city" and "the part where people actually live" — the lake is both, every day, for everyone.

Two old towns, one river apart

The Niederdorf, the tangle of narrow lanes on the Grossmünster side of the river, is Zurich loosening its collar: bars, small restaurants, students, a little noise after dark. Cross the river to the Fraumünster side and the streets go quiet and orderly again, guildhalls and church spires and very little resembling nightlife. That contrast isn't an accident of city planning — it's the same duality that shows up everywhere else here, austere Reformed order on one register and something looser and more human running just underneath it, a five-minute walk away. The Fraumünster itself carries its own quieter footnote: its stained-glass windows, several designed by Marc Chagall in the 1970s, are some of the only unapologetically colorful, decorative artwork allowed into a major Reformed church in this city — a small crack in five hundred years of plainness, and worth the detour inside just to see it.

Joyce's second home

James Joyce first came to Zurich in 1915, fleeing a Trieste emptying out for the First World War, and he lived here until 1919, writing much of Ulysses in rented rooms around the old town. He came back in late 1940, this time fleeing the Nazi occupation of France, and he died here in January 1941 after ulcer surgery, quickly enough that his family barely had time to process it. He's buried in Fluntern Cemetery on the wooded hill above the city, near the zoo, under a statue of himself seated with a book and a cane, legs crossed, looking faintly amused. His wife Nora and son Giorgio are buried beside him. It's a strange kind of tribute — an Irish writer who spent barely a combined six years of his life in this city ended up choosing it, twice, as the place to disappear into when the rest of Europe was on fire. That should tell you something about what kind of refuge Zurich has quietly offered, across several different centuries, to people the rest of the continent had no room for.

What to carry with you

Every thread in this city runs back to the same instinct: strip away the decoration and keep only what's actually load-bearing. Zwingli whitewashed the church walls because he thought the ornament was a lie standing between people and what mattered. The guilds tore down the idea of inherited nobility and replaced it with craft. The bankers built a fortune on being invisible rather than impressive. Even Dada, which looks like the one exception — pure noise, pure decoration for its own sake — was actually the same move turned inside out: stripping away the pretense that European "reason" meant anything, using nonsense to get at a truth orderly language couldn't reach anymore.

So when you walk Bahnhofstrasse and it looks like just a nice shopping street, or you stand in the Grossmünster and it looks unfinished, or a banker in a plain grey suit rides the tram next to you and gives no sign of what he's worth — you're not missing the good part. The plainness is the message. Zurich has never needed to look like what it is. It just needs to be it, quietly, and let you notice or not. Most people don't. You now will.
