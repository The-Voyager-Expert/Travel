A city carved into desert cliffs by a people who controlled the world's most lucrative trade routes — and then disappeared so completely that the Western world forgot they had existed.

The Siq and what happens to you in it

You enter Petra through the Siq — a narrow gorge that cuts through sandstone cliffs for about 1.2 kilometers. The walls rise to 80 meters in places and press to within 2 meters of each other at the narrowest points. The rock is layered in red and ochre and purple, and the colors shift as the gorge turns and the light changes. The path follows a water channel the Nabataeans cut into the rock nearly two thousand years ago, and the horse-drawn carriages that come through in the mornings rattle the walls in a way that makes you grateful for the moments when they're gone and you can hear only the sound of your own feet on stone.

The Siq is not merely an entrance. It is a deliberate compression. The Nabataeans, who built Petra, were engineers and merchants who thought architecturally about how space makes people feel. A visitor arriving through the Siq was being processed — stripped of the desert's openness, funneled through increasing darkness and tightening rock — so that what came at the end would land with maximum force. It does. Every time. The Treasury reveals itself through a crack in the stone, rose-red and geometrically precise and impossibly large, and even if you have seen the photographs ten thousand times, the actual moment of seeing it stops you. That is not an accident. The Nabataeans engineered it.

Who built this and why

The Nabataeans were an Arab people, nomadic in origin, who by the fourth century BCE had settled in the Wadi Musa valley and established what would become one of the most strategically placed cities in the ancient world. Petra sat at the intersection of the trade routes that moved incense from southern Arabia to the Mediterranean, spices from India through the Gulf to the Levant, and luxury goods across the ancient world's most lucrative supply chains. Frankincense and myrrh — the substances that were, in the ancient economy, roughly as valuable per weight as oil is now — passed through Petra in convoys of thousands of camels, and the Nabataeans taxed every one of them.

This wealth was not spent on war, which is unusual in the ancient world. The Nabataeans fought when they had to — they repelled two Seleucid sieges and frustrated Alexander the Great's successors — but their primary investment was infrastructure. They cut channels and cisterns throughout the surrounding mountains to capture winter rains and route them into Petra, creating a water system sophisticated enough to support a city of perhaps 30,000 people in a desert that receives about 150 millimeters of rain per year. They developed a distinctive style of thin-walled pottery so fine that the Romans, who conquered most of the known world, could not figure out how to replicate it. They created a script, derived from Aramaic, that became the ancestor of modern Arabic.

The Nabataeans were the first people in history to get rich off the fact that everyone else needed things that came from far away.

What they built in Petra — the monuments you walk among — is only a part of the city. The tombs and temples carved into the cliff faces are the imperishable portion. The city itself, built of mud brick and wood in the valley below, has largely returned to the ground. You are visiting the parts built to last forever and missing the parts where people actually lived.

The Treasury and what it actually is

Al-Khazneh — the Treasury — is the most photographed monument in Petra and almost certainly not what most visitors think it is. The name comes from a Bedouin legend that an Egyptian pharaoh hid treasure inside the large stone urn at the top of the facade, which is why the urn is pocked with bullet holes from centuries of hopeful shooting. There is no treasure inside. There never was. The building is a tomb, almost certainly for a Nabataean king, carved sometime in the first century BCE or CE.

Look at the facade carefully when you arrive. It is divided into two levels. The lower level is a Classical portico with six Corinthian columns — a design borrowed directly from Hellenistic architecture, because the Nabataeans were sophisticated enough to know that the visual language of Greek monumental buildings communicated power to the Mediterranean world that their trade network connected them to. The upper level is more inventive: a broken pediment, a central tholos, figures carved into the niches that may represent Isis, the Dioscuri, Amazons, or Nabataean deities depending on which scholar you ask. No one is entirely sure. The inscriptions that might have clarified who built it and why have not survived.

The interior is three empty rectangular chambers cut into the rock. No decoration, no sarcophagi, no indication of use beyond burial. Stand in the doorway and look in, then look back at the facade. The Nabataeans put all of their architectural energy on the outside — the face that spoke to the living, the face that announced who was buried here and what they had been worth. The inside was for the dead, and the dead don't need ornament.

Beyond the Treasury: the city most visitors don't see

The Treasury stops most visitors. They take the photographs, buy water from the vendors who have positioned themselves exactly where the light is best, and within an hour are retracing their steps through the Siq. This is a significant error. The Treasury is the introduction, not the destination.

Past the Treasury, the gorge opens into a broad valley and Petra reveals itself as a city of hundreds of monuments: the Street of Facades, where rows of smaller tombs are cut into the cliff like an apartment building of the dead; the Roman Colonnaded Street, where you are walking on paving stones laid in the second century CE over the earlier Nabataean road; the Great Temple complex, one of the largest structures in Petra and still being excavated by archaeologists from Brown University who have been working here for decades; the Royal Tombs carved high into the cliff face, their scale visible only because the entire cliff is the canvas.

Go to the Monastery. It requires a climb of 800 steps cut into the rock — about 45 minutes from the valley floor, depending on your pace — but it is twice the size of the Treasury and sees a fraction of the visitors. The facade is simpler, more massive, more assured. It was probably a temple, possibly converted to a church during the Byzantine period (a cross is carved into the back wall of the interior chamber). At the top of the climb, just before the Monastery comes into view, there is a narrow terrace with a view west over the Wadi Araba toward Israel. On a clear day you can see the Dead Sea. Stop here before you go to the building. The landscape tells you something the building cannot.

Rome and what happened next

In 106 CE, the Roman Emperor Trajan annexed the Nabataean kingdom without a battle. The last Nabataean king, Rabbel II, had died, and his heirs accepted Roman rule rather than fight an empire they could not defeat. Petra became the capital of the new Roman province of Arabia Petraea. The Romans built the colonnade, the nymphaeum, the temples they always built, and the city continued to prosper for another century under the Pax Romana.

Then the trade routes shifted. In the third century CE, Palmyra in Syria emerged as a rival trading hub, and the roads that had once funneled goods through Petra began routing around it. The Byzantine period brought churches — one was built inside the Urn Tomb, another excavated in the 1990s with an extraordinary mosaic floor, still visible if you find the site — but the economic logic that had made Petra great was gone. By the seventh century, the city was largely abandoned. The earthquake of 363 CE had destroyed much of the Roman-period construction. The Byzantine community that remained retreated to the upper reaches of the mountains. The Crusaders built a small castle here in the twelfth century and then they left too.

The Western world forgot Petra existed for roughly six centuries. It had to be rediscovered by a Swiss traveler who disguised himself as a Muslim pilgrim to get in.

In 1812, Johann Ludwig Burckhardt — a Swiss traveler and scholar who had spent years in the Arab world learning Arabic and Islamic custom — persuaded a local guide to take him to a site the Bedouin called Wadi Musa, by claiming he wanted to make a sacrifice at the tomb of Aaron on the mountain above the valley. He was the first Westerner documented to have entered Petra in the modern era. His account brought the site to European attention and began the process of archaeological investigation that has never stopped. About 85 percent of Petra is still unexcavated. Archaeologists believe there are major structures under the valley floor that have never been seen since the Byzantine period.

The Bedouin and what you should understand about them

The Bdoul tribe of Bedouin have lived in the Petra basin for centuries, some of them in the caves and rock-cut chambers of the ancient city itself. In the 1980s, the Jordanian government resettled most of the Bdoul in a purpose-built village called Umm Sayhoun on the plateau above Petra, as part of the process of making the site a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a national park. A small number refused to leave and still live in the caves. Most of the young men you encounter in Petra — selling tea from carved-out niches, offering horse rides, running the small restaurants at the top of the Monastery climb — are Bdoul, people whose families have lived here for generations and who now operate within the tourist economy that replaced their traditional pastoral one.

The tea sellers in the niches along the main trail are worth stopping for. Not for the tea specifically, though it's good. For the conversation, if you slow down enough to have one. The Bdoul know things about this landscape that archaeologists don't — where the water channels go, which caves connect to which, where the unexplored sections are. They also know, with some bitterness, that the world showed up to look at a city built by people who lived here two thousand years ago and pays considerably less attention to the people who live here now.

What to carry into the Siq

Petra by Night is a separate ticketed event, three times a week, in which you walk the Siq and sit before the Treasury by the light of several hundred candles while a Bedouin musician plays. It is not an archaeological experience — it is theatrical and occasionally crowded. Go anyway. Sitting on the sand in front of the Treasury at night, with the candle flames moving and the rose-red rock disappearing into darkness at the edges, you understand something the daylight visit doesn't give you: this city was always about the drama of revelation. The Nabataeans built the Siq to compress you into darkness so that light would feel like a gift. In the day, you can see how they did it. At night, it just works on you.

Here is the thing to carry with you through the Siq: the Nabataeans are the most successful people you have probably never heard of. They got rich by understanding that the world needed things that traveled, and that the person who controlled the road between supply and demand could name their price. They turned that understanding into a city that outlasted their civilization by two thousand years. Their writing became the alphabet that a billion people use today. Their water engineering kept a city alive in a desert for five centuries. And then they vanished so completely from historical memory that the Western world forgot they had existed, and had to rediscover their capital in 1812 as though finding it for the first time. Once you know that story, the Treasury stops being a beautiful photograph and starts being evidence of something: that the most important civilizations in history are not always the ones history remembered to write down.
