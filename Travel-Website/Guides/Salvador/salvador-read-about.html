Brazil's first capital was also its largest slave port — and the city that emerged from that wound became the most African place in the Americas.

The city the Portuguese built on someone else's labor

Salvador was the first capital of colonial Brazil, founded in 1549 on a bluff above the Bay of All Saints — Baía de Todos os Santos — where the Portuguese crown built a walled administrative city on the high ground and a commercial port below. For two centuries it was the center of the Portuguese colonial empire in South America, richer than anything in the Caribbean, shipping sugar to Europe in quantities that made the plantation owners of Bahia among the wealthiest people on earth.

That wealth was produced entirely by enslaved Africans. Brazil was the largest importer of enslaved people in the Atlantic world — somewhere between four and five million people were brought here between the sixteenth and nineteenth centuries, roughly forty percent of the entire transatlantic trade. A large portion passed through or stayed in Bahia. The plantation economy required constant replenishment because the conditions of sugar production killed people fast. Salvador was not a waystation in this system. It was the system's capital.

This is the fact on which everything else in Salvador rests. The city's food, its music, its religion, its dance, its language — all of it bears the imprint of people who were brought here against their will, stripped of their names and language, and yet managed to preserve and transmit a culture that now defines an entire region of Brazil.

The Pelourinho: what the square was for

The Pelourinho is the name for both the historic upper city and the central square at its heart. The word means pillory. This was where enslaved people were publicly punished, and the name has never been changed, which tells you something about how Salvador holds its history — not hidden, not prettified, just present.

Walk through the Pelourinho today and you're moving through one of the most intact colonial-era urban districts in the Americas. The streets are steep cobblestone, lined with buildings in ocher and rose and faded blue, many of them four or five stories with wrought-iron balconies. The churches are enormous — there are said to be more than three hundred churches in Salvador, a number that reflects not piety alone but the colonial imperative to plant a cross on every visible hill in the conquered territory.

The gold inside the Church of São Francisco didn't come from a mine. It came from the fields. Every gilded surface has a labor history.

The Church of São Francisco is the most extraordinary of them. The interior is sheathed in carved Brazilian cedar, covered in hundreds of kilograms of gold leaf applied by enslaved craftsmen in the early eighteenth century. It is one of the most spectacular interiors in the Americas, and it is genuinely difficult to know how to receive it: the craftsmanship is breathtaking, and the circumstances in which it was produced were monstrous. Standing in that nave, both things are true at once. Salvador doesn't resolve this tension for you. It just holds both facts in the air and waits.

Candomblé and the religion that survived in disguise

The Portuguese colonial administration required enslaved Africans to be baptized as Catholics. What actually happened is one of the most extraordinary acts of cultural survival in human history. The Yoruba, Fon, and Bantu peoples brought to Bahia were not Christian, and when they were forced to adopt the outward forms of Catholicism, they placed their own orixás — the divine forces of the Yoruba religious universe — behind the faces of Catholic saints. Yemanjá, goddess of the sea, moved behind the Virgin Mary. Oxalá, the sky father, stood behind Jesus. The Portuguese thought they were witnessing conversion. What they were actually witnessing was preservation.

This religion is called Candomblé, and Salvador is its world capital. It is not a folk superstition. It is a sophisticated theological and ritual system with a vast oral literature, an elaborate cosmology, and a priesthood — the mães de santo and pais de santo, mothers and fathers of saints — who train for years before being initiated. The terreiros where ceremonies are held are active religious communities, not tourist attractions, and they should be treated accordingly. If you're invited to attend a ceremony, go with respect and dress conservatively. If you're not, don't try to find one to crash.

The orixás are everywhere in Salvador even outside the terreiros. Yemanjá appears in blue and white offerings floated out to sea each February. Exu, the trickster at the crossroads, has his shrines at intersections. On the streets of the Pelourinho, vendors sell the beaded necklaces whose colors correspond to each orixá — yellow for Oxum, red and black for Exu, white for Oxalá. The city is one large religious landscape if you know how to read it.

Capoeira: the fight that looked like a dance

In the Pelourinho on Tuesday nights, and in the squares and courtyards of the upper city most afternoons, you'll see capoeira — two people moving in a continuous fluid exchange of kicks, sweeps, and evasions, accompanied by the single-string berimbau and a small drum. It looks like dance because it was designed to look like dance.

Capoeira was developed by enslaved Africans in Brazil as a martial art disguised as performance. Under slavery, enslaved people were forbidden from practicing combat arts. The solution was to make the fight look like something else — to incorporate music and movement patterns that could be explained as religious ritual or entertainment while preserving the fighting techniques underneath. Whether the colonial authorities were actually deceived or simply chose not to notice is unclear. The technique worked either way.

After abolition, capoeira was banned in Brazil until 1932, when a mestro from Bahia named Mestre Bimba formalized it as a martial art and sport and began teaching it openly. His academy in Salvador was the first, and the city still considers itself the authentic home of the practice. The roda — the circle of players and musicians within which the fight/dance takes place — is one of the most purely beautiful things you'll watch in Brazil. The point isn't just the technique. It's the conversation between the two practitioners, which is what experienced players call it: a dialogue conducted in movement.

The sound the city makes

Salvador is a music city in a way that even Rio and São Paulo are not. The Bahian contribution to Brazilian popular music is so large that it's almost impossible to outline: axé, pagode, afrobeat, samba-reggae, forró, ijexá — and beneath all of it, the polyrhythmic drum tradition that came directly from West and Central Africa and was never lost.

The Olodum drum group, founded in the Pelourinho in 1979, is the most internationally famous expression of this. They play on the cobblestones of the Pelourinho every Tuesday night, and the sound — forty or fifty surdo and repique drums in interlocking rhythms, dense and physical — is something you feel in your chest as much as hear. Paul Simon recorded with them on The Rhythm of the Saints in 1990. Michael Jackson filmed a video here. Neither of these facts particularly impresses anyone in Salvador. The drums were here long before the visitors arrived, and they'll be here after.

Salvador's Carnaval has no floats, no costumes competition, no TV coverage to compete for. It's just two million people on the streets for six days, and the trio elétrico trucks moving through the crowd.

In February, Salvador hosts what it claims — with some justification — to be the largest street carnival in the world. Rio's Carnaval is famous for its televised Sambódromo parades and samba school competition. Salvador's is a different thing entirely: enormous sound trucks called trios elétricos moving through the streets with bands performing on top, and the crowd — millions of people — moving with them. The music is Bahian music. The best axé acts are household names in Brazil. For six days the city essentially stops being a city and becomes a single organism moving through its own streets.

The lower city and the bay

The Elevador Lacerda, the iron-and-concrete public lift that connects the upper and lower cities, has been running since 1873 — first hydraulic, then electric — and makes the seventy-two-meter descent in about twenty seconds. Step out at the bottom and you're in the Comércio, the old commercial district that runs along the water, and then, to the south, the Mercado Modelo in a nineteenth-century customs building where vendors sell everything from lacework to percussion instruments to Candomblé ritual items.

The Bay of All Saints is the largest bay in Brazil. On a clear morning the water is a deep blue-green and you can see the islands — Itaparica, the largest, is close enough to visit by ferry — and the shipping traffic that has moved through here for five centuries. The schooner tours that leave from the Mercado Modelo dock are worth doing for the view alone: the city from the water, with the upper city's colonial skyline visible above the bluff, looks like a painting that someone forgot to update for four hundred years.

What Bahia is trying to tell you

Tourists from Rio and São Paulo come to Salvador and often describe a feeling they can't quite name — something about the pace, the heat, the street food, the music. Baianos have a word for this quality of life: axé. In Candomblé theology, axé is the divine energy that flows through all living things, the life force that the orixás embody and that rituals channel. In everyday Bahian speech it has become something closer to vitality, to the force that makes things happen and people connect. It's the word that ends a ceremony, a song, a greeting.

What Salvador wants you to understand — the thing worth carrying with you when you leave — is that the most alive city in Brazil was built from the most brutal conditions in the Americas, and that what survived was not despite those conditions but through them. The food, the music, the religion, the martial art, the carnival — every one of these things is the record of people who were supposed to be erased and weren't. The Africans brought to Bahia didn't assimilate into colonial Brazil. They transformed it. That transformation is the city you're walking through. Once you see it that way, you see it in every drum pattern, every offering by the water, every gold saint's face in a colonial church that was paid for by the work of its worshippers' ancestors.

That's the city. That's what the heat and the music and the food are made of. Carry that with you.
