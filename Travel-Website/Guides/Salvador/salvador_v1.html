2. Igreja e Convento de São Francisco
↳ Built between 1708 and 1723, the interior is covered floor to ceiling with carved woodwork overlaid with roughly 800 kilograms of gold leaf — the largest gilded church interior in the Americas. The altarpiece and decorative panels merge Portuguese Baroque style with local craft traditions.

3. Igreja da Ordem Terceira de São Francisco
↳ The sandstone facade is the only example of Hispanic-American Churrigueresque carving in Brazil, dating to 1703. The ornate surface was discovered under plain plaster in 1936 after more than 200 years of concealment. The interior holds a small museum of sacred art and silver processional objects.

4. Pelourinho Historic Center
↳ 12 blocks of 17th-century Baroque and colonial architecture painted in saturated primary colors, recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The cultural heart of Afro-Brazilian life — capoeira circles, Candomblé drumming, and Baianas in white dress selling acarajé fill the squares daily.

1. Museu Afro-Brasileiro
↳ Founded in 1982 within the 18th-century Faculdade de Medicina building, the museum documents African and Afro-Brazilian culture through roughly 1200 objects — ceremonial items, Candomblé iconography, and 27 carved wooden panels by artist Carybé, each depicting one of the Orixás.

2. Elevador Lacerda
↳ Connects Cidade Alta at 72 meters elevation to Cidade Baixa and the waterfront. Inaugurated in 1873 as a steam-powered lift and electrified in 1906, the four-cabin system makes over 900000 trips annually. The 30-second descent offers an unobstructed view over the Baía de Todos os Santos.

3. Igreja da Conceição da Praia
↳ Patron Saint of Bahia's church on the Comércio waterfront, completed in 1765. Built with prefabricated stone carved and shipped from Portugal — the only prefabricated church in Brazil. The interior holds a polychrome statue of the Virgin and ex-voto offerings.

4. Forte de Santo Antônio da Barra
↳ Positioned at the mouth of the Baía de Todos os Santos where it meets the Atlantic, Brazil's oldest surviving military fortification on dry land, built in the early 17th century. The lighthouse — Farol da Barra — has been operational since 1698. The complex includes a nautical museum of bay history.

1. Igreja do Nosso Senhor do Bonfim
↳ The most revered church in Bahia, built in 1745. The Sala dos Milagres is lined with thousands of ex-voto offerings: wax body parts, photographs, crutches, and written prayers left by devotees. The Lavagem do Bonfim each January draws millions to watch Baianas wash the church steps.

2. Porto da Barra Beach
↳ A protected cove where the Baía de Todos os Santos meets the Atlantic at the tip of the Barra peninsula. The beach faces west, making it the most popular sunset spot in the city. Calm waters result from the bay opening on one side and the fort breakwater on the other.

↳ The Museum of Modern Art of Bahia occupies a 17th-century colonial sugar mill complex on the Baía de Todos os Santos waterfront. The compound preserves original mill machinery, a slave warehouse, and a chapel alongside contemporary Brazilian art exhibitions.

4. Dique do Tororó
↳ A Dutch-era reservoir built in the 17th century, encircled by a 3.5-kilometer public promenade. The lake holds eight large sculptures of the major Orixás commissioned in 1998 by artist Tatti Moreno — Iemanjá, Oxalá, Xangô, Oxum, and others — mapping Candomblé sacred geography onto a central public park.

🍮 Local Tastes
Acarajé
↳ Black-eyed pea fritters deep-fried in dendê palm oil, filled with vatapá, caruru, and dried shrimp. Sold by Baianas in white dress — rooted in Candomblé offerings to the Orixá Iansã.
📍 Largo do Pelourinho · Largo do Bonfim
Moqueca Baiana
↳ Fish or shrimp stew cooked in dendê palm oil with coconut milk, tomato, onion, and coriander. Served in a clay pot and distinct from the Espírito Santo version by the palm oil and coconut.
📍 Sorriso da Dadá · Amado
Vatapá
↳ A thick paste of stale bread, dried shrimp, peanuts, cashews, coconut milk, and dendê palm oil. Eaten with rice or as filling inside acarajé; a ceremonial offering to Xangô in Candomblé.
📍 Tempero da Dinha · SENAC Pelourinho
Caruru
↳ Okra dish cooked with shrimp, peanuts, cashews, and dendê palm oil. A ritual food in Candomblé, traditionally prepared for the feast of the twin saints Cosme and Damião.
📍 Tempero da Dinha · SENAC Pelourinho
Abará
↳ Black-eyed pea dough steamed inside a banana leaf — same base as acarajé but without frying. The banana-leaf technique was brought directly from West Africa to Bahia.
📍 Baianas in Pelourinho
Caldo de Feijão
↳ Black bean soup thickened to a dense broth, seasoned with garlic, onion, and dried meat. A street-food staple in bars and lanchonetes, served with farofa and hot sauce.

🌊 Two Geographies of the Same City

Salvador operates on two sacred geographies simultaneously. The Lavagem do Bonfim — the ritual washing of the church steps that draws millions each January — takes place at a Catholic church dedicated to Christ. But the ceremony belongs to Oxalá, the Candomblé Orixá of creation and the sky, whose energy the Baianas invoke through perfumed water. The church steps are the threshold of two religions at once, and neither disputes the other's presence.

The Dique do Tororó reservoir has been considered sacred to Oxum, the Orixá of freshwater and fertility, since before the colonial period. The eight Orixá sculptures installed in 1998 made explicit what practitioners already knew about the lake. A Dutch-engineered reservoir, built for colonial water supply, became a Candomblé altarpiece at the center of a modern city — functioning as both, simultaneously, without contradiction.
