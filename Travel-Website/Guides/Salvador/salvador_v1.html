Day 1 — Pelourinho
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 3 min · 🚕 2 min → Pelourinho Historic Center
1. Pelourinho Historic Center
↳ Salvador's UNESCO-listed historic center covers 12 blocks of 17th-century Baroque and colonial architecture painted in saturated primary colors. Once the commercial and administrative hub of the Atlantic slave trade, Pelourinho remains the cultural heart of Afro-Brazilian life — capoeira circles, Candomblé drumming, and Baianas in white dress selling acarajé fill the squares daily.
📖 Wikipedia: Pelourinho

2. Igreja e Convento de São Francisco
↳ Built between 1708 and 1723, the interior is covered floor to ceiling with carved woodwork overlaid with roughly 800 kilograms of gold leaf — the largest gilded church interior in the Americas. The main altarpiece and decorative panels show the merging of Portuguese Baroque style with local craft traditions.

3. Igreja da Ordem Terceira de São Francisco
↳ The sandstone facade is the only example of Hispanic-American Churrigueresque carving in Brazil, dating to 1703. The ornate surface was discovered hidden under plain plaster in 1936, having been covered for more than 200 years. The interior holds a small museum of sacred art and silver processional objects.

Day 2 — Museum, Elevator & Fort
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 4 min · 🚕 3 min → Museu Afro-Brasileiro
1. Museu Afro-Brasileiro
↳ Founded in 1982 within the 18th-century Faculdade de Medicina building, the museum documents African and Afro-Brazilian culture through roughly 1,200 objects: ceremonial items, Candomblé iconography, and a dedicated room of 27 carved wooden panels by artist Carybé, each depicting one of the Orixás. One of the most significant African diaspora museums in Latin America.
📖 Wikipedia: Afro-Brazilian Museum
🎟 museuafrobrasil.com.br

2. Elevador Lacerda
↳ Salvador's landmark public elevator connects Cidade Alta at 72 meters elevation to Cidade Baixa and the waterfront. Inaugurated in 1873 as a steam-powered lift and electrified in 1906, the four-cabin system makes over 900,000 trips annually. The 30-second descent offers an unobstructed panoramic view over the Baía de Todos os Santos.
📖 Wikipedia: Elevador Lacerda
🎟 transalvador.salvador.ba.gov.br

3. Forte de Santo Antônio da Barra
↳ Positioned at the mouth of the Baía de Todos os Santos where it meets the Atlantic, this is Brazil's oldest surviving military fortification on dry land, built in the early 17th century. The lighthouse within the fort — Farol da Barra — has been operational since 1698. The complex includes a nautical museum covering the history of the bay and early Portuguese colonial navigation.

Day 3 — Bonfim, MAM & Tororó
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 22 min → Igreja do Nosso Senhor do Bonfim
1. Igreja do Nosso Senhor do Bonfim
↳ The most revered church in Bahia, built in 1745. The entrance hall — the Sala dos Milagres — is lined with thousands of ex-voto offerings: wax body parts, photographs, crutches, and written prayers left by devotees who believed they received miracles. The Lavagem do Bonfim, held each January, draws millions of people to watch Baianas ritually wash the church steps with perfumed water.
📖 Wikipedia: Senhor do Bonfim Church
🎟 senhordobonfim.com.br

2. MAM Bahia / Solar do Unhão
↳ The Museum of Modern Art of Bahia occupies a 17th-century colonial sugar mill complex on the waterfront of the Baía de Todos os Santos. The compound preserves the original mill machinery, a slave warehouse, and a chapel alongside contemporary Brazilian art. The sea-wall terrace and garden face the bay; the interior holds rotating exhibitions and works from the permanent collection.
📖 Wikipedia: Museum of Modern Art of Bahia

3. Dique do Tororó
↳ A Dutch-era reservoir built in the 17th century, now encircled by a 3.5-kilometer public promenade. The lake holds eight large sculptures of the major Orixás commissioned in 1998 by artist Tatti Moreno — Iemanjá, Oxalá, Xangô, Oxum, and others — each standing in or near the water, mapping Candomblé sacred geography onto a central public park.
📖 Wikipedia: Dique do Tororó

🍮 Local Tastes
Acarajé
↳ Black-eyed pea fritters deep-fried in dendê palm oil, split open and filled with vatapá, caruru, and dried shrimp. Sold street-side by Baianas in white dress — a food with roots in Candomblé offerings to the Orixá Iansã.
📍 Largo do Pelourinho · Largo do Bonfim
Moqueca Baiana
↳ Bahian fish or shrimp stew cooked in dendê palm oil with coconut milk, tomato, onion, and coriander — distinct from the Espírito Santo version by the palm oil and coconut, both essential. Served in a clay pot at the table.
📍 Sorriso da Dadá · Amado
Vatapá
↳ A thick, creamy paste made from stale bread or cassava, dried shrimp, peanuts, cashews, coconut milk, and dendê palm oil. Eaten with rice or as the filling inside acarajé; also a ceremonial offering to Xangô in Candomblé.
📍 Tempero da Dinha · SENAC Pelourinho
Caruru
↳ Okra-based dish cooked with shrimp, dried shrimp, peanuts, cashews, and dendê palm oil. A ritual food in Candomblé, traditionally prepared for the feast of the twin saints Cosme and Damião, associated with the Ibeji Orixás.
📍 Tempero da Dinha · SENAC Pelourinho
Abará
↳ Black-eyed pea dough steamed inside a banana leaf — the same base as acarajé but without frying. Softer and milder in flavor. The banana-leaf cooking technique was brought directly from West Africa to Bahia.
📍 Baianas in Pelourinho
Caldo de Feijão
↳ Black bean soup, thickened to a dense broth, seasoned with garlic, onion, and dried meat. A street-food staple in Salvador bars and lanchonetes, commonly available as a late-afternoon snack alongside farofa and hot sauce.

Salvador Carnival
↳ One of the world's largest street festivals, drawing over two million people in February or March. Entirely street-based: massive trios elétricos — motorized sound trucks — lead crowds of tens of thousands through three competing circuits (Dodô, Osmar, Batatinha) across the city over a full week.

🌊 Two Maps of the Same City

Salvador operates on two sacred geographies simultaneously. The Lavagem do Bonfim — the ritual washing of the church steps that draws millions each January — takes place at a Catholic church dedicated to Christ. But the ceremony belongs to Oxalá, the Candomblé Orixá of creation and the sky, whose energy the Baianas invoke through perfumed water. The church steps are the threshold of two religions at once, and neither disputes the other's presence.

The Dique do Tororó reservoir has been considered sacred to Oxum, the Orixá of freshwater and fertility, since before the colonial period. The eight Orixá sculptures installed in 1998 made explicit what Salvador's practitioners already knew about the lake. A Dutch-engineered reservoir, built for colonial water supply, became a Candomblé altarpiece at the center of a modern city — functioning as both, simultaneously, without contradiction.
