Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 12 min · 🚕 5 min → Maritime Museum of San Diego
1. Maritime Museum of San Diego
↳ The Maritime Museum preserves California's seafaring heritage through a fleet of historic vessels on the Embarcadero, crowned by the Star of India — the world's oldest active iron-hulled sailing ship, built in 1863. The collection spans bark-rigged tall ships, a Russian submarine, and the HMS Surprise replica.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~2 hrs
📍 1492 N Harbor Dr · Embarcadero
🚶 7 min · 🚕 4 min → USS Midway Museum
2. USS Midway Museum
↳ Moored at Navy Pier, this decommissioned aircraft carrier served for 47 years — the longest of any US carrier in the 20th century. The 4-acre flight deck holds 29 restored aircraft, while 60 exhibits below decks tell the story of 225,000 sailors who served aboard.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~2 hrs 30 min

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 15 min · 🚕 7 min → Balboa Park
1. Balboa Park
↳ A 1,200-acre urban park shaped by the legacy of the 1915 Panama-California Exposition. The El Prado promenade runs through a sequence of Spanish Colonial Revival buildings — among the finest of their kind in the American West — framed by the ornate California Tower and flanked by sculpted gardens.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 1549 El Prado · Balboa Park
🚶 1 min · 🚕 1 min → Botanical Building
2. Botanical Building
↳ The largest lath-structure greenhouse in the world at the time of its 1915 construction, this open-air wooden lattice pavilion filters California sunlight across 2,100 plants — rare cycads, cascading orchids, towering palms, and vivid seasonal displays changing throughout the year.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 4:00pm

3. Old Town San Diego State Historic Park
↳ California's first European settlement, founded in 1769. The park preserves adobe buildings, reconstructed plazas, and historic homes recreating Spanish and Mexican California, with free guided tours departing daily at 11:00am and 2:00pm from the Robinson-Rose Visitor Center.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 25 min → La Jolla Cove
1. La Jolla Cove
↳ A sheltered sea-carved inlet where sandstone cliffs plunge into crystal-clear water within a protected ecological reserve. Sea lions and harbor seals haul out on the rocks year-round, and seven sea caves carved into the headland — including the 80-foot Clam Cave — are accessible by guided kayak.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2 hrs
📍 1100 Coast Blvd · La Jolla
🚕 15 min → Torrey Pines State Natural Reserve
2. Torrey Pines State Natural Reserve
↳ One of the rarest pines on Earth — fewer than 6,000 wild Torrey pines survive on the planet, all within these 1,750 acres of eroded sandstone bluffs above the Pacific. Eight miles of trails cross dramatic ravines and badlands; the Razor Point and Guy Fleming trails deliver the most commanding ocean panoramas.
📖 Wikipedia

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 25 min → Cabrillo National Monument
1. Cabrillo National Monument
↳ A 160-acre national park marking the 1542 landing of Juan Rodríguez Cabrillo — the first European to set foot on the West Coast. The Old Point Loma Lighthouse stands at the tip of the peninsula above some of Southern California's most biodiverse tide pools, packed with sea stars, urchins, and anemones.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Cabrillo National Monument
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~2 hrs 30 min
📍 1800 Cabrillo Memorial Dr · Point Loma

2. Sunset Cliffs Natural Park
↳ A 68-acre coastal park along the western edge of Point Loma where volcanic rock formations and sea-carved arches frame the open Pacific. At sunset the eroded basalt cliffs glow amber above surge channels; three small pocket beaches and exposed tide pools reward explorers willing to scramble.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7

🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 10 min · 🚕 5 min → Broadway Pier · 🚢 15 min → Coronado Ferry Landing · 🚕 7 min → Coronado
1. Hotel del Coronado
↳ Opened in 1888 and designated a National Historic Landmark in 1977, this Victorian wood-frame resort is one of the last surviving examples of American seaside resort architecture. The Crown Room, red-tile turrets, and wraparound verandas define Coronado's shoreline — the filming location for Some Like It Hot.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2 hrs

🍮 Local Tastes
Fish Taco
↳ Born from Baja California's street food culture — crispy beer-battered white fish in a warm corn tortilla, topped with shredded cabbage, crema, salsa, and lime. Everywhere in the city but never the same twice.
📍 Oscar's Mexican Seafood · Puesto · Tacos El Gordo
California Burrito
↳ A San Diego original — carne asada, crispy french fries, guacamole, sour cream, cheese, and salsa wrapped in a large flour tortilla. The fries inside are the distinguishing invention; no other major city does this.
📍 Rigoberto's Taco Shop · Cotixan Mexican Food · Lucha Libre Gourmet Taco Shop
West Coast IPA
↳ San Diego pioneered the West Coast IPA style — aggressively hopped, crystal-clear, and bone-dry with pine and citrus aromatics. The city now hosts over 150 craft breweries; the style has been exported worldwide.
📍 AleSmith Brewing · Ballast Point · Modern Times Beer

🌊 A City That Belongs to the Water

San Diego is deceptive. Arrive expecting theme parks and naval bases and you'll find them — but keep going toward the water and the city reveals something quieter: a place where the Pacific has been shaping daily life longer than the city has had a name.

The Star of India on the Embarcadero is the oldest active sailing ship in the world. The cove at La Jolla has been carved over thousands of years into caves no human designed. Cabrillo planted his flag on the Point Loma bluff in 1542 because ships couldn't find a better natural harbor on the entire West Coast.

The fish taco, the craft IPA, the long-running ease of the streets in February when the rest of the country is frozen — all of it traces back to the same source: a geography so inherently livable that the city barely has to try. That relaxation isn't laziness. It's clarity about where you are.
