Most cities grow up around a palace. Split grew up inside one — and people are still living there, which tells you everything about how this place works.

The emperor's retirement plan

Diocletian was born into slavery near Salona, the Roman provincial capital a few miles from where Split now stands. He rose through the military and in 284 AD became emperor of Rome — the last emperor to rule a unified empire, which he governed for twenty-one years before doing something no Roman emperor had ever done: he voluntarily retired. He chose this coastline for his retirement palace, and the result is one of the best-preserved Roman structures anywhere in the world, still standing because people moved in and never left.

The Palace of Diocletian was completed around 305 AD. It covered roughly thirty-eight thousand square meters — a fortified complex with walls eight meters high and two meters thick, four gates, towers at the corners, barracks for the emperor's guard, and at the center a private apartment block, a mausoleum, and a temple to Jupiter. It was designed as a city within a city, a place where an aging emperor could live in imperial comfort while the empire went on without him. Diocletian lived here until his death around 311 and spent his time, according to contemporary accounts, growing cabbages. He is the only Roman emperor to have died peacefully in his own bed.

How a palace became a city

After Diocletian died, the palace went through the usual Roman afterlife: garrison, storehouse, occasional imperial residence. When the nearby city of Salona was sacked by Avars and Slavs in the early seventh century, the survivors did what desperate people do — they moved into the most defensible structure available. They took up residence inside the imperial palace walls. They converted the mausoleum into a cathedral. They turned the temple of Jupiter into a baptistery. They built houses in the colonnaded passages and blocked up the arcades and expanded into every available space until the palace was full of the ordinary machinery of a functioning medieval town.

This process never really stopped. Walk through the Diocletian's Palace today and you are walking through seventeen centuries of continuous occupation, each century adding its own layer over the Roman foundation. The narrow streets of the Stari Grad — the Old Town inside the palace walls — are Roman in their layout and medieval in their construction and inhabited by people who hung their laundry this morning. A family's apartment occupies what was once part of the emperor's substructure. A restaurant serves dinner in a space that was once a Roman cellar. The stone walls are original. The WiFi password is chalked on a blackboard by the door.

The Vestibule — the domed entrance hall to Diocletian's private apartments — stands open to the sky because its roof collapsed in the Middle Ages and no one ever put it back.

The Peristyle and what it means

At the center of the palace complex, just south of the cathedral, is the Peristyle — the colonnaded courtyard where citizens would gather to see the emperor appear on the loggia above. The columns are still standing, the original Roman stone, and the courtyard is still a gathering place. On summer evenings, opera singers perform here — the acoustics are outstanding because Diocletian's architects, without understanding acoustics as a science, built a space that happens to be excellent for sound. People sit on the steps of the cathedral to listen, on the same stones where Roman subjects once waited to catch a glimpse of the most powerful man in the world.

The cathedral itself deserves the most attention of any single structure in Split. Built inside Diocletian's octagonal mausoleum, it is the oldest Catholic cathedral in the world that has been in continuous use in its original building. The irony is worth sitting with: Diocletian was the last major persecutor of Christians in the Roman Empire — the Diocletianic Persecution of 303–313 AD was the most severe and systematic attempt to eradicate Christianity before it became the empire's official religion. His mausoleum became a church. His sarcophagus was eventually moved to make room for a baptismal font. The Christians won, and they built their victory monument inside the man who tried to destroy them.

Dalmatia and what connects it

Split is the capital of Dalmatia, the coastal region that runs from Zadar in the north to the Montenegrin border in the south — a thin strip of coast and islands separated from the rest of the Balkans by the Dinaric Alps. The geography has shaped everything. Dalmatia was always easier to reach by sea than by land, which is why it spent most of its history under maritime powers: Rome, then Byzantium, then Venice, then the Habsburg Empire, then briefly Napoleon, then the Habsburgs again, then Yugoslavia, then Croatia. The Venetian period — roughly 1420 to 1797 — left the most visible marks: the Lion of Saint Mark carved above city gates, the loggia designs, the specific shade of amber that old stone takes on in this light.

The Adriatic coast and islands are the reason most people come to Dalmatia, and they deserve the reputation. The water is clear, warm, and genuinely blue — not the blue that tourist brochures manufacture, but the specific shade of a limestone seabed in strong Mediterranean light. The islands visible from Split — Brač, Hvar, Šolta, Vis — are reachable by fast ferry in under an hour. Brač has the quarry where the stone for the White House was cut. Hvar is the place the Venetians fortified most heavily and that now has the most concentrated nightlife. Vis was a Yugoslav military island, closed to foreigners until 1989, which means it missed thirty years of tourism development and still has the texture of a place where people actually live.

The market and how to read it

The Pazar — the open market that runs along the eastern wall of the palace, just outside the Silver Gate — opens at dawn and winds down by midday, and it is the most useful single hour you can spend understanding Split. The market sells what the region produces: lavender from the islands, olive oil from the groves on Brač, figs and pomegranates and mandarins in season, the small sweet tomatoes that taste like tomatoes are supposed to taste, the hard dried sheep's cheese that Dalmatian farmers have been making in this form for centuries. The vendors are mostly women from the surrounding villages. The buyers are mostly local.

Across the street from the Pazar is the fish market, covered, open from early morning. The fish here were in the Adriatic last night. Dalmatian cooking is not complicated — it doesn't try to be. It is olive oil, fish, seasonal vegetables, sometimes lamb from the Dalmatian hinterland cooked under a peka (a domed clay lid buried in coals), a bread called pogača. The complexity is in the quality of the ingredients, which is very high. Don't order pasta with seafood in a tourist trap. Find a konoba — a traditional tavern, usually family-run, usually inland from the waterfront — and eat what they feel like making that day.

The city outside the palace

Most visitors stay inside the palace walls and miss the fact that Split is a working city of a quarter million people with neighborhoods worth seeing. The Varoš district, climbing the hillside immediately west of the palace, is where Split's fishermen lived for centuries — narrow stone lanes, old chapels, cats sleeping in doorways. The Meštrović Gallery, about two kilometers north along the coast road, is the home and studio of Ivan Meštrović, the Croatian sculptor who was one of the most significant artists of the twentieth century and whose work is almost unknown outside the former Yugoslavia. The views from the top of the hill above the gallery — Marjan Hill, a forested promontory that juts into the sea — show you the whole coastline from elevation.

The Riva, the marble promenade along the waterfront south of the palace, is where Split goes to see and be seen — coffee in the morning, crowds in the evening, ferries coming and going from the terminal at the western end. It was redesigned in 2007 by local architects who replaced a cluttered waterfront with a clean, wide expanse of white stone and a row of identical café umbrellas. Locals had feelings about this redesign. They still do. But the Riva is where you understand that Split is not primarily a tourist attraction — it is a city that happens to have an extraordinary Roman palace at its center, surrounded by everything a city of this size needs.

The thing to carry

Here is what changes how you see Split: this is a place where the line between ancient monument and current address has never been drawn. People have been living inside Diocletian's walls for seventeen hundred years without ever deciding the palace needed to become a museum. The emperor's mausoleum is a cathedral not because anyone planned it that way, but because the people who moved in needed a church and the mausoleum was the biggest domed space available. Every building in the Old Town is a negotiation between what was there before and what the current inhabitants needed.

When you walk through the Vestibule and look up through the open dome at the sky — the dome that has been open to rain and sun since its roof fell in during the Middle Ages — you're looking at a monument that survived not because it was preserved, but because it was used. That's a different kind of continuity. It means the city that exists today is not a museum of what came before. It is what came before, still going.
