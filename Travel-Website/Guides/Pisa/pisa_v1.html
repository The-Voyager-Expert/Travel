Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 13 min · 🚕 5 min → Pisa Baptistery
1. Pisa Baptistery
↳ Italy's largest baptistery, a marble drum begun in 1152 that rises from Romanesque arcades to Gothic pinnacles. Inside are Nicola Pisano's 1260 pulpit and a famous echo — ask a custodian to sing and the double dome holds the note for seconds.

2. Pisa Cathedral
↳ The cathedral that gave Pisan Romanesque its name, begun in 1063 from the spoils of a naval victory. Inside: Giovanni Pisano's carved pulpit, a gilded coffered ceiling, and the swinging bronze lamp said to have set Galileo thinking about pendulums.

3. Leaning Tower of Pisa
↳ The cathedral's bell tower, tilting since the third storey went up in 1178 on soft river silt. Eight storeys of stacked marble arcades climb to a 294-step spiral; a decade-long rescue finished in 2001 steadied it and reopened the climb to the top.

4. Camposanto Monumentale
↳ A vast walled cloister said to enclose soil shipped from Golgotha. Its marble galleries hold Roman sarcophagi and once-lost frescoes — including the harrowing Triumph of Death, scorched in a 1944 bombing and painstakingly restored over decades.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → Piazza dei Cavalieri
1. Piazza dei Cavalieri
↳ Medieval Pisa's civic heart, remade by Vasari in the 1500s for the Knights of St Stephen. The sgraffito-covered Palazzo della Carovana — now the elite Scuola Normale — anchors a square that traded political power for scholarship; Galileo studied here.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Piazza dei Cavalieri · Centro Storico
🚶 7 min · 🚕 4 min → Orto Botanico di Pisa
2. Orto Botanico di Pisa
↳ The world's first university botanical garden, founded in 1543 by Luca Ghini and moved here in 1591. Gravel paths thread past centuries-old magnolias, a ginkgo, ranks of glasshouses and a shell-crusted fountain — a quiet green pocket by the tourist crush.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Santa Maria della Spina
↳ A jewel-box of Pisan Gothic at the river's edge, bristling with gables, pinnacles and Pisano-workshop statues. Built around 1230 for a thorn from Christ's crown, it was taken apart in 1871 and rebuilt higher to escape the Arno's floods.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~15 min

🚶 9 min · 🚕 4 min → Tuttomondo
4. Tuttomondo
↳ Keith Haring's last great public mural, painted on the wall of Sant'Antonio church in 1989, months before his death. Thirty interlocking figures in flat colour climb the wall like a puzzle of human connection — New York street art on a medieval Tuscan town.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~15 min

🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 8 min · 🚕 4 min → Museo Nazionale di San Matteo
1. Museo Nazionale di San Matteo
↳ Pisa's principal art museum, housed in a former Benedictine convent on the north Lungarno. The medieval galleries hold the finest collection of Pisan and Tuscan sculpture outside Florence — Nicola and Giovanni Pisano originals, Simone Martini's polyptych, and a trove of painted crucifixes tracing a century of experimentation before Giotto.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Palazzo Blu
↳ A 14th-century Gothic palace on the south Lungarno, its deep blue facade facing the river. Inside, the permanent collection traces Pisan art from medieval panel paintings to 20th-century works, displayed across restored frescoed rooms and a modern lower floor. The building also hosts the city's most important temporary exhibitions.

3. Piazza delle Vettovaglie
↳ Pisa's oldest and most vivid public square, enclosed on two sides by Romanesque market porticoes that have sheltered stalls since the 12th century. During the morning the square fills with a fresh produce and flower market; by afternoon the bars and tables take over. The real city, far from the tourist crowds of the Campo dei Miracoli.

4. Fortezza Guelfa
↳ A 14th-century fortress at the western bend of the Arno, its crenellated towers and thick walls built to control the river mouth and the road into the city. The rampart walk above the water gives one of the quietest and most unexpected elevated views in Pisa — the river below, the medieval towers above.

🍮 Local Tastes
Cecìna
↳ A thin golden flatbread of chickpea flour and olive oil, baked in a copper pan until crisp-edged and creamy inside. Pisa's signature street food — eaten hot, often tucked into a wedge of focaccia.
📍 Pizzeria Il Montino · L'Ostellino
Torta Coi Bischeri
↳ A shortcrust tart from the Pisan countryside filled with chocolate, rice, pine nuts, raisins and candied fruit, its edges pinched into the little points — "bischeri" — that give it its name.
📍 Pasticceria Salza
Bordatino Alla Pisana
↳ A hearty peasant soup of cannellini beans, black cabbage and cornmeal, slow-cooked into a thick warming bowl that long fed Pisa's river workers.
📍 Osteria dei Cavalieri · Trattoria Sant'Omobono

✨ A Few Things I Noticed

Pisa rewards you for staying after dark. The day-trippers bussed in from Florence photograph the Tower and leave by mid-afternoon, and by evening the Field of Miracles empties out — the marble glows and you can hear the Baptistery's echo without a crowd.

If your dates are flexible, aim for the night of June 16. For the Luminara di San Ranieri, Pisa switches off its street lights and outlines every palace, bridge and church along the Arno with tens of thousands of candles in glass cups — a once-a-year sight staged here since the 1600s, on the eve of the city's patron saint.

One quiet thread ties this trip together: Galileo. He was baptised in the Baptistery on Day 1, supposedly timed the swinging cathedral lamp beside it, and studied on the Piazza dei Cavalieri you start Day 2 on. The whole town is, in a sense, his campus.
