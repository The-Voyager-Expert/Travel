A bay too deep for its latitude, a film set that became a city, and the narrow strip of cobblestone between the Sierra Madre and the sea.

Puerto Vallarta sits at the northern end of Banderas Bay — one of the deepest natural bays in the Americas, measuring 40 kilometres across and reaching 1,800 metres at its floor. The Sierra Madre Occidental mountains descend almost directly into the water here, a geological collision that gives the city its topography: steep hillside barrios, narrow cobblestone streets, and a coastline dramatically different from the flat beach resorts farther north and south along the Pacific coast of Mexico.

The bay itself is the defining fact of the city's character. Its depth creates unusual upwelling conditions that concentrate marine life — manta rays, humpback whales, yellowfin tuna, and large pelagic fish — in relatively shallow and accessible water. The underwater sanctuary at Los Arcos, a cluster of granite rock formations jutting from the bay six kilometres south of downtown, is among the most biodiverse marine sites on Mexico's Pacific coast. This geography explains why Puerto Vallarta developed a distinct culture around ocean activity rather than simply beach tourism.

The Making of a Resort City

Settlement at the mouth of the Río Cuale dates to the mid-19th century, when a small fishing and farming community called Las Peñas de Santa María de Guadalupe grew around the natural harbour. The surrounding mountains and the difficulty of road access kept the town essentially isolated well into the 20th century. There was no road connecting Puerto Vallarta to Guadalajara until 1945, and before that the only reliable link to the outside world was by sea or by mule trail through the Sierra Madre.

Isolation preserved something that larger coastal cities lost earlier. The vernacular architecture of El Centro — white-plastered colonial houses with terracotta tile roofs, wrought-iron balconies, and bougainvillea cascading over stone walls — remained largely intact because there was no commercial pressure to replace it. The Church of Our Lady of Guadalupe, completed in 1921 with its distinctive crown-shaped tower (a replica of the crown worn by Empress Charlotte of Mexico), became the organizing landmark of the town, visible from nearly every rooftop in the centre.

The transformation began in the 1950s, when Mexican director Emilio Fernández brought a film crew to shoot scenes in the area. The real arrival came in 1963, when John Huston chose the location to film The Night of the Iguana, starring Richard Burton. Burton brought Elizabeth Taylor, who was not in the film but stayed anyway. The presence of two of the most photographed people in the world in this inaccessible village created a media event that introduced the place to an international audience overnight. Taylor and Burton's relationship, played out in a village with no international airport and barely a reliable telephone connection, generated sustained press coverage for years. The pink Puente del Amor connecting the twin mansions Burton gave Taylor across a Gringo Gulch alley remains the most visited private structure in the city.

Two Cities in One

Puerto Vallarta today operates as two distinct environments. The hotel zone north of El Centro, running along Boulevard Francisco Medina Ascencio toward the airport, is a chain-hotel corridor built on flat land reclaimed from the estuary in the 1970s and 1980s. It has virtually no character of its own — it is architecture designed to deliver beach access, swimming pools, and proximity to the marina, not to reflect anything particular about its location.

South of the Río Cuale is the opposite. El Centro and Zona Romántica together form one of the most coherent historic urban environments on Mexico's Pacific coast. The cobblestone streets were not installed as tourist amenity; they predate the resort era and remain because the hillside grades and narrow widths of Gringo Gulch make them impractical to replace. The commercial activity here evolved organically: small restaurants, independent galleries, mezcal bars, and neighbourhood markets operating in buildings that are, in many cases, still the same structures visible in the John Huston film footage from sixty years ago.

Zona Romántica — the eight blocks between the Río Cuale and Lázaro Cárdenas Park — carries an additional layer of significance as one of Mexico's most established and welcoming LGBTQ+ neighbourhoods, a character that developed from the 1970s onward and that today makes it the social and commercial centre of one of Latin America's largest LGBTQ+ expatriate communities.

The River and the Island

The Río Cuale divides the city physically and symbolically. The river descends from the Sierra Madre through a narrow canyon east of the city and deposits a sand and gravel delta into the bay — creating Isla Río Cuale, the long narrow island that now separates the two branches of the river as it enters the sea. The island has been a public park since 1974, when the city expropriated it from private owners and turned it into a promenade connecting El Centro to Zona Romántica. John Huston's bronze statue, installed in 1988, sits at the western tip.

The canyon above the city — the Río Cuale valley — runs into the Sierra Madre and provides the access corridor for the adventure tourism that has become central to the economy: zipline operators, off-road vehicle companies, and hiking guides all stage their operations in the lower canyon where the road follows the river before the terrain becomes impassable. The same canyon geography that once isolated the city now funnels the outdoor activity that sustains a significant share of its tourism revenue.

Banderas Bay and the Whale Migration

Between November and March, Banderas Bay hosts one of the highest concentrations of humpback whales accessible from any Mexican city. The whales migrate from Alaskan feeding grounds to breed and calve in warm Pacific waters, and the bay's protected geography — a large enclosed area with minimal shipping traffic — makes it an unusually productive breeding site. The population using Banderas Bay represents part of the Eastern North Pacific humpback group, which was removed from the Endangered Species List in 2016 as population levels recovered from commercial whaling.

What makes the whale presence unusual in practical terms is that the migration corridor runs directly through the routes used by every water taxi and tour boat operating out of Los Muertos Pier. Sightings are not restricted to dedicated whale-watch tours — encounters happen on ferry crossings to Yelapa, on the Las Caletas dinner-boat route, and on snorkel trips to Los Arcos. The bay is not large enough for the whales to be far from human activity during peak season.

The Sierra Madre Backdrop

Visitors oriented toward the ocean often miss the fact that Puerto Vallarta sits at the end of a deep mountain corridor. The Mirador de la Cruz — the hilltop viewpoint above El Centro — makes the relationship visible: from there, the red-tile rooftop city fills a narrow strip between the forest-covered Sierra Madre and the bay, with almost no flat terrain between them. The mountains run uninterrupted from the city east through Jalisco and north into Nayarit, and the road south along the coast rapidly becomes impractical past the Boca de Tomatlán inlet.

The Vallarta Botanical Garden, 24 kilometres south on the coastal highway, exists specifically because of this geography. The garden's position at 400 metres elevation and immediate proximity to undisturbed Sierra Madre forest allows it to maintain one of Mexico's largest living orchid collections and a native plant library of regional agaves, palms, and tropical trees. The garden opened in 2004 as a private conservation initiative and has since documented over 1,200 plant species on the property — a figure that reflects the exceptional botanical diversity of the Sierra Madre Occidental microclimate directly adjacent to the coast.

Jalisco and Nayarit

Puerto Vallarta sits in Jalisco, but the bay it faces opens into Nayarit, and the state line runs through the water. Nuevo Vallarta — the purpose-built all-inclusive resort development visible across the bay to the north — is in Nayarit, not Jalisco. Sayulita, the Pueblo Mágico village 38 kilometres north that functions as an overflow experience for visitors looking for a smaller, more bohemian version of what Puerto Vallarta was before it was famous, is also in Nayarit.

Jalisco's cultural claim on the city is strong. Tequila, mariachi, and birria — the three most globally recognized markers of Mexican culture — all originate in Jalisco. Birria, the slow-cooked meat stew now consumed worldwide as a taco filling, remains in its home state a more elaborate dish: a rich broth-based preparation served as both soup and taco dunked in consommé, available at neighbourhood lunch counters throughout El Centro in a form that predates the birria taco's international adoption by decades. The food in Puerto Vallarta is the food of coastal Jalisco — a Pacific seafood tradition laid over a meat-and-chile Jaliscan base, inflected at the high end by the same chef-driven contemporary Mexican movement that has transformed Guadalajara's restaurant scene.
