A bay too deep for its latitude, a film set that became a city, and the narrow strip of cobblestone between the Sierra Madre and the sea.

Look at a map of Mexico's Pacific coast and you'll notice something odd about Puerto Vallarta's bay. Banderas Bay is 40 kilometres across and drops to 1,800 metres at its floor — one of the deepest natural bays in the Americas. The Sierra Madre Occidental doesn't ease down to the water here. It crashes into it. That geological collision is the first thing you feel when you arrive: the city doesn't sprawl, it clings. Steep hillside barrios stack above narrow cobblestone streets, red-tile rooftops tilt toward the sea, and the jungle-covered mountains behind you are close enough to hear at night.

Most Mexican beach destinations sit on flat coastal plains. You're not at one of those. The depth of this bay creates upwelling conditions that pull nutrients to the surface — which is why, if you take a boat to Los Arcos (the granite formations jutting from the water six kilometres south of downtown), you'll find yourself snorkelling over one of the most biodiverse marine sites on the Pacific coast. Manta rays, humpback whales, yellowfin tuna. This isn't a city that happened to have a beach. It's a city that grew around an ocean.

How a Film Set Became a City

Before you dismiss Puerto Vallarta as another resort town, understand how recently it was nothing of the kind. Until 1945, there was no road connecting it to Guadalajara. The only way in was by sea or by mule trail through the Sierra Madre. The settlement at the mouth of the Río Cuale — originally called Las Peñas de Santa María de Guadalupe — was a fishing and farming village, and it stayed that way for decades longer than geography alone required.

That isolation preserved something. Walk El Centro today and you're looking at vernacular architecture that survived because nobody had a reason to tear it down: white-plastered houses with terracotta tile roofs, wrought-iron balconies, bougainvillea spilling over stone walls. The Church of Our Lady of Guadalupe, completed in 1921 with its crown-shaped tower (modelled on the crown of Empress Charlotte of Mexico), still anchors the skyline. You can see it from nearly every rooftop in the centre — the same view the town had when it was too small to appear on most maps.

Then, in 1963, John Huston showed up to film The Night of the Iguana with Richard Burton. Burton brought Elizabeth Taylor, who wasn't in the film but stayed anyway. Picture it: two of the most photographed people alive, conducting a tabloid-consuming love affair in a village with no international airport and barely a working telephone. The press descended. The world found out. Taylor and Burton's twin mansions in Gringo Gulch, connected by the pink Puente del Amor across the alley, remain the most visited private structure in the city — and the reason you're reading about this place at all.

The cobblestone streets weren't installed as tourist charm. They predate the resort era and remain because the hillside grades make them impractical to replace. You're walking on the original city, not a recreation of it.

North of the River, South of the River

You need to understand that Puerto Vallarta is two cities sharing one name. The hotel zone north of El Centro — Boulevard Francisco Medina Ascencio running toward the airport — is a chain-hotel corridor built on flat land reclaimed from the estuary in the 1970s and 1980s. Skip it. It has no character of its own. It's architecture designed to deliver pool access and proximity to the marina, interchangeable with resort strips anywhere in the world.

South of the Río Cuale is where you want to be. El Centro and Zona Romántica together form one of the most intact historic urban environments on Mexico's Pacific coast. The restaurants, galleries, mezcal bars, and neighbourhood markets here operate in buildings that are, in many cases, the same structures visible in John Huston's film footage from sixty years ago. Nothing was reconstructed. The commercial life grew into the old architecture because the old architecture was still standing.

Zona Romántica — the eight blocks between the Río Cuale and Lázaro Cárdenas Park — carries a layer you should know about. Since the 1970s, it has developed into one of Mexico's most established and welcoming LGBTQ+ neighbourhoods, and today it functions as the social and commercial centre of one of Latin America's largest LGBTQ+ expatriate communities. The openness is visible and genuine, and it shapes the neighbourhood's energy in ways you'll notice immediately.

The River That Splits the City

Cross any of the bridges over the Río Cuale and look down. The river descends from the Sierra Madre through a narrow canyon and deposits a gravel delta into the bay, creating Isla Río Cuale — the long, narrow island that separates the two branches of the river at its mouth. Since 1974, the island has been a public park, a shaded promenade connecting El Centro to Zona Romántica. You'll find John Huston's bronze statue at the western tip, facing the bay he made famous.

Follow the river the other direction — east, away from the ocean — and you're looking up the canyon corridor into the Sierra Madre. This is where the adventure tourism stages: zipline operators, off-road vehicles, hiking guides, all working the lower canyon where the road follows the river before the terrain becomes impassable. The same geography that kept this city isolated for a century now funnels the outdoor activity that sustains a major share of its economy. If you book a jungle tour, you're tracing the old mule trail.

The Whales in Your Way

If you're here between November and March, you're sharing the bay with humpback whales. They migrate from Alaskan feeding grounds to breed and calve in these warm waters, and Banderas Bay's protected shape — wide, enclosed, minimal shipping traffic — makes it one of their preferred sites. The population here is part of the Eastern North Pacific group that was removed from the Endangered Species List in 2016 as numbers recovered from commercial whaling.

Here's what makes this different from whale watching elsewhere: the migration corridor runs directly through the routes used by every water taxi and tour boat operating out of Los Muertos Pier. You don't need a dedicated whale-watch excursion. You'll see them on the ferry to Yelapa. You'll see them on the dinner-boat route to Las Caletas. You'll see them on a snorkel trip to Los Arcos. The bay simply isn't large enough for you and the whales to avoid each other during peak season.

Turn Around — the Mountains Are Right There

Most people who visit Puerto Vallarta face the ocean for the duration and leave without registering what's behind them. Don't make that mistake. Climb to the Mirador de la Cruz — the hilltop viewpoint above El Centro — and you'll see the relationship laid out: the red-tile city fills a narrow strip between the jungle-covered Sierra Madre and the bay, with almost no flat terrain between them. The mountains run uninterrupted east through Jalisco and north into Nayarit. The road south along the coast becomes impractical past Boca de Tomatlán.

The Vallarta Botanical Garden, 24 kilometres south on the coastal highway, exists because of this compression. Sitting at 400 metres elevation, right at the edge of undisturbed Sierra Madre forest, it holds one of Mexico's largest living orchid collections and over 1,200 documented plant species — a number that reflects what happens when a tropical coast and a mountain range meet with no buffer zone. You can stand in the garden and look back toward the bay, and the distance between jungle and ocean is startlingly small.

You're in Jalisco. Everything that follows — the tequila, the mariachi, the birria — comes from the state you're standing in. This isn't imported culture. It's the home field.

What You're Really Eating

Puerto Vallarta sits in Jalisco, but the bay faces Nayarit, and the state line runs through the water. Nuevo Vallarta, the purpose-built all-inclusive resort visible across the bay to the north, is technically in a different state. So is Sayulita, the Pueblo Mágico village 38 kilometres north where people go looking for a smaller, more bohemian version of what Puerto Vallarta was before it was famous.

But Jalisco's cultural claim on this city is absolute. Tequila, mariachi, and birria — the three most globally recognized markers of Mexican culture — all originate in this state. When you sit down at a neighbourhood lunch counter in El Centro and order birria, you're eating it in its original form: not the birria taco that went viral on social media, but the slow-cooked stew served as broth and meat, your taco dunked in consommé, in a preparation that predates the internet-famous version by decades. The food here is coastal Jalisco — Pacific seafood layered over a meat-and-chile Jaliscan base — and at its best, it's informed by the same chef-driven contemporary Mexican movement reshaping Guadalajara's restaurant scene three hours inland.

The Thing to Carry With You

Here's what most people miss about Puerto Vallarta, and what changes how you see everything once you get it: this city was never designed to be found. It existed for a century behind a mountain wall with no roads, perfectly content as a fishing village at the mouth of a river. Then a film director showed up, two movie stars fell in love, and the world arrived — but the city that was already here didn't go away. It just absorbed the attention and kept its shape. The cobblestones are original. The church tower is the same one the fishermen saw. The bay is still deeper than anyone needs it to be. You're not visiting a place that was built for you. You're visiting a place that was built for itself, and it's letting you in.
