↳ A 2.5-mile ranger-led hike through the homeland of the Agua Caliente Band of Cahuilla Indians, ending at a 60-foot waterfall dropping into a granite pool. Ancient rock art and grinding stones mark the canyon walls throughout.

↳ Three adjacent canyons — Palm Canyon, Andreas Canyon, and Murray Canyon — contain the world's largest California fan palm oasis and over 15 miles of trails. The Agua Caliente Band has inhabited this land for generations; granaries, rock art, and village sites appear throughout.

↳ Five natural palm oases emerge where underground water is forced to the surface by fault pressure. The Thousand Palms Oasis is the largest — hundreds of Washingtonia filifera, the only California-native palm, rising from a spring-fed marsh surrounded by open dunes.

Day 7
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 40 min → Salton Sea
1. Salton Sea
↳ California's largest lake by surface area exists by accident — an irrigation canal breach flooded the Salton Sink for two years in the early 1900s. The sea now sits below sea level: an inland body ringed by abandoned fishing resorts, rusted piers, and bleached shoreline. Strangely beautiful, genuinely surreal.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~3 hrs

🍮 Local Tastes
Medjool Date Shake
↳ The Coachella Valley grows most of America's Medjool dates. Rich, caramel-sweet, thick, with a distinctive grainy texture unlike any other shake — a regional institution for generations. Best served straight from the date farms.
📍 Shields Date Garden · Indio · Old Town La Quinta farm stands
Stuffed Medjool Dates
↳ Whole Medjool dates split and filled — cream cheese and walnuts, almond paste, or dark chocolate and sea salt. Sold fresh at date farms throughout the valley. Every farm has its own signature filling.
📍 Shields Date Garden · Indio
Fish Tacos
↳ Battered or grilled fish, shredded cabbage, lime crema, and pico de gallo in a warm corn tortilla. A reflection of the valley's deep Baja California influence. Every taqueria does them well.
📍 Restaurante Los Primos · TQLAS Agave Bar & Grill · La Quinta

✨ The Desert Paradox

Palm Desert keeps defying expectations. A valley floor that should be uninhabitable — active fault lines running underneath, surrounded by bare mountains — supports one of the most concentrated resort cultures in America. Date palms that need no irrigation because the San Andreas Fault pushes groundwater up through the soil. An aerial tram that lifts you from desert to alpine forest in ten minutes. An inland sea that exists entirely by accident and refuses to be ignored.

The guide moves outward from comfort. Early days hover near Palm Springs and its elevation relief. Later days push into deeper geology — the fault canyons and the Joshua Tree wilderness. The final day completes the loop with what the desert does to the human settlements it eventually reclaims. The resort holds the same temperature throughout. Use it as base camp.
