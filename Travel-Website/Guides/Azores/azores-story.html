Nine islands in the middle of the Atlantic, formed by the same volcanic hotspot that's still pushing rock upward today — the Azores are not so much discovered as stumbled upon, and most visitors leave wishing they'd allotted more time.

Islands That Came From Nowhere

The Azores sit roughly 1,500 kilometers west of Lisbon, planted in the middle of the Atlantic on a volcanic hotspot where three tectonic plates — the North American, Eurasian, and African — meet in an arrangement found almost nowhere else on Earth. The islands are young by geological standards. São Miguel, the largest, began forming about four million years ago; Flores, the westernmost, is a bit younger. The volcanic activity that created them hasn't stopped. Furnas, a village on São Miguel's east end, sits inside an active caldera where the ground itself vents steam and the locals cook food in pots lowered into geothermal pools.

Portuguese sailors discovered the archipelago in the 15th century — though the exact sequence of who found which island when is contested enough that no single explorer gets clean credit. What's undisputed is that the Portuguese began settling the islands around 1439, starting with Santa Maria, and that the Azores quickly became a critical waypoint for ships crossing the Atlantic. Columbus stopped here on his return from the Americas in 1493. The islands' position — roughly equidistant from Europe and North America — made them a refueling and reprovisioning stop for centuries of Atlantic crossings, and later a strategic communications hub: the first transatlantic telegraph cables came ashore on Faial in the 1870s.

São Miguel: The Green Island

Most visitors to the Azores fly into Ponta Delgada on São Miguel, and many never leave. The island is large enough to occupy a week without repetition. The interior is dominated by calderas — collapsed volcanic craters now filled with crater lakes. Sete Cidades, on the island's western plateau, holds two lakes — one blue, one green — separated by a narrow bridge in an arrangement that has been on Portuguese postcards since photography existed. Furnas, on the eastern end, holds the Caldeiras das Furnas thermal springs and the Lagoa das Furnas, a caldera lake whose muddy shores vent enough heat to cook the traditional cozido das Furnas stew buried in clay pots since morning.

The island's signature color is the improbable green of its pastures — a green so saturated it looks processed, the result of year-round mild temperatures and Atlantic rain. Azorean dairy cattle produce the milk for the local butter and cheeses that appear at every meal. Hydrangeas line the roads, not because anyone planted them decoratively, but because they were originally introduced as hedgerows to separate agricultural plots and have since colonized most of the island's roadsides. In summer, the blue of the hydrangeas against the green pastures and the black basalt walls is the visual vocabulary of the place.

"The islands look like something a set designer would create if asked to invent the ideal Atlantic escape — then you remember the volcanoes are still active."

The Whales and the Sea

The waters around the Azores are among the best whale-watching locations in the world. The Gulf Stream and the Atlantic current systems concentrate prey here, and the deep water just offshore gives sperm whales the diving depth they need. Sperm whales are year-round residents; blue whales, fin whales, sei whales, and multiple dolphin species pass through seasonally. The Azorean tradition of whale-watching predates tourism by centuries — the archipelago had an active whaling industry from the 18th century through 1987, when Faial's last shore-based whaling station closed. The same wooden lookout posts on clifftops that whale hunters once used to spot spouts are now staffed by spotters who radio coordinates to whale-watching boats below.

The transition from whaling to whale-watching happened faster here than almost anywhere. Former whalers became guides. The hunting boats became observation platforms. Faial's Museu do Scrimshaw holds one of the world's largest collections of carved whale teeth — objects made by Azorean whalers during long voyages, a craft that is now both artifact and uncomfortable window into an industry that ended within living memory. The whales haven't forgiven or forgotten; they simply continue showing up in the same deep water they always used.

Island Differences: The Archipelago as a System

The nine islands divide into three groups. The central group — Faial, Pico, São Jorge, Graciosa, and Terceira — is where many serious travelers end up spending the bulk of their time. Pico, separated from Faial by a narrow channel, is dominated by the eponymous volcano: at 2,351 meters, it's the highest point in Portugal. Climbing it is a full-day commitment that starts in the dark and ends in cloud. Faial is the island where the Blue Peter bar in Horta harbor has hosted transatlantic sailors since the 1950s — the walls are covered in boat names and flags left by crews who crossed the Atlantic and made this their first landfall in Europe.

Terceira, the third-largest island, holds Angra do Heroísmo — a city with a historic center designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983. Angra was the most important city in the Azores for centuries, a major stop on the route between Portugal and Brazil, and its downtown grid of 16th and 17th-century buildings survived largely intact. The bulls run through its streets on summer weekends in touradas à corda — a version of bullfighting unique to the Azores where the bull is never harmed and is controlled by a long rope held by eight men called pastores.

The Food: Everything Comes From Here

Azorean food is not internationally famous, which is one of the more puzzling omissions in European food culture. The dairy is exceptional — São Miguel's Queijo São Jorge, a firm semi-aged cow's milk cheese from the island of that name, has a complexity that competes easily with anything from northern Portugal. The beef is grass-fed from pastures that never go dry. Seafood reflects the position: limpets (lapas) are grilled with garlic and butter at nearly every restaurant; tuna appears in every form; the chicharros (Atlantic horse mackerel) are a staple served simply fried. Alcatra, a slow-braised beef stew cooked in clay pots, is the signature dish of Terceira and requires very little beyond good meat, wine, lard, and time.

The tea grown on Gorreana and Porto Formoso plantations on São Miguel is the only commercial tea produced in Europe. The plants arrived in the 19th century as an experiment; most of the other islands' plantations eventually failed, but Gorreana has been producing continuously since 1883. A visit to the plantation is short — the operation is small, the factory is old, the processing is straightforward — but the tea, drunk on site with a view over terraced plants to the ocean, is better than it has any right to be.

Getting There and Why It Still Feels Remote

Azores Airlines (SATA) and Ryanair now fly direct to Ponta Delgada from several European cities, and United Airlines runs a seasonal direct flight from Boston. The accessibility has increased substantially over the past decade. But the islands still feel remote in ways that matter. Cell service is inconsistent outside towns. Driving a rental car on São Miguel means navigating roads that were designed for ox carts and widened only slightly since. Weather changes fast — a clear morning on the caldera lookout can become fog-socked in twenty minutes. The Atlantic is not decorative from here; it's present in the wind, the light, and the fact that the next land west is North America.

What Changes When You Understand Where You Are

The thing that shifts your perception of the Azores is the geological reality of standing on an active hot spot above three tectonic plates. The islands you're visiting are not stable ground in any geological sense — they're temporary features in an ongoing process. Furnas village, built inside a caldera, has been periodically evacuated due to volcanic unrest. The last significant eruption on São Miguel was in the 1630s; on Faial, a new volcanic vent opened in 1957 and built an entirely new peninsula over the course of 13 months, burying the village of Capelinhos and forcing the evacuation of about 2,000 people, many of whom emigrated to the United States. The lava fields from that eruption are still bare and black on Faial's western tip, looking like something that happened last year.

That's the thing about the Azores that stays with you. The landscape isn't scenery — it's geology in progress. The hydrangeas and the green pastures and the caldera lakes are all resting on rock that came up from the ocean floor, and more rock is on its way. Visiting with that awareness changes the scale of everything you see.
