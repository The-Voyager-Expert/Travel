Three small Caribbean islands bought for submarine defense, shaped by sugar and slavery, still driving on the left in American cars — and still standing after two Category 5 hurricanes hit within two weeks.

The deal

The United States bought the Virgin Islands in 1917 for twenty-five million dollars — roughly the same price, adjusted for nothing, that it paid for Alaska fifty years earlier. The buyer's motive had nothing to do with beaches. Germany controlled nearby colonies, and the U.S. Navy wanted to guarantee that no European power could park a submarine fleet in the Caribbean and threaten the Panama Canal. Denmark, which had held the islands for two and a half centuries, was in no position to argue. The deal went through. The islands changed flags. The Danish warehouses on the Charlotte Amalie waterfront stayed exactly where they were.

You can still read the transaction in the architecture. Charlotte Amalie — the capital, on St. Thomas — is named for a Danish queen. Fort Christian, the red-brick fortress at the harbor's center, was built by Danes in the 1670s and is the oldest standing structure in the territory. The 99 Steps, the famous staircase cut into the hillside above town, were laid with Danish ballast bricks carried as dead weight in empty sugar ships sailing from Copenhagen. The warehouses along the waterfront are Danish commercial architecture, built to store rum and sugar and molasses, and they still stand in neat rows along streets that carry Danish names. The United States acquired these islands, but it did not rebuild them. It just moved in.

The sugar islands

Before they were American, before they were a naval calculation, the Virgin Islands were a sugar colony — and everything that phrase implies. The Danes arrived in the late 1600s and immediately cleared the land for cane. St. Croix, the largest island, was almost entirely given over to plantations. The labor was enslaved Africans, transported across the Atlantic in conditions that need no elaboration here, and the profits flowed back to Copenhagen.

The scale was staggering for islands this small. At the peak of production, St. Croix alone had over 200 plantations worked by more than 20,000 enslaved people on an island roughly the size of Brooklyn. The ruins of those plantations are still visible across the island — stone windmill towers, great house foundations, the crumbling walls of boiling houses where cane juice was reduced to raw sugar. They are not preserved as monuments. They are simply there, too solid to disappear.

The Danes emancipated 8,000 enslaved people in the Danish West Indies on July 3, 1848 — not because the colonial government decided to, but because the enslaved population decided for them.

Emancipation came in 1848, and it did not come from above. On the morning of July 3, thousands of enslaved people on St. Croix marched into Frederiksted and surrounded the fort. They gave the governor an ultimatum. Governor Peter von Scholten, understanding that the alternative was a massacre he could not win, declared emancipation on the spot. Copenhagen ratified the decision later, having no practical choice. The freed population got freedom but not land, not capital, and not a functioning economy — the plantation owners were the ones who received compensation. The resulting labor disputes culminated thirty years later in the Fireburn of 1878, when laborers on St. Croix set fire to plantations, sugar works, and much of Frederiksted itself. The Fireburn is the foundational event of Virgin Islands labor history, and October 1 — the anniversary — is a territorial holiday.

The tower on the hill

Blackbeard's Castle sits on a hilltop above Charlotte Amalie, and the first thing you should know about it is that Blackbeard almost certainly never lived there. The tower is real — it was built by the Danes in the 1670s as a watchtower, part of the same fortification effort that produced Fort Christian — but the Blackbeard association is a nineteenth-century invention, layered onto the structure long after the pirate era ended. Edward Teach, the historical Blackbeard, operated primarily off the Carolina coast and in the Bahamas. He may have passed through the Virgin Islands. Every pirate in the Caribbean passed through the Virgin Islands at some point. But the tower was a Danish military installation, not a pirate's lair.

None of this diminishes the tower itself, which is a genuinely old piece of seventeenth-century Caribbean fortification with a commanding view of the harbor. You climb to the top and look out over Charlotte Amalie spread below — the red roofs, the cruise ships at dock, the green hills running up behind the town — and the view is worth the climb regardless of whose name is on the sign. The pirate branding is marketing. The stone is real.

Magens Bay

Magens Bay, on the north side of St. Thomas, is the beach that ends arguments about beaches. It sits at the end of a long, narrow bay shaped like a horseshoe, with forested hills rising on both sides and water so calm it barely qualifies as ocean. The sand is white, the water is transparent, and the bay is deep enough that even on the busiest cruise ship days the beach absorbs the crowd without feeling overrun.

The bay is a protected park, donated to the territory in the 1940s by Arthur Fairchild, an American who had the uncommon instinct to buy a perfect beach and then give it away rather than develop it. The hills behind the bay are still green. The approach road descends through trees and then the bay opens below you like something from a film location scout's fantasy. You have seen this beach in photographs. The photographs are accurate. It actually looks like that.

Driving on the left in American cars

The Virgin Islands drive on the left side of the road. In American-made cars. With the steering wheel on the left. This means you are sitting on the center-of-road side of the vehicle, with your passenger on the curb side, and every left turn is an act of faith because you cannot see oncoming traffic past your own dashboard.

The explanation is historical and completely logical: the Danes drove on the left, as was common in the Caribbean colonies, and when the Americans took over in 1917 they changed the flag but not the traffic pattern. American cars arrived because the islands are American territory and import American goods. Nobody reconciled these two facts, and a century later they remain unreconciled. You get used to it in about a day. You never stop finding it strange.

The roads themselves are another matter. St. Thomas in particular is almost vertically hilly, and the roads follow old donkey paths up switchbacks that would give a mountain goat pause. The speed limit is mostly theoretical. Local drivers navigate by a system of horn signals — one tap means hello, two taps mean thank you, a long blast means I am coming around this blind curve and you should not be there — that you will not find in any driving manual but that functions with remarkable efficiency.

The rum

The Virgin Islands have been making rum since the plantation era, and the Cruzan Rum distillery on St. Croix is still operating on a site that has produced spirits for over 250 years. Rum in the Caribbean is not a novelty product. It is the direct descendant of the sugar industry — molasses is a byproduct of sugar refining, and distilling it into rum was how plantation owners extracted value from every part of the cane. The economics have changed. The distillery is now a modern operation. But the continuity is unbroken: the same crop, the same island, the same basic process, for a quarter of a millennium.

The territory's rum production is also, improbably, a significant source of government revenue. Federal excise taxes on rum produced in the territories are returned to the territorial government — a provision that has made rum money a structural part of the Virgin Islands budget in a way that has no parallel in any U.S. state.

The storms

In September 2017, Hurricane Irma crossed the Virgin Islands as a Category 5 storm with sustained winds near 185 miles per hour. It destroyed or damaged the majority of structures on St. Thomas and St. John. Two weeks later — two weeks — Hurricane Maria arrived, also Category 5, and hit St. Croix. The combined damage was catastrophic. Power was out across the territory for months. Some areas went without electricity for nearly a year.

Two Category 5 hurricanes in fourteen days. The islands rebuilt. They are still rebuilding.

The recovery has been long and uneven. Parts of the territory rebuilt quickly; other parts are still visibly marked. Blue tarps on roofs, which became the universal symbol of post-hurricane Caribbean life, were visible on St. Thomas for years after the storms. The territorial government's capacity to manage a recovery of this scale was overwhelmed. Federal aid arrived, but the relationship between the territory and the federal government is structurally complicated — Virgin Islanders are U.S. citizens who cannot vote in presidential elections and whose congressional delegate cannot vote on the House floor — and the recovery exposed those complications in ways that are still being sorted out.

What the storms did not do is make the islands less beautiful. The vegetation grew back faster than the buildings. The bays are the same bays. The water is the same water. The resilience is not a talking point — it is a visible, physical fact. You can see where Irma hit, and you can see what grew back over it, and the second thing is winning.

What you are walking into

The Virgin Islands are American territory that does not feel like America. The currency is the dollar and the language is English and the postal service delivers, but the pace is Caribbean and the architecture is Danish and the food is West Indian and the relationship to the ocean is not recreational but fundamental. People here live with the sea in a way that mainland Americans, even coastal ones, do not. The sea is the economy, the weather, the transport network, and the reason anyone is here in the first place.

Charlotte Amalie is a port town that has been a port town for three hundred and fifty years, and it carries that history in its bones. The synagogue on Crystal Gade — the second oldest in the Western Hemisphere, with a sand floor that tradition says commemorates the desert crossing — sits a few blocks from the warehouses where Danish merchants stored their goods. Fort Christian anchors the harbor the way it has since the 1670s. The 99 Steps climb the hill behind town past old stone walls and bougainvillea that has been growing over those walls for longer than the United States has existed.

You are not visiting a resort destination. You are visiting a place with a history as complicated and as layered as anywhere in the Americas, compressed onto three small islands where you can drive from one end to the other in under an hour. The beaches are real. The history is real. The rum is real. Go.
