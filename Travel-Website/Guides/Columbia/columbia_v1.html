1. South Carolina State House
↳ The working seat of South Carolina's government, built between 1851 and 1903 on the highest ground in the city. Six bronze stars on the north and west faces of the building mark the exact spots where artillery shells from General Sherman's troops struck during the burning of Columbia in February 1865. The Italian Renaissance dome rises 180 feet, and the grounds hold a collection of monuments spanning from the Revolutionary War to the Korean War.

2. Columbia Museum of Art
↳ The state's largest fine art museum, housed in a modern building at the corner of Main and Hampton. The permanent collection of more than 6,000 works runs from Renaissance and Baroque painting to American modernism — anchored by Sandro Botticelli's The Nativity, one of the few works by Botticelli in the American South, acquired from the Samuel H. 3. South Carolina State Museum
↳ The largest museum in the Carolinas, spanning four floors of natural history, cultural history, science and technology, and fine art inside the renovated Columbia Duck Mills — a 1893 textile building that was the world's first all-electric textile factory. The museum's 4D theater and observatory-grade telescope round out one of the most comprehensive survey museums in the Southeast.

1. USC Historic Horseshoe
↳ The original campus of the University of South Carolina, a brick-walled oval of nine antebellum buildings from 1801 to 1855 — the oldest landscaped academic quadrangle in the American South in continuous use. During the Civil War, the buildings served as a Confederate hospital and then a Union hospital. The 1840 Maxcy Monument at the center is the oldest campus monument in the country still standing on its original site.

2. Hampton-Preston Mansion
↳ The finest surviving antebellum townhouse in Columbia, built in 1818 and expanded through the 1850s by successive owners including Wade Hampton I and the Preston family. During the Union occupation of 1865, it served as headquarters for General Slocum and was spared from the burning of Columbia. The mansion retains one of the most complete period furnishing collections of any house museum in South Carolina, covering a full half-century of plantation-era domestic life.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Robert Mills House
↳ The only surviving residence designed by Robert Mills — the first architect born and trained in the United States, and the man who designed the Washington Monument, the Treasury Building, and the US Patent Office. Built in 1823 for Ainsley Hall and later occupied by the Presbyterian congregation, the house is a National Historic Landmark and the anchor of the Historic Columbia campus on Blanding Street.

4. Museum of Reconstruction Era at Woodrow Wilson Family Home
↳ The boyhood home of Woodrow Wilson — built in 1872 and occupied by the Wilson family from 1872 to 1875, when Wilson's father served as a Presbyterian minister in Columbia — has been deliberately reframed by Historic Columbia as the Museum of Reconstruction Era. The reinterpretation makes the president's childhood home a lens for examining the period that defined Black political life in South Carolina and the forces, including Wilson's own later policies, that dismantled it.

1. First Baptist Church Columbia
↳ A National Historic Landmark organized in 1809. On December 17, 1860, the South Carolina Secession Convention convened here — the meeting that set in motion the vote to leave the Union three days later, making this the building where the Civil War effectively began. The sanctuary completed in 1859 retains its original interior slave gallery, and its position at Hampton and Marion places it within the dense web of antebellum Columbia that survived Sherman's 1865 fire.

2. Mann-Simons Site
↳ One of the few documented and preserved 19th-century African American urban homesteads in the South. Celia Mann arrived in Columbia enslaved, earned her freedom, and established a home and business on Richland Street around 1840. The property passed through four generations of her family, surviving emancipation and the collapse of Reconstruction. Historic Columbia has preserved the site and added interpretive structures that outline the footprints of demolished buildings, making the invisible visible on a street that has otherwise erased its Black history.
📖 Wikipedia

🍮 Local Tastes
South Carolina Mustard BBQ
↳ The Midlands of South Carolina is the heartland of America's only mustard-based barbecue tradition. Pork is smoked low and slow, then sauced with a tangy yellow mustard base — a style brought by German settlers in the 18th century and found almost nowhere outside this region of the state.
📍 Shealy's BBQ · True BBQ
BBQ Hash over Rice
↳ A Midlands staple inseparable from mustard BBQ — a thick stew made from smoked pork offcuts slow-cooked with onions and sometimes potatoes, ladled over plain white rice. Hash and rice is served as a side at virtually every traditional BBQ restaurant in central South Carolina and almost nowhere else on earth.

🏛️ The Building Where the Civil War Began

On December 17, 1860, South Carolina's Secession Convention convened at First Baptist Church on Hampton Street. Three days later, the delegates adjourned to Charleston and signed the Ordinance of Secession. The chain of events that followed killed 620,000 Americans and remade the country. The building still stands, still holds services, and still has its original slave gallery intact — the one the congregation's Black members were required to sit in during services where the congregation planned to leave the Union in order to preserve slavery.

Four blocks away on Blanding Street, the only surviving house designed by Robert Mills — the man who designed the Washington Monument — sits next to the Hampton-Preston Mansion, which was spared from Sherman's 1865 burning because Union General Slocum set up his headquarters there. Two blocks further, the boyhood home of Woodrow Wilson now carries the name "Museum of Reconstruction Era." Historic Columbia deliberately renamed it. Wilson's administration resegregated the federal government in 1913, firing or demoting nearly every Black federal employee. The building where he grew up now tells the story of the period his presidency helped end.

Columbia is a small city with a very dense history. Almost every block downtown has a name attached to a consequence.
