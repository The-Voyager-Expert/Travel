A Southern capital that burned, rebuilt, and then spent a century and a half arguing about what the burning meant — and what the rebuilding owes.

The fire and everything after

On February 17, 1865, General William Tecumseh Sherman's Union troops entered Columbia, South Carolina. By the next morning, a third of the city was ash. Who set the fire is a question that has never been settled to everyone's satisfaction — Sherman blamed retreating Confederates who torched cotton bales, Confederate defenders blamed drunken Union soldiers, and the wind that night turned whatever started it into an inferno that consumed eighty-four blocks.

You should know this because Columbia still carries the fire. The South Carolina State House, on Main Street, has bronze stars on its western and southwestern facades marking the spots where Sherman's cannons hit during the approach. The stars were placed there in 1879, not to memorialize victory but to memorialize damage — to make sure no one walking past the building would forget what happened. Other Southern capitals were occupied. Columbia was burned. The distinction matters here in ways that a visitor from outside the South may not immediately register.

The city that rebuilt after 1865 was a different place than the one that burned. The prewar Columbia had been a small planter-class capital — wealthy, slaveholding, politically dominant. The postwar city was broke, occupied by federal troops, and navigating Reconstruction under conditions that were genuinely revolutionary. For a brief period in the 1870s, South Carolina's state legislature had a Black majority, and Columbia was the seat of that government. The story of what happened next — the violent overthrow of Reconstruction, the imposition of Jim Crow, and the century-long consequences — is not ancient history here. It is the architecture of the city you are walking through.

The Horseshoe and the university

The University of South Carolina's Historic Horseshoe is the oldest part of campus — a U-shaped green lawn surrounded by eleven antebellum buildings connected by brick pathways and shaded by massive live oaks. It was built starting in 1805, when the institution was called South Carolina College and its purpose was to educate the sons of the planter class. The buildings are Federal and Greek Revival, and they survived Sherman's fire because the university was being used as a Confederate hospital.

What makes the Horseshoe remarkable is not the architecture, which is handsome but not unique. It is the density of history in a small space. The wall around the Horseshoe was built partly to keep students in and partly to keep the city out. The live oaks are older than the university itself. Walking the brick paths on a warm evening, when the light goes gold through the Spanish moss, you get a version of the Southern campus experience that is hard to find this well-preserved anywhere else.

The State House and what it displays

The South Carolina State House is the building you need to see, not because it's architecturally exceptional — it is competent Corinthian Revival, started in 1855 and not completed until 1907 — but because it is the stage on which Columbia's contested history plays out. The Confederate flag flew over the dome until 2000, when it was moved to a monument on the grounds. It was removed entirely in 2015, after the Emanuel AME Church shooting in Charleston. The flagpole still stands. The monument to Confederate soldiers still stands. The African American History Monument, installed in 2001, stands nearby.

All of these things are within a few hundred feet of each other, and the State House grounds function as an open-air argument about whose history gets memorialized and how. The concentration here is unusually dense, and the proximity of the monuments to each other makes the argument visible in a way that is harder to avoid than in cities where the memorials are scattered across town.

Columbia is a city where the argument about history is, itself, the history. The monuments face each other. So does everything else.

The Mann-Simons site and Reconstruction

The Mann-Simons Site on Richland Street is a small house — modest, easily missed — that carries one of the most complete histories of Black life in the American South. Celia Mann, a formerly enslaved woman, walked to Columbia from Charleston in the 1840s and purchased this property with money she earned as a midwife. The house stayed in her family for four generations, through slavery, the Civil War, Reconstruction, Jim Crow, and the civil rights movement.

The house is now a museum, and it is the kind of place that earns its significance through specificity rather than scale. The objects are real. The rooms are small. The story is documented through deeds, tax records, and family papers. What you learn here is not an abstraction about the Black experience in the South — it is the story of one family, in this house, on this street, navigating 130 years of American history without the protections that most Americans took for granted. Visit it before the State House, if you can. The order matters.

First Baptist and the civil rights map

First Baptist Church Columbia, on Hampton Street, is one of the oldest Black congregations in the city, and its history tracks the full arc of African American religious and political life in the South. During Reconstruction, the church was a center of Black political organizing. During the civil rights era, it was again.

Columbia's civil rights history is less famous than that of Birmingham or Selma, but it is no less real. The lunch-counter sit-ins in Columbia began in March 1960, led by students from Allen University and Benedict College, two historically Black institutions in the city. The students were arrested. The cases went to the Supreme Court in Edwards v. South Carolina (1963), which ruled that peaceful protest on public property was protected by the First Amendment. If you know where to look, the civil rights map of Columbia is as dense as the Civil War one.

The rivers and the heat

Columbia sits at the confluence of the Broad and Saluda rivers, which merge to form the Congaree. The Congaree National Park, southeast of the city, preserves the largest tract of old-growth bottomland hardwood forest in the southeastern United States — massive loblolly pines, bald cypresses, and tupelos rising from a floodplain that is periodically inundated by the river. The Boardwalk Loop trail, a 2.4-mile elevated walkway through the forest, gives you the primeval landscape without the mud.

The heat is the other fact of life. Columbia is one of the hottest cities in the eastern United States — summer highs regularly exceed 100 degrees Fahrenheit, and the humidity adds a physical weight to the air that northerners find genuinely shocking. This is not a complaint. It is context. The heat shaped everything about the city's architecture, its daily rhythms, its relationship to the rivers. Plan outdoor time for early morning. By noon in July, the city belongs to the air conditioning.

Most visitors pass through Columbia on the way to Charleston or the mountains. The ones who stop discover a city that is more honest about the South's hardest stories than almost anywhere else.

At the gate

Columbia does not market itself aggressively. It is not a cute Southern town with boutique hotels and a food scene designed for Instagram. It is a working state capital with a large university, a complicated racial history, and a political culture that is visibly, sometimes painfully, in the process of reckoning with what came before.

What they come for, or should, is the history — layered, contested, unfinished. A city where the State House still wears its cannon scars, where a formerly enslaved woman's house is a museum on the same street as mansions built by the people who enslaved her, where the Supreme Court case that protected the right to protest came from a lunch counter downtown. Columbia does not smooth its contradictions into a tourist narrative. It leaves them where they are and lets you walk between them. That is harder to do than it sounds, and it is worth more than most cities offer.
