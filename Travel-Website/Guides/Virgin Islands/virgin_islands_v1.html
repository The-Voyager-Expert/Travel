Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 15 min → Frenchtown
1. Frenchtown
↳ St. Thomas's oldest French-Creole neighborhood, settled by Huguenots from St. Barthélemy in the 1800s. Fishing boats still line the dock at dawn and the lanes retain a distinct character from Charlotte Amalie's bustle — small bars, local restaurants and wooden houses in faded island colors.

🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Frenchtown · Charlotte Amalie

2. Blackbeard's Castle
↳ A 1679 Danish watchtower on one of Charlotte Amalie's highest hilltops — one of five national historic landmarks on St. Thomas. The tower gave Danish forces clear sight lines over the harbor; the complex today includes terraced gardens, a museum and 360° views over Charlotte Amalie and the surrounding islands.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 9:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Blackbeard's Hill · Charlotte Amalie
🚶 6 min · 🚕 3 min → St. Thomas Synagogue
3. St. Thomas Synagogue
↳ Founded in 1796 by Sephardic Jews who fled the Spanish Inquisition via Curaçao — the second-oldest synagogue in continuous use in the Western Hemisphere. Its sand floor honours the Exodus; the tradition of silent worship has survived hurricanes, fires and two centuries of colonial rule.
📖 Wikipedia

🚫 Closed Saturday & Sunday

🚕 18 min → Mountain Top
4. Mountain Top
↳ The highest accessible point on St. Thomas at 1,547 ft, overlooking Magens Bay — voted one of the world's most beautiful beaches by National Geographic. Views stretch to Tortola on clear days. Mountain Top is credited as the birthplace of the banana daiquiri, first mixed here in the 1940s.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 25 min → Magens Bay
1. Magens Bay
↳ A sheltered crescent of white sand almost a mile long, backed by hills and palm groves, and protected from ocean swells by two headlands. The water is calm enough to swim to the far shore. National Geographic named it one of the world's ten most beautiful beaches.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 6:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~2 hr
📍 Magens Bay Rd · St. Thomas
🚕 10 min → Drake's Seat
2. Drake's Seat
↳ A hilltop viewpoint on Crown Mountain Road with one of the most commanding panoramas in the Caribbean — Magens Bay curving below, Tortola and the British Virgin Islands visible on clear days. Legend holds that Sir Francis Drake surveyed his fleet here in the 16th century.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Crown Mountain Rd · St. Thomas
🚕 18 min → 99 Steps
3. 99 Steps
↳ A staircase of 103 brick steps — named before anyone counted accurately — built by the Danes in the 1700s to connect the harbor to the hilltop neighborhood above. One of the best-preserved Danish colonial structures in Charlotte Amalie, and the steepest shortcut in the city.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Kongensgade · Charlotte Amalie
🚶 10 min · 🚕 5 min → Fort Christian
4. Fort Christian
↳ The oldest standing structure in the US Virgin Islands, built 1672–1680 as the first Danish foothold on St. Thomas. Red-brick and fortified against sea attack, it served as courthouse, church, jail and governor's residence at different points over 300 years.
📖 Wikipedia

🍮 Local Tastes
Conch Fritters
↳ Deep-fried fritters packed with conch — sweet sea snail seasoned with scotch bonnet, lime and island herbs; served as appetizer or street snack across St. Thomas. The essential USVI bite.
📍 Iggies Oasis · Bolongo Bay
Fungi
↳ The Virgin Islands' national side dish — dense cornmeal porridge cooked with okra, similar to polenta. Served alongside fish or stewed meat in local restaurants; a weekday staple across all three islands.
📍 Tuck Shop · Back Street · Charlotte Amalie
Johnny Cakes
↳ Fried cornmeal flatbreads — crispy outside, soft within, subtly sweet. A Caribbean staple found at roadside stands and bakeries island-wide; in the USVI they are fried, not baked.
📍 Tuck Shop · Back Street · Charlotte Amalie
Pates
↳ Flaky fried pastry turnovers stuffed with spiced meat, saltfish or conch — the quintessential USVI street food sold from vendors and small bakeries across St. Thomas.
📍 Charlotte Amalie Waterfront
Painkiller
↳ The signature Caribbean rum cocktail — Pusser's Rum with pineapple juice, orange juice and cream of coconut, topped with grated nutmeg. Invented at the Soggy Dollar Bar on Jost Van Dyke; the USVI's unofficial drink.
📍 Iggies Oasis · Bolongo Bay

🏝️ The Sale of an Empire

On March 31, 1917, the United States took possession of the Virgin Islands — the largest per-acre purchase of territory in American history, more than ten times what was paid per acre for Alaska. The Danish Empire had controlled St. Thomas since 1672; the Americans wanted the harbor to protect the Panama Canal from the German Navy during World War I. The transaction was remarkably bureaucratic for such a profound transfer of 26,000 human lives: Danish and American officers exchanged flags over Government House in Charlotte Amalie, and overnight, people who had never chosen to be Danish became Americans — without being asked.

The harbor they traded away had been one of the most important in the Western Hemisphere. Charlotte Amalie was a declared free port under Danish rule — neutral in European wars, open to all flags — and at its 18th-century peak it was also one of the busiest slave markets in the Americas. The Emancipation Garden at the center of town marks where, on July 3, 1848, the enslaved population of the Danish West Indies rose in revolt and forced the colonial governor to declare freedom unilaterally — 15 years before the American Civil War, and more than a decade before Denmark's Parliament ratified it. A few blocks away, the sand floor of the Synagogue is a quiet counterpoint to all of it: Sephardic Jews who had already fled one empire found sanctuary here under another, and built a house of worship that has outlasted both.
