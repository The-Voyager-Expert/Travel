An island that was almost entirely destroyed in 1953 and rebuilt itself from rubble into one of the most visited destinations in the Mediterranean — and the turtles that gave it a reason to protect the coast.

The Earthquake That Remade the Island

On the morning of August 12, 1953, a magnitude 7.2 earthquake struck the Ionian Islands. Zakynthos was at the centre of it. The shockwave levelled roughly 90 per cent of the buildings on the island, including most of Zakynthos Town, which had been a densely built Venetian-era port city with multi-storey stone arcades, baroque churches, and painted facades that had survived centuries of Venetian, French, and British rule without significant loss. Nearly all of it came down in under a minute.

A secondary fire burned through what the quake had left standing. Of the island's population, around 480 people were killed and the majority of the island's 40,000 residents were homeless. International aid arrived quickly — Britain sent HMS Bermuda, the United States sent Navy ships — and a systematic reconstruction effort began within weeks. The decision was made to rebuild Zakynthos Town along the same street plan as the original but with reinforced concrete, earthquake-resistant structures, and the same colonnaded arcade style that had defined the Venetian city. The result is what you see today: a 1950s approximation of an 18th-century Venetian port, precise in its proportions but hollow in its materials.

A few things survived. The Byzantine icons and ecclesiastical treasures that now fill the Byzantine Museum of Zakynthos were not in churches when the earthquake hit — many had already been collected and moved to a storehouse. Saint Dionysios's silver reliquary, housed in the church bearing his name, was intact. The Venetian Castle above the town, built in the 15th century into bedrock, was damaged but not destroyed. These became the anchors of the rebuilt city's identity: the streets looked new, but the sacred objects inside were old.

Navagio and the Ship That Made a Beach Famous

The MV Panagiotis ran aground in October 1980 on a beach on the northwestern coast of Zakynthos that was almost entirely inaccessible from land — reachable only by sea, enclosed on three sides by 200-metre limestone cliffs, and closed to the open Ionian on the fourth. The Greek coastguard believed the ship was carrying smuggled cigarettes. Whether it was or wasn't, the crew was never charged, and the ship was simply abandoned where it beached.

The hull rusted into its current colour over the following decade. By the 1990s, boat operators from Agios Nikolaos were running excursions into the cove, and a photograph taken by a drone showing the red hull against the white sand and turquoise water had begun circulating internationally. By 2010, Navagio — meaning "shipwreck" in Greek — was appearing on lists of the most photographed beaches in the world. The cove gets between 2,000 and 5,000 visitors per day in peak summer by boat, and the clifftop viewpoint has been developed into a paved platform accessible by road.

The beach itself is closed to overnight stays and to private boats anchoring without a permit, and the cliff path down to the sand collapsed years ago. There is no way in from above on foot. What you can see from the platform is the full geometry of the cove — a perfect right-angle pocket in the cliff face — and the hull of the ship in the centre of it. Up close by boat, the scale of the limestone walls and the silence inside the cove are more striking than any photograph suggests.

Navagio became one of the world's most photographed beaches not because of its sand but because of a rusting cargo ship that no one bothered to remove.

The Sea Turtles and What Protected Them

The loggerhead sea turtle — Caretta caretta — has been nesting on the beaches of the Gulf of Laganas in southern Zakynthos for at least several thousand years. The beaches around Laganas, Gerakas, and Kalamaki are among the most important nesting sites in the entire Mediterranean: each summer between May and August, approximately 1,200 to 1,800 nests are laid on roughly 5 kilometres of beach, producing close to 100,000 hatchlings in a good year.

By the 1980s, the combination of unregulated beach tourism, speedboat traffic in the nesting areas, and artificial lighting that disoriented hatchlings was putting the population under serious stress. Greek environmental groups and international conservation organisations flagged the Gulf of Laganas as one of the most acutely threatened turtle habitats in Europe. Conflict between conservationists, who wanted beach closures and boat restrictions, and local tourist operators, who depended on the beach economy, ran through the 1990s into a series of legal challenges, protests, and incidents that reached the European Court of Justice.

The Zakynthos National Marine Park was formally established in 1999, covering 135 square kilometres of sea and beach. It imposed nesting-season closures on the most sensitive beach sections, restricted motorised boat access within the bay, and mandated night-lighting controls near nesting areas. The park has since become the primary example cited in European conservation law for a functioning marine protected area that accommodates both tourism and wildlife. Nest counts have stabilised, and the glass-bottom boat industry operating out of Laganas now runs under a permit system that limits boat numbers and keeps vessels away from nesting concentrations.

The Venetians and What They Left Behind

Venice ruled Zakynthos from 1484 to 1797 — longer than any other power in the island's recorded history. The Venetians called it Zante and treated it as a significant agricultural and commercial asset: the island's currant production (a small seedless grape still known internationally as "Zante currant") was a major export commodity through the 17th century, and the protected harbour made it a useful waypoint on eastern Mediterranean trade routes.

Three centuries of Venetian rule left deep marks on the island's culture, architecture, and social structure. The island's nobility was organised on the Venetian model, with a Golden Book registering the aristocratic families who held civic authority. The Church of Saint Dionysios, the island's patron, shows Italian baroque influence in its belltower and facade. The musical tradition of kantádes — sung in Italian-influenced harmonies by groups of men, particularly at Easter and festivals — is a direct inheritance of Venetian musical culture. You can hear it performed informally in tavernas in the old town.

The most consequential cultural legacy may be the literary one. Zakynthos in the 18th and early 19th century produced a disproportionate number of Greek poets relative to its size, including Dionysios Solomos, who wrote "Hymn to Liberty" in 1823 — the poem later adopted as the Greek national anthem. Solomos wrote primarily in the Zakynthian vernacular rather than classical Greek, which aligned him with the broader Romantic movement toward literary vernaculars across Europe. His manuscripts and personal effects are in the Solomos Museum on Solomos Square, a few metres from the Byzantine Museum.

Saint Dionysios and Why the Reliquary Matters

Dionysios of Zakynthos was born in 1547 to a noble family on the island, became a monk and eventually the abbot of Anafonitria Monastery in the northwest hills, and died in 1622. The biographical fact that defines his legacy occurred some time around 1579: a man came to the monastery asking to be sheltered from pursuers. Dionysios sheltered him, and the man later confessed that he had murdered Dionysios's brother. Dionysios forgave him and helped him escape the island.

He was canonised in 1703, and his preserved remains — kept in a silver reliquary in the church bearing his name on Zakynthos Town's seafront — have been carried in procession through the town twice a year for centuries: on August 24 (his feast day) and on December 17 (the anniversary of his death). The procession is one of the most attended events on the island's religious calendar. The church's lower floor contains a museum of ecclesiastical art and vestments, and the reliquary itself is visible behind glass on the main floor.

The Anafonitria Monastery where he served retains his original stone cell, a medieval defensive tower, and a small church with 16th-century frescoes. It is the quietest and most historically intact site in the island's interior — the buildings date to the 14th century and the setting is unchanged: terraced hillside with olive groves, a view toward the western cliffs and the Ionian, silence that makes the distance from Laganas feel much larger than 35 kilometres.

The Blue Caves and How They Work

The Blue Caves of Agios Nikolaos on the island's northeastern coast were known to local fishermen for centuries before they became a tourist attraction. They are sea caves — cavities cut into the base of the limestone cliffs by wave action over thousands of years — with underwater openings that allow light to enter from below. When sunlight passes through the clear shallow water and reflects upward off the white sandy sea floor before bouncing off the cave walls, it produces a vivid blue-white illumination that intensifies as the sun rises and the angle of incidence changes through the morning.

The effect is most pronounced between roughly 9:00am and noon, when the sun is at the right angle to push maximum light through the underwater openings. Later in the day the light flattens out and the colour fades to ordinary turquoise. The caves are only accessible by small boat — the standard excursion from Agios Nikolaos takes 20 to 30 minutes to reach the site and operates between roughly 9:00am and 3:00pm before wind conditions on the open water typically make the approach more difficult. Most excursion boats combine the Blue Caves with a pass around the northern cape and, optionally, a beach landing at Navagio on the same circuit.

The Thing to Carry Home

Zakynthos is a small island that has been remade multiple times — by Venice, by an earthquake, by mass tourism, and by conservation policy — and every layer is still visible. The baroque church was rebuilt from rubble. The Venetian arcade is reinforced concrete cast to look like stone. The turtle boats run on permits because the turtles nearly didn't make it. The shipwreck beach is famous because no one removed the ship. None of this is hidden or complicated. You see the rebuilt town and you know it was built recently. You see the nest closure signs on Gerakas Beach and you know the park negotiated their existence against resistance. The island's character is entirely in the surface — in what was chosen to keep, what was chosen to rebuild, and what the turtles showed up to defend.
