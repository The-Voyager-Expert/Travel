3. Flattop Mountain
↳ Alaska's most-climbed peak. A 1,350-foot ascent earns a 360° panorama: Anchorage below, Cook Inlet stretching west, and Denali visible to the north on clear days. The trail is straightforward but rocky near the summit — start early to beat summer crowds.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 40 min → Alaska Wildlife Conservation Center
1. Alaska Wildlife Conservation Center
↳ A 200-acre drive-through sanctuary 40 miles south of downtown. Grizzly bears, black bears, wood bison, moose, musk oxen, caribou, lynx, and wolves roam naturalistic enclosures. One of the finest accessible wildlife encounters in North America.

2. Portage Glacier
↳ One of Alaska's most accessible glaciers, reached by a 1-hour cruise across iceberg-studded Portage Lake aboard the M/V Ptarmigan. Blue ice towers above the water at the glacier face. The Begich Boggs Visitor Center tells the stark story of the glacier's retreat since 1914.

1. Knik Glacier Helicopter Tour
↳ A helicopter flight over the Chugach Range followed by a landing on Knik Glacier — a vast, slow-moving river of ice filling a mountain valley north of Palmer. The panoramas from the glacier surface are staggering: seracs, meltwater rivers, crevasse fields. The most viscerally Alaskan experience in this itinerary.

↳ A compact waterfront deck centered on a bronze statue of Captain Cook, who anchored in Cook Inlet in 1778. On clear days Denali and the full Alaska Range rise above the skyline beyond the water — one of the finest urban panoramas in the state.

🚶 3 min · 🚕 2 min → Tony Knowles Coastal Trail
2. Tony Knowles Coastal Trail
↳ Alaska's most celebrated urban trail: 11 miles of paved path above Cook Inlet from Resolution Park to Kincaid Park. The views of the Alaska Range are extraordinary. In summer, beluga whales surface in the inlet and moose browse the vegetation beside the path.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2 hr
📍 900 West 2nd Avenue · West Downtown
🚕 10 min → Earthquake Park
3. Earthquake Park
↳ On Good Friday 1964, a 9.2-magnitude earthquake — the largest ever recorded in North America — triggered a landslide that collapsed the Turnagain Heights neighborhood into Cook Inlet in minutes. The park preserves the fractured terrain: slumped bluffs, tilted earth, and Cook Inlet views reaching to the Alaska Range.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 5101 Point Woronzof Road · West End

↳ The only road-accessible section of Kenai Fjords National Park. A short walk leads to the glacier face, where dated stakes chart ice retreat across more than a century — one of the clearest physical records of climate change in the world. The Harding Icefield Trail climbs 3,000 feet above for vast icefield panoramas.

↳ A 4-hour cruise from Seward's Small Boat Harbor into Resurrection Bay. Orcas, humpback whales, Steller sea lions, sea otters, tufted puffins, and bald eagles are regular sightings. Book the full-day cruise if staying overnight in Seward — it reaches tidewater glaciers in Kenai Fjords National Park itself.

🏔 On Ice and Distance

Anchorage sits at an unusual junction. The 4th Avenue outside the hotel looks like any mid-sized American city — coffee shops, a brewery, a parking structure — but look south and the Chugach Mountains rise in an unbroken wall above the rooftops. The city doesn't gradually give way to wilderness. The edge is immediate and total, and you feel it every time you get into a car heading anywhere interesting.

This guide visits five glaciers. That begins to feel like excess until you learn that Alaska contains roughly 27,000 glaciers covering about 5% of the state's area. The five here — Knik, Matanuska, Portage, Exit, and the Kenai Fjords icepack — represent the most accessible fraction of a system that stores more fresh water than most countries. All five are retreating. The marker stakes at Exit Glacier, planted decade by decade to chart the ice margin, are one of the clearest physical records of climate change anywhere in the world.
