Seven hundred islands scattered across the Atlantic — and a history far stranger than the resort brochures suggest.

The first landfall

On October 12, 1492, Christopher Columbus stepped ashore on an island the Lucayan Taino people called Guanahani. He renamed it San Salvador and claimed it for Spain, which was bold given that people had been living there for roughly five hundred years before he showed up. This was the first point of contact between Europe and the New World — not Hispaniola, not Cuba, not the American mainland. The Bahamas.

The Lucayans had arrived from South America via the Lesser Antilles, paddling north through the Caribbean chain in dugout canoes. They were farmers and fishermen who lived in villages of thatched houses and practiced a form of agriculture that worked with the thin coral soil rather than against it. Columbus described them in his journal as gentle, generous, and easy to enslave. Within twenty-five years, the entire Lucayan population — estimated at forty thousand people — had been shipped to Hispaniola to work in mines and on plantations. By 1520, the Bahamas were empty.

The islands stayed empty for over a century. No European power bothered to settle them. The soil was too thin for plantation agriculture, the freshwater too scarce, the harbors too shallow for large ships. The Bahamas existed as a void on the map — seven hundred islands that nobody with a flag wanted badly enough to take.

The pirate republic

What the Bahamas lacked in farmland, they made up for in geography. The shallow banks and maze-like channels between the islands were perfect for small, fast ships and terrible for the deep-drafted warships that navies used to chase them. By the early 1700s, Nassau had become the base of operations for the largest concentration of pirates in the Atlantic world.

This was not a romantic enterprise. The pirates of Nassau — Blackbeard, Charles Vane, Calico Jack Rackham, Anne Bonney, Mary Read — were violent opportunists who preyed on merchant shipping along the trade routes between the Caribbean and Europe. But they also built something that looked, from certain angles, like a functioning society. Nassau in 1716 had no governor, no law, no taxes, and roughly a thousand residents who answered to no crown. Historians call it the Republic of Pirates, and for about two years it operated as one of the only democratic communities in the Western Hemisphere.

For two years Nassau answered to no crown — a thousand pirates running the closest thing to democracy in the colonial Americas.

It ended in 1718, when Woodes Rogers arrived as the first Royal Governor of the Bahamas with a pardon in one hand and a gallows in the other. Most pirates took the pardon. The ones who didn't were hanged at the eastern entrance to Nassau Harbor, and their bodies left on display as a message. Fort Charlotte and Fort Fincastle, the stone fortifications that still stand above Nassau, were built in the decades that followed — not to repel foreign navies, but to make sure the pirates never came back.

The loyalist inheritance

The event that shaped modern Bahamian society more than any other was not a local one. After the American Revolution ended in 1783, thousands of British Loyalists — colonists who had backed the losing side — fled the new United States. Many went to Canada. Several thousand came to the Bahamas, and they brought their enslaved workers with them.

The Loyalists transformed the islands. They built the plantation-style houses that still line the streets of Nassau's historic core and the Out Island settlements. They introduced cotton farming, which failed within a generation because the coral soil couldn't sustain it. They established the rigid racial hierarchy that would define Bahamian society for the next two centuries. And they brought a population of enslaved Africans who, after emancipation in 1834, became the demographic majority and eventually the political inheritors of the islands.

The Queen's Staircase in Nassau — sixty-six steps carved from solid limestone — was cut by enslaved laborers in the late eighteenth century, connecting Fort Fincastle on the ridge to the town below. The number is said to honor the sixty-six years of Queen Victoria's reign, though the staircase predates most of that reign and the association was likely applied after the fact. What is not in dispute is who did the cutting. The steps are smooth and precise, carved through living rock with hand tools in tropical heat. They are one of the most visited sites in Nassau, and they are the work of people whose names were not recorded.

Seven hundred islands, thirty inhabited

The Bahamas stretches across roughly 100,000 square miles of ocean, an area larger than the United Kingdom. Within that space sit approximately seven hundred islands and cays, of which roughly thirty are permanently inhabited. The rest are empty — limestone and scrub and beach, visited occasionally by fishermen or boaters and otherwise left to the iguanas and the seabirds.

This emptiness is the defining physical fact of the country. Nassau and Paradise Island, where most visitors land, hold nearly three-quarters of the national population on a combined landmass smaller than a midsize American suburb. Step off New Providence and onto any of the Out Islands — Eleuthera, the Exumas, Andros, Long Island, Cat Island — and you enter a version of the Caribbean that has largely vanished elsewhere. Roads with no traffic. Settlements of fifty people. Beaches that genuinely have no one on them.

Andros, the largest island, is bigger than all the other Bahamas islands combined and has a population of about eight thousand. Its western coast drops off into the Tongue of the Ocean, a deep-water trench that plunges more than six thousand feet — one of the steepest underwater walls in the Western Hemisphere. The island's interior is largely unexplored mangrove and pine forest, home to blue holes — vertical underwater caves that descend hundreds of feet into the limestone bedrock. Some of these holes have never been fully mapped.

The Atlantis question

In 1994, the South African hotel magnate Sol Kerzner bought a struggling resort on Paradise Island and turned it into the Atlantis, a thirty-four-acre complex themed around the lost city of Plato's imagination. It cost several hundred million dollars and was, at the time, the largest resort investment in the Caribbean. The complex includes the largest open-air aquarium in the world, a water park, a casino, and a marine habitat that houses more than fifty thousand sea creatures. It employs roughly eight thousand Bahamians — making it the largest private employer in the country.

Atlantis represents something specific about the modern Bahamas that you will encounter whether or not you set foot inside it. The resort is, for many visitors, the destination itself. Cruise passengers arrive at the port in Nassau, take a cab over the bridge to Paradise Island, spend the day inside the Atlantis compound, and return to the ship without ever engaging with the country they are technically visiting. The resort has its own beaches, its own restaurants, its own version of Caribbean culture — curated, climate-controlled, and priced in US dollars.

This model — the resort as self-contained world — is not unique to the Bahamas, but the Bahamas perfected it. The tension between Atlantis and Nassau, between the resort economy and the local economy, between the version of the islands that visitors experience and the version that Bahamians live in, runs through everything here. It is not a simple tension. Atlantis pays wages that most Bahamian businesses cannot match. The cruise ships bring revenue that the government depends on. But the question of who the Bahamas is for — visitors or residents — has no clean answer, and walking through downtown Nassau on a day when three cruise ships are in port makes the question feel urgent.

Junkanoo

If there is a single event that belongs entirely to the Bahamas — not to the tourists, not to the resorts, not to the cruise lines — it is Junkanoo. The parade takes place on Boxing Day (December 26) and again on New Year's Day, beginning at two in the morning on Bay Street in Nassau and running until dawn.

Junkanoo starts at 2 a.m. on Boxing Day. By dawn the street belongs to the dancers, and the cruise ships have nothing to do with it.

The origins are African. During slavery, enslaved people were given a few days off around Christmas, and they used the time for celebrations that drew on West African masquerade traditions — drumming, costumed dancing, procession. The name may derive from John Canoe, a Ghanaian chief, or from the French gens inconnus (unknown people), referring to the masked dancers. Nobody is entirely certain.

Modern Junkanoo is a structured competition. Groups spend months building elaborate costumes from crepe paper, cardboard, and fabric, working in shacks called "sheds" that function as workshops and social clubs. The costumes can be twelve feet tall. The music is cowbell, goatskin drum, brass, and whistle — a wall of rhythmic sound that fills Bay Street from building face to building face. The groups are judged on costume, music, and choreography, and the rivalry between the top groups — the Valley Boys, the Saxons, the Roots — is genuine and fierce.

What matters about Junkanoo is that it belongs to Bahamians in a way that almost nothing else in Nassau does. The cruise ships are docked. The resorts are irrelevant. Bay Street, which on any other day is a corridor of duty-free shops aimed at tourists, becomes a performance space for a tradition that predates every hotel on the island by two centuries. If you want to understand what the Bahamas is when it is not performing for visitors, Junkanoo is where you look.

What the water holds

The Bahamas sits on two vast shallow-water platforms — the Great Bahama Bank and the Little Bahama Bank — where the ocean floor is close enough to the surface that the water turns a pale, transparent turquoise that looks artificial in photographs and real in person. These banks are the reason the Bahamas exists at all. The islands are the exposed tops of coral limestone that built up over millions of years on these shallow platforms, rising just high enough above the waterline to hold soil and vegetation.

The water is the point. It has always been the point. The Lucayans fished it. The pirates hid in it. The Loyalists tried to farm the land above it and failed. The modern economy floats on it — literally, in the case of the cruise ships, and figuratively in the case of everything else. The Exumas' swimming pigs, the nurse sharks at Compass Cay, the thunderball grotto, the sandbars that appear at low tide and vanish at high — these are the images that sell the Bahamas to the world, and they are not false. The water here is extraordinary.

But the water also holds the country's future in its hands. Sea level rise projections for the Bahamas are among the most alarming in the Western Hemisphere. Eighty percent of the landmass sits within five feet of sea level. Hurricane Dorian in 2019 demolished Marsh Harbour and much of Abaco with a storm surge that reached twenty-three feet. The recovery is still incomplete. The Bahamas is, in the most literal sense, a country that exists at the mercy of the ocean that surrounds it — and that ocean is rising.

None of this is visible from a beach chair at Atlantis. But it is the context in which everything else here happens, and it is worth knowing.
