A city that had no business existing where it does — and built itself anyway, water and all, out of sheer will and a genius for illusion.

A basin, not a place

Start with the geography, because everything else follows from it. Los Angeles sits in a coastal basin ringed by mountains — the San Gabriels to the north, the Santa Monicas cutting through the middle, the hills closing it off from the sea on one side and the desert on the other. There is no major river here. There never was enough fresh water to support more than a modest settlement. The basin traps air the way a bowl traps water, which is why the smog that made LA famous in the mid-twentieth century wasn't bad luck — it was physics. You are standing in a natural container, and everything the city has ever done, it has done inside that container.

This matters because most cities grow where geography makes growth easy — a natural harbor, a river confluence, a defensible hill. Los Angeles grew where geography said no, and grew anyway, past ten million people, because at some point enough people decided it should exist and then built the infrastructure to make that decision real. Keep that in mind everywhere you go this week. Nothing here is an accident. It's all engineering, dressed up as a lifestyle.

Whose city this was first

Before any of it, the Tongva (also called the Gabrielino, a name Spanish missionaries gave them) lived across this basin and the Channel Islands for thousands of years, in villages along the streams that fed what little water the land offered. When you're stuck in traffic on the 101 or wandering the Getty's hilltop gardens, you are moving through Tongva land, and their descendants are still here — a fact the city has only recently started acknowledging in any visible way. It's worth knowing before you walk past the plaques and murals downtown that gesture at it.

The Spanish arrived in 1781 and founded El Pueblo de Nuestra Señora la Reina de los Ángeles with a small group of settlers recruited from Mexico — the founding padrón lists a population that was mostly of African, Indigenous, and mixed descent, not the Spanish colonial elite you might picture. That's worth sitting with: the city that would later build an entire global image industry on a very narrow, very white version of glamour was founded by a multiracial group of settlers who were, in the framework of their time, considered mixed-race and marginal. Olvera Street, in the historic plaza downtown, is the closest thing LA has to a founding-era relic — a preserved (and yes, romanticized) stretch that gestures at the Mexican pueblo this city actually was for its first several decades. It's small and a little touristy. Go anyway. It's the only place in LA where you can stand roughly where the city started.

The theft that made the city possible

Los Angeles could not have grown past a small regional town on its own water supply. The basin simply didn't have enough. What changed that was the Los Angeles Aqueduct, completed in 1913 under the engineering direction of William Mulholland, which carried water more than two hundred miles from the Owens Valley on the eastern side of the Sierra Nevada down to the city. The water rights were acquired — depending on who's telling the story — through legitimate purchase, aggressive maneuvering, or outright deception of Owens Valley farmers and ranchers who didn't fully understand what they were signing away until their valley had dried into a dust bowl and their agricultural economy was gone.

"There it is. Take it." Mulholland's line at the aqueduct's opening ceremony is the closest thing this city has to a founding motto, and it says more than he probably intended.

This is the event, more than any other, that turned Los Angeles from a modest Southern California city into an engine capable of unlimited growth. Every lawn, every pool, every orange grove that once covered the San Fernando Valley, every subsequent decade of sprawl — all of it runs back to that aqueduct. The Owens Valley, meanwhile, never recovered; it remains one of the starker cautionary tales in American water history, and it's the real-world backdrop for the plot of Chinatown, which fictionalizes the scandal but gets the underlying moral right. When you turn on a tap in LA, you're using water that used to belong to somewhere else.

How the industry that runs everything got here

Hollywood exists in Los Angeles largely by historical accident layered on top of opportunism. In the early 1900s, the film business was based in New York and New Jersey, dominated by Thomas Edison's Motion Picture Patents Company, which controlled the key camera and projector patents and aggressively sued or intimidated anyone using the technology without a license. Independent filmmakers went west — partly for the reliable sunlight and varied scenery that Southern California offered for outdoor shooting, and partly because it was a long, inconvenient trip from Edison's enforcers, with the Mexican border conveniently close if a quick escape was ever needed.

By the 1910s and 1920s, the studios that would define the industry — many of them founded by Jewish immigrant entrepreneurs who'd been shut out of more established East Coast businesses — had built their operations across the Los Angeles basin: Hollywood, Culver City, Burbank. What started as a workaround to a patent dispute became the central organizing industry of an entire metropolis. It's easy to think of Hollywood as decoration on top of Los Angeles. It's closer to the truth to say Hollywood is the city's actual civic religion — its largest employer of the imagination, its main export, and the lens through which the rest of the world has learned to see this place, whether or not that image bears any resemblance to the neighborhoods you'll actually walk through.

The streetcars that vanished and the freeways that replaced them

Here's something most visitors don't know: Los Angeles once had one of the largest electric rail networks in the world. The Pacific Electric Railway — the Red Cars — connected the region with over a thousand miles of track at its peak in the early twentieth century, linking downtown to the beaches, the valleys, and outlying towns that are now just neighborhoods. The system was dismantled over the following decades, piece by piece, as automobiles, buses, and a tangle of business interests (real, exaggerated, and conspiratorial accounts of this all circulate — the truth is messier than any single story) made rail increasingly unprofitable and politically unpopular.

What replaced it, starting in earnest after World War II, was the freeway system — an equally massive feat of engineering, built on the same scale of ambition as the aqueduct, that reshaped the entire basin around the automobile. Whole neighborhoods, many of them Black, Latino, and working-class, were cut through or erased to make way for interchanges. The sprawl you'll experience this week — the sense that LA isn't really one city but forty small ones stitched together by asphalt — isn't an accident or a failure of planning. It's the result of a deliberate mid-century choice, made at massive public expense, to build a city around private cars instead of public rail. LA is now, slowly and expensively, trying to rebuild some of what it tore out, one Metro rail extension at a time.

A city of arrivals

Los Angeles has been remade repeatedly by waves of people arriving from somewhere else — the Dust Bowl migrants of the 1930s, Black Americans moving west during the Great Migration and settling largely in South LA, Japanese immigrants and their American-born children building Little Tokyo before being forcibly removed to incarceration camps during World War II, Korean immigrants building Koreatown into one of the densest and most vibrant enclaves in the country, waves of Mexican and Central American immigration that make LA one of the largest Latino cities on Earth, and refugee and immigrant communities from Armenia, Iran, Vietnam, the Philippines, and dozens of other countries that gave whole stretches of the city their character.

Boyle Heights, east of downtown, is maybe the clearest single illustration of this churn — a neighborhood that was at various points a Jewish, Japanese, Mexican-American, and now predominantly Latino community, each layer leaving something behind. This is not incidental texture. The diversity of Los Angeles isn't a marketing line; it's the actual mechanism by which the city has continuously reinvented its economy, its food, and its culture every couple of decades. When you eat well in this city — and you will — you're tasting the direct result of a hundred years of people arriving with nothing and building something.

The Olympics and the habit of reinvention

Los Angeles has hosted the Summer Olympics twice — in 1932, during the Depression, when the city built the infrastructure (including what's now the Coliseum) to announce itself as a serious American metropolis, and again in 1984, when the Games were run at a private-sector profit that changed how cities think about hosting them entirely. The city will host a third time in 2028. There's a pattern worth noticing here: LA periodically uses a giant, artificial, engineered event to force itself to grow up a little and prove something to the rest of the world. It did that with the aqueduct. It did that with Hollywood. It does it with the Olympics. Watch for the construction cranes this week — some of what you're seeing is 2028 already underway.

What to actually tell yourself before you land

Here is the idea worth carrying with you the whole trip. Los Angeles is a place that does not exist by nature's permission. There's no river deep enough, no harbor natural enough, no water supply large enough to explain a city of this size sitting in this basin. Every drop you drink, every lawn you see, every freeway you sit on is the result of a decision made by people, backed by enormous engineering and no small amount of ethically questionable maneuvering, to build a metropolis where one wasn't supposed to grow.

And the city's signature industry is the art of manufacturing convincing illusions — sets that look like other places, faces reshaped into other faces, stories engineered to feel true whether or not they are. That's not a coincidence. A city built on the willful override of its own geography ended up specializing, as its main export, in the willful override of reality itself. Los Angeles decided to exist, and then it built the machinery — hydrological, industrial, and finally mythological — to make that decision permanent. Everything you'll see this week, from the water in your hotel shower to the studio lot down the street, is downstream of that same original act of will. Once you see it that way, the whole city reads differently.
