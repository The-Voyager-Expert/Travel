Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 1 min · 🚕 2 min → Bryggen
1. Bryggen
↳ The old Hanseatic wharf and the reason Bergen exists — a tight row of gabled wooden merchant houses in ochre, red and cream along the harbour. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, it was the German trading hub for dried cod for four centuries, and the leaning facades are the city's signature image.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Bryggen · Bryggen
🚶 5 min · 🚕 2 min → St Mary's Church
2. St Mary's Church
↳ Bergen's oldest surviving building, a Romanesque stone basilica raised around 1130–1170. Its twin west towers survived the fires that razed the wooden city; inside are a gilded late-Gothic altarpiece and a baroque pulpit. Locals still call it Tyskekirken — the German Church.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Monday - Friday 10:00am - 4:00pm
🚫 Closed Saturday & Sunday
⏰ ~30 min

3. Rosenkrantz Tower
↳ The keep of Bergenhus Fortress and Norway's foremost Renaissance monument. A 1270s royal residence was rebuilt in the 1560s by governor Erik Rosenkrantz into a fortified residence and cannon tower. Narrow stone stairways climb past royal chambers to the roof, with a sweeping view over Vågen harbour and Bryggen.
📖 Wikipedia

4. Fløibanen Funicular
↳ Bergen's much-loved funicular, running since 1918, climbs from the centre to the 320-metre summit of Mount Fløyen in about six minutes. The top is a panoramic balcony over the seven mountains, the harbour and the fjord, with forest trails, a troll forest and a café behind the viewpoint.

1. KODE 3
↳ Bergen's finest art museum — the century-old Rasmus Meyers building housing the city's collection of Edvard Munch, J.C. Dahl and Norwegian masters from the Romantic to the Modernist era. Four interconnected galleries look over the Lille Lungegårdsvannet lake.

2. Troldhaugen
↳ The villa where Edvard Grieg composed for 22 years, 1885–1907, perched on a wooded spur over the Nordåsvannet lake. The yellow clapboard house remains as he left it; the composer's hut where he actually worked is down by the water, and a small concert hall hosts summer recitals.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Ulriken
↳ The highest of Bergen's seven mountains at 643 metres, reached in six minutes on the red cable car from the city's east side. The summit plateau gives a 360-degree panorama of fjords, city and open sea — on a clear day you can see as far as the Hardanger plateau.

1. Gamle Bergen Museum
↳ An open-air museum of 55 historic wooden buildings moved from across the city and reassembled as an 18th- and 19th-century Bergen street. Costumed guides animate the baker's, photographer's studio, post office and merchant's house.

2. Bergen Aquarium
↳ The largest aquarium in Norway, on the Nordnes peninsula since 1960, with penguins, seals and a two-million-litre tank of West Norwegian sea life. Twice-daily feeding shows make it a natural stop before the walk out to the Nordnes headland.

3. Nordnes
↳ The long tongue of land west of Bryggen, ending in a park and a 1916 sea bathing pool with cold fjord water and sauna. The residential streets between are Bergen's best-preserved old wooden-house district and the setting for the city's quietest harbour view.

🌧️ A City That Wears the Rain Well

Bergen sees rain on roughly two out of every three days, and instead of fighting it the city built an identity around it — locals will tell you there's no bad weather, only bad clothing. Pack a shell, and a grey sky over Bryggen's wooden facades turns out to be the most flattering light the city has; the colours deepen and the harbour goes to pewter.

There's a neat thread running through this one-day route: it climbs through Bergen's whole timeline in a few hundred metres. Bryggen is the medieval trading town, St Mary's the stone church the Germans worshipped in, the Rosenkrantz Tower the Renaissance seat of royal power — and then the Fløibanen, a 1918 feat of engineering, lifts you above all of it to see how the seven mountains pin the city to the sea. Four stops, eight centuries, one harbour.
