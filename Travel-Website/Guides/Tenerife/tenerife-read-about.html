A Spanish island with an African latitude, a subtropical coastline and a snowcapped volcano — Tenerife is a place where the Atlantic, the Sahara and six centuries of colonial history arrive at the same shore.

The Island

Tenerife is the largest of the Canary Islands, a Spanish archipelago lying in the Atlantic Ocean off the northwest coast of Africa — closer to Morocco than to mainland Spain, and with an African climate that delivers year-round warmth and sunshine to the coast while the 3,718-metre peak of Mount Teide catches Atlantic cloud and occasional winter snow. The island covers 2,034 km² and is home to around one million permanent residents; it receives over 5 million tourists a year, making it one of the most visited destinations in the world.

Puerto de la Cruz, on the north coast, is the island's historic tourism capital — a Spanish town with botanical gardens, Canarian architecture and the seawater pools of Lago Martiánez. The south coast (Los Cristianos, Costa Adeje) is the resort belt, purpose-built for sun holidays. Teide National Park, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, separates north from south and dominates the island from every angle.

The Guanche

Tenerife was home to the Guanche people — Berber-speaking inhabitants who arrived from North Africa thousands of years before the Spanish conquest of 1496. The Guanche left behind a language, place names, rock carvings and a relationship to the landscape that still shapes the island's identity. The dragon tree (Dracaena draco), sacred in Guanche cosmology for the red resin it bleeds when cut, is the island's symbol; the oldest known specimen at Icod de los Vinos is estimated to be around 800 years old.

The Spanish colonial capital of La Laguna, founded in 1496 and declared a UNESCO World Heritage City, was the first planned non-fortified colonial settlement in the Americas and Europe — its grid and plaza model was copied in every Spanish city built in the New World that followed.

Mount Teide

Teide is a stratovolcano and the central fact of Tenerife. At 3,718 metres it is the highest point in Spain and the third-tallest volcanic island peak on Earth measured from the ocean floor; it is also the most visited national park in Spain and one of the most visited in the world. The UNESCO designation covers the extraordinary variety of volcanic geology: ancient calderas, lava tubes, basalt formations called Los Roques de García, and a moonscape plateau called Las Cañadas that was created by the collapse of a much larger volcanic edifice some 150,000–200,000 years ago.

The cable car (Teleférico del Teide) runs from 2,356 metres to 3,555 metres in eight minutes; a permit is required to ascend the final 163 metres to the summit crater, and these are issued in limited numbers and must be booked in advance. The skies above Teide, at altitude and above the cloud inversion layer, are designated an internationally recognised stargazing zone — some of the clearest night skies in Europe are found here, and observatories on the island and on neighbouring La Palma carry major ESA and international telescope programmes.

The North and South

The two coasts of Tenerife are climatically distinct. The north is greener, wetter and cooler — trade winds from the northeast carry Atlantic moisture that breaks against the mountains, producing cloud, mist, banana plantations and a lush, subtropical landscape. Puerto de la Cruz was developed as a resort in the 1960s and retains a character that is recognisably Spanish rather than resort-fabricated — colonial plazas, a working port, a historic centre and César Manrique's extraordinary seawater lagoon complex.

The south coast is drier, sunnier and more uniformly dedicated to mass tourism: Playa de las Américas and Los Cristianos were farmland and fishing villages fifty years ago, and the resort strip that replaced them has little architectural character, but the beaches, the constant sun and Siam Park are the draw. Costa Adeje, to the north of the main resort belt, is the upmarket end — better hotels, fewer high-rises, and the marine zone where whale-watching boats operate out of Los Gigantes.

Landscape and Wildlife

The Anaga massif at the island's northeastern tip is geologically the oldest part of Tenerife — ancient, heavily eroded volcanic peaks covered in a dense cloud forest of laurisilva (laurel woodland) that is one of the last remnants of a subtropical forest biome that covered much of southern Europe and the Mediterranean before the ice ages. Declared a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve in 2015, Anaga contains more endemic plant species per square kilometre than almost anywhere in Europe. The deep ravines (barrancos) that cut down from the ridge to the coast were inhabited by the Guanche and contain some of the oldest continuously occupied rural settlements on the island.

The waters off Tenerife's southwest coast are a permanent habitat for at least two species of cetacean: a resident pod of short-finned pilot whales and common bottlenose dolphins that live in the channel between Tenerife and La Gomera year-round, attracted by the deep underwater canyon system that drops to 2,000 metres close to shore. Loggerhead turtles are also occasionally seen in the same waters.

Food and Drink

Canarian cuisine is a separate tradition from mainland Spanish cooking — closer in some ways to Portuguese and North African food, and with its own staples that appear on every island table. Papas arrugadas (wrinkled potatoes) are boiled Canarian new potatoes in heavily salted water, served with mojo — either mojo rojo (red pepper, garlic, cumin, vinegar) or mojo verde (coriander, garlic, olive oil). Gofio is the ancient Guanche grain flour — wheat, maize or barley, roasted before grinding — that appears as a porridge, kneaded into balls, stirred into fish broth (escaldón), or used as a thickener.

Fresh fish is central to the diet: parrotfish (vieja), sea bream (sama), and amberjack (medregal) are prepared simply, grilled or baked, with papas and mojo. The barraquito is Tenerife's layered coffee drink — condensed milk, Licor 43, espresso, foamed milk and a sliver of lemon and cinnamon, served unstirred in a glass — and is found only on the Canary Islands. The island also produces wine from high-altitude vineyards on the slopes of Teide; the Denominación de Origen Tacoronte-Acentejo on the north slope grows listán negro, the native red grape variety.

Practical Notes

Tenerife operates on Western European Time (UTC+0, same as the United Kingdom) year-round — it does not observe daylight saving time, so it is one hour behind mainland Spain in summer. The currency is the euro. Spain is in the Schengen Area; EU/EEA citizens enter freely, US/UK/Canadian citizens do not require a visa for stays under 90 days.

The island has two airports: Tenerife North (Los Rodeos, TFN) serves mainly domestic Spanish routes and inter-island flights; Tenerife South (Reina Sofía, TFS) handles the majority of international flights. Puerto de la Cruz is 15–20 minutes from TFN and around 60–75 minutes from TFS. Public transport across the island is operated by TITSA; the bus network is comprehensive for the main routes. A tram (tranvía) connects Santa Cruz de Tenerife with La Laguna on the east coast, but does not extend to the tourist north. Rental cars are the practical choice for access to Teide, Anaga and the west coast. Altitude illness is a genuine consideration on Teide above 3,000 metres — the cable car ascends rapidly and altitude headaches are common.
