A city built as a British colonial outpost on the unceded territory of the Lekwungen peoples, where the empire's architecture remains but the conversation about whose land you're standing on has finally started.

An island capital

Victoria sits on the southern tip of Vancouver Island, separated from the British Columbia mainland by the Strait of Georgia and from Washington State by the Strait of Juan de Fuca. You cannot drive to Victoria from anywhere in Canada without taking a ferry. This geographic fact — that the provincial capital is on an island, reachable only by sea or air — shapes everything about the city. Victoria is slower, smaller, and quieter than its mainland counterpart, Vancouver. It has the pace of a place that is literally disconnected from the highway system that links the rest of the country.

The Lekwungen peoples — specifically the Songhees and Esquimalt Nations — have lived on this land for thousands of years. The Inner Harbour, where the ferries now dock and the tourists now gather, was a Lekwungen village site long before the Hudson's Bay Company arrived in 1843 and built Fort Victoria as a fur-trading post. You should know this because the harbour that Victoria presents as its most beautiful feature is also the site of a displacement that the city has only recently begun to acknowledge publicly.

The British layer

Victoria became the capital of the Colony of Vancouver Island in 1849 and then the capital of the united Colony of British Columbia in 1868. The British colonial administrators built a city in their own image: the Parliament Buildings, the Empress Hotel, the Royal BC Museum, the Government House — all of them facing the Inner Harbour in a composition that looks more like a Victorian resort town in England than anything else on the Pacific coast of North America.

The Parliament Buildings are the centerpiece. Designed by Francis Rattenbury, a young English architect who arrived in Victoria in 1892 and won the competition at age twenty-five, they are a Romanesque fantasy in local stone — domes, turrets, and a central copper dome that lights up at night with thousands of bulbs. Rattenbury also designed the Empress Hotel, the massive chateau-style building that dominates the harbour front and still serves afternoon tea in a ceremony that is at once genuinely charming and deeply colonial in its performance of Britishness at the edge of the Pacific.

Victoria's British architecture is beautiful and sincere and built on land that was never ceded by the people who lived here first. Both of those things are true at the same time, and the city is learning to say both out loud.

The British layer is not a costume. Victoria's Englishness was, for a long time, its main identity and its main tourist draw — the gardens, the tea, the double-decker buses, the hanging baskets on the lampposts. What has changed in recent years is the examination. The conversations about indigenous land rights, the unmarked graves at residential school sites across British Columbia, and the Songhees and Esquimalt Nations' growing public presence in the city have shifted the frame. Victoria is still beautiful. It is no longer uncomplicated.

Chinatown and the gold rush

Victoria's Chinatown is the oldest in Canada and the second-oldest in North America after San Francisco's. It was established in the 1850s by Chinese workers who came for the Fraser River gold rush and the railroad construction that followed. Fan Tan Alley, the narrow passage between Fisgard Street and Pandora Avenue, is the most photographed spot — barely wide enough for two people to walk abreast, lined with small shops that now sell souvenirs but were once opium dens, gambling houses, and the social infrastructure of a community that was deliberately excluded from mainstream Canadian life.

The history of Chinese Canadians in Victoria is not a feel-good immigration story. The Chinese head tax, imposed from 1885, charged every Chinese immigrant an entry fee that rose to the equivalent of two years' wages. The Chinese Exclusion Act of 1923 stopped Chinese immigration almost entirely until its repeal in 1947. The Gate of Harmonious Interest on Fisgard Street, the red-and-gold arch that marks Chinatown's entrance, was donated by the government of the Republic of China in 1981. It is a gesture of recognition that came over a century late.

The gardens and the rain shadow

Victoria's gardening culture is not a quaint hobby. It is a consequence of geography. The city sits in the rain shadow of the Olympic Mountains across the strait in Washington State — the mountains wring the moisture out of Pacific weather systems before they reach Victoria, giving the city about half the rainfall that Vancouver receives. The result is a mild, relatively dry climate with enough winter rain to keep things green but enough summer sun to grow things that have no business surviving this far north.

Butchart Gardens, twenty kilometers north of the city, is the horticultural destination that draws the tour buses. Jennie Butchart started the garden in 1904 in her husband's exhausted limestone quarry, and over the following decades she transformed the industrial pit into a sunken garden of extraordinary beauty — terraced beds, waterfalls, and a rose garden that peaks in July. The garden has been continuously maintained by the family for over 120 years and remains privately owned.

Government House opens its grounds to the public — fifteen acres of formal gardens, Garry oak meadows, and a heritage orchard. The Garry oaks here are worth seeking out. Quercus garryana is the only native oak in British Columbia, and the wind-shaped specimens, some of them centuries old, are the signature tree of the region — gnarled, spreading, impossibly photogenic against the coastal sky.

The water and the whales

The Strait of Juan de Fuca is one of the most reliable whale-watching corridors on the Pacific coast. Southern resident killer whales — orcas — have hunted Chinook salmon in these waters for thousands of years, and the whale-watching industry based out of Victoria's harbour has grown into a major tourism operation. The encounters are often close — orcas surfacing within a hundred meters of the vessel, their dorsal fins cutting the water in a line that is immediately, viscerally recognizable.

The southern resident orca population is endangered. There are fewer than 75 individuals. The threats are cumulative: declining salmon runs, vessel noise that interferes with echolocation, and contaminants that accumulate in the food chain. The experience has shifted from pure spectacle to something more complicated — the thrill of seeing an orca in the wild, combined with the knowledge that this population is in decline and the factors driving the decline are not yet under control.

The orcas were here before the fort, before the parliament, before the tea service at the Empress. Whether they'll still be here in fifty years depends on decisions being made right now.

Craigdarroch and the coal barons

Craigdarroch Castle, on a hill in the Rockland neighborhood, is a four-story Scottish Baronial mansion built in the late 1880s by Robert Dunsmuir, a Scottish immigrant who became the richest man in British Columbia through coal mining and railroad building. He died before the house was finished. The castle has eighty-seven rooms, stained glass by the ton, and the kind of woodwork that only existed in an era when labor was cheap and the owners didn't care what it cost.

Dunsmuir's wealth came from coal mines on Vancouver Island that were worked under conditions that would today constitute criminal negligence. The Chinese workers who made up a large portion of the mining workforce were paid less than white miners for the same work. Craigdarroch Castle is a beautiful building, and it is also a monument to extraction — of coal, of labor, of the island's resources — in the most literal sense.

At the gate

Victoria seduces quickly. The harbour is beautiful, the gardens are immaculate, the pace is gentle, and on a summer evening the light across the strait turns the Olympic Mountains lavender against a pink sky. You can spend three days here and feel like you've had a rest that cost very little effort — afternoon tea, a whale-watching trip, Butchart at golden hour, fish and chips at Fisherman's Wharf, and a stroll through Beacon Hill Park where the peacocks roam free and the Garry oaks frame the strait.

But the thing to carry with you is this: Victoria is beautiful because people made choices about what to build and what to erase. The Parliament Buildings are there because the Lekwungen villages are not. The Empress serves tea because the colonial order it represents had the power to define what a capital city should look like. Chinatown survived because a community refused to disappear under a policy designed to eliminate it. The whales are in the strait because the salmon were in the strait, and both are now in trouble. Victoria is one of the loveliest small cities in North America, and it is also a place where every beautiful thing has a more complicated story underneath it. The city is getting better at telling both versions. That's the most encouraging thing about it.
