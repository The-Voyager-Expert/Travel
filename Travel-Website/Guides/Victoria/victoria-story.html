The capital of British Columbia sits on an island, not the mainland. The province's biggest city is somewhere else entirely. This is not an oversight — it's the kind of political relic that tells you everything about how this place came to be.

A Capital on an Island

Here is the thing that catches most visitors off guard: Victoria is the capital of British Columbia, but Vancouver — the province's largest city — is on the mainland. The capital sits on Vancouver Island, separated from the rest of the province by the Strait of Georgia. You cannot drive here without getting on a ferry or a floatplane.

This is not an accident. When British Columbia was still a pair of colonies — Vancouver Island and mainland British Columbia — Victoria was already the seat of power on the island. When the colonies merged in 1866, Victoria kept the title. The mainland lobbied hard for it, and lost. By the time Vancouver exploded into a major port city in the late 1800s, the capital was already entrenched across the water. So it stayed. The government buildings went up, the bureaucrats settled in, and a ferry system grew to bridge the gap. You are visiting a capital city that exists where it does because of a decision made before most of the province's population arrived.

The Architect and the Scandal

The two buildings that define Victoria's Inner Harbour — the Empress Hotel and the Parliament Buildings — were both designed by Francis Rattenbury. He arrived from England at twenty-five, won the Parliament commission in an open competition, and became the most prominent architect in the province. The Empress went up in 1908, a Chateau-style landmark that still anchors the waterfront.

Rattenbury's end was tabloid material. He left his first wife for a much younger woman, Alma, and the couple moved to England. In 1935, Alma's eighteen-year-old chauffeur and lover, George Stoner, bludgeoned Rattenbury to death with a mallet. The trial was a sensation. Alma was acquitted but killed herself days later. Stoner served seven years. The buildings, of course, outlasted all of it. You walk past the Empress and the Parliament every day you are in Victoria, and the stone does not care about the biography of the man who drew it.

The most British city in Canada is the most British city in Canada on purpose. The hanging baskets are a civic program. The double-decker buses are a deliberate choice. The afternoon tea at the Empress is a performance that has run for over a century.

The Britishness — and What It Covers

Victoria leans into its English identity harder than any city in the country. The hanging flower baskets — thousands of them — are maintained by city crews every summer. The double-decker buses are real transit vehicles, not tour novelties. Afternoon tea at the Empress has been served since the hotel opened, and it remains one of the most booked experiences in the city. The whole thing works because Victoria commits to it fully, without winking at the visitor.

But the Britishness is a layer. Underneath it is a much older story. The Lekwungen peoples — the Songhees and Esquimalt Nations — have lived on this land for thousands of years. The Inner Harbour, where the Empress and Parliament now stand, was a Lekwungen village site. The reconciliation work happening in Victoria today is more visible and more honest than in most Canadian cities. You will see it in the public art, in the language used on interpretive signs, in the ceremonial protocols at civic events. It is not finished work. But it is real work, and Victoria does not hide it behind the flower baskets.

A Quarry That Became a Garden

Butchart Gardens began as an exhausted limestone quarry on the Saanich Inlet. Jennie Butchart, the wife of the cement manufacturer who had mined it out, started filling the pit with topsoil and plants in 1904. By the 1920s the Butcharts were welcoming thousands of visitors a year. Today the gardens cover 55 acres, draw close to a million visitors annually, and are designated a National Historic Site.

It sounds touristy because it is. Tour buses line up in the parking lot. The gift shop is extensive. None of that changes what you see when you walk in. The Sunken Garden — the original quarry — is one of the most dramatic designed landscapes in North America. The scale of the transformation, from industrial scar to layered garden, is the point. You do not need to be a garden person to understand what Jennie Butchart pulled off here.

The Mildest Corner of Canada

Victoria's climate is an anomaly. While the rest of Canada deals with real winters, Victoria sits in a rain shadow created by the Olympic Mountains to the south and the Vancouver Island Ranges to the west. The result is something close to a Mediterranean microclimate — mild, damp winters and warm, dry summers. Palm trees grow here. Not as a joke or a tourist stunt, but because the climate genuinely supports them.

This is why Victoria has always attracted retirees, gardeners, and people who want to live in Canada without enduring a Canadian winter. The average January low is around 1 degree Celsius. Snow is an event, not a season. The city blooms earlier than anywhere else in the country, and the growing season stretches longer than it has any right to at the 48th parallel.

The ferry from Tsawwassen to Swartz Bay threads through the Gulf Islands — rocky, forested, and scattered across the strait like something left behind by a retreating glacier. Which is exactly what they are.

The Ferry Ride

You will almost certainly arrive in Victoria by ferry, and the approach is half the experience. The BC Ferries route from Tsawwassen on the mainland to Swartz Bay on Vancouver Island takes about ninety minutes and passes through the Gulf Islands — Galiano, Mayne, Saturna, Pender, Salt Spring. The islands are rocky and forested, scattered across the Strait of Georgia, and the ferry weaves between them at a pace that makes you put your phone away.

This is one of the great ferry rides in North America. Not because of any single dramatic moment, but because the whole transit is beautiful in an understated, Pacific Northwest way. Eagles circle. Seals surface. The islands slide past. By the time you dock at Swartz Bay and drive the thirty minutes into Victoria, you have already decompressed in a way that an airport arrival never allows.

A Waterfront That Works

Fisherman's Wharf, south of the Inner Harbour, is a floating community of houseboats, fish-and-chips stands, and small vendors built on docks. It is not a developer's creation or a tourism board project. It grew from a working waterfront, and it still has that slightly unpolished quality — the kind of place where a painted houseboat sits next to a commercial fishing operation.

The food stalls sell fresh fish tacos, chowder, and ice cream. Harbor seals bob in the water between the docks, fat and unafraid, because visitors have been feeding them for years (you should not, but they have learned to show up). The whole scene is small, walkable, and genuine in a way that manufactured waterfront districts never manage.

Small City, Big Table

Victoria's food and drink scene is disproportionate to its size. The craft brewery density is among the highest in Canada — Phillips, Driftwood, Hoyne, and Category 12 all operate within a short radius. The farm-to-table movement here is not a marketing phrase; Vancouver Island's farms, fisheries, and foragers supply restaurants that would hold their own in cities five times larger.

This is partly a function of geography. The island produces exceptional ingredients — salmon, Dungeness crab, spot prawns, lamb, berries — and the short supply chain means they show up on plates the same day. It is also a function of the city's personality: Victoria is small enough that chefs know their farmers, and competitive enough that nobody coasts.

Ninety Minutes and a World Apart

Vancouver is ninety minutes away by ferry, and the two cities share a province, a coast, and very little else. Vancouver is a glass-tower Pacific Rim metropolis. Victoria is a low-rise garden city that closes early and runs on government salaries, university rhythms, and tourist seasons. Vancouver moves fast. Victoria does not, and does not apologize for it.

You will hear Victorians define their city against Vancouver the way any smaller sibling does — with affection, mild rivalry, and the quiet confidence that comes from knowing they chose the slower pace on purpose. The ferry back to the mainland feels longer than the ferry in. You are not leaving a place so much as re-entering the speed you left behind.
