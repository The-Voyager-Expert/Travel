A colonial hilltop town where the Portuguese rebuilt exactly what the Dutch burned, the drums never stopped, and the carnival belongs to the street — not the stadium.

The Name on the Hill

The story you hear in every guidebook goes like this: when the Portuguese first climbed the hill above the Atlantic coast of Pernambuco, someone looked out at the view and said "Ó linda!" — Oh, beautiful! — and the name stuck. Whether that actually happened is beside the point. What matters is that the name still fits. You stand on that same hill today, look past the red-tile roofs and the coconut palms toward the glass towers of Recife shimmering across the water, and you understand why someone felt the need to say it out loud.

Olinda was founded in 1535, one of the earliest Portuguese settlements in Brazil. It became the capital of the captaincy of Pernambuco and, for nearly a century, one of the wealthiest towns in the colony — sugar money pouring in from the plantations that spread across the coastal lowlands. The churches went up fast. The convents followed. By the early 1600s Olinda had the architecture of a place that fully expected to stay important forever.

What the Dutch Left Behind

In 1630 the Dutch West India Company invaded Pernambuco, and in 1631 they burned Olinda to the ground. It was strategic — they wanted the port at Recife, not the hill — but it was also thorough. The churches, the monastery, the government buildings, the sugar lord mansions: all of it gone. The Dutch ruled Pernambuco for twenty-four years under Johan Maurits van Nassau, who built his capital at Recife and barely looked back at the charred hilltop.

When the Portuguese retook Pernambuco in 1654, they did something unusual. They rebuilt Olinda almost exactly as it had been — same footprints, same baroque facades, same winding streets following the contours of the hill. The town you walk through today is that reconstruction, layered with three more centuries of paint and plaster and bougainvillea. UNESCO inscribed it as a World Heritage Site in 1982, recognizing both the original layout and the stubbornness of putting it all back.

The Dutch burned it flat. The Portuguese rebuilt it from memory. The town you walk through is that act of defiance, layered with four centuries of color.

The Hilltop and the Skyline

The Alto da Sé — the cathedral square at the summit of the hill — is where the geography of this place makes itself clear. You are standing on a ridge that drops steeply toward the Atlantic on one side and slopes into the floodplain on the other. Recife is right there, close enough that its high-rises look like they could topple into the mangroves that separate the two cities. The distance is about six kilometers. The difference is about four centuries.

The Catedral da Sé itself was first built in 1540, destroyed by the Dutch, rebuilt, and has been tinkered with so many times that its current form is a palimpsest of colonial intentions. But the view from its terrace is the real draw. Vendors sell tapioca crepes and coconut water along the edge. The light in the late afternoon turns everything — the rooftops, the palms, Recife's glass facades — the same shade of warm amber. You do not need anyone to tell you why someone once said the word "linda."

The Churches That Survived

Olinda has more churches per square meter than almost any town its size in Brazil. The Convento de São Francisco, started in 1585, is the oldest Franciscan convent in the country — its cloister walls carry hand-painted azulejo tiles telling the story of Genesis. The Mosteiro de São Bento, rebuilt after the Dutch fires, is famous for its gilded baroque altar and a Gregorian chant tradition that has continued, more or less without interruption, for centuries. If you show up for Sunday mass, you hear monks singing in Latin in a chapel dripping with gold leaf while ceiling fans turn slowly overhead. It is not a performance. It is Tuesday morning for them.

These buildings are not museums. They are working churches in a deeply Catholic town, and the contrast between the ornate interiors and the crumbling streets outside is part of what makes Olinda feel so specific. The sugar money that built them is long gone. The faith that maintains them is not.

Carnival on the Ground

If you have any image of Brazilian carnival, it probably involves Rio — the Sambódromo, the grandstands, the sequined floats, the television cameras. Olinda's carnival is the opposite of all of that. It happens in the streets, on the hills, and it belongs to the people walking in it, not the people watching from above.

In Rio, carnival is a spectacle you watch. In Olinda, it is a current you step into — and it carries you uphill.

The signature of Olinda's carnival is the bonecos — giant puppets, some over three meters tall, made of papier-mâché and cloth, carried through the streets on the shoulders of dancers. They depict local heroes, politicians, cultural figures, sometimes just absurd characters with enormous grins. The puppet-making tradition is a craft passed down through families, and you can visit the workshops year-round in the old town. The Casa dos Bonecos Gigantes keeps dozens of retired puppets standing in rows like a warehouse of benevolent giants.

The music is frevo — a fast, syncopated brass-band style born in Recife and Olinda in the early 1900s, danced with a small umbrella held overhead in stabbing, acrobatic movements. Frevo is physically demanding in a way that looks effortless from a distance and impossible up close. During carnival, frevo bands lead blocos (street processions) through Olinda's steep, narrow lanes, and the whole town becomes a single moving party with no ropes, no bleachers, no VIP section. You are either in it or you are watching from a doorway, and the doorway option does not last long.

The Drums Before the Drums

Beneath the frevo, beneath the carnival, beneath everything in Pernambuco, there is maracatu. It is an Afro-Brazilian percussion tradition rooted in the enslaved communities of the sugar plantations — a ritual procession that originally crowned a king and queen of the Congo nation within the enslaved population. The drums are alfaias, large rope-tuned bass drums played with mallets, and when a maracatu group processes through the streets the sound is physical. You feel it in your sternum before you hear the melody.

Maracatu predates frevo, predates carnival as a formal season, predates almost every cultural expression in northeast Brazil. It is still practiced in nações (nations) — community groups that maintain their own rhythms, songs, and regalia. Seeing a maracatu nação rehearse in Olinda is not a tourist attraction. It is a Tuesday evening in someone's neighborhood, and the fact that you are welcome to stand and watch does not mean it is being performed for you.

The Artists' Hill

Sometime in the 1960s and 1970s, artists and musicians started moving to Olinda because it was beautiful, cheap, and unbothered. The colonial houses were falling apart and cost almost nothing. The light was extraordinary. Recife was right there for supplies and galleries but far enough that no one came looking for you. Olinda became to Recife what Brooklyn was to Manhattan in the early 1990s — the place where the creative work actually happened, in studios behind crumbling walls with cats sleeping on the windowsills.

That era set the tone for what the old town feels like today. The ateliers are still there — painters, printmakers, ceramicists working in converted colonial rooms with the doors open to the street. The Ribeira market area at the bottom of the hill mixes art studios with bars and small restaurants in a way that feels neither curated nor accidental. Olinda never became a resort. It never got a waterfront boardwalk or a cruise terminal. It just kept being a place where people make things, and the colonial architecture is the backdrop, not the product.

Twin Cities, Nothing Alike

Olinda and Recife are technically separate municipalities, but they share a metro system, a coastline, and a carnival season. The boundary between them is essentially invisible — you cross it on a bus without noticing. But the cities could not feel more different. Recife is flat, modern, commercial, loud, built on a river delta with bridges and canals and traffic. Olinda is vertical, old, quiet between festivals, built on a hill with churches and trees and the sound of someone practicing drums three streets away.

The relationship is symbiotic and lopsided. Recife has the airport, the economy, the hospitals, the universities. Olinda has the heritage, the cultural weight, the thing that makes people fly to Recife in the first place. Locals move between them without thinking about it — lunch in Recife, sunset drinks at the Alto da Sé, dinner back down the hill. You will probably do the same. The two cities make sense only as a pair, and Olinda is the one that stays with you.

The View You Take Home

At some point during your time on the hill you will stand somewhere — the cathedral terrace, a rooftop bar, a random corner where the street drops away — and look out at the same view that made someone say the name. The red roofs. The palms. Recife across the water. The light doing what tropical light does in the last hour before it disappears. It has not changed in any way that matters. The sugar lords are gone, the Dutch are gone, the captaincy is gone, but the hill is still here and the view is still the same and someone nearby is almost certainly playing drums. That is Olinda. It endures not because it was preserved but because the people on the hill never stopped rebuilding it, painting it, drumming through it, and refusing to let Recife absorb it. When you leave for the airport, you will be thinking about the drums.
