🍮 Local Tastes
Tapioca
↳ Discs of cassava starch toasted on a griddle and folded over coconut, cheese, or carne de sol; the defining breakfast and street snack sold by tapioceiras throughout the historic center.
📍 Tapioceiras · Alto da Sé
Bolo de Rolo
↳ Ultra-thin sponge layers rolled around sweet guava paste; a registered product of Pernambuco protected by state law since 2007; visible spiral when sliced is legally required.
📍 Cafeteria Alto da Sé
Carne de Sol
↳ Dry-salted beef air-cured 24–48 hours in the sertão wind, then grilled to order; served on every menu in the historic center with macaxeira and manteiga de garrafa.

Paçoca de Carne
↳ Sun-dried beef pounded with toasted manioc flour in a wooden mortar until it crumbles into a dry savory mix; served as a side or standalone plate at northeastern kitchens.

Cajuína
↳ Still, unfermented juice of the cashew apple, slow-processed and bottled in Pernambuco; sweet with a faintly astringent finish; served ice-cold at most bars and restaurants.

🥁 The Oldest Living Carnival

Olinda's Carnival predates Rio's and runs by different logic. Where Rio fills a stadium and sells tiered ticket access to one enormous procession, Olinda opens its streets and lets the city become the stage. The bonecos gigantes — the four-metre papier-mâché puppets that lead the processions — are not decorations; they are satirical documents, each commissioned to make a specific point about a specific public figure at a specific moment. A boneco that stops appearing in Carnival has made its argument and retired.

Frevo, the music driving all of it, was engineered as confrontation. It evolved in the early 20th century from the clash between brass bands and the capoeira practitioners who escorted rival groups through the streets; the fast lurching tempo was designed to be impossible to fight to while staying on beat. The small colored parasols carried by frevo dancers were originally weapons. The choreography preserves the evasive footwork of capoeira stripped of its martial intent.
