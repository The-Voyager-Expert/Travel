Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 15 min · 🚕 5 min → Tegallalang Rice Terraces
1. Tegallalang Rice Terraces
↳ Carved into steep hillsides just north of the hotel, the Tegallalang terraces follow the ancient subak cooperative irrigation system — inscribed in Bali's UNESCO World Heritage Cultural Landscape. Arrive before 8am to walk the dike paths between the paddies before the swing-and-pool cafes open.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 7:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~2h
📍 Jl. Raya Tegallalang · Tegallalang · Gianyar
🚶 15 min · 🚕 5 min → Alas Harum · Bali Swing
2. Alas Harum · Bali Swing
↳ Alas Harum is the original Bali Swing venue, set on a ridge above dense jungle with sweeping valley views toward the rice terraces. Multiple swing sizes and photo packages are available on-site; the giant swing is bookable in advance to avoid queues during peak midday hours.

🚕 20 min → Pura Tirta Empul
3. Pura Tirta Empul
↳ Built around natural springs in 962 CE, Tirta Empul is a Hindu water temple where pilgrims immerse in a sequence of 30 sacred fountains to cleanse body and spirit. A sarong and sash — required for entry — are available at the gate; visitors may join the melukat purification ritual alongside worshippers.

🚕 15 min → Goa Gajah
4. Goa Gajah
↳ Goa Gajah — the Elephant Cave — is a 9th-century archaeological site named for the carved stone demon face at its cave entrance. Inside are meditative chambers used by Hindu and Buddhist monks; outside, ancient bathing pools and sacred spring fountains date from the same era.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 45 min → Mount Batur base
1. Mount Batur
↳ Mount Batur is an active stratovolcano rising 1,717 m above a vast caldera lake. The pre-dawn ascent with a registered local guide takes 2 hours; the summit rewards with a 360° sunrise panorama over Lake Batur and, on clear days, the Lombok strait beyond.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Pura Ulun Danu Bratan
↳ Built in 1633 on Lake Bratan in Bedugul's mountain highlands, Pura Ulun Danu Bratan is dedicated to Dewi Danu — goddess of the lake and source of all springs on the island. The multi-tiered meru towers appear to float on the water surface on calm mornings.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 7:00am - 7:00pm
⏰ ~1h
⚠️ Admission tickets at the gate — sarong provided
📍 Candikuning · Baturiti · Tabanan
🚕 25 min → Jatiluwih Rice Terraces
3. Jatiluwih Rice Terraces
↳ Jatiluwih is part of the Balinese Cultural Landscape inscribed as UNESCO World Heritage in 2012, encompassing 600 ha of working rice paddies maintained by the ancient subak irrigation system. The terraces are broader and more open than Tegallalang, with panoramic walking trails and far fewer visitors.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:30am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~2h
⚠️ Conservation fee at the gate

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 1h 30min → Tanah Lot
1. Tanah Lot
↳ Tanah Lot is a centuries-old Hindu sea temple perched on a tidal rock formation just offshore; at high tide the temple is surrounded entirely by ocean. The clifftop esplanade fills fast at sunset — arrive by 4:30pm to claim a vantage point before the crowd peaks at dusk.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 7:00am - 7:00pm
⏰ ~2h
⚠️ Admission tickets at the gate
📍 Beraban · Kediri · Tabanan
🚕 35 min → GWK Cultural Park
2. GWK Cultural Park
↳ The Garuda Wisnu Kencana Cultural Park is home to a 121-metre bronze statue of Vishnu astride the Garuda — Indonesia's national symbol, completed in 2018 after 28 years of construction. The 60-hectare clifftop park hosts daily Kecak and Barong dance performances on open-air amphitheater stages.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 10:00pm
⏰ ~2h
⚠️ Admission tickets at the gate

🚕 15 min → Pura Luhur Uluwatu
3. Pura Luhur Uluwatu
↳ Perched 70 metres above the Indian Ocean on the Bukit Peninsula's limestone cliff, Uluwatu is one of Bali's six directional temples believed to guard the island against evil spirits from the sea. Long-tailed macaques roam the grounds — keep sunglasses, hats, and phones secured before entering.
📖 Wikipedia

🚶 5 min · 🚕 2 min → Kecak Fire Dance
4. Kecak Fire Dance
↳ The Kecak is a choral drama performed by a circle of 70 to 150 bare-chested men who chant a rhythmic cak-cak-cak while enacting scenes from the Ramayana. The Uluwatu cliff-top amphitheater — sun setting over the Indian Ocean behind the performers — is the most spectacular Kecak venue on the island.

🍮 Local Tastes
Babi Guling
↳ Suckling pig slow-roasted on a spit with a fragrant paste of lemongrass, turmeric, ginger, and shrimp paste — Bali's most celebrated ceremonial dish, now served daily at many Ubud warungs.
📍 Ibu Oka · Pak Malen
Nasi Campur
↳ A rice plate heaped with small portions of multiple dishes — lawar, sate lilit, pork sausage, fried tofu, and chili sambals — the everyday Balinese meal that tells you where you are in one bite.
📍 Warung Men Weti · Cok Ubi
Sate Lilit
↳ Minced seafood or pork mixed with grated coconut, galangal, and kaffir lime, wrapped around lemongrass sticks and grilled over charcoal — sweeter and more aromatic than mainland satay.
📍 Warung Sate · Locavore To Go
Lawar
↳ A traditional mixed salad of minced meat, grated coconut, and raw vegetables seasoned with Balinese spices — the ceremonial version includes fresh blood; the restaurant version usually does not.
📍 Warung Lawar Kuwir · Ibu Oka

✨ Claude's Take

Bali asks nothing of you but presence. The rice terraces don't care about your itinerary; the Kecak dancers don't need your applause to feel the Ramayana. Give the island your full attention and it gives back something rare — a place where the sacred and the ordinary share the same road, and a ceremony at the temple gate is not a performance but just Tuesday.

Start each morning earlier than feels necessary. The sunrise from Batur's rim, the mist curling off Lake Bratan, the empty terraces at Tegallalang before the first swing opens — Bali's best moments are in those first quiet hours before the day has fully decided what it wants to be.

And take the Kecak seriously. It is tempting to treat it as a sunset photo opportunity — the ocean light is that good. But sit close enough to feel the chant in your chest and watch the performers' faces. The fire, the circle, the 150-voice percussion — it is one of the oldest living performance traditions anywhere, and it is still made exactly the way it was made in 1930.
