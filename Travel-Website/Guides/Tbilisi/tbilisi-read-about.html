A city at the crossing of every old road between Europe and Asia — and how that position made it one of the most layered, resilient, and genuinely surprising capitals in the world.

The city at the crossroads

Tbilisi sits in a narrow valley where the Kura River cuts through the Caucasus, at a point where the mountains briefly allow passage between the Black Sea and the Caspian. This is not a coincidence of settlement. Every major route linking Europe to Persia, and later to the Silk Road, passed through or near this valley. The city that grew here was a consequence of geography before it was anything else — a place where traders had to stop, where armies had to pass, and where cultures collided and mixed over the course of nearly two millennia.

That collision is still visible. Tbilisi's Old Town holds a Persian bathhouse district, an Armenian church, a Jewish synagogue, a Zoroastrian fire temple, and an Orthodox Christian basilica within a few minutes' walk of each other. This is not a curated display of multiculturalism. It is the physical record of a city that was held, traded, conquered, and rebuilt so many times by so many different powers that it absorbed pieces of all of them.

The founding legend and the hot springs

The traditional account of Tbilisi's founding belongs to the fifth century, to a Georgian king named Vakhtang I Gorgasali. The story goes that he was hunting in the forest when a pheasant he had shot fell into a hot spring and was cooked by the thermal waters. Gorgasali recognized an opportunity and ordered a city built around the springs. The name Tbilisi derives from the Old Georgian word for warm — tbili — and the sulfurous hot springs of Abanotubani have been in continuous use ever since.

The historical reality is more complicated, but the thermal springs are real. The Abanotubani district — its domed bathhouses rising from the cliff face above the river like stone mushrooms — has been the social and commercial heart of the city across every dynasty that held it. Alexander Dumas visited in 1858 and wrote about the baths. Pushkin bathed here. The spring waters, which reach 37 degrees Celsius and carry enough sulfur to be smelled before they are seen, have never stopped running.

Tbilisi has been sacked and burned more than forty times. Each time, the city rebuilt — on the same site, over the same springs, in the same valley.

The long record of conquest

Few cities of Tbilisi's size have a history of conquest as dense as this one. The Persians held it. The Byzantines took it. The Arabs captured it in 645 CE and held it for four centuries, during which it functioned as the seat of an Arab emirate while the surrounding Georgian kingdoms remained Christian. The Seljuk Turks sacked it in 1080. The great Georgian king David IV — David the Builder — retook it in 1122 and inaugurated the Georgian Golden Age, a century of cultural and political expansion that stretched Georgian territory from the Black Sea to the Caspian.

Then the Mongols came. Genghis Khan's forces devastated Tbilisi in 1226. After the Mongols, the Timurids. After Timur, fragmentation into small competing kingdoms. The Safavid Persians and the Ottoman Turks fought over the city and the surrounding region for two centuries, with Tbilisi changing hands repeatedly. Shah Abbas I of Persia sacked it in 1616 and reportedly deported between 100,000 and 200,000 Georgians into slavery — one of the largest forced population transfers in the early modern period.

Georgia turned to the Russian Empire for protection in 1783, and Russian troops annexed Tbilisi in 1801. Russian rule brought stability and the end of the Persian raids, along with the colonial infrastructure that reshaped the city's center. Then the twentieth century brought Soviet rule, Stalinist purges, and finally independence in 1991 — followed almost immediately by civil war, economic collapse, and further conflict with Russia in 2008.

The architecture of layering

What this history produces architecturally is a city unlike almost any other in Europe or Asia. Tbilisi's Old Town is not a preserved historic district in the Western sense — it is not frozen at any particular moment. Instead, it is a vertical and horizontal accumulation: Persian bathhouses below, wooden balconied houses from the nineteenth century overhanging the streets, Soviet-era concrete blocks visible on the surrounding hills, and a scattering of contemporary architecture inserted into gaps. The glass-and-steel Peace Bridge, built in 2010 across the Kura, would look at home in Rotterdam. It sits three minutes' walk from a sixth-century basilica.

The wooden balconies of the Old Town deserve particular attention. These carved latticed structures — projecting outward over the narrow streets, draped with vines, sagging slightly under their own weight — are a specifically Tbilisian form. They are not decorative in origin. They are a practical response to the dense urban fabric of the valley: extensions of the interior space into whatever air is available above the street. That they have become the defining visual signature of the city is a consequence of their ubiquity, not a deliberate aesthetic program. Many are in serious disrepair. Some have collapsed. The ongoing question of what to do about them — restore, rebuild, demolish, or leave — is a proxy war for larger questions about what Tbilisi wants to be.

The church and the state

Georgia converted to Christianity in 327 CE, making it one of the first countries in the world to adopt Christianity as a state religion. The conversion is attributed to a Cappadocian woman named Nino, who reportedly performed miracles in the Georgian court and convinced Queen Nana and then King Mirian III to accept the new faith. The Georgian Orthodox Church has been a continuous institution since that moment — seventeen centuries of unbroken institutional presence, surviving the Arab emirate, the Mongol invasions, the Persian sackings, and Soviet atheism.

This continuity has consequences. The Georgian Orthodox Church is not simply a religious institution. It is a carrier of national identity, a repository of the language and culture that survived every attempt to suppress or erase them. When Georgians say that the church preserved Georgian identity through the centuries of foreign domination, they mean it literally. The monasteries were where manuscripts were copied, where the Georgian alphabet was maintained, where the traditions were kept alive while the cities burned.

The Georgian alphabet is one of only fourteen alphabets in the world still in active use. It has been written continuously for fifteen centuries.

The Holy Trinity Cathedral — Sameba — built between 1995 and 2004 and visible from almost every point in the city, is the largest religious building in Georgia. Its construction was deliberate: a declaration, after Soviet rule, that the church was still here and intended to remain. The cathedral is enormous, perhaps ostentatiously so. But standing inside it, in the candlelight, with the smell of incense and the sound of Orthodox chanting, the historical logic is legible. This is an institution that has survived a great deal.

The wine

Georgia has been making wine for eight thousand years. This is not a marketing claim — it is the archaeologically established fact, based on residue found in clay vessels in the Caucasus that predate every other known winemaking tradition by several millennia. The Georgian method, which involves fermenting wine with the grape skins, seeds, and stems in large clay vessels called qvevri buried underground, produces a style of wine — amber, tannic, oxidative — that has no real equivalent in the European tradition.

This tradition survived Soviet collectivization, which standardized Georgian wine production into industrial output and largely eliminated the diversity of the country's approximately 525 native grape varieties. Since independence, and particularly in the last two decades, there has been a significant revival of traditional qvevri winemaking. Georgia now has an internationally recognized natural wine movement built on indigenous varieties — Rkatsiteli, Saperavi, Mtsvane — and the ancient method that the rest of the world is now discovering as if for the first time.

In Tbilisi, wine is not a specialty or a luxury. It is the default drink. The wine bars in the Old Town serve Georgian natural wines by the glass; the restaurants pour Saperavi with everything. The culture around wine here — the supra, the elaborate feast presided over by a tamada who proposes toasts that can last for hours — is a social institution as much as a drinking custom. Georgians do not toast casually. Each toast is a small speech: to peace, to the homeland, to departed souls, to the guests. The wine is the occasion for the words.

The post-Soviet city

Tbilisi today is a city in rapid, visible transformation. The chaos of the 1990s — when the city experienced blackouts, water shortages, and the near-collapse of state services — has given way to a functioning modern capital with a growing economy and a tourism industry that did not exist twenty years ago. The Old Town has been renovated, if unevenly. New hotels have opened. Restaurants serving Georgian food at a quality level previously unavailable in the city have proliferated. Craft cocktail bars occupy buildings that were derelict a decade ago.

The city's population has grown significantly in the last few years, partly due to the arrival of Russian emigrants following the 2022 sanctions and mobilization — a development that has strained housing, complicated local politics, and produced a visible new presence in certain Tbilisi neighborhoods. The relationship between Georgians and Russians is historically complex, and this newest chapter adds further layers to it.

What the city retains, through all of this, is a quality that visitors consistently notice and find difficult to name precisely. Tbilisi is not trying to be anything other than what it is. The Old Town is genuinely old, genuinely inhabited, genuinely worn. The food is not adapted for foreign palates — the flavors of walnut paste, fenugreek, sour cherry, and pomegranate are the flavors because they are the flavors, not because they test well with tourists. The hospitality is not a product. Georgian culture has an ancient tradition of guest-friendship — stumarmaspindzloba — that predates the tourism industry by several thousand years.

In Georgian tradition, a guest is sent by God. The obligation of the host is accordingly serious.

The view from Narikala

From the ruins of the Narikala fortress on the hill above the Kura, you can see most of what matters in Tbilisi at once. The sulfur-smelling steam rising from the bathhouse domes. The wooden balconies of the Old Town stacked above the riverbank. The silver ribbon of the Kura. The gold dome of Sameba in the distance. The cable car crossing from the old city to the Narikala hill itself — the same route, more or less, that visitors have been taking to reach this viewpoint for centuries.

The fortress dates to the fourth century. It has been damaged in earthquakes and rebuilt, captured and recaptured, expanded by the Arabs and the Persians and the Georgians. What remains is a ruin, and the ruin is instructive. Tbilisi has always been worth taking and worth rebuilding. The city that exists today is the latest iteration of an argument that has been running for sixteen hundred years, between the valley and everyone who wanted to hold it.

The argument, for now, belongs to Tbilisi.
