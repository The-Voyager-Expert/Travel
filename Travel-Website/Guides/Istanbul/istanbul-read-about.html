The city that was the center of three empires across fifteen centuries — and still acts like it knows something everyone else has forgotten.

The city between everything

Istanbul is the only city in the world that sits on two continents. This is not a tourist brochure fact — it is a geographic reality that has determined everything about the city's history, character, and the particular weight you feel walking its streets. The Bosphorus strait, running south to north through the middle of the urban area, is the boundary between Europe and Asia: about 700 meters of fast-moving gray water that has been a choke point of trade, war, and ambition for three thousand years.

Stand on the Galata Bridge at the Golden Horn and look south toward the Bosphorus. To your left is Europe. To your right is Asia. The ferries shuttling between the two shores every fifteen minutes carry commuters who cross continents twice a day without thinking about it. Istanbul's 16 million people have metabolized something that would feel philosophically significant to almost everyone else on earth. This is useful context. The city has been at the intersection of things for so long that being exceptional has become ordinary.

Three of the most powerful empires in history — the Roman, the Byzantine, and the Ottoman — each chose this city as their capital. Constantinople, as it was called for twelve centuries, was the largest and richest city in the Christian world for the better part of a millennium. When the Ottomans took it in 1453 and renamed it Istanbul, they inherited an urban machinery of such sophistication that Mehmed II, the sultan who conquered it, reportedly wept when he walked through the ruins of what the final siege had cost.

What Hagia Sophia actually is

When you walk through the doors of the Hagia Sophia — and you should go early, before the crowds fill it — you are stepping inside a building constructed in 537 AD that was, for nearly a thousand years, the largest enclosed space in the world. The dome rises 55 meters above the floor and appears, through a trick of the windows at its base, to float without support. Byzantine architects achieved this by hiding the weight-bearing elements inside the walls and puncturing the transition zone with 40 windows that flood the space with light. The effect, after fifteen centuries, is still astonishing.

The building has been three things in its life: a Christian cathedral, a mosque, and a museum. When Mehmed II conquered Constantinople in 1453, he rode his horse directly to the Hagia Sophia, dismounted at the door, and converted it to a mosque on the spot — pausing to scoop up a handful of earth to pour over his head as an act of humility before God. The minarets went up over the following century. The mosaics of Christ and the saints were plastered over. For almost five hundred years it was one of the great mosques of the Islamic world.

Atatürk converted it to a museum in 1934 — a secular state's deliberate claim that this building belonged to history, not to any living religion. In 2020 it became a mosque again. The debate it triggered was about what the building means, which is to say it was about what Turkey means.

When you're inside, look up at the half-dome over the apse. You can see two things simultaneously: the Ottoman calligraphy discs with the names of Allah and the Prophet hanging from the dome's pendentives, and the gold mosaic face of the Virgin Mary above the altar, recently uncovered. The building contains its own argument about what it has been. You don't have to resolve it. Just look.

The Ottomans and what they built

The Ottoman Empire at its peak in the sixteenth century controlled territory stretching from Hungary to the Persian Gulf, from Algeria to the borders of Iran. It was the most powerful state in the world, and Istanbul was its nerve center — a city of half a million people at a time when London had a hundred thousand. The Topkapi Palace, where you'll spend several hours if you go properly, was not just a royal residence but the administrative headquarters of a global empire, housing the treasury, the archives, the imperial harem, and the offices of the viziers who ran everything.

What the Ottomans built in Istanbul is still the city's skyline. The Blue Mosque — technically the Sultan Ahmed Mosque, built between 1609 and 1616 — has six minarets, which was considered an act of extraordinary presumption when it was built: only the mosque at Mecca was supposed to have six. The architect Sinan, who worked for three sultans across the sixteenth century, designed 374 buildings in Istanbul alone, including the Süleymaniye Mosque on the Third Hill, which is the one serious students of Islamic architecture consider his masterpiece over the Blue Mosque's more famous silhouette.

The Grand Bazaar, built in 1455 — two years after the conquest — has 61 covered streets, more than 4,000 shops, and somewhere between 250,000 and 400,000 visitors on a busy day. It is not a tourist attraction that happens to sell things. It is a working commercial district that has been operating continuously for 570 years, inside which tourists are one category of customer among many. The carpet and jewelry dealers near the entrance are the most aggressive; go deeper, past the gold shops and the tourist ceramics, and you start to find the sections that sell hardware and electrical supplies and kitchen equipment to the city's restaurants and workshops. That part feels like what it actually is.

The city Atatürk made

The Ottoman Empire collapsed after the First World War, dismantled by the same treaties that redrew the map of Europe. What rose from the wreckage was the Turkish Republic, declared in 1923 by Mustafa Kemal — Atatürk, "Father of the Turks" — who proceeded to redesign the country from the ground up with a speed and radicalism that still feels startling a century later. He moved the capital from Istanbul to Ankara. He abolished the caliphate — the Islamic political authority that had been a central institution of the Ottoman state. He replaced the Ottoman script with the Latin alphabet and required the entire country to learn it in eighteen months. He replaced Islamic law with secular civil codes adapted from Switzerland. He gave women the right to vote in 1934, eleven years before France.

Istanbul lost its status as capital but remained the country's economic and cultural center, and it carries the Atatürk transformation at every level. His face is on every banknote. His mausoleum in Ankara is the most-visited site in Turkey. His image appears in government offices, schools, and restaurants. The secular Turkish state he created has been under pressure for decades from Islamic conservative politics, and the tension between the two visions of what Turkey should be runs through every major political event in the country since the 1980s. The Hagia Sophia reconversion in 2020 was not a religious decision in isolation. It was a statement in that long argument.

The Bosphorus as the city's real center

Istanbul has two European sides and one Asian side, which tells you something about how the geography works. The European side is split by the Golden Horn inlet into the historic peninsula — where the Hagia Sophia, the Grand Bazaar, and Topkapi Palace stand — and the Beyoğlu district, which climbs north from the Galata Bridge to Taksim Square. The Asian side, called Kadıköy and Üsküdar, is where most Istanbul residents who can't afford European-side rents actually live, and it is a different city: less touristy, more residential, with better fish restaurants and a different pace.

The ferry is the right way to cross. The Bosphorus ferries run on a schedule you can find at the dock and cost almost nothing, and a crossing takes about twenty minutes. You can also take a two-hour Bosphorus cruise that goes north toward the Black Sea and back, passing the Dolmabahçe Palace on the European bank and the Beylerbeyi Palace on the Asian bank and the Rumeli Fortress, where Mehmed II staged his cannon batteries before the 1453 siege. The fortresses still stand, across the strait from each other, at the narrowest point of the Bosphorus. They are 550 meters apart. Mehmed built the European one in four months in 1452, specifically to block Byzantine supply ships from the Black Sea. The city fell the following year.

The Bosphorus is not background scenery. It is the reason every empire wanted this city and the reason controlling it was worth almost any cost.

What the city eats and drinks

Turkish food in Istanbul is better than Turkish food you have eaten anywhere outside Turkey, which is not a surprise but is still worth stating. The simit — the sesame-coated bread ring sold from carts across the city — is what you eat for breakfast walking to the ferry. The fish sandwich boats moored at the Galata Bridge have been cooking mackerel on charcoal grills since at least the nineteenth century: fresh fish grilled to order, served in a bread roll with onion and lettuce, eaten standing up on the waterfront. It costs almost nothing. It is one of the best things you will eat in Istanbul.

The mezze culture is strong: a meal at a good meyhane — a traditional Turkish tavern — involves a parade of small dishes that come before the main, including things like ezme (spiced tomato paste), haydari (yogurt with herbs and garlic), and stuffed grape leaves that bear no resemblance to the canned version you may have encountered. The raki — anise-flavored spirit, turned white by water, drunk throughout — is the drink of these meals. You are supposed to drink it slowly, with food, with conversation.

The Turkish coffee is served in small cups with thick grounds at the bottom: don't drink those. The tea is served in tulip-shaped glasses everywhere, all day, offered as hospitality by shopkeepers and carpet dealers and neighbors. Accepting it commits you to nothing except about fifteen minutes of conversation, which is usually a fair trade.

The thing to carry with you

Before you board: here is what changes how you see everything in Istanbul. This city has been the most important city in the world, and it knows it — not with the loud self-advertisement of New York or Paris, but with the quiet certainty of something that has been at the center of history long enough to know that empires end and the city continues. The Byzantines fell. The Ottomans fell. The secular republic is struggling with questions about what it wants to be. And through all of it, the ferries run across the Bosphorus, the tea gets brewed, the fish sandwiches come off the grill, and Istanbul absorbs it.

The buildings encode the argument. The Hagia Sophia holds its Christian mosaics and its Islamic calligraphy simultaneously. The Topkapi Palace was the nerve center of an empire that no longer exists and is now visited by the citizens of countries that empire once controlled. The Süleymaniye Mosque sits on a hill and looks out over a city that has been sacked, burned, reconquered, and rebuilt more times than any history book can make feel real.

Istanbul's gift to its visitors is a particular kind of perspective: the sense that what feels permanent is temporary, and what feels temporary — the human capacity to build something worth inheriting — tends to survive. You will see this in the architecture and feel it at the water. Once you see it, you see it everywhere.
