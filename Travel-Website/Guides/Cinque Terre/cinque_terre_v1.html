Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 46 min → Riomaggiore
1. Riomaggiore
↳ The easternmost of the five villages stacks pastel tower-houses up a steep ravine to a tiny harbor wedged between cliffs — the postcard image of the Cinque Terre. Its one main street, Via Colombo, drops from the station to the boat slip; the Cinque Terre Express train reaches it from Monterosso in minutes.

🏛️ Open 24/7

🚶 4 min · 🚕 3 min → Via dell'Amore
2. Via dell'Amore
↳ The Path of Love is a 900-metre cliff promenade carved into the rock between Riomaggiore and Manarola — the easiest and most romantic walk in the park, hanging right above the sea. Long closed by a rockfall, it has fully reopened and now runs on timed, reserved entry.

⏰ ~30 min

🚶 4 min · 🚕 1 min → Manarola
3. Manarola
↳ Arguably the most photographed of the five — a cluster of rainbow houses tumbling down a black-rock promontory above a swimming cove. The Nessun Dorma terrace and the seaside cemetery frame the classic shot, while vineyards producing Sciacchetrà wine climb the hills behind.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2 hr
📍 Via Renato Birolli · Manarola
🚕 27 min → Corniglia
4. Corniglia
↳ The only village not set on the water, Corniglia perches on a 90-metre headland reached by the Lardarina — a brick staircase of 382 steps — or a shuttle bus. Quieter and more rural than its neighbors, it rewards the climb with terraced-vineyard views in both directions along the coast.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Via Fieschi · Corniglia
🚕 35 min → hotel
Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 36 min → Vernazza
1. Vernazza
↳ Widely called the prettiest of the five, Vernazza wraps a natural harbor with a café-lined piazza, the round Belforte watchtower, and the church of Santa Margherita right at the water's edge. The ruined Doria Castle above the rooftops gives the classic aerial view back over the village.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2 hr
📍 Piazza Marconi · Vernazza
🚶 17 min · 🚕 1 min → Sentiero Azzurro
2. Sentiero Azzurro
↳ The Blue Trail's headline stretch links Vernazza and Monterosso — 3.3 km of cliff path winding through vineyards and terraces, the hardest and most spectacular section of the whole coastal route. The Cinque Terre Card covers access; buy it online or at any village station before the trailhead.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ parconazionale5terre.it
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 50 min
📍 Sentiero Azzurro trailhead · Vernazza
🚶 7 min · 🚕 1 min → Monterosso al Mare
3. Monterosso al Mare
↳ The largest and northernmost village splits into a medieval old town, Borgo Vecchio, and the beach-lined new town, Fegina. It has the Cinque Terre's only real sand beaches, the giant Neptune statue called Il Gigante, and a name built on salted anchovies and lemons. This is your home base.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2 hr

↳ Hand-rolled Ligurian pasta twists tossed in basil pesto — basil, pine nuts, garlic, Parmesan, Pecorino, and local olive oil, traditionally crushed by hand. The region's signature plate.

↳ Monterosso's prized salted anchovies, a Slow Food presidium — served marinated in lemon, lightly fried, or stuffed and baked. The village's most famous catch.

↳ A rare sweet passito wine pressed from sun-dried Bosco, Vermentino, and Albarola grapes grown on the cliff terraces — poured in tiny glasses as a dessert wine.

⛲️ Day Trips by Train
Levanto · 4 min
↳ The sandy beach town just north of Monterosso — surf, a long crescent of sand, and a flat seaside cycle path through old rail tunnels to Bonassola and Framura. An easy, low-key half-day by the sea.
🎫 book at: Trenitalia or Omio
La Spezia · 25 min
↳ The gateway city at the head of the Gulf of Poets — a lively covered market, the Amedeo Lia art museum, a naval museum, and a palm-lined waterfront. The transfer point for high-speed trains and the ferry.
🎫 book at: Trenitalia or Omio
Santa Margherita Ligure · 50 min
↳ An elegant Belle Époque resort on the Riviera, palm-fringed and full of pastel villas — and the easy gateway to Portofino, a short bus or boat ride further along the headland.
🎫 book at: Trenitalia or Omio
Genoa · 1 hr 30 min
↳ Liguria's grand maritime capital — a vast medieval old town of caruggi, the Rolli palaces of Via Garibaldi, Europe's biggest aquarium, and the birthplace of pesto and focaccia.
🎫 book at: Trenitalia or Omio
Pisa · 1 hr 30 min
↳ The Leaning Tower and the Piazza dei Miracoli, with its cathedral and baptistery, make an easy day out — change at La Spezia for the direct regional line down to Pisa Centrale.

❗️ Heads Up
❗️ Via dell'Amore — Timed reservation required
↳ Entry is by reserved 30-minute slot, capped at 200 people; walk-ups often sell out in peak season.
Workaround: Book a slot on the official Via dell'Amore booking site before you arrive.
❗️ Corniglia — Steep 382-step station climb
↳ The village sits on a 90-metre headland; the Lardarina staircase of 382 steps is the main way up.
Workaround: Take the ATC shuttle bus, included with the Cinque Terre Card, up from the station.
❗️ Cinque Terre trails — Card checks and storm closures
↳ The Sentiero Azzurro requires a Cinque Terre Card in season, and sections close after heavy rain.
Workaround: Check open trail sections on the national park site the morning you hike.
❗️ Ferry — No stop at Corniglia
↳ The coastal ferry skips Corniglia, which has no harbour, and does not run in winter.
Workaround: Reach Corniglia by the Cinque Terre Express train or the ATC bus instead.
🍋 Notes from the Cliffs

Until the railway tunneled through these headlands in the late nineteenth century, the five villages were linked only by mule paths and boats — which is why each one still feels like its own small world. That same isolation is what makes the Cinque Terre Express so addictive today: the train spends most of its time in the dark, then bursts out for a four-minute hop and you step off into an entirely different village.

Look up at the hills and you are seeing one of the largest hand-built landscapes in Europe. The dry-stone walls — the muretti a secco — terracing these slopes for vineyards are estimated to run to some 7,000 km laid end to end, longer than the Great Wall of China, all stacked without mortar by generations of farmers.

A small trick that changes the trip: take the train one way and the ferry back the other. From the rails you get the villages in quick, intimate flashes; from the water you finally see what the place actually looks like — those impossible stacks of houses clinging to the cliffs, the way sailors first saw them.
