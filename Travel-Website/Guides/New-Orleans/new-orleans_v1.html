1. Jackson Square
↳ Historic plaza at the heart of the French Quarter, anchored by St. Louis Cathedral and flanked by the Cabildo and the Presbytere. One of the most visited public spaces in the American South and the geographic and cultural center of old New Orleans.

2. The Cabildo
↳ The Louisiana Purchase was signed here in 1803. Now a Louisiana State Museum housing one of the most significant collections of Louisiana history and culture, from colonial-era artifacts to Mardi Gras memorabilia, in a building that predates the United States itself.

3. St. Louis Cemetery No. 1
↳ New Orleans' oldest surviving cemetery, established in 1789. Its above-ground whitewashed tombs form a dense marble city within a city. Entry requires a licensed guided tour; independent access has been prohibited since 2015 to prevent vandalism to the historic tombs.
📖 Wikipedia

4. National WWII Museum
↳ The most comprehensive World War II museum in the United States, located in New Orleans because the Higgins boat — the landing craft that made D-Day possible — was designed and built here. Its campus covers both fronts through immersive exhibits, original aircraft, and first-person accounts.

🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 20 min → Audubon Park
1. Audubon Park
↳ A 350-acre riverside park laid out on the grounds of the 1884 World Cotton Centennial exposition, with a lagoon, live oak allées, and "The Fly" — a broad lawn overlooking a bend in the Mississippi. The green anchor of Uptown New Orleans.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 5:00am - 10:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 6500 Magazine St · Uptown
🚶 28 min · 🚕 6 min → Garden District
2. Garden District
↳ A self-guided walk through the best-preserved collection of antebellum mansions in the American South, developed on former plantation land between 1832 and 1900 for New Orleans' wealthy American newcomers. A National Historic Landmark district since 1974.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Prytania St & Washington Ave · Garden District

4. Mardi Gras World
↳ A working 300,000-square-foot warehouse where Blaine Kern Studios builds the floats for New Orleans' Mardi Gras parades year-round. Guided tours walk through active float construction, storage of past-parade giants, and a den of Carnival props found nowhere else.

1. Oak Alley Plantation
↳ A restored 1839 Greek Revival mansion on the west bank of the Mississippi, named for the 800-foot avenue of live oaks planted nearly a century before the house itself. The tour covers the main house, the grounds, and the reconstructed slave quarters documenting the plantation's enslaved community.

🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 40 min → Honey Island Swamp
1. Honey Island Swamp
↳ A 70,000-acre wetland on the West Pearl River, more than half of it permanently protected wildlife — one of the least-altered river swamps in the country. A flat-boat tour with a local guide moves through cypress groves and a Cajun houseboat village, spotting alligators and herons along the way.
📖 Wikipedia

2. City Park & Besthoff Sculpture Garden
↳ A 1,300-acre park built around lagoons and mature live oaks draped in Spanish moss, home to the free Sydney and Walda Besthoff Sculpture Garden — more than 90 works by international artists set among the trees and water. New Orleans' largest green space, distinct in character from Uptown's Audubon Park.
📖 Wikipedia

↳ Built in 1813 as an architectural twin to the Cabildo across St. Louis Cathedral, the Presbytère now houses the Louisiana State Museum's two defining exhibits: "Mardi Gras: It's Carnival Time in Louisiana" and "Living with Hurricanes: Katrina and Beyond."

🍮 Local Tastes
Beignet
↳ Fried dough square dusted heavily with powdered sugar, introduced to Louisiana by French colonists. The French Quarter version served hot from open fryers is one of the defining street foods of the American South.
📍 Café Du Monde · Café Beignet
Po-Boy
↳ Louisiana sandwich on crusty French bread stuffed with fried shrimp, oysters, or roast beef. Originated in New Orleans in the 1920s during a streetcar workers' strike when vendors gave free sandwiches to the strikers.
📍 Parkway Bakery and Tavern · Domilise's
Gumbo
↳ Thick stew combining West African okra traditions with French roux technique and Choctaw filé powder. New Orleans gumbo is the direct ancestor of the dish served across Louisiana — no two versions are exactly alike.
📍 Dooky Chase's · Mr. B's Bistro
Café au Lait with Chicory
↳ New Orleans coffee tradition: dark-roasted coffee blended with dried chicory root, served with equal parts hot scalded milk. The chicory tradition dates to Civil War-era shortages and became the defining taste of the city's café culture.
📍 Café Du Monde

🌊 Flood Lines

The Cabildo and Jackson Square sit at the same elevation they did in 1803 when the Louisiana Purchase was signed inside those walls. The land under the French Quarter is the highest ground in the city — a natural ridge left by the Mississippi River — which is why it was settled first and why it survived Hurricane Katrina without flooding. Most of the rest of New Orleans was built on drained swampland and sits below sea level. This geographical accident is the reason there is a French Quarter at all.

The above-ground tombs at St. Louis Cemetery No. 1 are often explained by the high water table making underground burial impractical. That is partly true. But the design also reflects the French and Spanish Catholic traditions the city's founders brought with them, where above-ground burial in family vaults was already the cultural norm. The water table confirmed what the culture already preferred.
