The Philippines has more than 7,000 islands, and Palawan is the one that the rest of the country calls the last ecological frontier — a designation that reflects both extraordinary biodiversity and a deliberate political choice about how fast to develop it.

The Shape of the Place

Palawan is a long, narrow island oriented northeast to southwest, running about 450 kilometers from tip to tip but rarely more than 50 kilometers wide. It is the westernmost significant landmass in the Philippines, closer to Borneo than to Luzon, and its fauna reflects that geography: the wildlife here has more in common with Malaysia and the Sundaland region of Southeast Asia than with the rest of the Philippine archipelago. Palawan is believed to have been connected to Borneo during the last ice age when sea levels dropped, allowing plants and animals to cross before the land bridge submerged. The island's species assemblage — including the bearcat, the Palawan peacock-pheasant, and the pangolin — is distinct from the rest of the Philippines and was shaped by that ancient connection.

The limestone karst geography that defines Palawan's look — those vertical rock towers rising from the sea at El Nido, the caves honeycombing the peninsula at Puerto Princesa — formed over hundreds of millions of years as coral reefs were pushed above sea level by tectonic movement, then slowly dissolved by rainwater. The towers and cliffs you see are the insoluble remnants of a process that erased billions of tons of limestone. The lagoons between them — their turquoise color coming from sunlight penetrating shallow water over white sand — were sea floor that gradually became enclosed as karst pinnacles grew around them.

The Underground River

The Puerto Princesa Subterranean River runs 8.2 kilometers through a cave system inside Saint Paul Mountain before emptying directly into the South China Sea. It is one of the longest navigable underground rivers in the world. The UNESCO World Heritage designation it received in 1999 — and the New Seven Wonders of Nature designation from a 2011 popular vote — made it Palawan's most famous attraction and set off the tourism development that now defines Puerto Princesa's economy.

The river is tidal in its lower section — seawater backs up into the cave during high tide, which means the cave's ecosystem is part marine and part freshwater depending on the time of day.

The cave tour takes you about 1.5 kilometers into the river on a paddle boat, far enough to see the main chambers: the Italian's Chamber with stalactites and stalagmites reaching toward each other over millions of years, the Cathedral Chamber where the cave opens into a vast vault, the sleeping colonies of bats that roost in the darkness above the waterline. Beyond where tours go, the cave continues for kilometers in passages that have been mapped but are not accessible to visitors. A nesting population of swiftlets — small birds whose saliva-built nests are harvested in other parts of Asia — occupies the cave's entrance zone, swooping through the tour boats without concern.

El Nido: What the Karst Made

The town of El Nido sits at the base of a limestone cliff at the northern tip of Palawan and is the jumping-off point for the Bacuit Archipelago — 45 islands and islets scattered across a bay enclosed by karst formations. The lagoons that have become the defining image of El Nido tourism — a kayaker paddling through a gap in a cliff into a body of turquoise water surrounded on all sides by vertical rock — were discovered by Western tourists in the 1980s and developed steadily from there.

El Nido was named for the edible bird nests — nido in Spanish — harvested from the cliff faces by the Tagbanua people, one of the indigenous groups of Palawan. The nests, built by swiftlets from their own saliva, were collected by climbers working rattan poles fixed into the cliff face. They were sold to Chinese merchants and eventually made their way into bird's nest soup. The trade was centuries old when Spanish missionaries arrived. The Tagbanua still hold traditional rights over certain areas of the Bacuit Archipelago, including harvesting rights that predate the national park designation.

Tubbataha: what a reef looks like without people

Tubbataha Reef National Park lies 150 kilometers southeast of Puerto Princesa in the middle of the Sulu Sea. Getting there means an overnight crossing on a liveaboard dive boat during the narrow window — March through June — when the seas are calm enough to make the trip. You leave the lights of Puerto Princesa behind around midnight and wake up somewhere that has no land in any direction. The reef rises from deep water with no island attached to it, no fishing village, no harbor. Just the atoll, the sea, and whatever lives in it.

What lives in it: six hundred species of fish, three hundred sixty species of coral, eleven species of shark, manta rays, sea turtles. Visibility regularly exceeds thirty meters. The walls drop hundreds of meters below you into open ocean. If you've dived Southeast Asian reefs that have been fished and bleached and silted, Tubbataha is disorienting — it's what the others looked like before. The reason it looks this way is simple: it was always too far from shore to destroy easily. The Philippine Navy has enforced protections aggressively since the UNESCO designation in 1993. You cannot get there on a day trip. You cannot get there without a permit. The distance is the protection, and the protection is the reef.

A deliberate choice about how fast to grow

Palawan's designation as the Philippines' last ecological frontier isn't marketing — it's a political position that has been actively defended. The provincial government's Strategic Environmental Plan, adopted in 1992, explicitly limits mining, logging, and industrial fishing. Palawan has significant nickel deposits. The plan says no. That choice gets contested regularly by interests who see the mineral wealth as an opportunity, and the restrictions have held largely because enough people in the provincial economy have a stake in the tourism and fisheries that intact ecosystems support.

The tension is real and ongoing. Small-scale mining has been documented inside protected areas. El Nido's rapid growth since the 2010s has put serious pressure on the lagoons that made it famous — boat limits have been imposed and revised, some lagoons closed and reopened. The island's reputation as an ecological success story is earned, and it is also actively contested in ways that don't always appear in the brochure. You're visiting a place in the middle of an argument about its own future, not a settled conservation paradise.

The Tagbanua and the Batak: Peoples Before the Parks

Palawan's indigenous communities — the Tagbanua, the Batak, the Palawan people — have inhabited the island for at least several thousand years. The Batak, one of the smallest and most endangered groups, number fewer than 500 people and live in the interior forests of central Palawan. Their forest knowledge, accumulated over generations of hunting and gathering in the same terrain, is the kind of thing conservation agencies now recognize as irreplaceable ecological data. The challenge, common across Southeast Asia, is that the economic and social forces pushing indigenous groups off customary land and into mainstream Philippine society are far more powerful than any conservation policy.

The national parks of Palawan were created on land that these communities had occupied and used for centuries, often without consultation or compensation. The relationship between indigenous rights and conservation law in the Philippines has improved since the Indigenous Peoples Rights Act of 1997 formalized ancestral domain claims, but implementation has been uneven. When you take the boat to the Underground River or kayak through an El Nido lagoon, you are in a landscape whose current management reflects decades of negotiation between the state, conservation interests, tourism operators, and the people who were here before any of them arrived.

What you're floating in

Before you go, here's the thing to carry with you: every place in Palawan that you're going to — the lagoon at El Nido, the Underground River, Tubbataha if you get there — exists because someone, at some point, decided not to extract it. The karst towers weren't blasted for limestone. The reef wasn't fished to the bottom. The river wasn't dammed. Those are not passive outcomes. They're the result of specific decisions, contested and re-contested, made by people who live here against pressure from people who don't.

You're not just a visitor to a beautiful place. You're one of the reasons the economics of protecting it work. The entrance fees, the guides, the boats, the guesthouses — they're the competing bid against the mine and the trawler. Palawan is a place that looks the way it does because a particular argument about value has been winning. When you paddle into a Big Lagoon enclosed on every side by karst that nobody dynamited, you're floating in the result of that argument. It changes what you're seeing.
