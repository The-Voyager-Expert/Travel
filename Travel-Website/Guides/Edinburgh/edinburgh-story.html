A city built on top of itself — volcanoes, plague pits, and a castle rock that has been occupied for three thousand years — and somehow also the city that gave the modern world its intellectual foundations.

The rock everything started on

Edinburgh Castle sits on a volcanic plug — the hardened core of a volcano that erupted 340 million years ago and was then ground down by glaciers until only this near-vertical column of basalt remained. When you look at the castle from Princes Street Gardens, you're looking at something the ice shaped. The glaciers scraped away everything soft and left the rock, and then left a long tail of harder ground trailing east from it, and the city built itself along that tail. The Royal Mile follows the exact ridge the glacier left behind.

People have lived on the rock for at least three thousand years. There was a hill fort here before there was a Scotland. The Votadini, a Celtic tribe, held it. Din Eidyn — the fort on the slope — gave Edinburgh its name. When the city eventually grew down off the rock and spread into the valley below, it carried that ridge logic with it: everything built outward from the castle, everything shaped by the high ground and the low. You feel this when you walk. Edinburgh is a city that keeps showing you where it came from.

The old town's vertical logic

The Old Town that grew up on the ridge between the twelfth and eighteenth centuries solved the problem of limited space the way medieval cities always do: it built upward. Edinburgh's tenements — called "lands" — reached ten and eleven stories at a time when almost no building in Europe touched six. Thousands of people lived stacked on top of each other along the Royal Mile, aristocrats on the middle floors (high enough to avoid the street filth, low enough not to have to climb too far), the poor in the cramped upper reaches and the cellars below.

The closes — the narrow alleys that cut off the Royal Mile at right angles, often tunneling under buildings — were not picturesque. They were the arteries of a city under constant pressure. Waste went out the window. Plague came through repeatedly. The smell, by most accounts, was extraordinary. Dr. Johnson, visiting in 1773, remarked that "the evening effluvia of Edinburgh" were the most powerful he had encountered anywhere. His hosts were not pleased.

When you duck into Mary King's Close off the Royal Mile, you're stepping into a street that was sealed up and built over in 1753 and left that way for two and a half centuries.

What the Old Town preserved by accident — by being so densely built that whole streets were simply buried under new construction — is now one of Edinburgh's most remarkable qualities. The layers are visible everywhere. The underground vaults beneath South Bridge were built in the 1780s as storage and workspaces; they were quickly occupied by the destitute, then abandoned, then forgotten, then rediscovered in the 1980s filled with the possessions of people who had simply left. The city has been finding itself under itself for centuries.

The Athens of the north

In the second half of the eighteenth century, Edinburgh did something improbable: it became the intellectual capital of the Western world. Not London, not Paris — Edinburgh. The Scottish Enlightenment produced, in the space of a few decades, an astonishing concentration of ideas. David Hume rewrote the foundations of philosophy. Adam Smith wrote The Wealth of Nations. James Hutton founded the science of geology. Joseph Black discovered carbon dioxide. James Watt perfected the steam engine. These men knew each other, argued with each other, ate dinner together, and disagreed productively in taverns on the High Street.

The reasons are complicated — Scotland's unusually democratic education system, the particular energy that comes from a small country trying to prove itself to its larger neighbor, the coffee house culture that mixed lawyers and ministers and scientists in the same rooms — but the results were real. The modern disciplines of economics, geology, chemistry, and sociology all have their roots in this city in this period. When you walk past St. Giles' Cathedral on the Royal Mile, you're a hundred meters from where Hume lived, where Smith argued, where the ideas that built the modern world were first articulated.

The New Town and what it meant

By the 1760s, Edinburgh's prosperity had outgrown the ridge. The Old Town was unsanitary, overcrowded, and increasingly embarrassing to a city that considered itself the intellectual equal of any in Europe. The solution was radical: drain the Nor' Loch — the pestilential body of water north of the castle — and build an entirely new city on the ground beyond it. The New Town that emerged from the 1760s through the 1820s is one of the most coherent examples of Enlightenment urban planning anywhere. George Street and Princes Street run in parallel, connected by Queen Street, all of them straight and wide and rationalist, lined with Georgian townhouses in a uniform neoclassical style.

The contrast with the Old Town is the point. The New Town was built to embody the values of the age: order, reason, proportion, cleanliness. The wealthy moved north almost immediately. The Old Town was left to the poor, which is why it survived — no one wealthy enough to demolish it and rebuild had any reason to stay. The two cities that resulted, separated by Princes Street Gardens, are the most dramatic illustration of class in urban form in Britain. You can see the old medieval ridge and the Georgian grid from the same spot in the gardens and understand two entirely different ideas about what a city should be.

The writers who invented Scotland

Edinburgh has always known how to turn itself into a story. Sir Walter Scott, born here in 1771, essentially invented the romantic idea of Scotland that the world still carries — the kilts, the misty highlands, the noble clans, the tragic Jacobites. His novels sold across Europe. They brought King George IV to Edinburgh in 1822 for the first royal visit in 171 years, a visit that Scott stage-managed almost entirely, dressing the king in a kilt and organizing Highland games and presenting the whole thing as a revival of ancient tradition that was, in large part, recently invented.

The Scott Monument on Princes Street — a Gothic rocket of carved stone 61 meters tall, the second-largest monument to a writer in the world — tells you exactly how seriously Edinburgh took this mythology. It was built in 1844, twelve years after Scott's death, while the debt he'd accumulated trying to maintain his estates was still a live topic. The city understood that the story was worth more than the man's financial failures.

Robert Louis Stevenson grew up in Edinburgh and fled it at the first opportunity, spending most of his adult life in warmer climates, but the city is in everything he wrote. The double life of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde is an Edinburgh story — the respectable New Town Georgian facade concealing something darker below. Deacon Brodie, the respectable city councillor who was also a burglar, was hanged on the Royal Mile in 1788 and gave Stevenson the idea. The bar named after Brodie still stands on the High Street.

The Festival, and what the city became

In 1947, eight theater companies that had been rejected by a new arts festival in Edinburgh showed up anyway and performed in whatever spaces they could find. This was the birth of the Edinburgh Festival Fringe — now the largest arts festival in the world, filling the city every August with more performances than any other festival on earth. The main Edinburgh International Festival and the Fringe together bring a quarter million visitors to a city of half a million people. For three weeks, Edinburgh doubles.

The rest of the year, the city is quieter and considerably easier. The festival infrastructure — the temporary venues, the posters plastered on every surface, the performers handing out flyers on the Royal Mile — gives way to a city that is actually quite manageable. The universities keep it young. The financial sector keeps it prosperous. The parliament, restored to Edinburgh in 1999 after nearly three centuries of union with Westminster, keeps it politically engaged in ways that feel genuinely unresolved.

Scotland voted to remain in the United Kingdom in 2014 by ten percentage points. Then it voted to remain in the European Union in 2016 by 24 points, and England voted to leave.

The constitutional question is not settled. Walk down the Royal Mile on any ordinary Tuesday and you'll pass Yes stickers in windows, EU flags alongside Saltires, the occasional poster arguing for a second independence referendum. Edinburgh is the capital of a country that isn't sure what it is yet, politically — and this uncertainty runs underneath everything. It gives the city an edge that purely tourist-facing cities don't have. Something here is still being contested.

What to carry with you

Before you go, here is the thing that changes how you see Edinburgh: this city has been arguing with itself about identity for as long as it has existed. The Old Town and the New Town argue about order and disorder. The Scottish Enlightenment argued with traditional religion. Scott invented a Scotland that had never quite existed. Stevenson wrote about the respectable facade hiding something else. The independence movement argues with the union. The Fringe argues with the main festival by refusing to be curated at all.

Edinburgh's particular genius is that it has always generated more energy from these arguments than it loses to them. The tensions don't resolve — they produce. The city on the volcanic rock, built in layers on top of itself, keeps finding new arguments to have about what it is and what it should be. Once you see that pattern, you see it in the castle and the parliament building and the closes and the streets. It's not a problem Edinburgh is trying to solve. It's how Edinburgh thinks.
