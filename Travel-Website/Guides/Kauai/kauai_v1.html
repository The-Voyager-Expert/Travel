Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 40 min → Wailua Falls
1. Wailua Falls
↳ Twin cascades of 80 feet plunging into a jungle pool at the edge of Kauai's east-side cane fields. One of Kauai's most recognised landmarks — the opening scene of the original Fantasy Island series was filmed here. The falls are fed by the wet slopes of Mt. Waialeale, one of the wettest places on Earth.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 7:00am - 7:45pm
⏰ ~30 min
🆓 Free

🚕 50 min → Waimea Canyon State Park
2. Waimea Canyon State Park
↳ The "Grand Canyon of the Pacific" — ten miles long and 3,000 feet deep. Iron-red and ochre rock walls, silver waterfalls threading green ridges. Mark Twain's comparison stuck: nothing on the island prepares you for the first overlook.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 7:00am - 7:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Waimea Canyon Dr · Hwy 550 · Waimea
🚕 20 min → Kalalau Lookout / Kōkeʻe State Park
3. Kalalau Lookout / Kōkeʻe State Park
↳ The most dramatic viewpoint on Kauai: a 4,000-foot cliff edge over the Kalalau Valley and the Nā Pali Coast. Kōkeʻe State Park surrounds the lookout with 4,345 acres of mountain forest — a cooler, quieter Kauai of native trees, wild plum groves, and trails.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 7:00am - 7:00pm
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Kōkeʻe Rd · Hwy 550 · Kōkeʻe
🚕 45 min → Spouting Horn
4. Spouting Horn
↳ Ocean swells force seawater through a lava tube at the Poipu shoreline, shooting it 50 feet into the air with a thunderous hiss. A second tube produces Spouting Horn's signature moan — audible before you see the water. Local artisan stands line the path.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Lawai Rd · Poipu

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 40 min → Kilauea Point National Wildlife Refuge
1. Kilauea Point National Wildlife Refuge
↳ A basalt headland on the island's northernmost point, topped by a 1913 lighthouse and protected as a federal wildlife refuge. Frigatebirds, red-footed boobies, Laysan albatross, and Hawaiian monk seals use the cliffs; on clear days the ocean view from the lighthouse extends across the channel toward Oahu.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Saturday 10:00am - 4:00pm
🚫 Closed Sunday, Monday
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Lighthouse Rd · Kilauea
🚕 15 min → Hanalei Valley Lookout
2. Hanalei Valley Lookout
↳ A roadside pullout on the rim above the Hanalei Valley — taro paddies threading the Hanalei River between thousand-foot fluted ridges. One of the oldest taro-growing areas in Hawaii, now a working farm and federal wildlife refuge for endangered Hawaiian waterbirds including the nēnē and the Hawaiian stilt.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~20 min
📍 Kūhiō Hwy · Princeville
🚕 5 min → Hanalei Bay
3. Hanalei Bay
↳ A two-mile crescent of sand backed by the fluted ridgelines of the Nā Pali — the quintessential Kauai image. The bay's depth and fetch make it one of the premier stand-up paddleboarding and windsurfing spots in Hawaii; in summer the water is calm enough for swimming from Black Pot Beach at the pier.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Weke Rd · Hanalei
🚕 20 min → Keʻe Beach
4. Keʻe Beach
↳ A pocket beach at the end of the north shore road — the last sand before the Nā Pali Coast makes the island impassable. The reef just offshore is the best snorkelling on the north shore; the Kalalau Trail, an 11-mile Na Pali cliff path, begins at the beach's west end above the lava shelf.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
⚠️ Parking reservation required in summer — book through Hawaii State Parks before visiting
📍 End of Hwy 560 · Haʻena

🍮 Local Tastes
Plate Lunch
↳ The everyday meal of Hawaii: white rice, macaroni salad, and a protein — kalua pig, chicken katsu, or loco moco. Descended from the bento boxes that plantation workers of multiple ethnicities shared; Kauai's most honest culinary expression.
📍 Mark's Place
Shave Ice
↳ Finely shaved ice packed into a cone and drenched in flavoured syrup. At its best: azuki beans at the bottom, ice cream underneath, and li hing mui powder over the top. Descended from Japanese kakigori brought by plantation workers.
📍 Jo-Jo's Shave Ice · Wishing Well Shave Ice
Saimin
↳ Hawaii's local noodle soup — thin wheat-egg noodles in dashi broth with fish cake, char siu, and wontons. Not ramen; saimin developed in the plantation camps from multiple Asian noodle traditions and is eaten here and nowhere else.
📍 Hamura's Saimin Stand
Loco Moco
↳ White rice topped with a hamburger patty, a fried egg, and brown gravy — invented in Hilo and now a staple throughout Hawaii. Simple, filling, and entirely its own thing. Available at every plate lunch counter and diner on the island.
📍 Mark's Place

🌊 The Wettest Spot on Earth · and the Driest Island

Mt. Waialeale, the volcano that created Kauai, sits in the middle of the island and receives close to 460 inches of rain per year — placing it in serious contention for the wettest spot on Earth. The summit is almost perpetually wrapped in clouds. But the south shore, where the Grand Hyatt sits, is in the rain shadow of that same mountain and averages 300 sunny days a year. A half-hour drive separates two of the most extreme weather environments in the United States. The canyon you visited today exists precisely because of this hydrological paradox: Waialeale's rain floods the Waimea River, and the Waimea River spent four million years carving the canyon.

Kauai is geologically the oldest of the main Hawaiian Islands — roughly five million years compared to the Big Island's half-million. By the time the Big Island is even as old as Kauai is now, Kauai itself may be largely submerged. The Hawaiian Islands are moving northwest over a stationary volcanic hotspot; the ones you see today are the current expression of that process, and older islands to the northwest including Niihau and the string of tiny atolls extending toward Midway mark the path the chain has already travelled. Wailua Falls, where the day started, flows directly off the flanks of that ancient five-million-year-old shield.

The name "Kauai" is often translated as "place around the neck" or "season" depending on the source — the exact meaning is debated. But in Hawaiian oral tradition, Kauai appears in the creation chant Kumulipo as one of the first islands named. It was also the only major Hawaiian island that Kamehameha I never conquered militarily — two invasion fleets were turned back, one by a storm, one by a disease outbreak among his troops. Kauai eventually joined the Hawaiian Kingdom through negotiation rather than battle, which makes it, by a certain reading, the one island that agreed to join.
