Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 20 min → Meiji Shrine
1. Meiji Shrine
↳ Japan's most visited Shinto shrine — Emperor Meiji enshrined in a forested precinct of 120,000 donated trees. The 700-metre approach is one of the great transitional walks: full street noise to near-silence within two minutes. The inner precinct holds the main shrine hall, a treasury, and the Iris Garden.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 5:30am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1h 30 min
📍 1-1 Yoyogikamizonocho · Yoyogi · Shibuya
🚶 28 min · 🚕 10 min → Shinjuku Gyoen
2. Shinjuku Gyoen
↳ A 58-hectare former imperial garden with three landscaping traditions: a French formal parterre, an English landscape garden of rolling lawns and ornamental ponds, and a traditional Japanese garden with stone lanterns and a teahouse. The most celebrated sakura viewing garden in the city — spring crowds are extreme.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Sunday 9:00am - 6:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~2h
📍 11 Naitomachi · Shinjuku

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 12 min → teamLab Borderless
1. teamLab Borderless
↳ A boundless digital art museum — 65 immersive artworks where light, water, and projection bleed between rooms and respond to visitors. Unlike Planets, Borderless has no fixed route — each room evolves through the day. Azabudai Hills adds new installations. Tickets sell out weeks ahead.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 7:00pm
⏰ ~2h 30 min
📍 Azabudai Hills · 1-2-4 Azabudai · Minato
🚶 9 min · 🚕 4 min → Mori Art Museum
2. Mori Art Museum
↳ Contemporary art museum on the 53rd floor of Roppongi Hills Mori Tower, 250 metres above street level. Exhibitions rotate two to three times per year, focusing on Asian and international contemporary artists. Entry includes Tokyo City View — a 360-degree observation gallery looking out across the Kanto plain.

↳ Four immersive installations in Toyosu where visitors move barefoot through the art — wading through water, lying on mirrored floors surrounded by crystalline spheres, stepping into a room that continuously blooms and dissolves in flowers. More physical and focused than Borderless. Timed entry required.

2. Tokyo Skytree
↳ At 634 metres the world's tallest tower. Two observation decks: Tembo Deck at 350 metres and Tembo Galleria at 450 metres, connected by a glass-floored spiral walkway. At night the tower is illuminated in two alternating colour schemes — Iki and Miyabi. The base holds Solamachi, a 300-shop dining and retail complex.

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 15 min → Tokyo National Museum
1. Tokyo National Museum
↳ Japan's oldest and largest museum — 120,000 objects across five buildings in Ueno Park. The main Honkan traces Japanese art from Jomon to Edo: samurai armour, Buddhist sculpture, ukiyo-e prints, lacquerware, and ceramics. The Toyokan covers Asian art. The Heiseikan holds rotating archaeological exhibitions.
📖 Wikipedia

🚕 15 min → Imperial Palace East Garden
2. Imperial Palace East Garden
↳ Former site of Edo Castle's innermost ward, redesigned as a landscape garden after the castle tower burned. Retains the massive stone foundations of Japan's tallest castle keep, three teahouses, and a landscape of ponds. The Ninomaru garden is planted with species from classical Japanese poetry. Free admission.
📖 Wikipedia

🚶 28 min · 🚕 10 min → Hamarikyu Gardens
3. Hamarikyu Gardens
↳ Tidal gardens at Tokyo Bay — former duckhunting grounds of the Tokugawa shoguns. Seawater fills the central pond at high tide, framing a teahouse on a small island. The garden is ringed by skyscrapers and elevated highways: the contrast between Edo-era stones and the glass towers is the entire point.

🍮 Local Tastes
Sushi · Edomae
↳ The Tokyo style — nigiri formed to order with hand-pressed rice to a precise vinegar balance, topped with fish marinated, cured, or aged to Edo-era technique. Tsukiji Outer Market has the best walk-in cluster of sushi counters in the city.
📍 Tsukiji Outer Market · Kyubey
Ramen · Tokyo Shoyu
↳ Clear dark-brown soy broth on chicken and dashi — lighter than Sapporo miso or Hakata tonkotsu. Toppings: chashu pork belly, soft-boiled egg, bamboo shoots, nori. Ginza Kagari and Fuunji are the closest quality ramen counters.
📍 Ginza Kagari · Fuunji
Monjayaki
↳ Tokyo's own pancake — soupy flour batter with fillings, cooked on an iron plate until it forms a crispy lace around a soft liquid centre. Found almost exclusively in Tsukishima, which has over 70 restaurants on one street.
📍 Tsukishima Monja Street
Yakitori
↳ Chicken — every part of the bird — skewered and grilled over binchotan charcoal. Seasoned with salt or sweet soy tare. The alleys under the train tracks at Yurakucho Station have been operating for decades and are 100 metres from the hotel.
📍 Yurakucho Yakitori Alley · Torikizoku
Wagashi
↳ Traditional confections of bean paste, rice flour, and agar — designed around the seasons and paired with matcha. Namagashi change shape each season and are sold same-day. Toraya and Higashiya Ginza are two minutes from the hotel.
📍 Toraya Ginza · Higashiya Ginza

❗️ Heads Up
Imperial Palace East Garden — Dual closure
↳ Closes both Monday and Friday — unusual for a city where most sites close only on Monday. Easily missed when scheduling Day 4.
Workaround: If Day 4 falls on a Monday or Friday, swap with any other day in the itinerary.
TeamLab Borderless and Planets — Book months ahead
↳ Both venues require timed-entry tickets booked online. Weekend slots sell out 4–8 weeks ahead; peak months sell out faster.
Workaround: Book both venues on the same day you book flights, at the same time.
Tokyo DisneySea — Date-specific tickets
↳ Tickets are tied to a specific calendar date and sold on the official website only. Weekends and school holidays sell out months ahead.
Workaround: Book the moment your travel dates are confirmed. Set a reminder for 60 days before your visit to book dining.
Nikko Toshogu — Early closure in winter
↳ The shrine closes at 4:00pm from November through March — one hour earlier than the rest of the year.
Workaround: Take the 7:40am train from Asakusa to allow adequate time before the site closes.
Mori Art Museum — Irregular Tuesday closures
↳ Closes on select Tuesdays throughout the year for maintenance. The monthly schedule is on the museum website.
Workaround: Plan Day 2 for a Wednesday through Monday to avoid any Tuesday closure.
🌊 The Space Between

Tokyo is the only city I know where the most interesting thing is what isn't there. The Japanese call it ma 間 — the pause, the gap, the deliberately held silence. It is the principle behind a Noh stage with no set, a tea ceremony where the most important moment is before the bowl is raised, a garden designed around the view through the wall rather than what is in front of it.

Tokyo enacts ma at city scale. The hypermodern glass tower and the cedar shrine forest they built it around. The silence inside Meiji Shrine — 200 metres from the neon of Harajuku — that begins the moment you pass the torii. The tidal garden at Hamarikyu holding still at the centre of a ring of elevated highways. The way a yakitori alley under a train line in Yurakucho can feel more permanently itself than any monument in Europe.

This city doesn't ask you to choose between the ancient and the contemporary. It asks you to feel both at once and to notice what lives in the space between them.
