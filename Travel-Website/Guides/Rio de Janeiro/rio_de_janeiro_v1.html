2. Museu do Amanhã
↳ Santiago Calatrava's science museum extends over Guanabara Bay like a white skeletal wing, its solar fins tracking the sun. Five chapters — Cosmos, Earth, Anthropocene, Tomorrow, Us — use real-time data and interactives to put the future of civilisation in your hands.

3. Escadaria Selarón
↳ Chilean artist Jorge Selarón tiled a crumbling Lapa staircase from the mid-1990s until his death, covering 215 steps with tile fragments collected from more than 60 countries. He called it his gift to the Brazilian people and never stopped adding to it.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 18 min → Corcovado Train Station
1. Cristo Redentor
↳ The Art Deco Christ stands with arms wide on the 709-metre summit of Corcovado — one of the most recognised monuments on earth. The view from the base, with all of Rio spread below in every direction, matches every expectation. Take the cog railway from Cosme Velho; it climbs through Atlantic Forest the whole way.

2. Cascatinha Taunay — Tijuca National Park
↳ A 35-metre waterfall inside Tijuca National Park — the world's largest urban forest, 3,200 hectares entirely within Rio's city limits. The trail passes giant ferns, bromeliads, and marmosets. It is extraordinary to be this deep inside living Atlantic Forest ten minutes from Copacabana.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~1 hr 15 min

🚕 20 min → Jardim Botânico
3. Jardim Botânico
↳ A garden founded for spice acclimatisation that became one of the world's great botanical parks. The 750-metre allee of 134 Imperial Palms forms a colonnade that shadows every photograph of Rio's green interior. Beyond it: a Japanese Garden, Victoria amazonica lilies, and 6,500 Atlantic Forest species.
📖 Wikipedia

4. Parque Lage
↳ An Italian Renaissance palace built by an industrialist for the soprano he loved, set against Atlantic Forest rising straight into Corcovado. From the arched central courtyard you can look up through the canopy and see Christ's back directly above. Now a public park housing Rio's School of Visual Arts.

1. Forte de Copacabana
↳ Built at the southern tip of Copacabana Beach with 360-degree views of the Atlantic and Guanabara Bay. Its museum documents the Revolta dos 18 do Forte, when eighteen officers marched against government troops on this beach in one of Brazil's defining modern political moments.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Sugarloaf Mountain
↳ Two cable cars — first to Morro da Urca, then to the 396-metre granite summit — deliver the view that makes Rio's layout comprehensible: the bay behind Niterói, the arc of Copacabana, Christ in the green hinterland, and the Atlantic beyond. Cable cars have run here since the early 20th century.

3. MAC Niterói
↳ Oscar Niemeyer designed this museum at 84 — a 50-metre disc of white reinforced concrete hovering over Guanabara Bay on a single cylinder. The circular terrace takes in Rio's entire skyline across the water. The collection covers Brazilian contemporary art; the building is the real exhibit.

🍮 Local Tastes
Caipirinha
↳ Brazil's national cocktail — cachaça, fresh lime, ice, and sugar. The quality gap between a well-made one and a bad one is enormous. Order it at Bar do Mineiro, any botequim, or on a sunset sailing tour.
📍 Bar do Mineiro · Santa Teresa
Feijoada
↳ A slow-cooked black bean stew with pork shoulder, ribs, and sausage — served on Saturdays throughout Rio with white rice, collard greens, farofa, and orange slices. The national dish, served seriously.
📍 Bar do Mineiro · Santa Teresa
Pão de Queijo
↳ Brazilian cheese bread made from tapioca starch — slightly chewy, hollow-crisp exterior, salty cheese centre, eaten hot in every café in the country. At its best at breakfast or late morning.
📍 Confeitaria Colombo · Centro
Açaí Bowl
↳ Thick frozen açaí puree topped with granola, banana, and honey — born as post-surf food on Rio's beaches and now consumed with competitive intensity citywide. Order from any beachfront kiosk on Copacabana.
📍 Copacabana beach kiosks · Av. Atlântica
Churrasco
↳ Brazilian barbecue is about one cut above all others: picanha — grilled whole and sliced at the table. The fat cap renders as it cooks. The rodízio format means it keeps arriving until you say stop.
📍 Churrascaria Palace · Copacabana

❗️ Heads Up
❗️ All Museums & Fort — Closed Monday
↳ Museu do Amanhã, Parque das Ruínas, Forte de Copacabana, and MAC Niterói are closed Monday — this affects Days 1 and 3.
Workaround: Schedule Days 1 and 3 for Tuesday through Sunday; Day 2 visits are safe on any day.
❗️ Corcovado — Book Tickets Before Arriving
↳ The cog railway sells out on weekends and holidays; walk-up tickets run out by 9am on busy days.
Workaround: Book at trem.rio at least 3 to 4 days ahead; select an 8:00am or 9:00am departure for the best summit visibility.
❗️ MAC Niterói — Allow Extra Time for the Ferry
↳ The ferry adds 20 minutes each way plus embarkation time; arriving after 4:00pm leaves minimal time before closing.
Workaround: Start Day 3 before 10:00am; the last ferry from Arariboia back to Praça XV runs around 11:00pm.
❗️ Confeitaria Colombo — Closed Sunday
↳ The café is closed Sunday; if Day 1 falls on a Sunday, begin at Museu do Amanhã instead.
Workaround: Colombo is best visited before noon when the salon is quietest; it opens at 9:00am Monday through Friday.
❗️ Cristo Redentor — Morning for Best Visibility
↳ Corcovado sits inside Atlantic Forest and is regularly enclosed by mist from mid-morning; clear views are most reliable before 11:00am.
Workaround: If visibility is poor on arrival, wait 30 minutes before descending — conditions at that altitude move quickly and often improve.
🌊 Rio as Geology

The shape of this city is stranger than it looks. Rio was built in the spaces that granite would not let anything else be built — in the narrow valley floors between peaks that rise straight from the sea, on the thin coastal plain between mountains and Atlantic. Copacabana was not accessible from the rest of the city until a tunnel was blasted through the coastal rock at the end of the 19th century. The geography that makes this place so photogenic is exactly the geography that made it so difficult to build.

Selarón died on the stairs he spent over two decades tiling. He was found in January, on the steps he had declared his gift to the Brazilian people, surrounded by tile fragments collected from dozens of countries. The cause was never conclusively determined. He had told interviewers that he would only stop working on the staircase when he died. This turned out to be literally accurate.
