A city that nature designed as a spectacle — and what four centuries of history built inside it.

The geography that made everything possible

Stand on the Sugarloaf — the granite plug rising 396 meters above the mouth of Guanabara Bay — and take a moment to understand what you're looking at. The bay below you is ringed by mountains, the city built into every possible space between them and the sea. Behind you is the Atlantic. Ahead, Corcovado peak rises from the Tijuca forest with Cristo Redentor at its summit, arms spread over the whole arrangement. Below are the beaches of Flamengo and Botafogo curving around the bay. Far to the right, past the bridge, are the beaches of Niterói. The entire city is a series of mountains and water, and everything — every neighborhood, every road, every social division — was built around the terrain that was already here.

This matters because in most cities, geography is background. In Rio it is the primary fact. The wealthy neighborhoods hug the southern beaches — Copacabana, Ipanema, Leblon — where the mountains meet the Atlantic and the view is unobstructed. The favelas — the informal hillside communities that house a third of the city's population — grew in the spaces the terrain made inconvenient: the steep hillsides above Zona Sul, the flat lowlands of the Zona Norte. You could draw Rio's class map from a topographic chart. Once you see it, you see it in everything.

How Rio became the center of the world, briefly

In 1808, something happened in Rio de Janeiro that had never happened before and has not happened since: a reigning European monarch moved his entire court to the Americas to escape a military threat at home. When Napoleon's army crossed into Portugal, the prince regent Dom João VI packed up the royal family, the royal treasury, the royal library of sixty thousand books, and approximately fifteen thousand courtiers, bureaucrats, and servants, and sailed for Brazil under British escort. He arrived in Rio de Janeiro in March 1808 and decided — since Lisbon was occupied and he was rather comfortable — to stay.

The city that received this influx was a colonial backwater of about sixty thousand people. What João VI did to it in the next thirteen years was transformative: he opened the ports to free trade (ending Portugal's monopoly), founded the Banco do Brasil, established the first printing press in Brazil, built botanical gardens and a royal library and a military academy, and invited European artists and scientists to come and document the country. The French Artistic Mission of 1816 brought architects and painters who built the neoclassical buildings that still anchor the city center. The Academy of Fine Arts they founded trained Brazilian artists for a century.

When João VI finally returned to Portugal in 1821, he left his son Pedro behind as regent. A year later, Pedro declared Brazilian independence from Portugal. The empire that moved here to escape Napoleon accidentally created a country.

Walk through the historic center today — the Praça XV de Novembro where the royal court landed, the Paço Imperial where João held court, the neoclassical facades of the surrounding streets — and you're standing in what was, for thirteen years, the capital of the entire Portuguese empire. Not a colonial outpost: the actual center. Europe came to Rio. The city has never quite gotten over it.

The debt that was never paid

Brazil was the last country in the Americas to abolish slavery, in 1888. Between the early sixteenth century and that date, approximately five million enslaved Africans were brought to Brazil — more than to any other country in the world, more than to the entire United States several times over. Rio de Janeiro was the largest slave port in the history of the Atlantic world. Ships arrived in Guanabara Bay for three centuries carrying people who had been kidnapped in West and Central Africa, and the city's economy, its port, its buildings, and its cultural life were built on that labor.

The Valongo Wharf, uncovered by archaeological excavations in the port area during preparations for the 2016 Olympics, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site now. It was the landing point for more than a million enslaved people between the 1770s and 1831. You can visit it. The cobblestones are original. What the museum there asks you to sit with — and it does ask — is not only the scale of the crime but the deliberateness of its erasure: the wharf was paved over in 1843 when Princess Leopoldina arrived from Europe and the city wanted to hide it.

The African roots of Rio's culture survived that erasure more completely than the city's official story acknowledged for most of its history. Samba — the music and dance that defines Rio's Carnival — came from candomblé, the Afro-Brazilian religion that preserved West African spiritual practices under Catholic disguise during slavery. The terreiros, the community houses where candomblé is practiced, were centers of cultural resistance. The samba schools that parade through the Sambódromo every Carnival are direct descendants of those communities. When you watch Carnival, you are watching the largest celebration on earth of a culture that was supposed to have been destroyed.

The favela and the asphalt

The word favela comes from a plant. After the Guerra de Canudos — a brutal conflict in the Brazilian backlands in the 1890s in which the government massacred a religious community of around thirty thousand people — the surviving soldiers were promised housing in Rio when they returned. The housing was never provided. They camped on a hill in the city center, near a plant called favela that they recognized from the backlands. The hill became known as Morro da Providência. It was the first favela.

The city has been trying to figure out what to do with its hillside communities ever since, and the answers have ranged from violent to complicated. The twentieth century saw repeated attempts at removal — clearing favelas and relocating residents to distant public housing projects — that failed not because the residents lacked political power (they often did) but because the communities were too embedded in the city's economic and social life to actually relocate. The women who worked in the houses of Ipanema and Leblon needed to live somewhere a short commute away. The Zona Sul's informal economy ran through the hills above it.

What exists today is a city of two overlapping systems — the asfalto (the asphalt, the formal city) and the favela — that interact constantly and unequally, and that are both, in their different ways, entirely Rio. The samba that fills Carnival comes from the hillside communities. So does a significant portion of the city's music, food, religious practice, and cultural energy. A visit to Rio that stays entirely in the Zona Sul — Ipanema, Copacabana, Santa Teresa — is visiting an edited version of the city.

The beach as common ground

Ipanema on a Saturday morning is one of the stranger sociological experiments on earth. The beach is organized — not formally, but by long tradition — into segments by neighborhood, by tribe, by interest: the families with children near one posto, the gay community at another, the young people from the Zona Norte who take the bus two hours to be here, the fitness-obsessed regulars who've been coming to the same spot for thirty years. The territorial markers are invisible to visitors and completely clear to everyone else.

What makes it interesting is that the beach is one of the few places in Rio where the divisions don't fully hold. The Atlantic doesn't know which social class you belong to. The vendors who move up and down selling mate and cheese bread and biscoito Globo serve everyone. The wave breaking on the shore in front of the favela on Vidigal is the same wave breaking in front of the Fasano hotel a kilometer south.

Cariocas — Rio residents — have a word for the philosophy the beach encodes: jeitinho brasileiro, the little way of doing things, the capacity to improvise a solution in the gap between what the rules say and what actually works. It's the most Brazilian idea Rio has produced.

The girl from Ipanema — the one in the 1964 bossa nova song that became the most recorded song in history after "Yesterday" — was Helô Pinheiro, a sixteen-year-old who walked past the Veloso bar on Rua Montenegro every day on her way to the beach. Tom Jobim and Vinicius de Moraes, who co-wrote the song while drinking at that bar, were watching her go by. The street is now called Rua Vinícius de Moraes. The bar is still there, now called Garota de Ipanema. The song they wrote sitting in it became bossa nova's global calling card — a sound that took the melancholy of samba and ran it through cool jazz harmonics and came out sounding like exactly what the beach looks like at ten in the morning in good light.

Cristo and what he's actually watching

Cristo Redentor — the art deco statue of Christ with outstretched arms that stands at 710 meters on Corcovado — was completed in 1931, designed by the French sculptor Paul Landowski and the Brazilian engineer Heitor da Silva Costa. The idea for it had been discussed since the 1850s. The site was chosen because it was visible from everywhere. The outstretched arms were a later modification to Landowski's original design, and the decision was aesthetic, not theological: the arms opened up the figure to the sky and made the silhouette readable from the city below.

From the platform at Cristo's feet you can see the entire city at once — Guanabara Bay, the Sugarloaf, the long arc of Copacabana, the hills of the Zona Norte, the airport on the landfill in the bay, the bridge to Niterói. What you're looking at is not just beautiful. It is a diagram of everything the story pages of this city are about: the port where the enslaved arrived, the neoclassical downtown that the Portuguese empire built, the hillside communities that refused to be erased, the beaches that function as the city's commons, the forest that covers 25 percent of the city's area — the largest urban forest in the world — because the deforestation of the mountains in the nineteenth century caused catastrophic flooding that eventually persuaded the city to replant the hills rather than clear them.

The Tijuca forest that now covers those hills was almost entirely replanted in the second half of the nineteenth century by order of Emperor Dom Pedro II, who understood that the watershed was collapsing. It is the world's largest urban reforested area: a nineteenth-century ecological restoration project the size of a small country, covering the mountains that ring the city, producing the waterfalls and the cool air that make the neighborhoods below them livable. You're walking through a rescue operation every time you hike in Tijuca.

The city behind the postcard

Rio has one of the most recognizable images of any city on earth, and that image — the beaches, the mountains, the carnival, the statue — is not wrong, exactly, but it is incomplete in the way that all postcards are incomplete. The Rio that exists behind the image is a city of enormous political complexity, significant violence concentrated in specific places, deeply embedded inequality, and also — genuinely, not as a tourist pitch — an extraordinary cultural vitality that comes from exactly those pressures.

The carioca personality — open, improvisational, physically expressive, constitutionally opposed to rigidity — is not a myth. It is a real cultural adaptation to a city where the formal systems have always been unreliable and the informal ones have always worked better. You feel it in the ease with which strangers talk to each other, in the flexibility around plans and timing, in the way a party becomes a better party when it moves to the street, in the samba circle that forms spontaneously in a square because someone brought a drum.

What to carry into the city

Before you go, here is the thing to carry with you: Rio de Janeiro has been spectacular since before it was a city. The Portuguese who landed in Guanabara Bay in 1502 named it "January River" because they thought the bay was a river mouth — they were wrong about the geography but right about the feeling. Every generation since has arrived and been stopped, the way you're stopped, by what this place looks like. That spectacle is real and it is not the whole story.

The whole story is that this is a city where an empire arrived to hide from Napoleon and accidentally created a country. Where enslaved Africans built the port and the buildings and the culture, and then — slowly, incompletely, still ongoing — insisted on being seen. Where a third of the population built their homes on hillsides above the city that was supposed to be theirs and invented the music that made it famous. Where the forest was destroyed and then regrown, by order, because someone understood what it was worth. Once you see that story in the landscape — the mountains, the favelas on the hillsides, the reforested peaks, the wharf under the port — what looked like a postcard starts to look like something more honest.

Go up to Cristo early, before the tour buses arrive. Turn around and look at the city the statue is watching. That's the briefing.
