Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 15 min → Cajueiro de Pirangi
1. Cajueiro de Pirangi
↳ A single cashew tree spread to cover 7,300 square meters — the size of 70 normal trees — earning a Guinness World Record. An elevated timber walkway above the canopy lets visitors trace all 70 trunks, which are the same tree's branching limbs resting on the ground and re-rooting.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1h 30min
⚠️ Gate entry fee applies
📍 Pirangi do Norte · Parnamirim
🚕 15 min → Ponta Negra Beach
2. Ponta Negra Beach
↳ Natal's most celebrated beach — a 4-kilometer crescent with calm warm waters. At the southern tip, Morro do Careca rises 120 meters from the shoreline; a sand dune whose bare wind-eroded patch became Natal's most recognized silhouette. Climbing is prohibited; the view from the base remains the experience.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1h 30min
📍 Av. Engenheiro Roberto Freire · s/n · Ponta Negra
🚕 20 min → Parque das Dunas de Natal
3. Parque das Dunas de Natal
↳ The second-largest urban park in Brazil, protecting 1,172 hectares of Atlantic Forest dunes in the middle of the city. Over 270 tree species inhabit the ecosystem. Three certified hiking trails wind through the Bosque dos Namorados public-use zone — all require advance booking with a minimum group of five.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Sunday 8:00am - 5:30pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~2h 30min
⚠️ Guided trails require advance booking: +55 84 3201-3985 · minimum group of 5
📍 Av. Alexandrino de Alencar · s/n · Tirol
🚕 5 min → Museu Câmara Cascudo
4. Museu Câmara Cascudo
↳ The largest museum in Rio Grande do Norte, named after Luís da Câmara Cascudo — the northeastern folklorist who catalogued the region's oral traditions and indigenous cultures. Collections span ethno-archaeology, paleontology, colonial-era objects, and folklore from across the sertão and coast.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 45 min → Lagoa de Pitangui
1. Lagoa de Pitangui
↳ A large freshwater lagoon 30 kilometers north of the city, ringed by white dunes and sheltered from the wind. Crystal-clear water reaches 5 meters deep at the center. The Pitangui Lagoa Park complex manages day-use access with hammock areas, kayaks, a restaurant, and a zip-line over the water.

⏰ ~2h
⚠️ Day-use entry fee at gate

2. Praia de Genipabu
↳ The open-sea beach at the base of the Genipabu dune system, where fishing boats anchor in calm surf and the dune wall rises directly behind the sand line. The only vantage point to see the full scale of the dune complex from sea level — the perspective from the water differs entirely from the park interior.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1h
📍 Praia de Genipabu · s/n · Genipabu · Extremoz

3. Dunas de Genipabu
↳ A protected ecological park where wind-sculpted white dunes reshape against the sea — the defining dune landscape of northeastern Brazil. Licensed buggy operators run the "com emoção" route with near-vertical descents, and the "sem emoção" circuit for families. Buggies seat up to 4, booked at the gate.

4. Lagoa de Genipabu
↳ A freshwater lagoon enclosed by white dune walls within the Genipabu complex, where turquoise water contrasts against the bleached sand. Pedal boats and kayaks can be hired at the lakeside. Vivid blue-green water framed by stark dune walls make it one of the most photographed scenes in Rio Grande do Norte.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 30 min → Forte dos Reis Magos
1. Forte dos Reis Magos
↳ A five-pointed star-shaped Portuguese fortress on a reef at the mouth of the Potengi River, retaining its original chapel, soldiers' quarters, well, and cannons. Declared National Historic Heritage. A 500-meter causeway across the reef gives sweeping views of the coastline and the open Atlantic.
📖 Wikipedia

🚫 Closed Monday

🚶 8 min · 🚕 3 min → Theatro Alberto Maranhão
2. Theatro Alberto Maranhão
↳ Natal's principal performance venue with an eclectic exterior carrying French Art Nouveau detail. Inside, gilded plasterwork, a painted ceiling mural, and three horseshoe balcony tiers make it one of the most ornate buildings in northeastern Brazil. Visitors walk freely through the lobby and hall when open.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Cidade Alta
↳ Natal's oldest neighborhood on elevated ground above the Potengi River. Buildings spanning colonial, neoclassical, Art Deco, and modernist styles line cobblestone streets. Praça André de Albuquerque — where the founding mass was celebrated — anchors the district, ringed by the Cathedral and historic market building.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1h 30min
📍 Praça André de Albuquerque · Cidade Alta
🚶 2 min · 🚕 2 min → Igreja Matriz Nossa Senhora da Apresentação
4. Igreja Matriz Nossa Senhora da Apresentação
↳ The first church in Rio Grande do Norte on Praça André de Albuquerque — where the founding mass was celebrated when the colony was established. It survived Dutch occupation, briefly used as a Calvinist temple, and a later restoration removed additions to recover the original colonial character.

🍮 Local Tastes
Ginga com Tapioca
↳ Natal's most iconic dish and official Intangible Cultural Heritage. Tiny Potengi River fish fried in dendê oil, wrapped in a tapioca crepe with fresh coconut and coconut milk. Authentic at Mercado da Redinha, Box 3 — Bar Sandra e Ivanize.
📍 Mercado da Redinha · Redinha
Tapioca
↳ Cassava-starch crepe cooked on a flat griddle, served at any hour with fillings ranging from coalho cheese and carne de sol to banana with condensed milk. Natal's street-food staple, sold at corner stands throughout the city.
📍 Corner stands · throughout the city
Bobó de Camarão
↳ Shrimp stew thickened with mashed manioc, coconut milk, dendê oil, and ginger. Natal's historic identity as the Potiguar — the shrimp eaters — makes this the flagship plate, appearing on menus throughout Ponta Negra.
📍 Restaurant menus · Ponta Negra
Carne de Sol
↳ Air-cured lightly salted beef from the sertão tradition, served with manteiga de garrafa and baião de dois. Found on nearly every northeastern restaurant menu in Natal and a reliable non-seafood anchor dish throughout the city.
📍 Restaurant menus · Ponta Negra
Queijo Coalho Grelhado
↳ Fresh cheese from the northeastern tradition, grilled on skewers and sold by vendors along Ponta Negra beach. Firm, squeaky, and mild — the canonical beach snack of Rio Grande do Norte.
📍 Beach vendors · Ponta Negra

🦐 Where the Shrimp Eaters Built a Star

The people of Natal are called Potiguar — a word from Tupi meaning "shrimp eater." The name comes from the indigenous Potiguar people who inhabited this coastline when the Portuguese arrived, and it has been worn with such pride that the whole state of Rio Grande do Norte adopted it as the demonym. Every person from RN is a Potiguar. The shrimp-eating identity is not an embarrassment but a founding fact, embedded in the language before the city existed.

Natal was founded on Christmas Day — the same day the Forte dos Reis Magos was started the year before, also on Christmas Day. The name "Natal" is the Portuguese word for Christmas. The founders named the fort for the Feast of the Magi on Epiphany, and named the city for the day it was born. Three acts on two consecutive Christmas Days produced a fort, a church, and a city — all named for the same calendar.

The ginga fish at the Mercado da Redinha is tiny and anchovy-like, living in the Potengi River — the same river that wraps around the reef where the Forte dos Reis Magos sits. The fort and the dish share the same water. The river appears in the founding documents as the reason the Portuguese chose this location; centuries later, the fish that live in it became an official Intangible Cultural Heritage.
