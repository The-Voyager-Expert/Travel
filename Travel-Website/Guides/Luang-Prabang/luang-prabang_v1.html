Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 16 min · 🚕 4 min → Mount Phousi
1. Mount Phousi
↳ A sacred hill rising 100 m above the old town, with the definitive view over Luang Prabang — the Mekong and Nam Khan converging around temple rooftops and teak shophouses, limestone karsts beyond. The 355-step staircase climbs through frangipani to a gilded stupa at the summit.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 50 min → Tat Kuang Si Bear Rescue Centre
1. Tat Kuang Si Bear Rescue Centre
↳ At the Kuang Si complex entrance, this Free the Bears sanctuary rehabilitates Asiatic black bears rescued from the illegal wildlife trade. The bears live in forested riverside enclosures; staff explain each bear's rescue history and the conservation pressures facing the species in Laos.

2. Kuang Si Waterfalls
↳ A multi-tiered limestone waterfall system in the forested hills south. Calcium carbonate deposits tint the pools a vivid turquoise. A forest trail climbs alongside the cascade to the main 60-metre drop. Allow three to four hours from the bear rescue entrance to the top and back.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 25 min · 🚕 7 min → Mekong pier
1. Pak Ou Caves
↳ Two sacred limestone caves at the Ou River mouth, 25 km upstream. The lower cave holds thousands of Buddha images donated by pilgrims over centuries. The upper cave requires a torch — gilded figures against a dark interior. Standard visit: a two-hour boat ride each way past cliffs and riverside villages.

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 30 min → Mandalao Elephant Conservation
1. Mandalao Elephant Conservation
↳ An ethical elephant conservation sanctuary on the Nam Khan River, 20 km from the city. Rescued Asian elephants live in a large forested reserve. Morning programs involve a guided jungle trek alongside the elephants, conservation briefings from staff, and a prepared Lao lunch.

🍮 Local Tastes
Or Lam
↳ Luang Prabang's signature stew — eggplant, wood ear mushrooms, lemongrass, and dill simmered with dried buffalo meat or vegetables; a slow-built dish rarely found elsewhere in this form.
📍 Tamarind · Dyen Sabai
Khao Soi Lao
↳ The Lao version — a clear pork-based broth with rice noodles and minced pork, topped with crispy fried garlic; completely distinct from the Northern Thai coconut curry soup of the same name.
📍 Khaiphaen
Kaipen
↳ Sheets of Mekong river weed dried in the sun, seasoned with sesame, garlic, and salt, then deep-fried into thin crispy wafers — a Luang Prabang snack with no real equivalent elsewhere.
📍 Tamarind · Khaiphaen
Mok Pa
↳ Mekong river fish steamed in a banana leaf parcel with lemongrass, galangal, makrut lime, and sticky rice — a traditional preparation found across LP's better restaurants.
📍 Dyen Sabai · Manda de Laos

🌊 What Stayed With Me

Luang Prabang is one of the few places where the stop list almost wrote itself — and then immediately crossed itself out. The city's fame rests almost entirely on its Buddhist temple complex, which is genuinely extraordinary. Remove temples from a guide to Luang Prabang and you've removed the reason most people go. What remained after applying the rules was a guide built around water and wildlife: a hill with a river view, a cave full of thousands of donated Buddhas reached by a slow boat, a conservation project rescuing bears at the edge of a waterfall, and elephants walking a forested riverbank. The constraint produced a trip that's stranger and more varied than a temple circuit — and probably harder to forget.

The Laos-China Railway detail that surprised me most: the track runs through more than 70 tunnels on the route south toward Vientiane, covering the full distance in under two hours. Luang Prabang's station sits 12 kilometres east of the old town at the foot of the mountains — because the railway cuts through the hills rather than following the river valley where the city sits. The train is faster than the Mekong slow boat that used to be the main way in, and it connects LP to the capital in one trip.
