The only city in North America that feels European without trying to — built on a mountain, spoken in French, and held together by smoked meat, brutal winters, and an argument about bagels that will never end.

The mountain that named a city

You will hear the name before you see the thing itself. Mont Royal — the Royal Mountain — gave Montreal its name, and it still anchors the city in a way that goes beyond geography. It sits in the middle of the island, 233 meters high, covered in old-growth forest and ringed by three of the city's most important neighborhoods. Frederick Law Olmsted, the man who designed Central Park, designed the park that wraps around the mountain in 1876. He wanted it to feel like a walk through wilderness, not a manicured garden, and he largely succeeded. The winding carriage roads he laid out are now walking paths, and on summer weekends the tam-tams — the drum circles that have gathered at the George-Etienne Cartier monument since the 1970s — send a low rhythmic pulse through the trees that you can hear before you reach the clearing.

The view from the top is the one that explains Montreal. From the Kondiaronk Belvedere — the stone terrace at the summit — you can see the entire city grid laid out below you: the glass towers downtown, the low brick neighborhoods of the Plateau, the silver curve of the St. Lawrence River, the flat expanse of the South Shore, and on clear days the blue outline of the Adirondacks across the border in New York State. The cross on the summit, illuminated at night and visible from nearly everywhere in the city, was first placed there in 1643 by Paul de Chomedey de Maisonneuve, the founder of the settlement, after the colony survived a catastrophic flood. He had made a vow: if the waters receded, he would carry a cross to the top of the mountain. The waters receded. He kept his word. The current steel cross dates from 1924, but the gesture is the original one.

The French fact

Montreal is the largest primarily French-speaking city in the Americas — a title once given as "second in the world after Paris," a claim that African megacities like Kinshasa and Abidjan have since outgrown by sheer population, even if French there is more often a lingua franca than a mother tongue. Either way, this is not a quaint leftover or a tourist gimmick. It is a political and cultural reality that defines every aspect of life here, from the language on the street signs to the names of the metro stations to the particular kind of argument you will overhear in any bar on a Saturday night.

But Montreal is not French. It is Quebecois, which is its own thing entirely. The French spoken here descends from the settlers who arrived in the 17th and 18th centuries, and it evolved in isolation after the British conquest of 1763. The result is a language that a Parisian can understand but will find strange — older in some of its constructions, more inventive in its slang, delivered at a different speed and with a different music. The working-class dialect, joual, borrows freely from English and pronounces French in ways that make linguists from France wince. Quebecois do not care what linguists from France think.

The Quiet Revolution of the 1960s remade Quebec in a single generation. Before it, the province was rural, Catholic, and dominated by the Church in a way that had no parallel elsewhere in North America. The parish priest was the most powerful man in most communities. Birth rates were enormous — la revanche des berceaux, the revenge of the cradles, was an explicit strategy to outbreed the English. Then, almost overnight, Quebec secularized. The birth rate collapsed. The universities expanded. The state took over education and health care from the Church. A new Quebecois identity emerged that was nationalist, modern, and fiercely protective of the French language.

Bill 101, the Charter of the French Language, passed in 1977, made French the sole official language of Quebec. Signs must be in French. Businesses above a certain size must operate in French. Immigrant children must attend French schools. The law is controversial, endlessly debated, and regularly challenged, but it accomplished what it set out to do: Montreal remained a French-speaking city when the economic pressures of English-speaking North America might otherwise have overwhelmed it.

The independence question came to a head twice. The 1980 referendum on sovereignty-association lost 60-40. The 1995 referendum lost by less than one percent — 50.58 to 49.42, a margin of about 54,000 votes out of nearly five million cast. The night of the 1995 vote is still a raw memory for many Quebecois. The question has receded since then, but it has not disappeared. You are in a place that nearly became a country, and that fact gives the culture a seriousness and self-awareness that you will not find in most North American cities.

Montreal is the only city on the continent where the language you speak is a political act and a love letter at the same time.

The underground city

Thirty-three kilometers of tunnels run beneath downtown Montreal, connecting metro stations, shopping malls, office towers, hotels, universities, and concert halls in a subterranean network called RESO. It is the largest underground pedestrian complex in the world, and it exists for a simple reason: the winters here are punishing. Montreal's average January temperature is on par with Moscow's. The average low in February is minus 15 Celsius. Wind chill can push the effective temperature to minus 30 or beyond. The underground city is not an attraction. It is a survival mechanism.

The network grew organically, starting with the Place Ville Marie complex in 1962 and expanding outward as each new downtown development connected to its neighbors. There is no master plan and no unified architecture. Some sections are sleek corporate corridors; others are fluorescent-lit mall concourses from the 1970s that have aged accordingly. The experience of walking through RESO is not glamorous. It is warm, dry, and functional, and in February that is all anyone asks of it. You can walk from the train station to your hotel to a restaurant to a concert hall without stepping outside, and on certain days that is not a luxury but a necessity.

Old Montreal and the bones beneath

The cobblestoned streets of Vieux-Montreal sit on top of the city's origins. The settlement that became Montreal was founded in 1642 as Ville-Marie, a missionary outpost intended to convert the Indigenous peoples of the St. Lawrence Valley. The founders — Maisonneuve, Jeanne Mance, Marguerite Bourgeoys — arrived with religious zeal and very little practical preparation for the reality of winters, isolation, and intermittent warfare with the Haudenosaunee Confederacy. That the colony survived at all is something close to improbable.

The Pointe-a-Calliere museum, at the western tip of Old Montreal, is built directly over the archaeological site of the city's founding. You walk down into the excavation and stand on the stone foundations of the original fort, the old river channel, and a Catholic cemetery from the 1640s. The bones are still there, beneath glass floors. It is one of the most effective museum experiences in Canada — not because of any particular artifact, but because of the simple fact of standing in the place where the thing happened, looking at the ground where it began.

Notre-Dame Basilica, a few blocks east on Place d'Armes, is the interior that justifies the trip to Old Montreal even if you have no interest in churches. Built in 1829 by the Irish-American architect James O'Donnell — who converted to Catholicism on his deathbed so he could be buried inside it — the basilica is Gothic Revival at its most theatrical. The ceiling is deep blue with gold stars. The altarpiece is carved wood gilded and painted in colors so intense they look backlit. The stained glass windows tell the story of Montreal's founding rather than biblical scenes. When Celine Dion was married here in 1994, the ceremony was broadcast live to a national audience, and the church became, briefly, the most-watched interior in the country.

The food city

Montreal's food reputation is legitimate in a way that most North American food reputations are not. This is not a city where restaurants are good because money poured in and attracted talent. This is a city where food culture grew from the street up — from specific immigrant communities, specific ingredients, specific arguments about how things should be done that have been running for decades.

Schwartz's Deli has been serving smoked meat on Saint-Laurent Boulevard since 1928. The restaurant is tiny, the line extends down the block, and the smoked meat — beef brisket cured in a spice blend for ten days, then smoked for eight hours — is unlike anything you will find outside this city. Montreal smoked meat is not pastrami, though the two are cousins. It is fattier, more heavily spiced, and sliced by hand rather than by machine. You eat it on rye bread with yellow mustard and a pickle, standing at the counter or squeezed into a table with strangers, and you do not complicate it with anything else.

Joe Beef, on a side street in Little Burgundy, became one of the most celebrated restaurants in North America under chefs David McMillan and Frederic Morin, who cooked the kind of excessive, deeply French, aggressively carnivorous food that made food writers lose their composure. The late Anthony Bourdain called it his favorite restaurant in the world. Its sister restaurant, Liverpool House, next door, is the same kitchen in a slightly calmer register. Both are still operating, and both are still worth the effort of getting a reservation.

Poutine done well is a serious dish. Poutine done badly — and most poutine is done badly — is cafeteria food that happens to contain cheese curds.

The bagel argument is real and ongoing. Montreal bagels are smaller, denser, and sweeter than New York bagels, boiled in honey water and baked in a wood-fired oven. The two temples are St-Viateur Bagel in Mile End and Fairmount Bagel a few blocks north — both open 24 hours, both claiming to be the original, both producing bagels that are objectively better than anything available in New York City. Saying this to a New Yorker will start a fight. Saying it in Montreal will get you a nod of confirmation and a recommendation for which one to visit first.

The festival city

Montreal in summer operates on a different frequency than Montreal in winter. The transformation is not gradual. It happens in late May when the terrasses — the outdoor restaurant patios — appear on sidewalks across the city, and it ends in September when they disappear again. In between, the city hosts more festivals per capita than any city in North America, and it runs them with the competence of a place that has been doing this for decades.

The Montreal International Jazz Festival, held every summer since 1980, is the largest jazz festival in the world. The outdoor stages on the Place des Festivals are free. The paid indoor concerts draw performers who would not play a festival in any other city. Just for Laughs, the comedy festival, launched the careers of performers from Dave Chappelle to Hannah Gadsby. Osheaga, the rock and electronic music festival on Ile Sainte-Helene, fills Parc Jean-Drapeau with 135,000 people over three days. The Canadian Grand Prix, run on the Circuit Gilles Villeneuve on the same island, brings Formula One to a city that treats the weekend as a municipal holiday.

The parks fill up. The mountain hums with runners and cyclists. The Lachine Canal, the old industrial waterway that cuts through the city's southwest, becomes a linear park where you can bike from the Old Port to the suburb of Lachine in an hour. The city remembers, collectively, why it tolerates the winters.

A city that feels like nowhere else

Montreal is the only city in North America that feels European without performing it. This is not because of the architecture, though Old Montreal helps, and not because of the language, though French gives the streets a different soundtrack. It is because of the culture — the assumption that a meal should take two hours, that walking is a reasonable way to get somewhere, that an argument about politics or art or the proper composition of a smoked meat sandwich is a legitimate use of an evening.

Toronto has more money. Vancouver has better weather. Neither of them has what Montreal has, which is a particular kind of civic personality — argumentative, self-aware, culturally serious, and slightly defiant — that comes from being a French-speaking island in an English-speaking continent, from nearly becoming a country and then deciding not to, from enduring winters that would break a less stubborn population, and from knowing, with the quiet confidence of a city that has been here for nearly four centuries, that none of this needs to be explained to anyone who has spent a week here in July.

The explanation is in the terrasse at ten on a Tuesday night. The explanation is in the drum circle on the mountain. The explanation is in the line outside Schwartz's at midnight, in the blue ceiling of the basilica, in the particular way a Montrealer switches from French to English mid-sentence without breaking stride, because both languages belong to this city and neither one owns it.
