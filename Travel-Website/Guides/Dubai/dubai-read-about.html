A city that didn't exist fifty years ago, built by people who mostly don't live here anymore, in a desert that still wins every summer — and why that's not the insult it sounds like.

The creek and what it meant

Before the towers, before the runway that receives more international traffic than almost any airport on earth, before the ski slope inside the mall, there was a creek. The Khor Dubai is a tidal inlet that cuts into the desert coast for about 14 kilometers. It is not impressive by any geographical measure — a thin blue tongue of saltwater threading between two low shores. But it was everything. It was a natural harbor in a coastline that had almost none, and in a region built on trade, a harbor was the beginning of everything.

The Bani Yas tribe settled at the creek in 1833 under Maktoum bin Butti, the ancestor of the family that rules Dubai today. They came from Abu Dhabi, and they came because they were traders who understood that the creek gave them something Abu Dhabi lacked: a place where ships could anchor in relative safety, where goods from India and East Africa and Persia could be unloaded, inspected, taxed, and sent inland. Within a generation, Dubai was a trading town of a few thousand people who fished, pearl-dived, and moved things from one side of the world to the other.

Walk through the Al Fahidi Historical Neighbourhood today — the mud-brick and coral-stone warren of lanes on the western bank of the creek — and you are standing in the only surviving piece of that city. The wind towers that rise above the rooflines are not decorative. They are the original air conditioning: tall rectangular shafts that catch the breeze above street level and funnel it down into the rooms below. Stand under one in the shade and you feel it working. Everything that came after Dubai — the entire project of the modern city — begins here, on this creek, with this problem of heat and trade and nowhere to shelter ships.

The pearl divers and the collapse that made everything possible

For centuries, the economy of the Gulf coast ran on pearls. Emirati men would spend the summer months aboard small wooden dhows in the waters of the Gulf, diving without equipment into water that could reach 40 meters, harvesting oysters by hand. The pearl trade made families wealthy, built the merchant houses of Al Fahidi, and connected the Trucial Coast — as the British called this stretch of territory — to jewelers in Mumbai and Paris and London.

In the early 1930s, Japanese cultured pearls entered the global market at a fraction of the price of natural pearls. The Gulf pearl industry collapsed within a decade. The men who had built their lives around the diving season found that skill worthless almost overnight. The timing was not accidental — it arrived just before the oil discoveries that would define the second half of the twentieth century — and it created the psychological condition that still shapes Dubai: an acute awareness that any single economic foundation can disappear, and a resulting obsession with diversification that is the engine behind everything the city has built since.

Dubai's obsession with building the next thing — the next tower, the next attraction, the next airport — is not vanity. It's a survival instinct learned from pearls.

Oil was discovered in Dubai's waters in 1966. Not in enormous quantities — Dubai's reserves were always modest compared to Abu Dhabi's. But they were enough to fund the infrastructure that would attract everything else: the port, the free trade zones, the airport, the roads. Sheikh Rashid bin Saeed Al Maktoum, who led Dubai from 1958 until his death in 1990, understood from the beginning that oil would run out and that the city would need to become something else before it did. The port of Jebel Ali, which he ordered built in the mid-1970s when Dubai already had a perfectly functioning port, was considered an act of lunatic ambition. It is now the largest man-made harbor in the world and the busiest port between Europe and Singapore.

The speed of it

No city in history has grown at the pace Dubai grew in the 1990s and 2000s. In 1990, Dubai was a city of roughly 500,000 people. By 2010, it was 1.9 million. Today it is over 3.6 million. The skyline that now defines the city — the Burj Khalifa, the sail-shaped Burj Al Arab, the artificial archipelagos — did not exist at the turn of the millennium. Most of it was desert.

When you stand on the observation deck of the Dubai Frame, the building that quite literally frames old Dubai and new Dubai in opposite windows, you are looking at what this compression of time looks like from above. On one side: the creek, the low-rise neighborhoods of Deira and Bur Dubai, the minarets, the geometry of the old city. On the other: the highway corridor of Sheikh Zayed Road, the cluster of supertall towers, the Palm Jumeirah extending into the Gulf like a pressed handprint. Both views are from 2024. They are separated by about forty years of construction.

The Museum of the Future on Sheikh Zayed Road captures what this pace of change feels like from the inside. The building itself — a torus wrapped in Arabic calligraphy, no right angles — announces that Dubai considers itself a prototype for something not yet named. Inside, the exhibits are part speculation and part provocation: what does a world with no disease look like, or a world that has mastered its relationship with nature? You can disagree with all of it. The point is that this is a city that thinks in decades, not quarters.

The city of strangers

Here is the fact about Dubai that recalibrates everything else: approximately 88 percent of its population is foreign-born. Of those, the vast majority are migrant workers — South Asian laborers who built the towers and staff the hotels and drive the delivery vehicles — and a smaller, louder cohort of skilled professionals who come for tax advantages, lifestyle, and the particular freedom of a place with no past to hold you accountable. Emiratis, the citizens of Dubai, are a minority in their own city.

This is not an accident. It is a policy. Dubai's economic model requires massive labor inputs that its small citizen population cannot provide. The solution has been to import workers on contracts, with residency tied to employment, in a system that creates a highly stratified city where nationality and profession determine almost everything about how you live. The workers who built the Burj Khalifa earned wages measured in hundreds of dollars per month and lived in labor camps outside the city. The financiers who occupy the building's corporate floors earn wages that put them among the highest-paid people in their fields anywhere in the world. Both populations are, in their different ways, temporary.

Walk the old spice and gold souks in Deira — still functioning traditional markets, not recreations — and most of the traders are South Asian families who have been in Dubai for two or three generations, people whose children were born here and whose parents are buried here, but who carry Indian or Pakistani passports and could theoretically be asked to leave. Dubai has created a city that runs on permanence — permanent institutions, permanent infrastructure, permanent brands — while treating most of the people who make it run as guests on variable-length visas.

Almost no one you meet in Dubai is from Dubai. Everyone came from somewhere else, for reasons that made sense at the time.

None of this is comfortable to sit with. But it is the operating system of the city, and you will see it more clearly if you go looking: an Abra ride across the creek at sunset costs a dirham and takes four minutes and puts you in the middle of a city that exists entirely because of people who are not supposed to stay.

What the desert does to architecture

Dubai builds things for the same reason it built the pearl trade and the port and the airport: because building is the argument it makes to the world that it is worth stopping at. The Burj Khalifa is 828 meters tall and was, when it opened in 2010, the tallest building ever constructed. It remains so. But the height is secondary. What the Burj Khalifa actually is, is a statement that this patch of desert has become so far from ordinary that the extraordinary is now expected.

Go up to the observation deck at sunset. The city spreads out in every direction at a scale that doesn't resolve into neighborhoods the way a European city does — there is no center, no historic quarter you can see from above that orients everything else. Dubai is a city of corridors and clusters: the creek district, the downtown, the marina, the palm. Each is its own world, connected by highways that are among the widest on earth. From above, at dusk, with the Gulf going gold on the horizon and the lights of the towers coming on below you, you see a city that is entirely sincere about what it is trying to do, even if you're not sure what you think about it.

The desert keeps reasserting itself. In summer, the heat climbs above 45°C and the outdoor city essentially closes. The fountains, the beach walks, the outdoor dining — all of it retreats indoors. Dubai has built one of the most elaborate indoor economies in the world partly as a response to a climate that makes outdoor life dangerous for four months of the year. The mall is not just retail. It is climate control at urban scale.

The Dubai Frame and what it's really for

The Dubai Frame was completed in 2018 and immediately became the most useful building in the city for a visitor trying to understand what they're looking at. It is exactly what it sounds like: a giant picture frame, 150 meters tall, positioned so that looking through one side you see the old city and looking through the other you see the new. The symbolism is not subtle. Dubai built it and is proud of the symbolism.

What the Frame actually tells you is that Dubai knows its own story and is actively curating it. This is a city with a communications department operating at the scale of a national government, because the difference between Dubai being perceived as a global destination and being perceived as a regional trading post is hundreds of billions of dollars in investment and tourism. The Frame, the Burj Khalifa, the Palm, the Museum of the Future — these are all arguments. They are also all genuinely impressive. The cynicism and the achievement coexist without resolving.

Spend a morning in Al Fahidi looking at the wind towers and the narrow lanes, and then spend an afternoon on the 124th floor of the Burj Khalifa looking at what happened next. The distance between those two things, measured in time, is less than a human lifetime.

Before you land

Here is the thing to carry with you into Dubai: this city was built on the premise that location can substitute for history. The Persian Gulf sits at the intersection of the Indian Ocean trade routes and the overland routes to Europe and East Africa, and for centuries the people who controlled that intersection controlled commerce across half the world. Dubai understood that airports and free trade zones and zero income tax are the modern version of the same geography — that if you make yourself impossible to route around, people will route through you forever. The city is not a mirage, even though it looks like one from the highway at noon. It is the most deliberate act of urban construction in human history, made by people who looked at a desert and a tidal creek and decided that was enough to start with. Once you see that logic, you see it everywhere: in the port, in the airport, in the Frame, in the fact that a city with almost no citizens has become indispensable to almost everyone.
