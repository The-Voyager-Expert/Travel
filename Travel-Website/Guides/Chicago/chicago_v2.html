1. Chicago Cultural Center
↳ A Beaux-Arts landmark that opened in 1897 as Chicago's central library, now the city's flagship free arts venue. Preston Bradley Hall houses the world's largest Tiffany stained-glass dome — 38 feet across, built from 30,000 pieces of glass.

2. The Rookery Building
↳ Chicago's oldest standing steel-skeleton skyscraper, completed in 1888 by Burnham and Root. The lobby is free to enter. Frank Lloyd Wright remodeled the light court in 1905, adding white marble and organic ironwork — two of America's greatest architects in the same room.

3. Buckingham Fountain
↳ One of the world's largest fountains, built in 1927 and modeled on the Latona Fountain at Versailles at three times the scale. Water shoots 150 feet into the air hourly from mid-April through mid-October, with an evening light show at dusk.

1. Field Museum
↳ One of the largest natural history museums on earth, built for the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition and housing 40 million specimens across four floors. The main hall is anchored by Sue, the largest and most complete T. rex skeleton ever found, with gems, Egypt, and ancient Americas filling the wings.

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 18 min → Robie House
1. Robie House
↳ Frank Lloyd Wright's masterpiece of Prairie Style, built in 1910 on the University of Chicago campus. Cantilevered eaves extend 20 feet without visible support, the Roman-brick banding reduces the house to pure horizontal geometry, and the art-glass windows dissolve the wall plane. UNESCO World Heritage Site.
📖 Wikipedia

🏗️ Chicago's Second Birth

The Great Chicago Fire of 1871 burned for two days and destroyed a third of the city. Within 20 years, the rubble had been replaced by the world's first skyscrapers — the Rookery in 1888, the Monadnock in 1891, the Reliance in 1895. The disaster, by forcing the complete reinvention of an entire city center in a single generation, accidentally created modern architecture. Every skyscraper on earth today is a direct descendant of what was built in these 16 blocks of the Loop between 1871 and 1900.

The Rookery Building holds a detail most visitors miss: in 1905, a young Frank Lloyd Wright was commissioned to renovate the light court of a building completed 17 years before he became famous. He added white marble and ornamental ironwork in his emerging Prairie style — which means the Rookery contains, in the same atrium, both the 19th-century industrial aesthetic of Burnham and Root and the 20th-century organic vision of Wright. Stand in the center and you're standing in both architectural centuries simultaneously.

Buckingham Fountain — modeled on the Latona Fountain at Versailles — was built three times larger than its source. The French original is elegant. The Chicago version is operatic. It feels like the city couldn't help itself.
