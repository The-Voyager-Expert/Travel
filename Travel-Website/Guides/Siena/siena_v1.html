1. Piazza del Campo
↳ Siena's shell-shaped medieval square, sloping toward the Palazzo Pubblico in nine paved segments — the social heart of the city and the stage for the twice-yearly Palio horse race. Ringed by Gothic palaces, it is one of Europe's greatest public spaces.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min

2. Palazzo Pubblico
↳ Siena's Gothic town hall on the Campo. Inside, the Museo Civico holds Ambrogio Lorenzetti's Allegory of Good and Bad Government — one of the most important secular fresco cycles of the Middle Ages — and the 102-metre Torre del Mangia climbs roughly 400 steps to the finest view in the city.

3. Siena Cathedral
↳ One of Italy's grandest Gothic cathedrals — banded black-and-white marble inside and out, a mosaic-inlaid floor of 56 panels worked by Sienese masters over two centuries, Nicola Pisano's carved pulpit, and the fresco-lined Piccolomini Library.

4. Santa Maria della Scala
↳ A vast former pilgrim hospital facing the cathedral, one of Europe's oldest, now a sprawling museum complex over four sloping levels. The Pellegrinaio hall blazes with 15th-century frescoes depicting the hospital's care of the sick and its charity — a rare secular cycle of everyday medieval life.

1. Pinacoteca Nazionale
↳ The world's most comprehensive gallery of the Sienese school, housed in the 15th-century Palazzo Buonsignori. Duccio's gold-ground Madonnas, Simone Martini's courtly panels, and the Lorenzetti brothers' pioneering landscapes span six centuries in chronological rooms.

2. Basilica di San Francesco
↳ A large Gothic Franciscan basilica begun in the 14th century on the city's eastern edge. The interior preserves fragments of Pietro and Ambrogio Lorenzetti's fresco cycles; the adjacent Oratorio di San Bernardino connects the complex to Siena's most important preacher.

3. Basilica of San Domenico
↳ The great Dominican fortress-church above the Fontebranda valley, built from 1226 in spare Gothic brick. The Cappella di Santa Caterina preserves the head of St. Catherine of Siena in a reliquary, flanked by Sodoma's 1526 frescoes of her ecstasies and stigmata.

4. Fortezza Medicea
↳ A massive brick fortress commissioned by Cosimo I de' Medici in 1560 to dominate the conquered city. The rampart walk gives the widest panorama in Siena; inside, the Enoteca Italiana hosts Italy's national wine library — hundreds of bottles available by the glass.

Poggibonsi · 30 min
↳ A Chianti town on the direct Siena-Empoli regional line and the closest train stop to San Gimignano — the medieval towers visible on the hillside. The town holds the unfinished Fortezza Medicea and Benozzo Gozzoli frescoes in the municipal museum.
🎫 book at: Trenitalia or Omio
Asciano · 30 min
↳ A quiet hill town set in the lunar clay badlands of the Crete Senesi — the Museum of Sacred Art with Duccio-school panels, and rolling ochre ridges that look nothing like the rest of Tuscany. Direct train on the scenic Trenitalia line via Siena FS.
🎫 book at: Trenitalia or Omio
Empoli · 50 min
↳ A Valdarno market town with the Museo della Collegiata holding Masolino and Pontormo altarpieces. The direct Siena-Empoli regional train terminates here at a mainline junction for Florence and Pisa.
🎫 book at: Trenitalia or Omio
Chiusi · 1 hr
↳ An Etruscan hilltop city perched above the Val di Chiana — the National Etruscan Museum with bucchero ware and bronze mirrors, a network of underground Etruscan tunnels, and a medieval labyrinth open for guided visits. Trenitalia regional trains serve Chiusi-Chianciano Terme on the Siena FS line.
🎫 book at: Trenitalia or Omio
Florence · 1 hr 11 min
↳ The cradle of the Renaissance — the Uffizi, the Duomo, the Accademia's David, and Ponte Vecchio. A direct Trenitalia regional train links Siena to Florence with no change.

🐚 The Shell and the Seventeen

Siena is a city you read with your feet. The Campo isn't flat — it tilts, nine brick segments fanning down to the Palazzo Pubblico like the inside of a scallop shell, and that slope is deliberate: medieval Siena built its civic heart as a basin so the whole population could gather facing its government. Stand at the low point and the Torre del Mangia rises exactly as the architects intended you to feel it — small below power.

The color has a name because of this place. "Burnt sienna" is the local earth, the raw ochre dug from these hills and roasted darker; the whole town is painted in versions of it, which is why the city seems to glow at the hour the light goes amber — the same warm stone that makes the cathedral's striped marble so startling, black and white and austere, against streets that are entirely terracotta.

And the rivalry never sleeps. The seventeen contrade — the Eagle, the Snail, the Wave, the Tower, the She-Wolf — are not folklore for tourists; they are the living social fabric, each with its own church, fountain, museum, and colors, and twice a year they send horses thundering around that shell-shaped square in the Palio. Walk the back lanes and you'll spot the boundary plaques and flags. You are never just in Siena. You are always in one of its seventeen smaller cities at once.
