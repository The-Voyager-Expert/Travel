Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 40 min → USS Arizona Memorial
1. USS Arizona Memorial
↳ The sunken USS Arizona lies where she sank on December 7, 1941, entombing 1,177 sailors. Oil still seeps slowly to the surface after eight decades. The 45-minute NPS program begins with archival film, then a Navy boat to the memorial.
📖 Wikipedia

📍 1 Arizona Memorial Place · Pearl Harbor
🚕 35 min → Nuʻuanu Pali Lookout
2. Nuʻuanu Pali Lookout
↳ A 1,186-foot pass through the Koʻolau Range where Kamehameha I won the Battle of Nuʻuanu in 1795, unifying Oʻahu. The lookout commands a panorama of the Windward Coast — Kāneʻohe Bay and Kailua visible below ridgelines that drop 3,000 feet. Wind gusts top 50 mph.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 6:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~45 min
🆓 Free
⚠️ Parking available · Fee applies for vehicles
📍 Nuuanu Pali Dr · Nuʻuanu
🚕 35 min → Hanauma Bay Nature Preserve
3. Hanauma Bay Nature Preserve
↳ A collapsed volcanic crater that became a coral reef — a Marine Life Conservation District with over 400 species of tropical fish at thigh depth, fearless of humans. Fish density is extraordinary. At the bay mouth, sea turtles pass within arm's reach.
📖 Wikipedia

📍 100 Hanauma Bay Rd · Hawaiʻi Kai
🚕 20 min → Diamond Head State Monument
4. Diamond Head State Monument
↳ The extinct volcanic tuff cone that defines Honolulu's skyline. The 0.8-mile trail climbs through WWII-era bunkers and a 99-step spiral staircase to the summit rim — 760 feet up — with a sweep of Waikiki, the Pacific, and the Koʻolau Range.
📖 Wikipedia

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 15 min → Iolani Palace
1. Iolani Palace
↳ The only royal palace ever built on American soil. Completed in 1882 for King Kalākaua with electric lights and telephones years before the White House had them. Queen Liliʻuokalani was imprisoned here for eight months after the 1893 overthrow of the Hawaiian Kingdom.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Saturday 9:00am - 5:00pm
🚫 Closed Sunday & Monday
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
⚠️ Advance booking recommended · Guided and self-guided tours available · Audio guide included
📍 364 S King St · Downtown Honolulu
🚶 25 min · 🚕 10 min → Bishop Museum
2. Bishop Museum
↳ The Bernice Pauahi Bishop Museum holds the world's largest collection of Hawaiian and Pacific cultural artifacts — 24 million specimens including feather cloaks worn by the aliʻi, ancient navigational tools, and a planetarium projecting traditional Polynesian star navigation.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Wednesday - Monday 9:00am - 5:00pm
🚫 Closed Tuesday
⏰ ~2 hr
⚠️ Purchase tickets at the gate
📍 1525 Bernice St · Kalihi
🚕 30 min → Byodo-In Temple
3. Byodo-In Temple
↳ A full-scale replica of the 900-year-old Byodo-In Temple in Uji, Japan, built in 1968 to mark the centennial of Japanese immigration to Hawaii. The vermilion pavilion floats at the base of the Koʻolau cliffs above a koi pond, with a 9-ton brass Buddha inside.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:30am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
⚠️ Admission fee at gate · Remove shoes before entering the temple
📍 47-200 Kahekili Hwy · Kāneʻohe
🚕 20 min → Kailua Beach
4. Kailua Beach
↳ A 2.5-mile arc of powdery white sand on the Windward Coast — repeatedly rated America's best beach for its calm turquoise water, consistent trade winds, and uncrowded feel compared to Waikiki. Sea kayaks and outrigger canoes launch from the park at the north end.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 526 Kawailoa Rd · Kailua Beach Park
🚕 35 min → hotel
Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 55 min → Waimea Valley
1. Waimea Valley
↳ A 1,875-acre botanical sanctuary in a valley sacred to Native Hawaiians, with 5,000 identified plant species along a 0.9-mile paved path to Waimea Falls — a 45-foot waterfall dropping into a swimming lagoon. One of the most significant cultural and archaeological sites on Oahu.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~2 hr
⚠️ Entry fee at gate · Water shoes recommended for waterfall swim · Last entry 4:00pm
📍 59-864 Kamehameha Hwy · Haleʻiwa
🚕 5 min → Laniakea Beach
2. Laniakea Beach
↳ Known as Turtle Beach — Hawaiian green sea turtles come ashore here every day to rest on the sand. The turtles, called honu in Hawaiian, are federally protected. Volunteers with the Malama na Honu program maintain a respectful 10-foot viewing distance.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
⚠️ Do not touch or approach the turtles · Volunteers present daily
📍 Kamehameha Hwy · Laniakea · Haleʻiwa
🚕 8 min → Haleiwa
3. Haleiwa
↳ The original North Shore surf town — a historic plantation-era streetscape that remains the cultural capital of Hawaiian surfing. Home to Matsumoto's shave ice, galleries, surf shops, and Aliʻi Beach Park where Pipeline legends first competed. Unhurried, uncrowded, and entirely itself.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~45 min
🆓 Free
📍 Haleʻiwa Town · North Shore
🚕 15 min → Dole Plantation
4. Dole Plantation
↳ The plantation founded in 1851 in Wahiawā, at the geographic center of Oahu. The pineapple garden maze holds a Guinness World Record at 3.1 acres. The Plantation Express train circles the grounds through pineapple fields. The Dole Whip soft-serve is a cult Hawaiian institution.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:30am - 5:30pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
⚠️ Garden maze and train ride have separate fees · Dole Whip at the snack bar
📍 64-1550 Kamehameha Hwy · Wahiawā
🚕 45 min → hotel

🍮 Local Tastes
Plate Lunch
↳ The quintessential Hawaiian working meal: two scoops of white rice, macaroni salad, and a protein — kalua pork, teriyaki beef, or chicken katsu. Born on the plantation era when workers of many nationalities shared food.
📍 Rainbow Drive-In · Highway Inn
Malasadas
↳ Portuguese-style fried doughnuts — no hole, coated in sugar, sometimes filled with custard or haupia. Brought to Hawaii by plantation-era workers. Leonard's Bakery on Kapahulu Ave remains the definitive version.
📍 Leonard's Bakery · Champion Malasadas
Loco Moco
↳ White rice topped with a hamburger patty, a fried egg, and brown gravy. Invented in Hilo on the Big Island and now a statewide institution. Pure, honest comfort food found everywhere on Oahu.
📍 Rainbow Drive-In · Zippy's
Shave Ice
↳ Not a snow cone — ultra-fine ice shaved from a block, packed into a cone, soaked in flavored syrups. Often served over vanilla ice cream or sweetened azuki beans. Matsumoto's and Waiola are the benchmarks.
📍 Waiola Shave Ice · Aoki's Shave Ice · Haleiwa

💡 On Oahu

Oahu puts the entire arc of Hawaiian history into a single day: the sunken battleship where American entry into World War II began, a royal lookout where Kamehameha I unified the islands by force, a volcanic crater bay where the fish have lived undisturbed long enough to swim in your hands.

The itinerary reads like history but feels like geography. Pearl Harbor is west. Nuʻuanu Pali is north. Hanauma Bay is east. Diamond Head is south. You circle nearly the whole island in a day, and every stop earns its place on its own terms. No other island in the world packs this particular combination of living reef and world-altering history into one morning and one afternoon.
