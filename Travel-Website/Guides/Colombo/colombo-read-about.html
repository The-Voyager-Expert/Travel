A city built by four empires on top of each other, still deciding what it wants to be — and more interesting for being mid-conversation about that question than any capital that has already settled it.

The fort that isn't a fort anymore

Start in the Fort, Colombo's old colonial core, and pay attention to the layers. The Portuguese arrived first, in 1505, and built a stockade around their trading post here. The Dutch took the city in 1656, tore down the Portuguese walls, and built their own — thicker, better engineered, connected to a canal system that made Fort a functional island. The British arrived in 1796, found the Dutch fortifications inconvenient, and demolished most of them. By 1870, the walls were gone and the moat was filled in. What remains is the name: Fort.

The Dutch Hospital is the most tangible remnant of what preceded the British. It's a long, arcaded building that went up in the seventeenth century to house VOC sailors and now contains restaurants and boutiques — the conversion was done well, which is not always the case with heritage repurposing. But walk the neighborhood and you'll find the layers bleeding through: Dutch street names, a Portuguese church, British Neoclassical government buildings, a handful of glass towers from the last twenty years. Nothing was ever fully cleared to make room for the next era. Colombo accumulates.

The Jami Ul-Alfar mosque on Second Cross Street — red and white candy-stripe, built in 1909 — is the one building visitors reliably photograph and the one that most accurately represents Colombo's actual demographic composition. The Moors, as Sri Lanka's Muslim minority is historically called, have been in Colombo since before any European arrived. The mosque sits in the middle of the Pettah, surrounded by the controlled chaos of the city's main bazaar. It looks nothing like anything else in the neighborhood, and it fits perfectly.

The Pettah, which is the actual city

Everything the Fort district is polished and legible, the Pettah is dense, loud, and resistant to being understood in a single visit. It is Colombo's wholesale and retail market neighborhood — the place where goods actually change hands — and it has been this since the Dutch period. The streets are organized loosely by trade: electronics on Main Street, fabrics on Keyzer Street, hardware on Front Street, dried goods and spices deeper in. The organization is loose enough that you'll find a mobile phone repair stall next to a sari shop next to a stall selling nothing but wire.

The Pettah is where the religious geography of Colombo becomes most compressed. Within a few blocks you'll pass the red-and-white Jami Ul-Alfar mosque, the Sri Ponnambalam Vanesar Kovil (a Tamil Hindu temple with a gopuram tower thick with painted deities), and the Wolvendaal Church, a Dutch Reformed church from 1757 that is the oldest Dutch colonial building in Sri Lanka. These institutions have coexisted on adjacent blocks for centuries. The coexistence is not ceremonial. The streets between them are shared by everyone.

The Pettah does not curate itself for visitors. Go in without a plan, follow what interests you, and expect to get at least briefly lost.

The best thing to eat in the Pettah is also the simplest: a kottu roti from a street stall, the vendor's twin metal blades clanging against the griddle as they chop and mix roti, egg, vegetables, and protein together in a rhythm that you can hear half a block away. The sound is Colombo's characteristic street music. It's loud, fast, and smells extraordinary.

What Britain built, and what it cost

The British made Ceylon — as they called Sri Lanka — one of the most profitable colonies in the empire. The key was tea. After a coffee blight wiped out Ceylon's coffee plantations in the 1860s, the Scottish planter James Taylor planted experimental tea on his estate in Kandy, and within two decades the highlands were covered in it. The tea that the world drinks as "Ceylon tea" was produced by Tamil laborers brought from South India to work the estates under conditions that were, by any modern standard, a form of indentured servitude.

The Tamil question — the relationship between the Sinhalese majority and the Tamil minority — is the thread that runs through all of modern Sri Lankan history, including Colombo's. The civil war between the government and the Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam (LTTE) ran from 1983 to 2009. At its peak, the LTTE controlled significant territory in the north and east and carried out bombings in Colombo itself — the Central Bank bombing in 1996 killed 91 people and wounded hundreds in the Fort district, a block from where tourists now eat brunch at the Dutch Hospital. The war ended in 2009 with a military victory that remains contested in its methods and aftermath.

Colombo today is rebuilding and, in some neighborhoods, booming. The Port City Colombo — a massive land reclamation project off the coast of the Fort district, funded largely by Chinese investment — is adding a new district to the city that is designed to function as a special economic zone. It is currently a construction site the size of several city blocks, rising from the Indian Ocean. Whether it becomes what its architects intend or something else entirely is a question Colombo is still in the middle of answering.

Buddhism, temples, and what the sacred looks like here

Sri Lanka is roughly 70 percent Buddhist, and the relationship between Buddhism and Sinhalese national identity is close enough that the two are sometimes hard to separate in political discourse. The Gangaramaya Temple in Colombo is a good place to understand this relationship because it is not a serene meditation space. It is a living institution that combines a functioning monastery, a museum crammed with donated objects from every Buddhist country on earth, a collection of luxury cars and vintage automobiles given by donors, a Bo tree under which monks meditate, a museum of currency, a collection of elephant howdahs, and elaborate festival decorations for the Navam Perahera — the February procession that moves through the city with decorated elephants, drummers, and fire dancers, drawing enormous crowds.

The temple is excessive by Western standards of what a sacred space should look like. It is also completely genuine — a place where people pray, monks study, and the connection between religious practice and community life is still intact. The mismatched donations (the luxury cars, a miniature Statue of Liberty, a model of the Eiffel Tower) are gifts from wealthy Sri Lankan diaspora communities around the world. The temple accepted them all. That acceptance of incongruity feels like something important about how this city actually works.

The promenade, the kites, and the sea

Galle Face Green is a long seafront promenade stretching south of the Fort along the Indian Ocean, originally cleared by the Dutch for military reasons — an open field of fire. The British maintained it as a green and added a promenade. Today it's where Colombo goes to breathe. In the late afternoon and evening, families spread out on the grass, kite vendors sell neon kites that rise above the breaking waves, couples sit on the seawall watching the sunset, and snack carts sell isso vadai (prawn fritters) and pol sambol on bread and paper bags of peanuts.

The Galle Face Green does what few open public spaces in tropical Asian cities manage to do: it feels genuinely public. Not curated, not charged, not developed. The old Galle Face Hotel on its southern edge — a massive colonial-era institution where everyone from Yuri Gagarin to Prince Charles has stayed, and where the Sunday brunch buffet remains an institution among Colombo's upper class — anchors one end. The Indian Ocean anchors everything else. In the early morning, before the vendors arrive, fishermen cast from the seawall into the surf.

The food, which carries the whole history

Sri Lankan cuisine is not Indian cuisine, though the Indian influence is present and significant. It is its own tradition, built around rice and curry in combinations that change by region and community. In Colombo you can eat Sinhalese food, Tamil food, Muslim food (distinct from both), and Burgher food — the cooking tradition of Sri Lanka's Eurasian community, descended from Dutch and Portuguese colonists who stayed after the empires left. Each of these traditions uses coconut in its own way, because coconut is everywhere: in the milk that thins the curry, in the sambol that accompanies the rice, in the arrack that is Sri Lanka's own spirit, made from coconut flower sap and aged in wooden barrels.

The mud crab is the dish most Colombo visitors will tell you about afterward. Sri Lanka's lagoon crabs are large, meaty, and handled here in ways that don't appear elsewhere: black pepper crab, chilli crab, butter crab, devilled crab. The Fisherman's Wharf at the Hilton and Ministry of Crab (founded by two Sri Lanka cricket internationals) are the names that appear on every recommendation list. Both are excellent. But a small seafood restaurant in the Dehiwala or Mount Lavinia area south of the city, with no name on a list, is often just as good and a fraction of the price.

The city that is mid-sentence

Colombo lacks the confident self-presentation of cities that have settled on what they are. Bangkok knows it is the capital of Southeast Asian street food and nightlife. Singapore knows it is clean, efficient, and expensive. Colombo is still mid-sentence, and this is not a failure. It is a city less than fifteen years out of a civil war, navigating between Chinese investment and its own traditions, between a Buddhist nationalist politics and a plural street-level reality, between the luxury towers going up along the Beira Lake waterfront and the fish market in the Pettah that hasn't changed since the Dutch were here.

The unfinished quality is not a flaw. It's what makes Colombo real in a way that more polished capitals have stopped being.

Most of the travelers who come through Sri Lanka are headed for the beaches, or the tea country, or the ancient ruins at Anuradhapura and Polonnaruwa. They treat Colombo as a transit city, spending a night at either end. This is understandable and wrong. Give Colombo two proper days and it will show you a version of South Asian urban life that the more famous cities — Chennai, Mumbai, Kolkata — are too large and too documented to offer in the same concentrated form.

Before you leave, here's the thing to carry: every layer of Colombo's colonial history — Portuguese, Dutch, British — was built by people who were absolutely certain of their permanence here. The Dutch built canal systems and a church that has now stood for 267 years. The British built a railway network and a legal system. All of them left. What stayed was the city's own population, absorbing and adapting, building mosques next to churches next to kovils, selling spices and saris and circuit boards in the same market where the Dutch VOC traders once bought cinnamon. Colombo's genius is not what it was built to be. It is what it kept after everyone who built it went home.
