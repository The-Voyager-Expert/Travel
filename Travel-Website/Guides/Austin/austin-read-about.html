A city that has always reinvented itself — and is now in the middle of doing it again, faster and more expensively than ever before.

What Austin actually is

Austin is three cities stacked on top of each other, and understanding which one you're in at any given moment is the key to making sense of the place. There is the capital city — the seat of Texas government, the limestone dome of the Capitol rising above Congress Avenue, the bureaucratic and political apparatus that has governed one of the largest states in the country for 175 years. There is the university town — the University of Texas at Austin, flagship of the state university system, 50,000 students, a medical school, a law school, a campus that bleeds into the city's east side. And there is the boomtown — the tech and music and creative migration that started in the 1990s and has not stopped accelerating since, turning a mid-sized southwestern city into one of the fastest-growing metropolitan areas in America.

These three cities coexist in a state of productive tension that gives Austin its particular character. The government buildings and the dive bars and the corporate campuses are layered on top of each other in ways that don't always make sense until you've walked it. The Capitol sits at the top of Congress Avenue like a civic anchor. Six blocks south, Sixth Street runs through the middle of one of the densest bar districts in Texas. Three blocks east of that, the old railroad depot neighborhood has turned into an innovation district. This is a city that built itself in overlapping waves, and none of the waves fully erased what came before.

The Texas capital and the land it sits on

Austin was chosen as the capital of the Republic of Texas in 1839 by Mirabeau Lamar, who saw the site on a buffalo hunt and decided that the hills above the Colorado River were where Texas should put its government. His contemporaries thought he was out of his mind. The Comanche nation controlled the region, the site was a full week's ride from the populated eastern settlements, and placing the capital here felt less like a practical decision and more like an act of expansionist willpower — a marker planted in contested ground to claim a direction of travel.

The Comanche had controlled the southern plains for more than a century at that point, and the Hill Country west of Austin was the eastern edge of their territory — the Comancheria, a political and military reality that the Republic of Texas could barely contest. The capital's location was a statement about where Texas intended to go, not a description of where it was. Sam Houston, Lamar's rival and predecessor, thought the whole idea was reckless and tried repeatedly to move the capital back east during his own terms. He failed. Austin stayed put and Texas expanded toward it.

The Texas State Capitol, completed in 1888, is seven feet taller than the US Capitol in Washington. This was intentional.

Stand on Congress Avenue and look up at the dome. The building is made of Sunset Red granite quarried from Marble Falls, eighty miles northwest, and it was built by convict labor — a detail that usually doesn't make it into the tour. The dome is larger than the national Capitol by design: Texas had just rejoined the Union after Reconstruction and the state was making a point about its own magnitude. The inscription inside the dome reads "Texas One and Indivisible." Every choice about this building is a political statement. That hasn't changed.

The music and where it came from

Austin's claim to be the Live Music Capital of the World is both marketing and truth. The city has more live music venues per capita than anywhere in the United States, and on a Friday night in the spring, the sound from the clubs on Red River Street spills into the street and overlaps — blues from one door, country from the next, something that sounds like noise from a third. The concentration is real and it predates the slogan by decades.

The roots of it go back to the late 1960s and early 1970s, when Austin was a college town with cheap rent and a counter-cultural scene that attracted musicians who didn't fit anywhere else. The Armadillo World Headquarters — a former armory converted to a music venue on South First Street — became the unlikely center of what was called the Austin Sound: a convergence of country, blues, folk, and rock that didn't have a genre name because it didn't fit the categories. Willie Nelson, who had been writing songs in Nashville for years without finding his audience, moved to Austin in 1972 and found his people immediately. Jerry Jeff Walker, Waylon Jennings, Doug Sahm — the Cosmic Cowboys, as they were called — played the Armadillo and defined a moment that still shapes how the city understands itself.

The Armadillo closed in 1980. The building became a bank and then a parking structure. The ethos scattered into the city's DNA and produced Antone's, which brought Chicago blues to Austin starting in 1975, and the Continental Club on South Congress, which has been running since 1957 and is still the best place in the city to hear genuine Texas music without the production values of a larger venue. Stevie Ray Vaughan played Antone's. He grew up in Dallas but he found himself in Austin, and the city was right to put his statue on the shore of Town Lake where you can see it from the Congress Avenue Bridge.

Sixth Street and what replaced it

Sixth Street was Austin's entertainment district for a generation and it is now a casualty of its own success. The stretch of bars and clubs between Congress and Red River became so well-known, and attracted such crowds, that it stopped being a place where interesting music happened and became a place where people came to be in a place they'd heard about. The music is still there, technically, but the venues cater to volume rather than quality and the street on a weekend night is a theater of inebriation that has more in common with Bourbon Street than with the Austin that made the Sixth Street myth.

The better district is Red River, one block east, where Stubb's outdoor amphitheater and the Parish and Emo's have maintained a genuine music culture with real booking and real artists. The East Side — the historically Black neighborhood east of I-35 that has been gentrifying since the late 2000s — now has the best concentration of bars and restaurants and small venues in the city. The street that everyone is on now is East 6th, east of the highway, which didn't exist as a scene fifteen years ago. Austin's interesting music has always moved eastward as the successful places priced out the interesting ones.

The divide that runs through the city

Interstate 35 cuts through Austin north to south and it is one of the most consequential pieces of highway in the American South. The interstate follows the route of what was known as the "Negro District Line" — the city's explicit racial boundary, established in the 1928 master plan, which confined Black and Hispanic residents to the east side of the road by restricting services, schools, and housing elsewhere. The highway was then built directly on top of that line, making a physical barrier out of what had been a legal one.

The east side of I-35 was where East Austin's Black and Mexican-American communities built their institutions: churches, schools, social clubs, businesses along East 11th and East 12th Streets that made up a genuine civic life under conditions of systematic exclusion. When the neighborhood started to gentrify in the 2000s, it was those communities — displaced by rising rents and changing neighborhood character — that bore the cost. The restaurants and bars that drew new residents are now on the same streets where Huston-Tillotson University and Victory Grill and the Rosewood neighborhood had sustained Black Austin for decades.

Victory Grill on East 11th Street opened in 1945 to serve Black soldiers returning from World War II. B.B. King played there. Bobby "Blue" Bland. James Brown. It's still standing.

The gentrification conversation in Austin is more charged than in most cities because the displacement is so recent and the timeline is so compressed. People who lived in East Austin in 2005 watched their neighborhoods transform around them in real time. The physical city looks dramatically better by most conventional measures. What it lost is harder to account for in those same measures, but the people who were there know what it was.

The boom and what it costs

Austin's growth since 2010 has been extraordinary by any metric. The metropolitan area has added more than a million people. Tesla moved its headquarters here. Oracle, Apple, Amazon, Google, and dozens of smaller tech companies have opened major offices. The airport is perpetually overcrowded. Housing prices have doubled and in some neighborhoods tripled. The city that used to be known for its relative affordability — the reason artists and musicians and writers could live here — is now expensive enough that those same people can't afford to move in.

The physical transformation is visible everywhere downtown. Cranes have been the dominant feature of the skyline for years. The towers going up along the waterfront and along the Congress Avenue corridor are changing the city's silhouette into something that looks more like Dallas, which is not how Austin people prefer to think about themselves. The Congress Avenue Bridge — the one with the million bats sleeping under it, the one with the Stevie Ray Vaughan statue at one end — now has glass towers on both sides of it that weren't there a decade ago.

What Austin is wrestling with is whether a city can grow this fast without losing the quality that made it worth moving to. The answer is probably no, but the city is trying to figure out what survives anyway. The barbecue culture has survived — Franklin Barbecue on East 11th Street has a line that starts before dawn and the food at the end of it is legitimately one of the best things you can eat in America. The music culture has survived in altered form. The outdoor culture — the greenbelt swimming holes, Barton Springs pool, the Town Lake trail — is still there and still free and still genuinely excellent. The informality that made Austin feel different from other American cities of its size is harder to find but not entirely gone.

The thing to carry with you

Here is the frame that changes everything: Austin has always been a city of arrivals. L'Enfant's DC was designed. Nashville grew around an institution. Austin grew because people kept coming here to become something they weren't yet. The musicians came in the 1970s because the city had space for reinvention. The tech workers came in the 1990s for the same reason. The latest wave came because the previous waves made the city look good on paper.

The original Austin slogan — Keep Austin Weird — was coined in 2000 by a local business owner as a call to support independent businesses. It became a license plate and a marketing campaign and finally a punchline. But the instinct behind it was real: a city that had grown up outside the Texas mainstream, that had always been slightly apart from what the rest of the state valued, was trying to preserve the conditions that made it different. The slogan failed as a preservation strategy because no slogan can hold back the economics. What it succeeded at was naming the thing worth protecting.

What Austin has always done — and what it is still doing, even now, even under all the cranes and the corporate campuses and the traffic — is make room for people who are in the middle of figuring something out. That's what the cheap rents were for. That's what the music scene was for. That's what Barton Springs is for on a Tuesday afternoon when you're skipping work. The city has always understood that the most valuable thing it could offer was not infrastructure or opportunity in the formal sense, but time and space to become. That's harder to find here than it used to be. But the impulse is still in the city's bones. You can feel it if you're paying attention.
