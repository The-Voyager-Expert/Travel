✨ The Planned City on a Swamp

Aracaju was not so much founded as engineered. In 1855, the provincial president Inácio Joaquim Barbosa ordered an entirely new capital built on a sandbar between two rivers — a grid city laid out from scratch on mangrove swampland. Contemporary critics called it a disaster. The ground was waterlogged, insects were relentless, and the city's first residents had to be compelled to relocate from the old capital São Cristóvão. What that forced founding created was something unusual: a Brazilian city shaped by a blueprint rather than by centuries of organic growth, which is why Aracaju's street grid remains startlingly regular compared to most northeastern capitals.

The Vaza-Barris River that feeds Croa do Goré on Day 3 also marks the original southern boundary of that planned settlement. The tidal sandbar itself was well-known to fishermen long before tourism arrived — it appears on 19th-century nautical charts of the Sergipe coast as a navigation hazard, not an attraction.
