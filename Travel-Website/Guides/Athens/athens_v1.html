Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 10 min · 🚕 6 min → Acropolis of Athens
1. Acropolis of Athens
↳ The ancient citadel crowning Athens, topped by the 5th-century BC Parthenon temple to Athena. The most important surviving monument of Classical Greece, it also holds the Erechtheion, the Propylaea gateway, and the small Temple of Athena Nike.

2. Acropolis Museum
↳ Bernard Tschumi's glass-and-concrete museum, opened in 2009, holds every major find from the Acropolis. Its top-floor Parthenon Gallery displays the surviving frieze at the temple's exact dimensions and orientation, with the real Parthenon framed through the windows.

3. Areopagus Hill
↳ A bare marble outcrop just below the Acropolis that served as the high court of ancient Athens. It is famous as the spot where the Apostle Paul preached to the Athenians, and today gives a free panorama over the Ancient Agora and the city rooftops.
📖 Wikipedia

4. Anafiotika
↳ A tiny Cycladic-style enclave built into the north slope of the Acropolis in the mid-19th century by stoneworkers from the island of Anafi. Its whitewashed cube houses, stepped lanes, and bougainvillea feel like a remote Aegean village dropped into central Athens.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 12 min · 🚕 7 min → Ancient Agora of Athens
1. Ancient Agora of Athens
↳ The civic and commercial heart of ancient Athens, where Socrates argued and democracy took shape. The grounds hold the Temple of Hephaestus — the best-preserved ancient Greek temple anywhere — and the reconstructed Stoa of Attalos, now the Agora Museum.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Ancient Agora

⏰ ~1 hr 30 min

🚶 6 min · 🚕 4 min → Roman Agora
2. Roman Agora
↳ The Roman-era marketplace of Athens, built under Julius Caesar and Augustus. Its centerpiece is the Tower of the Winds, a 1st-century BC octagonal marble structure that combined a sundial, water clock, and weather vane — often called the world's first meteorological station.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Hadrian's Library
↳ A grand complex built by Emperor Hadrian in 132 AD, holding papyrus-scroll libraries, reading rooms, lecture halls, and a courtyard with a central pool. Its surviving Corinthian-columned west facade faces Monastiraki Square.
📖 Wikipedia

4. Monastiraki Square
↳ A lively square and flea-market district at the foot of the Acropolis, named after the little Pantanassa church at its center. It blends the Ottoman Tzistarakis Mosque, Hadrian's Library, the metro station, and wide-open views up to the Parthenon.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 8 min · 🚕 5 min → Temple of Olympian Zeus
1. Temple of Olympian Zeus
↳ A colossal temple to Zeus begun in the 6th century BC and finished by Emperor Hadrian in 131 AD — some 638 years later. Once Greece's largest temple with 104 Corinthian columns, fifteen still stand. The adjacent Arch of Hadrian marked the line between the old and Roman city.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Panathenaic Stadium
↳ The only stadium in the world built entirely of marble. Reconstructed on an ancient site for the first modern Olympic Games in 1896, the horseshoe of white Pentelic marble seats around 45,000 and still serves as the finish of the Athens Marathon.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Panathenaic Stadium

🚶 12 min · 🚕 6 min → Syntagma Square
3. Syntagma Square
↳ Athens' central square, fronting the Hellenic Parliament and the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier. The marble cenotaph is guarded around the clock by Evzones in traditional kilted uniform; their changing-of-the-guard ceremony runs hourly, with a grand version Sunday at 11:00am.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Plateia Syntagmatos · Syntagma
🚶 22 min · 🚕 9 min → Mount Lycabettus
4. Mount Lycabettus
↳ At 277m, the highest point in central Athens, a limestone hill crowned by the whitewashed 19th-century Chapel of St. George. The summit gives the city's widest view — over the Acropolis, the Saronic Gulf, and the full sprawl of Athens — reached on foot or by the hillside funicular.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Lofos Lykavittou · Kolonaki
🚶 24 min · 🚕 11 min → National Archaeological Museum
5. National Archaeological Museum
↳ The largest museum in Greece and one of the world's great collections of ancient Greek art, spanning prehistory to late antiquity. Highlights include the gold Mask of Agamemnon, the bronze Artemision Zeus, the Antikythera Mechanism, and the Jockey of Artemision.

🏺 From the Notebook

Athens rewards a slow eye for time stacked on time. The Temple of Olympian Zeus took 638 years to finish — begun by a tyrant in the 6th century BC and only completed by a Roman emperor — so the columns you stand under were already "ancient history" to the Romans who topped them off. A few streets over, the Tower of the Winds in the Roman Agora quietly claims a different superlative: built in the 1st century BC with a sundial, a water clock, and a wind vane, it is often called the world's first weather station.

The city also keeps surprising villages inside itself. Anafiotika, the cluster of whitewashed cube houses on the Acropolis' north slope, was built in the 1800s by stonemasons from the island of Anafi who were homesick enough to rebuild a piece of the Cyclades under the Parthenon. And the Panathenaic Stadium — all gleaming Pentelic marble — is the same quarry-stone the ancient Athenians used; the 1896 Olympic organizers simply re-dug the old running track and re-clad it.

One practical thread worth pulling: a single combined ticket covers the Acropolis plus the Ancient Agora, Roman Agora, Hadrian's Library, and the Temple of Olympian Zeus — five of this guide's stops — valid across several days. If you go early, the Acropolis at 8:00am opening is a genuinely different, near-empty place than it is by mid-morning.
