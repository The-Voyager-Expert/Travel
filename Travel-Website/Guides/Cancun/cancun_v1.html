Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 10 min → KM 14.6 departure dock
1. Laguna Nichupté Jungle Tour
↳ The mangrove channels threading behind the Hotel Zone hold crocodiles, tropical birds, and dense jungle canopy accessible only by speedboat. A 90-minute circuit threads through the labyrinth, pauses at a crocodile feeding station, and opens onto the wide lagoon.

2. El Rey Archaeological Zone
↳ A pre-Columbian Maya city occupied from around 900 CE to the 1500s, El Rey spreads across the southern Hotel Zone and is rarely crowded. Dozens of stone platforms and pyramid bases are walkable in near-solitude, with iguanas sunning on the ancient walls and lagoon views on one side.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Zona Arqueológica El Rey · 4.1⭐ · inah.gob.mx
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr

📍 Blvd. Kukulcan KM 17 · Zona Hotelera
🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min
3. Playa Delfines
↳ The only Hotel Zone beach without a hotel backdrop — a wide open strip of white sand facing the open Caribbean with no development behind it. An elevated mirador gives a panoramic view of turquoise water and the iconic CANCUN sign. Waves here are among the strongest in the zone; better for viewing than swimming.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Blvd. Kukulcan KM 17.5 · Zona Hotelera
🚕 5 min
4. Museo Maya de Cancún
↳ One of the most significant Maya museums in Mexico, with over 350 artifacts from sites across the Yucatán Peninsula. The collection spans Maya civilization from earliest settlements through the Spanish conquest, with jade masks, ceramic figurines, and carved stelae.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Museo Maya de Cancún · 4.6⭐ · inah.gob.mx
🏛️ Tuesday - Sunday 9:00am - 6:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Blvd. Kukulcan KM 16.5 · Zona Hotelera

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 10 min → Playa Caracol pier · 🚢 25 min → Isla Mujeres
1. El Garrafón Natural Reef Park
↳ A protected reef park at the southern tip of Isla Mujeres where a shallow natural pool and surrounding reef hold tropical fish, sea turtles, and rays in water clear enough to count scales. The park includes snorkeling gear, kayak rentals, and a cliffside infinity pool overlooking the Caribbean.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Garrafón Natural Reef Park · 4.3⭐ · garrafon.com
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~2 hr
📍 Carretera Garrafón · Isla Mujeres
🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min
2. Punta Sur · Templo Ixchel
↳ The southernmost point of Isla Mujeres is also the easternmost point of Mexico. A small ruined Maya temple dedicated to Ixchel, goddess of the moon, sits at the tip of rocky cliffs above crashing surf. Cliff-edge sculptures by local artists surround it with ocean views on three sides.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Punta Sur · Templo Ixchel · 4.5⭐ · islamujeres.gob.mx
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Carretera Perimetral · Punta Sur · Isla Mujeres
🚕 10 min
3. Downtown Isla Mujeres
↳ The compact colonial village at the north end of the island runs along pedestrian Avenida Hidalgo — lined with restaurants, jewelry shops, and painted buildings. The central plaza, best restaurants, and ferry pier are all within a few minutes' walk of each other.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Avenida Hidalgo · El Centro · Isla Mujeres
🚶 10 min · 🚕 5 min
4. Playa Norte
↳ Consistently ranked among the best beaches in Mexico, Playa Norte is a crescent of powdery white sand with water so calm and shallow it reads turquoise in the afternoon light. The beach faces west toward the mainland, keeping the water flat regardless of weather — ideal for swimming or wading.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Playa Norte · Isla Mujeres

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 2h 30min → Chichén Itzá
1. El Castillo · Chichén Itzá
↳ The stepped pyramid of El Castillo dominates a sprawling ancient city among the most powerful in the Maya world from 600 to 1200 CE. It contains the Great Ball Court, the Temple of the Warriors, and dozens of carved platforms across a jungle clearing. Arrive at opening — by 10:00am coach tours fill the paths.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Chichén Itzá Archaeological Zone · 4.7⭐ · inah.gob.mx
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~2 hr
⚠️ Two separate fees: federal INAH admission + Yucatán state admission
📍 Pisté · Yucatán
🚶 15 min · 🚕 10 min
2. Sacred Cenote
↳ The Sacred Cenote is a natural sinkhole 60 metres wide and 27 metres deep that served as the site of Maya ritual offerings. Divers have recovered jade, gold, ceramics, and human remains from its dark waters. You cannot swim here but standing at the edge of this moss-covered pit carries its own weight.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~20 min
⚠️ Included with Chichén Itzá site admission
📍 1 km north of El Castillo · Chichén Itzá site
🚕 5 min
3. Cenote Ik Kil
↳ A circular open-sky sinkhole 40 metres deep with lush vines cascading down the limestone walls to a swimming platform at the base. The deep blue water is cold, clear, and connected to the underground river system. A staircase spirals down from the surface.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Cenote Ik Kil · 4.6⭐ · google.com
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Carretera Mérida–Puerto Juárez · Tinum · Yucatán
🚕 35 min
4. Valladolid
↳ A small colonial city 40 kilometres east of Chichén Itzá with a preserved Spanish-era street grid and pastel facades. The main plaza anchors a walkable centro with the Cathedral of San Gervasio, food stalls, and Cenote Zaci within the town limits. The right place to slow down before the drive back.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Parque Francisco Cantón Rosado · Valladolid · Yucatán
🚕 2h 15min → hotel
Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 1h 30min → Akumal Bay
1. Akumal Bay
↳ A protected cove where sea turtles graze on seagrass beds in waters rarely deeper than 4 metres — one of the most consistent spots in Mexico to snorkel with wild turtles. A certified guide is required by law. Arrive before 8:00am; by 10:00am the beach fills with tour groups.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Gran Cenote
↳ The most accessible entrance to the Sistema Sac Actun — the world's largest underwater cave system. An open cavern fed by crystal-clear groundwater through stalactite chambers. Snorkel past limestone columns and blind fish, then surface in an open pool under the sky. Cash only on site.
📖 Wikipedia

🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 4:45pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
⚠️ Cash only — no ATM on site · last entry 4:15pm
📍 Carretera Tulum–Cobá KM 5 · Tulum
🚕 10 min
3. Tulum Ruins
↳ A walled Maya port city perched on 12-metre cliffs above the Caribbean, occupied into the 16th century. El Castillo sits at the cliff edge with the turquoise sea as its backdrop. The ruins are compact and walkable, but the cliffside views over tropical water are unlike anything else on the peninsula.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~1 hr 30 min

📍 Zona Arqueológica de Tulum · Quintana Roo
🚶 10 min · 🚕 5 min
4. Tulum Beach
↳ Directly below the ruins, a path descends to a crescent of white sand backed by the same cliffs the Maya temples sit atop. The water is calmer than the Hotel Zone — swimming is possible — and the late-afternoon light turns the ruins above golden. The Caribbean in front, ruins overhead.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~1 hr
⚠️ Accessed via ruins path — included with site admission
📍 Below Tulum Archaeological Zone · Quintana Roo

🍮 Local Tastes
Cochinita Pibil
↳ The defining dish of the Yucatán: pork marinated in bitter orange and achiote, slow-roasted in banana leaves. Pull-apart meat on tortillas with pickled red onion and habanero.
📍 Taquerias · El Centro
Marquesitas
↳ A Yucatecan street snack: a thin crisp crepe rolled into a cylinder, filled with Edam cheese and sweet filling — cajeta or Nutella. Carts appear at night around Parque Las Palapas.
📍 Parque Las Palapas · El Centro
Sopa de Lima
↳ A Yucatecan citrus chicken broth with shredded turkey, tortilla strips, and a squeeze of sweet Yucatecan lima — less acidic than Persian lime. On lunch menus throughout El Centro.
📍 Labná · Av. Margaritas 29 · El Centro
Tikin Xic
↳ A Maya coastal preparation: whole fish marinated in achiote and bitter orange, wrapped in banana leaves, grilled over an open flame. Best versions on Isla Mujeres waterfront.
📍 Waterfront restaurants · Isla Mujeres
Xtabentún
↳ A Yucatecan honey liqueur made from the xtabentún flower and anise. Produced in the Yucatán since pre-Columbian times; sipped neat after dinner at El Centro mezcalerias.
📍 El Socio Naiz · Av. Nader · El Centro

✨ Why Cancun

Cancun rewards the traveler willing to look past the Hotel Zone. The ribbon of beach resorts along Blvd. Kukulcan is undeniably appealing, but within a day's reach lie some of the most significant archaeological sites in the Western Hemisphere, a network of underground rivers with no parallel on earth, an island time largely forgot, and a colonial city that still moves at the pace of the interior.

The Yucatán Peninsula is a small enough geography that you can stand at a Maya ruin at dawn and be back at your pool by afternoon. The Hotel Zone is a base, not a destination — the destination is everything within a two-hour drive of it.
