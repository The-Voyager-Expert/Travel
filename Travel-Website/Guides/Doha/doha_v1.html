Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 15 min → Museum of Islamic Art
1. Museum of Islamic Art
↳ Built on its own artificial island in Doha Bay, I.M. Pei's stacked geometric volumes hold fourteen centuries of Islamic art — metalwork, ceramics, textiles, jewellery, and calligraphy from Spain to Central Asia. Octagonal cutouts flood the galleries with light; the West Bay skyline frames the scene from the water.

2. National Museum of Qatar
↳ Jean Nouvel's interlocking disc structure, evoking Qatar's desert rose crystal, wraps around the old palace of Sheikh Abdullah Al Thani. Inside, galleries trace Qatar from ancient seabed through pearl-diving and the oil era. Among the most architecturally ambitious museum buildings in the world.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 25 min → Barzan Towers
1. Barzan Towers
↳ Two whitewashed watchtowers rising above the scrub plain of Umm Salal Mohammed, built for the Al Thani family to watch the desert and coastal approaches. The taller tower stands over a restored madrasa, mosque, and palm garden; the shorter sits near the road. Free to enter — Qatar's defensive past open to the sky.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Aspire Zone
↳ Qatar's 250-hectare sporting campus: Khalifa International Stadium, a FIFA World Cup venue, the Aspire Dome, world's largest indoor multi-sport hall, and the 300-metre Aspire Tower. The park and lake circuit is free and open — a walk around the grounds shows the full scale of what was built.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 11:00pm
⏰ ~2 hrs

🚕 15 min → Katara Cultural Village
3. Katara Cultural Village
↳ Qatar's cultural campus on the beach between West Bay and The Pearl: galleries, theatres, an amphitheatre, and restaurants along a seafront promenade. The Katara Mosque anchors it with an extraordinary Iznik-tiled blue facade. Families fill the alleys on Friday evenings with musicians near the fountain.
📖 Wikipedia

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 1h 30min → Al Zubarah Fort
1. Al Zubarah Fort
↳ Qatar's only UNESCO World Heritage Site: an 18th-century pearl-trading town whose 60+ hectares of coral-stone ruins survive under the desert. The fort houses the interpretive centre and a lookout platform. The 100 km drive north through flat scrub-desert is as much the draw as the site itself.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Mathaf: Arab Museum of Modern Art
↳ The Arab world's foremost museum of modern art, in a converted school on Education City campus, holds 9,000+ works tracing Arab artistic practice from the mid-19th century to today. The permanent collection is the strongest case anywhere for modern Arab painting — movements rarely seen outside the region.

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 15 min → Doha Corniche
1. Doha Corniche
↳ Seven kilometres of palm-lined waterfront tracing Doha Bay's arc, West Bay glass towers rising from the water on one side, dhow-scattered sea on the other. The promenade shifts from the MIA island at the south end through fishing piers to the skyline at the north — the most photographed cityscape in Qatar.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Corniche Street · bay promenade
🚶 12 min · 🚕 3 min → Al Bidda Park
2. Al Bidda Park
↳ Doha's largest city park spans 40 hectares along the Corniche: a central lake, shaded walking trails, and an amphitheatre. The park's sightlines look out to the bay and back to the skyline — a rare green lung between the waterfront and the urban grid.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 6:00am - 12:00am
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Corniche Street · Al Bidda
🚶 10 min · 🚕 3 min → Al Koot Fort
3. Al Koot Fort
↳ Built in the late 19th century by the Ottoman garrison, Al Koot — also called Doha Fort — is the city's oldest standing military structure. Its square plan and round corner towers sit at the edge of the old quarter, now housing a small museum charting Doha's transition from fishing village to capital.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Sunday - Thursday 8:00am - 7:00pm
🚫 Closed Friday & Saturday
⏰ ~45 min
🆓 Free
📍 Al Jasra Street · Old Quarter
🚕 10 min → The Pearl-Qatar
4. The Pearl-Qatar
↳ Qatar's largest artificial island, reclaimed from the sea off West Bay, is a residential and marina district evoking Mediterranean harbour towns. Porto Arabia's circular marina is lined with restaurants at water level; Qanat Quartier's Venetian-style canals add a second ring.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7

Day 5
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 30 min → Sheikh Faisal Bin Qassim Al Thani Museum
1. Sheikh Faisal Bin Qassim Al Thani Museum
↳ Qatar's largest private collection fills four halls in Al Shahaniya: Islamic manuscripts, antique Rolls-Royces and American cars, Qatari pearl-diving era artefacts, and coins from across the Islamic world. Assembled by a single collector over decades — one of the most eclectic collections in the Gulf.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Sunday - Thursday 9:00am - 5:00pm
🚫 Closed Friday & Saturday

2. Education City Mosque
↳ Qatar Foundation's campus mosque, designed by Ibrahim Al Jaidah with a distinctive drum-and-dome form: a circular ribbed drum supporting a white dome visible across the Education City campus. The interior holds 1,800 worshippers under a geometric arabesque ceiling. Open to non-Muslim visitors outside prayer times.
📖 Wikipedia

🚶 8 min · 🚕 2 min → Qatar National Library
3. Qatar National Library
↳ Rem Koolhaas designed this diamond-shaped building with the Heritage Library — Qatar's rare books and manuscripts — suspended at the centre of the main reading hall. Over 200,000 volumes across three floors; the bowl-shaped space funnels light down into the stacks — one of the Gulf's finest public buildings.

Day 6
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 25 min → Al Wakrah Heritage Village
1. Al Wakrah Heritage Village
↳ Al Wakrah is Qatar's best-preserved historic coastal town, 25 km south of Doha. The heritage village clusters around Wakra Fort and a restored fishermen's quarter of coral-stone houses, wind towers, and a mosque. The seafront promenade and beach give this city a human scale absent from Doha.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 7:00am - 10:00pm
⏰ ~2 hrs
🆓 Free
📍 Al Wakrah · 25 km south
🚕 45 min → Lusail Stadium
2. Lusail Stadium
↳ The 80,000-seat Lusail Stadium hosted the FIFA World Cup final — Argentina vs. France. The facade is modelled on a fanar lamp from Islamic tradition, its perforated geometric surface casting light-patterns across the exterior. The approach from Lusail's promenade reveals the full scale of the structure.
📖 Wikipedia

🍮 Local Tastes
Machboos
↳ Qatar's national dish: basmati rice slow-cooked with lamb or chicken, dried lime, cinnamon, cardamom, and coriander. Rice absorbs the meat juices as everything braises — deeper and more fragrant than biryani.
📍 Al Hazm Mall · Al Markhiya Street
Karak Tea
↳ Strong black tea simmered with cardamom, saffron, sugar, and evaporated milk — an Indian influence absorbed so deeply into Gulf daily life it's now as Qatari as anything. Served in small cups, very sweet.
📍 West Bay
Luqaimat
↳ Small, deep-fried dough balls — crispy outside, soft inside — drizzled with date syrup and sesame seeds. Eaten as a street snack or café dessert, common during Ramadan. Found at sweet shops throughout the city.

✨ Claude Inspiration

Doha rewards the kind of visit that doesn't rush the surface. Day 1 puts you in front of two of the most architecturally extraordinary museum buildings anywhere — I.M. Pei's island floated in the bay, and Nouvel's interlocking discs that read differently every time the light changes.

Day 2 pulls back to show the physical scale of what Qatar has built in a generation: watchtowers from a desert past north of the city, a sporting campus that hosted a World Cup, and a cultural village that works as a place people actually want to be in the evening.

Day 3 is the inversion — 100 km of flat scrub-desert highway to reach a walled trading town that went silent two centuries ago, then back south to a free art museum that is seriously, quietly excellent.

Day 4 belongs to the Corniche and the waterfront: the promenade walk, the park that makes the city feel inhabitable, the Ottoman fort hiding behind the old quarter, and The Pearl's enormous reclaimed island at the end.

Day 5 crosses the desert to a private collector's four-hall museum — the car collection alone is worth the drive — then back through Education City for Koolhaas's library and Al Jaidah's drum-and-dome mosque, both serious buildings.

Day 6 covers the full north-south extent: Al Wakrah in the south is Qatar's most human-scaled town, an antidote to Doha's glass ambition; Lusail in the north is the stadium that hosted the most-watched football final in history.
