A medieval city that never became a museum — still doing what it was built to do, with a million visitors a year passing through like an especially heavy caravan season.

The red city and why it's red

Marrakech is called the Red City, and if you arrive at the right time of day — late afternoon, when the light comes horizontal from the west — the color is extraordinary: the pisé walls of the medina glow the particular ochre-pink of local clay, the same clay that has been building here since the city was founded in 1070. The walls of the medina, the towers of the Koutoubia Mosque, the palaces and riads and the surface of Jemaa el-Fnaa — all of it is the same material, the same color, a kind of visual coherence that European cities, with their stone and brick and plaster in competing shades, rarely achieve.

The Almoravids founded the city in 1070. They were Berber — from the indigenous population of North Africa that predates the Arab conquest of the seventh century — and they built Marrakech as the capital of an empire that eventually controlled Morocco, Algeria, and most of the Iberian Peninsula. Yusuf ibn Tashfin, the Almoravid leader who established the city, also crossed into Spain in 1086 to prevent the Reconquista from overrunning the Muslim kingdoms there. For a century, Marrakech was the capital of an empire larger than France and Spain combined.

That empire is gone, but the city it created is still here — still organized around the medina that the Almoravids built, still operating according to a street logic that hasn't needed a grid because it grew according to a different kind of order: by neighborhood, by craft, by family, by proximity to the mosque.

The medina as a living system

Most medieval cities in Europe are primarily museums of themselves — the medieval center is preserved and visited, while the actual city life has moved outward. Marrakech is different. The medina — the old walled city — is still the economic, social, and spiritual center of the place. The souks are not a tourist recreation of historical commerce; they are where the city buys and sells things. The tanneries are not a display; they are tanneries, producing leather the same way they have for a thousand years, and the smell hits you before you can see them.

The souk system is organized by trade, a medieval arrangement that made logical sense when buyers needed to find and compare goods quickly and sellers needed to be near their suppliers. The dyers' souk is near the wool merchants. The coppersmiths are grouped together so their hammering doesn't disturb the rug sellers. The spice market is near the pharmacies that use the same ingredients. Walking through it without a destination is the right move — let the souk reorganize your sense of how a market works.

The medina has 40,000 inhabitants and hundreds of thousands of visitors a year passing through its lanes. It absorbs both with the equanimity of something that has seen caravans from Timbuktu and Fez and Baghdad and now sees tour groups from Paris and New York. The category has changed; the function hasn't.

You will get lost. This is not a failure of navigation; it is how the medina is designed. The layout is deliberately complex — defensible in case of siege, intimate in case of community, legible to residents and opaque to strangers. After an hour or two of being lost you develop an intuition for it: the minarets are landmarks, the souks cluster, the residential derbs (dead-end lanes) are private and quiet and usually not through-routes. The city resolves itself slowly.

Jemaa el-Fnaa and what it actually is

The name Jemaa el-Fnaa translates roughly as "the assembly of the dead" — a reference to the public executions that were once carried out here. Today it is the largest open-air performance space in Africa: a vast plaza that transforms through the day from a morning fruit and juice market to an afternoon bazaar of henna artists, snake charmers, and street performers to, after dark, something that resembles nothing else in the world.

At night, the square fills with dozens of food stalls that materialize out of nowhere in about forty-five minutes — grills, tagine pots, stalls selling snails in broth and sheep's heads and lamb's hearts and calf's foot stew. The smoke from the grills drifts across the square. The storytellers who work the square — halqa performers who draw a crowd in a circle and spin tales for hours in Darija, the Moroccan Arabic dialect — have been practicing this tradition since the medieval period. UNESCO recognized the square's oral traditions as an Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2001. Go at 10pm and stand on the terrace of the Café de France and look down at what's happening. It will recalibrate your idea of what a public square is for.

The Islamic architecture they hid for centuries

The Saadian Tombs were sealed in the seventeenth century by the Alaouite sultan Ahmad al-Mansur's successor, who apparently wanted to erase the previous dynasty's memory, and remained hidden until 1917 when a French aerial survey revealed them. They contain the tombs of the Saadian dynasty's sultans and their families, built in the late sixteenth century in a style that draws on Andalusian Moorish craftsmanship: stucco latticework of extraordinary delicacy, carved cedarwood ceilings, geometric tile floors in turquoise and cobalt and gold. The main chamber — the Hall of the Twelve Columns — is the finest example of Saadian craftsmanship that survives. It was hidden for 250 years. Somehow it survived better than most things that were tended.

The Ben Youssef Madrasa, a block from the Koutoubia Mosque, was founded in the fourteenth century and rebuilt in its current form in 1565. It was the largest Quranic school in North Africa, housing up to 900 students. The courtyard is the thing to stand in: a rectangular pool reflecting the carved stucco walls above it, the cedarwood of the upper stories projecting over the zellige tile base, everything oriented toward the sky and the call to prayer. Students lived in the cells on the upper floors; they studied in the courtyard. The madrasa closed in 1960 and is now a monument, but it feels like a functioning building, not a ruin — too complete, too precise in its craftsmanship to read as anything but purposeful.

The French period and what it left

France controlled Morocco as a protectorate from 1912 to 1956, and the administrative choice the French made in Marrakech was consequential: rather than rebuilding the medina in the European style (which they did in some Moroccan cities), they built a new French city — the Ville Nouvelle — adjacent to it. This is why the medina is intact. The French needed offices and wide boulevards and a grid, and they built those in Guéliz, north of the medina walls, and left the old city largely alone.

The consequence is that Marrakech is two cities: the medina with its medieval street pattern and Islamic architecture, and the Ville Nouvelle with its Art Deco buildings and European café culture and the Majorelle Garden, which a French painter named Jacques Majorelle spent forty years creating. Majorelle built the famous blue villa — the intense cobalt color he used on the buildings, now called Majorelle Blue, was his own invention — inside a botanical garden of cacti and palms and pools that he opened to the public in 1947. When he died, the property was sold, and Yves Saint Laurent and his partner Pierre Bergé bought it in 1980 specifically to prevent it from being turned into a hotel. After Saint Laurent died in 2008, his ashes were scattered in the garden's rose garden. His name is now on the museum next door. The garden itself is still everything it was designed to be.

The Majorelle Garden is fifteen minutes from the medina and feels like a different planet — which is to say the French Ville Nouvelle and the Almoravid medina coexist with a proximity that should be strange but somehow isn't.

The Atlas Mountains on the horizon

On a clear morning — and mornings in Marrakech are often clear — you can see the Atlas Mountains from almost anywhere in the city. The High Atlas, with peaks above 4,000 meters, rises directly south of the city at a distance of about 60 kilometers. The summit of Jebel Toubkal, the highest peak in North Africa at 4,167 meters, is visible on good days. The mountains are snow-capped well into spring.

The Berber villages in the Atlas foothills — particularly in the Ourika Valley, about an hour's drive south — are a different Morocco from Marrakech: quieter, agricultural, organized around water and the terraced cultivation that has sustained mountain communities for centuries. The Ourika River runs through a valley of walnut orchards and saffron fields, and the villages along it have maintained a way of life that the medina's intensity makes easy to forget exists in the same country.

The Berbers — who call themselves Amazigh, meaning "free people" — are the indigenous population of North Africa and make up roughly 40% of Morocco's population. The Arabic spoken in Marrakech is Darija, a dialect so far from Modern Standard Arabic that speakers of Gulf Arabic often can't follow it; it has absorbed centuries of Berber and French vocabulary into its grammar. The Amazigh language, Tamazight, was recognized as an official language of Morocco only in 2011. In the mountain villages south of Marrakech, it's the language people actually use.

The thing to carry with you

Before you board: here is what changes how you see Marrakech. Most cities that draw tourists at the scale Marrakech does have been at least partially reshaped by that tourism — the authentic thing and the performance of the authentic thing begin to blur. Marrakech does this less than almost anywhere.

The souks sell to tourists and to the residents of the medina and to merchants from the Atlas villages and to buyers from Casablanca and Fez and Agadir, all in the same lanes, all on the same terms. The tanneries tan leather for Morocco's shoe and bag industry, not for your photographs from the terrace above them. Jemaa el-Fnaa transforms at night into something that exists for the people who live in the medina, with tourists as observers of a thing that isn't happening for their benefit.

The medina is nearly a thousand years old, and its streets were laid down for purposes that have nothing to do with you. Walk into it knowing that and the city opens differently. You are not the point. The city was here before you arrived and will be doing exactly this when you leave. That's not an obstacle to enjoying it — it's the reason it's worth enjoying. The medieval city that actually stayed medieval has a quality no reconstruction or living-history museum can touch. You can feel the difference. Go in without a plan and feel it.
