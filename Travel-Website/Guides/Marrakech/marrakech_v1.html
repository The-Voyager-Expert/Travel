Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 25 min · 🚕 8 min → Jemaa el-Fnaa
1. Jemaa el-Fnaa
↳ The beating heart of the medina — an immense open square where snake charmers, storytellers, acrobats, and musicians perform alongside food carts and orange juice vendors. By day a marketplace in motion; by dusk the entire square transforms into a grand open-air theater.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 12:00am
⏰ ~1.5h
🆓 Free
📍 Jemaa el-Fnaa · Medina
🚶 8 min · 🚕 3 min → Ben Youssef Madrasa
2. Ben Youssef Madrasa
↳ A 14th-century Quranic college with one of the finest Islamic courtyard interiors in North Africa — carved cedar ceilings, intricate stucco arabesque, and a marble-paved central court. The student cells ring the upper galleries. A place of extraordinary stillness in the middle of the medina's noise.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Ben Youssef Madrasa · 4.6⭐ · 2100+ reviews — Viator
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 7:00pm
⏰ ~1h
📍 Rue Souk Smata · Medina
🚶 4 min · 🚕 2 min → Maison de la Photographie
3. Maison de la Photographie
↳ A restored 19th-century riad housing 4,500 historical photographs of Morocco from 1870–1950 — Berber villages, mountain caravans, desert encampments, and the old medina before the age of electricity. The rooftop terrace looks over the medina to the Atlas Mountains on a clear day.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:30am - 7:00pm
⏰ ~1h
📍 46 Rue Ahl Fès · Medina
🚶 8 min · 🚕 3 min → Souks of Marrakesh
4. Souks of Marrakesh
↳ The medieval guild market stretching north from Jemaa el-Fnaa — separate districts for spice sellers, dyers, silversmiths, leather tanners, carpet weavers, and lantern makers each in their own labyrinthine alleys. The dye pits run every color; the spice stalls are spectacularly arranged and deeply fragrant.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 9:00pm
⏰ ~1h
🆓 Free
📍 Northern Medina
🚶 25 min · 🚕 8 min → hotel
Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 20 min · 🚕 6 min → Bahia Palace
1. Bahia Palace
↳ A sprawling 19th-century palace built for the Grand Vizier Ba Ahmed ben Mossa — 8 hectares of interconnected courtyards, zellij-tiled floors, carved plaster ceilings, and painted cedar canopies. The scale is palace-grade but the atmosphere remains intimate. Easy to get lost in, as intended.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1.5h
📍 Rue Riad Zitoun el Kdim · Medina
🚶 10 min · 🚕 3 min → Saadian Tombs
2. Saadian Tombs
↳ The mausoleum of the Saadian dynasty, sealed by the Alaouite sultan Moulay Ismail in 1672 and rediscovered in 1917. Three halls of ornate carved plaster, Italian Carrara marble columns, and golden cedar ceilings — an architectural crown on the city's most intimate historic site.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Rue de la Kasbah · Medina
🚶 8 min · 🚕 3 min → El Badi Palace
3. El Badi Palace
↳ A 16th-century palace built by Sultan Ahmed al-Mansour after the Battle of the Three Kings — stripped of its gold, onyx, and Italian marble by Moulay Ismail, leaving a vast ruin of sunken gardens, crumbling ramparts, and stork-nested towers. Walk the battlements at golden hour. The emptiness is the point.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1h
📍 Rue de la Kasbah · Medina
🚶 10 min · 🚕 3 min → Dar Si Said Museum
4. Dar Si Said Museum
↳ A late 19th-century riad palace converted into Morocco's premier museum of traditional arts and crafts — carved cedar ceilings of extraordinary complexity, antique Berber jewelry, High Atlas carpets and weavings, painted Moroccan furniture, and ornate wedding palanquins. The courtyard alone is worth the visit.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Wednesday - Monday 9:00am - 5:00pm
🚫 Closed Tuesday
⏰ ~1h
📍 Rue de la Bahia · Medina
🚶 15 min · 🚕 5 min → hotel
Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 22 min · 🚕 7 min → Mellah
1. Mellah — Jewish Quarter
↳ The historic Jewish quarter of Marrakech, established in 1558 — narrow whitewashed lanes, wooden balconies that project over the street, and the Lazama Synagogue still in use. Moroccan Jewish culture shaped the city's trade, craftsmanship, and intellectual life for centuries. A quiet pocket of the medina.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1h
🆓 Free
📍 Mellah · Medina
🚶 12 min · 🚕 4 min → Koutoubia Mosque
2. Koutoubia Mosque
↳ The largest mosque in Marrakech — its 70-metre minaret, completed 1199, was the template for the Giralda in Seville and Hassan Tower in Rabat. Non-Muslims may not enter; the surrounding gardens and lawns facing the minaret are magnificent in the late afternoon.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
⚠️ Non-Muslims may not enter the mosque interior
📍 Avenue Mohammed V · Medina
🚶 15 min · 🚕 4 min → Dar el Bacha Museum
3. Dar el Bacha Museum
↳ A 19th-century palace converted to a museum of Islamic arts and civilizations — cedar-carved ceilings, zellij courtyard floors, and exhibits tracing the cultural connections between Marrakech and Andalusia, sub-Saharan Africa, and the Arab world. Fewer tourists than the palaces and better context than most.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Sunday 10:00am - 6:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~1.5h
📍 Rue Dar el Bacha · Medina
🚶 5 min · 🚕 2 min → Le Jardin Secret
4. Le Jardin Secret
↳ A restored 16th-century Saadian riad garden hidden behind an unmarked doorway in the Mouassine quarter — two walled gardens, an Islamic water garden with fountains and orange trees, and an exotic garden of palms and rare planting. Quiet, thoughtfully restored, and one of the medina's best discoveries.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:30am - 6:30pm
⏰ ~45 min
📍 121 Rue Mouassine · Medina

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 12 min · 🚕 4 min → Jardin Majorelle
1. Jardin Majorelle & Musée Yves Saint Laurent
↳ A surrealist garden created by French painter Jacques Majorelle in 1923 — electric cobalt-blue buildings, bamboo groves, cacti, and koi ponds. Yves Saint Laurent bought it in 1980 to save it from demolition; the adjacent YSL museum houses his fashion archive and Moroccan textile collection.

2. Menara Gardens
↳ A 12th-century Almohad royal garden of olive groves and a vast reflecting pool, with a 19th-century green-tiled pavilion on its bank and the Atlas Mountains as a backdrop. The views are best in late afternoon when the mountains glow behind the pool. One of the largest and oldest royal gardens in Morocco.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Agdal Gardens
↳ A 12th-century royal garden covering 400 hectares south of the medina — orchards of olive, orange, fig, pomegranate and apricot trees surrounding two irrigation basins. Open to the public Fri & Sun when the king is not in residence. The scale is immense and the silence total.

Day 5
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 45 min → Ourika Valley
1. Ourika Valley
↳ A long valley carved by the Ourika River into the High Atlas foothills — Berber villages perched on cliffs above terraced gardens, and the Atlas peaks visible above. Walk into one of the villages, accept mint tea if offered. This is rural Morocco as it has been for centuries.

2. Ourika Waterfall
↳ A cascade at the head of the valley reached by a scramble up the riverbed between boulders — a short hike but the views open up as you climb. The falls pool in a clear basin below. Best in spring when snowmelt swells the river; still impressive in summer. Wear shoes you can get wet.

Day 6
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 10 min · 🚕 6 min → Palmeraie
1. Palmeraie Palm Grove
↳ A 13,000-hectare grove of date palms on Marrakech's northern edge — thousands of trees in golden light, the Atlas visible on clear days, camels weaving between the palms. Ride through at sunrise or late afternoon when the light is low. A desert oasis inside a city.

2. Anima Gardens
↳ A private garden created by Austrian artist André Heller — 8 acres of lakes, tropical planting, stone sculptures, and a small museum of Moroccan and world art objects. Quieter and less famous than Majorelle but equally crafted, and the art embedded in the landscape gives it a genuinely unusual atmosphere.

Day 7
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 2h 30min → Essaouira
1. Essaouira Medina
↳ A fortified coastal medina — UNESCO World Heritage City built by architect Théodore Cornut in the 18th century for Portuguese then French trade. Whitewashed walls, blue shutters, wind-carved alleyways. Quieter and more intact than Marrakech's medina.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1.5h
📍 Essaouira Medina · Essaouira
🚶 8 min · 🚕 3 min → Essaouira Skala de la Ville
2. Essaouira Skala de la Ville
↳ The 18th-century sea bastion on the north end of the medina — a long rampart wall bristling with cast-iron Portuguese cannons, with the full Atlantic breaking below. Walk the entire length from tower to tower. The wind is relentless and the views are extraordinary.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:30am - 12:00pm · 2:30pm - 6:00pm
⏰ ~45 min
🆓 Free
📍 Skala de la Ville · Essaouira
🚶 10 min · 🚕 4 min → Essaouira Harbor
3. Essaouira Harbor
↳ The working fishing harbor — blue wooden boats packed in rows, fishermen mending nets, seagulls circling, and the catch laid out on the dock. The color of the boats against the blue-white walls is as good as photography gets in Morocco. Walk to the far end where the boats are repaired and the industry is rawest.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 6:00am - 4:00pm
⏰ ~45 min
🆓 Free
📍 Port de Pêche · Essaouira

Day 8
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 22 min · 🚕 7 min → Almoravid Koubba
1. Almoravid Koubba
↳ The only surviving Almoravid structure in Marrakech — a 12th-century ablution pavilion whose carved stucco and stone decorative vocabulary became the template for all Moroccan architecture that followed. Tiny, sunken below street level, and usually uncrowded. An architectural origin point.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Monday - Saturday 9:00am - 6:00pm
🚫 Closed Sunday
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Place Ben Youssef · Medina
🚶 2 min · 🚕 2 min → Musée de Marrakech
2. Musée de Marrakech
↳ A major late-19th-century riad — Dar Mnebhi — converted into a museum of Moroccan art, ceramics, and calligraphy. The central courtyard is one of the most beautiful rooms in the medina: a central fountain, zellige floors, carved plaster walls, and a hand-painted cedar ceiling three stories high.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1h
📍 Place Ben Youssef · Medina
🚶 12 min · 🚕 4 min → Maison Tiskiwin
3. Maison Tiskiwin — Bert Flint Collection
↳ A private collection by Dutch anthropologist Bert Flint — the most complete museum of Saharan Moroccan material culture in existence. Room by room it traces the trans-Saharan trade route: textiles, jewelry, musical instruments, saddles, and carved doors. An undervisited gem.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 12:30pm · 3:00pm - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1h
📍 8 Rue de la Bahia · Medina
🚶 10 min · 🚕 4 min → Bab Agnaou
4. Bab Agnaou
↳ The most ornate gate in Marrakech — a 12th-century Almohad ceremonial entrance carved from blue-grey Guéliz stone, its arch layered with five bands of geometric kufic script and interlaced floral rosettes. No other city gate in Morocco matches it for decorative ambition. Free to visit and perpetually uncrowded.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~20 min
📍 Place Laksour · Medina
🚶 20 min · 🚕 7 min → hotel
Day 9
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 1h 30min → Imlil
1. Imlil Village
↳ A Berber mountain village at 1,740m in the High Atlas, below North Africa's highest peak — Mount Toubkal at 4,167m. The starting point for Toubkal summit attempts; day visitors walk the ridge trail for views of the valley and the peak. Total contrast to Marrakech below.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2h
📍 Imlil · Toubkal National Park
🚶 30 min · 🚕 8 min → Toubkal National Park ridge trail
2. Toubkal National Park
↳ Morocco's first national park — glacial valleys, Atlas cedar forest, and the massive scree slopes of Jebel Toubkal. The 2-hour ridge trail from Imlil gives full views of the summit and the High Atlas spine without requiring a guide.
📖 Wikipedia

🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 30 min → Agafay Desert
1. Agafay Desert
↳ A rocky desert plateau 30 km southwest of Marrakech — not sand dunes but a moonscape of grey rock, scrub, and ancient stone, with the High Atlas peaks as a constant backdrop. The silence is absolute. Late afternoon the rocks turn amber and the Atlas turns purple.

🍮 Local Tastes
Tagine
↳ Morocco's national dish — slow-cooked stew in a clay vessel, named after the conical pot it cooks in. Lamb with prunes and almonds, chicken with preserved lemon and olives, or vegetable with chickpeas. The slow steam makes meat fall apart.
📍 Le Foundouk · Dar Moha · Zeitoun Café
Pastilla
↳ Phyllo pastry filled with shredded pigeon or chicken, slow-cooked eggs, toasted almonds, and warming spices — dusted with powdered sugar and cinnamon. Sweet, savory, and fragile. A Fassi dish that traveled to Marrakech.
📍 Dar Cherifa · Al Baraka · Le Foundouk
Harira
↳ Tomato-based soup with chickpeas, lentils, celery, and fresh coriander — eaten at breakfast, used to break the Ramadan fast. Thick, fragrant, warming. Order it with chebakia · honey sesame pastry · on the side the way Moroccans do.
📍 Mechoui Alley · Café des Épices
Moroccan Mint Tea
↳ Fresh spearmint steeped in green tea with a generous pour of sugar — poured from a height to create a froth, refilled multiple times. Refusing the third glass is considered impolite. The ritual matters as much as the taste.
📍 Everywhere in the medina
Argan Oil
↳ Pressed from the nuts of the argan tree, endemic to southern Morocco — nutty, rich, and different from olive oil. The culinary grade — toasted — goes on couscous, salads, and amlou · almond and argan paste. Tied to the Souss region.
📍 Dar Moha · Nomad
