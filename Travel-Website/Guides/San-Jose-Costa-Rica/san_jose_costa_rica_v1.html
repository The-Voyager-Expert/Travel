Day 1

🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 8 min · 🚕 3 min → Museo Nacional

Museo Nacional de Costa Rica

↳ The national museum occupies a 1910 fortress with exhibits on Costa Rican history, art, and archaeology. The holdings include pre-Columbian artifacts, colonial paintings, and contemporary works displayed across symmetrical courtyards connected by galleries. The fortress itself—whitewashed stone walls and turrets—is as much an attraction as the collection inside.

Mercado Central

↳ A covered market built in 1880, teeming with vendors selling produce, souvenirs, spices, and local crafts. Narrow aisles wind through stalls of fresh fruit, flowers, and prepared foods. The market is the beating heart of downtown San José—crowded, alive, and quintessentially Costa Rican. Ground-floor food vendors sell fresh-squeezed juices and traditional dishes.

Teatro Nacional

↳ A lavish 1897 theater built with wealth from the coffee trade. The ornate facade is topped with a dome; the interior features red velvet seating, crystal chandeliers, gilt woodwork, and ceiling murals. Visitors can tour the lobbies, balconies, and auditorium even when no performance is scheduled. The building anchors downtown San José architecturally and culturally.

Day 2

🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 45 min → Poás Volcano

Poás Volcano National Park

↳ An active volcano north of San José with a massive crater lake filled with turquoise-green water. The crater rim trail offers a vantage point overlooking the steaming crater and lake 300 feet below. Clear mornings reveal Arenal and Irazú volcanos in the distance. The park protects cloud forest habitat and mountain meadows above 7,000 feet.

🚶 2 min · 🚕 20 min → La Paz Waterfall Gardens

La Paz Waterfall Gardens

↳ A private botanical reserve with a series of five waterfalls, hanging bridges, and cultivated gardens showcasing orchids, bromeliads, and heliconia flowers. Trails descend through cloud forest to viewpoints at each waterfall base. The reserve also maintains a hummingbird garden, butterfly atrium, and serpent exhibit. The setting combines wild forest beauty with curated flower displays.

Sarapiquí River

↳ A river in the Caribbean lowlands known for whitewater rafting, wildlife viewing, and jungle scenery. The Class II–III rapids flow through primary rainforest populated by sloths, macaws, and toucans. Tour operators run half-day and full-day float and paddle trips. The river is also a gateway to lodges and adventure activities in the Puerto Viejo de Sarapiquí region.

Day 3

🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 90 min → Carara National Park

Carara National Park

↳ A protected forest on the Pacific side hosting a transition zone between dry and wet climates, supporting dense biodiversity. Trails range from easy 1-hour walks to full-day hikes. The park is known for scarlet macaws, crocodiles along the Tárcoles River, and lush vegetation. The reserve also functions as a gateway to nearby beaches and the Tarcoles Bridge wildlife-viewing area.

🚶 4 min · 🚕 10 min → Tarcoles Bridge

Tarcoles Bridge

↳ A highway bridge spanning the Tárcoles River, famous as a viewing platform for American crocodiles basking on riverbanks and in shallow water. Dozens of massive crocodiles gather year-round beneath the bridge. The area is a wildlife spectacle and a popular photography spot. Early morning visits offer the best crocodile sightings and cooler temperatures.

Manuel Antonio National Park Beach

↳ A protected beach area at the southern edge of Manuel Antonio National Park, fringed by palm trees and rocky headlands. The water is calm and suitable for swimming. The adjacent park contains trails through tropical forest where monkeys, sloths, and coatis are commonly sighted. The beach is one of Costa Rica's most scenic—combining forest and white sand.

🍮 Local Tastes

San José reflects Costa Rican cuisine rooted in rice, beans, tropical fruits, and fresh seafood. The city's food culture centers on:

Casado — a plate meal standard in Costa Rican comedores (lunch counters) combining rice, black beans, salad, plantains, and a protein (chicken, fish, beef, or tofu).

Gallo pinto — a breakfast dish of rice and beans fried together, typically served with eggs and fresh fruit.

Fresh tropical fruit — mangoes, papayas, pineapples, and avocados sold at Central Market and in juice bars throughout the city.

Empanadas and patacones — fried pastries and thick-cut fried plantain slices, common street food and appetizers.

Coffee culture — Costa Rican coffee (café típico) served strong and black, or mixed with hot milk as café con leche, particularly in the morning.

🚌 Getting Around

Ride Share: Uber and local ride-sharing apps operate throughout San José. Fares within the city are affordable; longer trips to parks and attractions cost $20–50 depending on distance.

Rental Vehicles: Car rentals are available at Juan Santamaría International Airport and downtown agencies. Driving allows flexibility for multiday excursions to Poás, Carara, and Manuel Antonio. Roads are generally well-maintained, though mountain roads can be steep.

Public Transit: A comprehensive local bus system connects San José neighborhoods and extends to surrounding towns. Buses are inexpensive but often crowded during peak hours. Long-distance buses run from terminals to Arenal, Monteverde, and coastal destinations.

Taxis: Licensed red taxis are plentiful. Agree on fares before entering (meters are uncommon). Hotels can arrange pickups in advance.

❗ Heads Up

Altitude and weather — San José sits at 3,800 feet above sea level in the Central Valley. Morning temperatures are mild (55–65°F), afternoons warm (75–80°F). Afternoon rain is common during green season (May–November). Pack layers and a light rain jacket.

City safety — San José's downtown is generally safe in well-traveled areas (Central Market, Teatro Nacional, Barrio Escalante) and during daylight. Avoid isolated neighborhoods and keep valuables out of sight. Travel in groups after dark.

Volcanic conditions — Poás Volcano can close without notice due to volcanic activity or poor visibility. Check park status before visiting. Early morning offers the best crater views before afternoon clouds settle in.

Water — Tap water in San José is safe to drink. Hikers should carry plenty of water, especially for Poás and Carara where facilities are limited.

Sun exposure — Costa Rica is close to the equator; UV index is high year-round. Sunscreen, hat, and sunglasses are essential for all outdoor activities.

Currency — The local currency is the Costa Rican colón (₡). US dollars are widely accepted in tourist areas. ATMs are abundant throughout the city.
