Most itineraries treat this city as the layover before the volcanoes. It's actually the reason the volcanoes are safe to visit.

A capital that isn't trying to impress you

San José does not look like a capital. There's no grand river, no ancient acropolis, no boulevard designed to make you feel small. It's a working highland city at nearly 1,200 meters, gridded into calles and avenidas, ringed by traffic, and — depending on the week — draped in either brilliant equatorial sun or a low gray mist that sits in the valley like a lid. Most visitors give it one night. Coming from the coast or the volcanoes, San José can feel like an afterthought: the place with the airport, not the destination.

That reputation is mostly earned, and Ticos — as Costa Ricans call themselves — will tell you so without much defensiveness. San José was never built to be beautiful. It was built to be functional, which is a very different ambition, and one that turns out to say a lot about the country around it.

The city coffee money built

Walk into the Teatro Nacional on Avenida 2 and the mismatch is immediate: a gilded, Italian-marble opera house with painted ceiling murals and red velvet seats, dropped into a modest Central American capital that otherwise has almost no grand 19th-century architecture at all. The story behind it is the story of the country's economy for a century. Coffee turned Costa Rica's central valley into serious money starting in the 1830s and 40s — the volcanic highland soil around San José happens to be some of the best coffee-growing land on earth — and by the 1890s a small class of coffee-exporting families, the so-called "coffee barons," had the wealth to think about culture, not just crops.

The building itself exists because of a wounded national ego. In 1890, a touring European opera star skipped San José because the city had nowhere fit to stage her. The coffee elite responded by taxing their own export earnings to fund a proper theater, finished in 1897, and it remains the only building downtown that looks like it belongs in Vienna rather than the tropics. It is still Central America's premier performing-arts venue, and you can walk through it on a daytime self-guided tour without ever seeing a performance — worth the hour even if opera isn't your thing, because the building is the argument: this small country decided early that it could have both cash crops and culture, and built a marble monument to prove it wasn't choosing one over the other.

A touring opera star once skipped this city because it had nowhere fit to put her. The coffee barons taxed themselves until that stopped being true.

A fortress with the war still on its walls

A few blocks away, the Museo Nacional occupies a squat 1910 building that used to be the Bellavista Fortress, the army's main garrison. Look closely at the exterior and you'll still find bullet pockmarks in the stone — scars from the 1948 civil war, a six-week conflict triggered by a disputed presidential election that briefly split the country and killed roughly 2,000 people. It's the most violent event in modern Costa Rican history, and the fortress wearing its wounds in public, rather than patching them over, tells you something about how the country chose to remember it.

The man who won that war, José Figueres Ferrer, did something almost no other victorious military leader in the hemisphere has done before or since: once in power, he abolished the army entirely.

The decision that explains everything else

On December 1, 1948, Figueres symbolically handed the keys of the Bellavista Fortress to the Minister of Education and had the wall knocked open for a public museum — which is the building you're standing in front of today, one of the few national museums on earth that used to be a barracks. Costa Rica wrote the abolition of its armed forces into the 1949 constitution and redirected the military budget into public education and healthcare instead.

It is hard to overstate how strange this decision looked at the time, in a region where every neighboring country was about to spend the second half of the 20th century absorbing coups, civil wars, and military dictatorships funded and re-funded on both sides of the Cold War. Costa Rica opted out of that entire pattern by removing the institution that usually drives it. The country has had no armed forces for over 75 years. It is also, not coincidentally, the region's most stable democracy, with literacy and life-expectancy numbers that rival wealthy nations far richer than it is.

The country's founding military decision was to not have one. Everything else — the stability, the quiet, the sense of safety that visitors feel almost immediately — traces back to that single choice in 1948.

You feel the downstream effect of this everywhere in San José, mostly by absence. There are no soldiers on street corners, no military parades, no defense-ministry monuments dominating public squares — the normal visual furniture of a Latin American capital that isn't here. The old fortress became a museum instead of staying a fortress. The national identity organized itself, slowly, around the idea that a small country's real strength is what it builds rather than what it can defend.

Pura vida, unpacked

You'll hear "pura vida" within your first hour in the country — as hello, goodbye, thank you, "it's fine," and "life is good," depending entirely on tone. Visitors tend to hear it as a marketing slogan, and by now the tourism industry has certainly made it one. But it started as something more specific: a phrase popularized by a 1956 Mexican film of the same name, adopted by Costa Ricans as shorthand for a stated national temperament — unhurried, non-confrontational, content with modest, stable prosperity rather than grasping for more.

Whether that temperament caused the demilitarization or the demilitarization reinforced the temperament is the kind of chicken-and-egg question Ticos will happily argue about over coffee. What's not in dispute is that the phrase and the policy point the same direction: a country that decided, deliberately, not to keep score the way its neighbors did.

The green that isn't an accident either

San José's other export to the world — its reputation as a rainforest and ecotourism capital — has the same non-accidental quality. Costa Rica holds close to 5% of the world's biodiversity on a landmass smaller than West Virginia, and roughly a quarter of the country is under some form of protected status. That didn't happen because the land was untouchable. Much of it had been logged or cleared for cattle by the mid-20th century. The forest cover you fly over on the way to Arenal or Monteverde is, in large part, land that was deliberately allowed to regrow, protected by a national park system built out starting in the 1970s by the same political culture that had already chosen schools over soldiers.

La Sabana, the big public park on the western edge of the city, makes this literal in miniature. It sits on the site of the country's original international airport, decommissioned in 1955 when a bigger one was built outside town. Instead of selling the land for development, the city planted it. What used to be a runway is now the largest patch of green space in San José — joggers, football pitches, a lake, families on weekends. It's a small, legible version of the national instinct: when something outlives its old purpose, turn it into something the public gets to keep.

What to actually see before you leave for the coast

Most itineraries give San José an afternoon between the airport and the rental car counter, and if that's all you have, spend it well. The Museo del Jade, a five-story building near Barrio Escalante, holds the largest collection of pre-Columbian jade on earth — worked by cultures who had no metal tools to shape it, only patience, sand, and centuries. The Teatro Nacional and the Museo Nacional are a 15-minute walk from each other downtown and together take under three hours. Barrio Escalante itself, the neighborhood where most of the city's better hotels now sit, has quietly become San José's actual dining district — a strip of restaurants doing serious food in a city few people credit with having any.

None of it competes with Arenal's volcano or Manuel Antonio's sloths, and it isn't supposed to. San José was never the postcard. It's the place that made the postcard possible.

The one thing to carry with you

Before you head out to the volcanoes and the cloud forest, here's the idea worth keeping: in 1948, a country that had just fought a small civil war chose to respond by making sure it could never easily fight another one — by getting rid of the army that would have fought it. Every other country in the region spent the following decades under the weight of the institution Costa Rica removed. That single decision is why this is the country where you can hike an active volcano's rim, raft a jungle river, and let your kids wander a beach with sloths overhead, all without the low hum of instability that shadows so much of the rest of Central America. San José looks unremarkable because the remarkable thing it did was administrative, not architectural: it disarmed itself and stayed that way. You won't see a monument to it. You'll just notice, eventually, how calm the whole country feels — and that calm has an exact birthdate.
