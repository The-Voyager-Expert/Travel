You will notice it fast — Santa Cruz picked surf culture, redwood forests, academic freedom, and a cheerful refusal to take the rest of California too seriously, and it has been defending that choice ever since.

What Was Here Before You

Before you do anything else, understand who lived here first. For thousands of years, the Awaswas-speaking Ohlone people built permanent villages along these rivers and this coast. Monterey Bay handed them everything — gray whales, sea otters, abalone, salmon, steelhead — and the redwood forests behind you offered shelter and building material. They never needed to farm. The land was that generous.

If you walk to the small park near downtown where a half-scale mission replica stands, you are looking at the ghost of Mission Santa Cruz, established in 1791 at the mouth of the San Lorenzo River. The original collapsed in an 1857 earthquake. The mission system was catastrophic for the Ohlone — forced conversion, confinement, epidemic disease — and within a few decades the indigenous population had been decimated. Santa Cruz was the 12th mission in the California chain and never a prosperous one: too remote, the local Ohlone too resistant, the civilian settlement next door too contentious. That replica is small for a reason.

The Redwoods That Built San Francisco

When you hike in the Santa Cruz Mountains and pass the ruins of old lime kilns — crumbling stone structures half-swallowed by ferns — you are standing in what was once an industrial corridor. After California became American territory in 1848, Santa Cruz found its first fortune in old-growth coast redwoods, some 350 feet tall and 15 feet across. San Francisco kept burning down, and Santa Cruz kept shipping the lumber to rebuild it. Sawmills appeared along every creek. A second industry ran alongside: limestone quarries in the hills above town supplied the quicklime that 19th-century construction could not do without.

By the 1870s, all that money had built a proper Victorian town — handsome houses, a commercial center on Pacific Avenue, and a growing reputation as a summer resort. The railway arrived in 1876, and suddenly San Franciscans could reach the beach by afternoon. Hotels and bathhouses multiplied. What you see today on Pacific Avenue is the descendant of that era, though you will have to squint past the earthquake damage to find it.

The Boardwalk That Outlasted Everything

Walk onto the Santa Cruz Beach Boardwalk and you are standing on the oldest surviving amusement park on the West Coast, open since 1907. Two of its original rides still run: the Giant Dipper roller coaster, a 1924 National Historic Landmark whose wooden frame has carried more than 60 million riders, and the Looff Carousel from 1911, with hand-carved horses and original chariot seating. Listen for the Ruth band organ playing automatically on the carousel — it is one of the last of its kind in the country.

The reason the Boardwalk survived when nearly every competitor closed is worth knowing. It never tried to compete with Disneyland. It stayed compact, stayed on the beach, stayed affordable, and kept drawing working-class and middle-class families from the Central Valley and the Bay Area who wanted a day at the ocean without a production. You will feel this immediately — the Boardwalk does not pretend to be anything other than what it is. That plainness is exactly what the purpose-built beach resorts down the coast cannot replicate.

Stand at the cliff above Steamer Lane on a good swell day and watch — the surfers below you are riding the same break that launched West Coast surf culture, on a point that has produced more competitive surfers than almost any spot in California.

Where Surfing Came Ashore

If you visit Cowell Beach, look for the plaque. In 1885, three Hawaiian princes attending a military academy in San Jose reportedly rode redwood boards in the San Lorenzo River mouth right here — possibly the first time anyone surfed in California. The account is debated, but Santa Cruz has embraced it completely, and the surfing culture that grew from this coast became one of the defining facts of the city.

By the 1960s and 70s, Santa Cruz was one of the most serious surfing communities in the state. The breaks at Steamer Lane, just west of the Boardwalk, and Pleasure Point, a few miles east, drew a local culture that was competitive, territorial, and obsessive about wave conditions. Head up to the lighthouse above Steamer Lane and you will find the Santa Cruz Surfing Museum — the first surfing museum in the world, opened in 1986. Inside, you can trace board design from the heavy redwood planks of the early 1900s through the shortboard revolution of the late 1960s, when boards went from 10 feet to 6 feet in a few years and changed the sport entirely.

And there is a practical legacy you will see everywhere in town. Jack O'Neill, who founded O'Neill Surf Shop here, invented the modern neoprene wetsuit in the early 1950s because Northern California water was too cold to surf year-round without one. That invention made cold-water surfing possible worldwide. His company grew into one of the largest surf brands on the planet, and the Jack O'Neill Invitational at Steamer Lane still runs.

A University in the Redwoods

Drive up the hill above Monterey Bay to UC Santa Cruz and you will immediately understand why this campus shaped the city. The University of California system chose this hillside in 1965 specifically because it looked like nothing else in the system: 2,000 acres of redwood forest, meadows, and coastal scrub that had been the Cowell Ranch. The founding chancellor, Dean McHenry, designed it as a cluster of small residential colleges on the Oxford model — embedded in the forest, no football stadium, an explicit commitment to undergraduate teaching and work that crossed disciplinary lines.

The timing mattered. The first class graduated into the Vietnam War and the Free Speech Movement, and UCSC became one of the most experimental campuses in the country. For decades, the school replaced letter grades with narrative evaluations. Faculty crossed boundaries that other UC campuses kept rigid. And in 1986, students voted to adopt the banana slug as mascot after rejecting the administration's preferred sea lion. Walk the forest paths on campus and you will see the actual slugs — slow, bright yellow, native to the redwood floor. The vote told you everything about the institutional culture, and it still does.

Walk Pacific Avenue and count the independent storefronts — university students, longtime locals, tech commuters, surfers — and you start to see why no two blocks feel like they agree on what kind of city this is.

The Earthquake You Can Still Read

When you walk Pacific Avenue, the main commercial street, notice how parts of it feel newer than others — cleaner lines, less character, buildings that could be anywhere. On October 17, 1989, the Loma Prieta earthquake, a 6.9-magnitude event centered in the Santa Cruz Mountains, demolished 30 buildings on this street, including structures that had anchored downtown life for a century. Sixty-three people died across the Bay Area. In Santa Cruz, the damage forced a decade-long rebuilding fight.

That fight shaped what you see now. Some residents wanted historic reconstruction. Others wanted modernization. What emerged was a compromise that disappointed preservationists but brought retail back. In the years since, Pacific Avenue has recovered a personality of its own — independent businesses, good restaurants, and the natural filtering effect of Santa Cruz's particular population. You will notice the mix on any given block: university students, longtime locals, tech workers who commute over the hill to Silicon Valley, surfers still damp from a morning session. No other downtown in California looks quite like this, because no other city has this exact demographic collision.

The Thing to Carry With You

Santa Cruz sits 30 miles from Silicon Valley. Every coastal town within commuting range of San Jose has been reshaped by tech money — housing prices pushed beyond what locals can afford, chain stores replacing independent ones, the rough edges sanded smooth. You will see that pressure here too. Santa Cruz is expensive now, unmistakably.

But look closer. A vocal renter population, a strong tenant protection movement, a university culture that prizes alternatives, and a geography that boxes the city in — mountains to the north, ocean to the west, farmland protections to the east — have kept Santa Cruz from tipping all the way into bedroom-community territory. The politics are among the most progressive in California. The result is a place that is simultaneously expensive and scruffy, academically serious and beach-casual, environmentally committed and physically messy. The banana slug as mascot was not an accident: this city chose the slow, unglamorous, native creature over the obvious beach-town option. Walk around long enough and you will see that choice still holding, block by block, in a state that is doing its best to sand it away.
