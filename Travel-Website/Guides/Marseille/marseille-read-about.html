France's oldest city has been here 600 years longer than France. It hasn't forgotten, and it doesn't need you to know.

Older than you think, and older than France knows what to do with

When you walk down to the Vieux-Port and look east toward the fish market, you're standing where Greek sailors from Phocaea — a city-state on what is now Turkey's western coast — pushed their boats into this harbor around 600 BC, found the hills sheltering it and the entrance narrow enough to defend, and decided not to leave. They named the place Massalia. Paris wouldn't exist for another six centuries. Rome was still a village. That harbor is the reason the city is here, and it's the same harbor, in the same location, for the same reasons, 2,600 years later.

Massalia was already old when Julius Caesar besieged it in 49 BC — it had backed Pompey in the civil war, which was the wrong side — and stripped the city of its fleet, its treasury, and its independence. It survived, as it has always survived, and kept trading, and kept speaking Greek in its courts and markets for centuries after Rome took over. The formal annexation into France came only in 1481, when René d'Anjou died without an heir and his territories passed to the French crown. Marseille negotiated special terms. It has been negotiating special terms with Paris ever since.

The Marseillais have a reputation throughout France for being loud, excitable, and prone to exaggeration. They wear it without embarrassment. They've been here longer than France has, and they know it. Hold that thought for the whole trip — it's the frame through which the city keeps making more sense.

The harbor that is the city

Go to the Quai des Belges at the eastern end of the Vieux-Port before eight in the morning and watch the fish market. The boats selling there went out the night before. Some version of this — boats, dock, catch, buyers — has been happening here since the Greeks dropped anchor. It's not a reconstruction. It's not staged. It's the same thing, in the same spot, still running.

The Vieux-Port is 800 meters long, 300 meters wide, and has been the commercial heart of Marseille since 600 BC. Every street in the city orients toward it. That's not civic planning. That's what a port city is.

Look west from the fish market and you'll see the MuCEM — the Museum of European and Mediterranean Civilizations, opened in 2013 — its lacy concrete facade sitting beside the seventeenth-century Fort Saint-Jean and connected to it by a suspended footbridge. Look carefully at the fort before you go in: its cannons pointed inland, not toward the sea. It was built to control Marseille's own population, not to defend against outside attack. The MuCEM is one of the best museums in France, and not because of the building — though the building is extraordinary. It's because its subject, the civilizations of the Mediterranean and their migrations and collisions, is exactly what Marseille has been living for 2,600 years. Walking through it feels like reading the city's own autobiography.

Head south from the city center along the Corniche Kennedy, the coastal road that runs above the limestone cliffs. The Calanques begin here — narrow inlets of turquoise water cut into white rock, running twenty kilometers toward Cassis. You can see the first ones from the road. Most tourists don't go further. Go further. Rent a kayak or hike the GR98. The Calanques are not managed. They require effort to reach, which is the reason they're still worth reaching.

What was there and what was dynamited

Go to Le Panier — the neighborhood that climbs the hill north of the Vieux-Port — and pay attention to where the buildings change. Up high, the streets are tight and medieval: washing strung between windows, staircases worn down by centuries of feet, facades patched and re-patched over time. Lower down, closer to the harbor, the buildings are wider, newer, and feel like a cleared site. That's because they are one.

In January 1943, German and French authorities ordered the evacuation of the old neighborhood on the north side of the Vieux-Port and then dynamited approximately 1,500 buildings over four days. Around twenty thousand people had a few hours to leave with what they could carry. The official justification was urban sanitation. The actual reason was that the labyrinthine medieval streets were sheltering Resistance fighters and Jewish refugees who were impossible to root out any other way. The demolition squads went in on January 24th. By January 28th, the hillside was rubble.

When you walk the Panier now, the survived neighborhood is above and behind that line — the upper streets, the ones the dynamite didn't reach. Below, where the medieval buildings once pressed against the harbor, you're walking through 1950s public housing rebuilt on a cleared site. The light is different down there. The streets are wider. It feels, if you're paying attention, like a gap in a sentence — something that should be there, and isn't.

Push up into the surviving streets. Past the tourist shops near the Vieille Charité — go inside: a seventeenth-century almshouse designed by Pierre Puget with a courtyard and a domed chapel more beautiful than most buildings that get more attention — and into the residential blocks above. Washing strung between windows. Someone arguing in a stairwell. A cat in a doorway. This is a lived neighborhood, not a performance of one. The best lunch you'll have in Marseille may be near here, at a place with no English menu and wine by the carafe.

The dish that started as failure

Bouillabaisse wasn't a recipe. It was a solution to a problem: what do you do with the fish nobody would buy? The rockfish, the scorpionfish, the weever — the creatures too bony or too ugly for the Vieux-Port market — the fishermen boiled them in seawater with fennel, saffron, garlic, orange peel, and olive oil, hard at first and then reduced so the broth didn't cloud. Bolhabaissa in Provençal: boil and lower. The name is the technique.

Three centuries of refinement turned fishermen's leftovers into a civic institution. In 1980, the city's restaurants published the Bouillabaisse Charter — a document specifying which fish must be present (rascasse, grondin, Saint-Pierre, vive, at minimum), exactly how they must be cooked, and how the dish must arrive at your table: broth in the bowl first, rouille on toast floated in it, then the fish and vegetables separately. The rouille — rust-colored, dense with garlic and saffron and olive oil — is not a condiment. It's a structural element of the dish. Use it like one.

Order the real thing. Don't order it somewhere cheap — the dish at a serious Marseille restaurant bears almost no resemblance to the red fish soup sold under the same name throughout the rest of Provence. Eat it slowly. It takes time, and that's what it's designed for. This is a city that turned fishermen's failure into its defining dish and then codified it in a charter. The bouillabaisse is the city's argument for itself, in a bowl.

La Bonne Mère and what the city keeps faith with

Walk up to Notre-Dame de la Garde — thirty minutes on foot from the Vieux-Port, up through the residential streets south of the old center. The basilica sits on the highest point in Marseille, 162 meters above the harbor, in a Romano-Byzantine style that manages to be grand and slightly eccentric at once: striped stone, a tower topped with a gilded bronze Virgin and Child eleven meters tall. The Marseillais call her la Bonne Mère — the Good Mother. They are not being poetic. They mean it as a statement of fact.

Inside, the walls are covered in ex-votos — paintings, model ships, plaques, photographs, ceramic panels — left by sailors, fishermen, and their families as offerings for surviving things that should have killed them. A storm survived off Corsica. A boat that came back when others didn't. A son who came home from a war. Some of the offerings are very old; some were left last year. Stand in front of the model ships — some of them painted with the moment of the rescue, the waves and the small boat and the basilica visible on its hill in the background — and you're looking at what this city has always understood: the sea can kill you and you go out anyway, and if you come back you say thank you to the woman on the hill.

Turn around at the top and look at the city below you. The harbor is a blue rectangle. The Calanques are white on the southern horizon. The Château d'If sits in the bay. This is the view Marseille has always had of itself.

Take the ferry from the Vieux-Port to the Château d'If. It was built in 1529 as a fortress and spent two and a half centuries as a state prison — political dissidents, Protestant prisoners after the Edict of Fontainebleau, anyone the crown found inconvenient. Alexandre Dumas set Edmond Dantès' imprisonment there in The Count of Monte Cristo. The fictional cell is labeled for tourists. The real cells held real people who mostly didn't escape. From the château's walls, looking back toward the city — the basilica on its hill, the harbor, the limestone cliffs — you're seeing what Massalia's sailors saw when they arrived in 600 BC. It still explains why they stopped.

What Marseille doesn't do for you

Most cities that want visitors have organized themselves around the experience of being visited. The sights are labeled. The menus have pictures. The experience is managed. Marseille has not done this, or has done it badly and incompletely, and the city is more interesting for it.

Go to the Noailles market — four blocks north of the Vieux-Port, just past the Canebière. It operates in Arabic and French at a pace that assumes you know what you want: dried fruits, spices, olives, preserved lemons, herbs, things in unlabeled jars. It's been there for generations and it is not performing itself for anyone. Walk through it without buying anything the first time. Just navigate. Marseille rewards the kind of attention that doesn't need everything explained.

The Calanques require hiking or a boat, and neither is trivial. The fish market requires being awake before most of your hotel floor. The bouillabaisse requires ordering at a restaurant where the menu may not be in your language. None of this is hostility. It's a city that has been doing these things for a very long time and hasn't restructured itself around making them easier. What opens up on the other side of that small friction is a city that is genuinely and specifically itself — which turns out to be rarer than it sounds.

The thing to carry with you

Here's the one idea to carry off the plane with you: Marseille is not a French city that happens to be old. It's an ancient port that happened to become French, and there's a real difference between those two things.

Every city that calls itself old has a founding date. Rome has 753 BC. Paris has something vague around the third century BC. Marseille has 600 BC, and the difference isn't just arithmetic — it's categorical. Rome became the Roman Empire, then the papacy, then a monument to its own past. Paris became France. Marseille became Marseille: a port, an arrival point, the place where things from somewhere else came through and sometimes stayed. The Greeks came and stayed. The Romans modified things and eventually left. The Genoese came to trade. The Armenians came to survive the genocide. The Algerians came to work. The Comorians came. Everyone who passed through this harbor left something in the city that's still there — not preserved, not labeled, just present, absorbed into a place that has always defined itself by what arrives from the sea.

What you're walking through is the accumulated result of 2,600 years of that exchange: the bouillabaisse in the bowl, the Arabic in the Noailles market, the ex-votos on the basilica walls, the gap in the Panier hillside where the buildings were. Once you understand what kind of city this is — not a place with a past but a place made of arrivals — you start seeing it in everything.
