Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 10 min · 🚕 5 min → MuCEM
1. MuCEM — Museum of European & Mediterranean Civilisations
↳ A cube wrapped in a filigree shell of fibre-reinforced concrete by Rudy Ricciotti, connected to the 17th-century Fort Saint-Jean by a 130-metre aerial footbridge above the sea. Collections trace 5,000 years of Mediterranean civilisation; the rooftop delivers panoramic harbour views.

2. Centre de la Vieille Charité
↳ A 17th-century Baroque almshouse by Pierre Puget in the heart of Le Panier. Four arcaded galleries in three storeys surround a courtyard with a domed oval chapel of Baroque invention. The complex now holds the Museum of Mediterranean Archaeology and the Museum of African Arts.

3. Notre-Dame de la Garde
↳ Marseille's defining landmark — a Romanesque-Byzantine basilica perched on a 162-metre outcrop, crowned by a gilded statue of the Virgin that watches over the city and its sailors. Panoramic views across the old port, the Frioul archipelago, and the Provençal coast are among the finest in France.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 3 min · 🚕 2 min → Quai des Belges · 🚢 20 min → Île d'If
1. Château d'If
↳ A 16th-century royal fortress on a limestone island in Marseille harbour — built by François I in 1524 and immortalised by Alexandre Dumas as the island prison of Edmond Dantès in The Count of Monte Cristo. The cells, battlements, and sea views are exactly as dramatic as the novel promises.

2. Cité Radieuse — Unité d'Habitation
↳ Le Corbusier's 1952 vertical city — 337 apartments, a hotel, shops, nursery school, gymnasium, and a rooftop terrace with a running track, all on pilotis. Declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2016, it is the most influential residential building of the 20th century.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → Abbaye Saint-Victor
1. Abbaye Saint-Victor
↳ Founded in the 5th century over the tomb of the martyr Saint Victor, this fortified abbey is the oldest religious site in Marseille and one of the oldest in France. The 11th-century church conceals a labyrinthine crypt holding sarcophagi from the 3rd and 4th centuries, pagan and early-Christian together.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Vallon des Auffes
↳ A tiny fishing cove tucked beneath the Corniche road and its 19th-century stone viaduct — coloured wooden boats moored alongside pastel cabins, the sea green and calm inside the walls of rock. It remains one of the last genuinely working fishing hamlets within the city limits of Marseille.

3. Corniche Kennedy
↳ Marseille's great seafront boulevard runs 3.7 kilometres along the Mediterranean from the Catalans beach to the Prado beaches, named for President Kennedy in 1963. The promenade passes rocky inlets, sunbathers on the flat limestone shelves, and the open sea stretching to the horizon — the city at its most elemental.
📖 Wikipedia

4. Palais Longchamp
↳ Built between 1862 and 1869 to celebrate the completion of a canal bringing water from the Durance River, this monumental palace flanks a triumphal cascade with colonnaded wings set in formal gardens. The left wing holds the Museum of Fine Arts; the right holds the Natural History Museum.

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 8 min · 🚕 3 min → Musée d'Histoire de Marseille
1. Musée d'Histoire de Marseille
↳ Marseille's main historical museum, built around excavated remains of the ancient Greek port of Massalia from 600 BC. A Roman merchant ship, amphorae, and wooden dock structures are displayed in situ above the harbour basin; the collection spans twenty-six centuries of continuous urban life.
📖 Wikipedia

🚫 Closed Monday

🚶 10 min · 🚕 5 min → Cours Julien
2. Cours Julien
↳ A wide Provençal avenue turned street-art district — stepped terraces and entire building façades painted by local and international artists, with independent record shops, vintage boutiques, and neighbourhood cafés on every corner. One of the few genuinely lived-in parts of central Marseille, unchanged by tourism.
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Cours Julien · Noailles
🚶 18 min · 🚕 8 min → Friche la Belle de Mai
3. Friche la Belle de Mai
↳ A former tobacco factory turned into one of France's largest arts complexes — sixty artistic organisations across 45,000 m² with concert halls, galleries, a rooftop terrace with city views, and artists' residences. Founded in 1992, it was central to Marseille's European Capital of Culture programme in 2013.

1. Les Arènes d'Arles
↳ A Roman amphitheatre built around 70 AD and among the best-preserved in the world, with two full tiers of arched galleries still standing. Listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, it once seated 20,000 spectators and remains in use today for bullfights and summer concerts.

2. Les Alyscamps
↳ A Roman and early Christian necropolis in use from the 4th to 12th centuries, listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The long avenue of sarcophagi beneath poplar trees was painted by Van Gogh and Gauguin in 1888; the site remains one of the most atmospheric ancient burial grounds in France.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Fondation Vincent van Gogh Arles
↳ A foundation dedicated to Van Gogh's legacy in the city where he created over 300 works in 15 months. The permanent collection pairs historical Van Gogh pieces with contemporary art created in response to his work; housed in a converted 15th-century mansion with a rooftop terrace overlooking the Roman theatre.

🍮 Local Tastes
Bouillabaisse
↳ Marseille's elemental fish stew — a minimum of three rockfish varieties simmered with saffron, fennel and orange peel, served with rouille and croutons. The city's most iconic dish, protected by recipe charter since 1980.
📍 Chez Fonfon · 140 Rue du Vallon des Auffes · Le Miramar · 12 Quai du Port
Pastis
↳ The anise-flavoured spirit of Provence, created in Marseille by Paul Ricard in 1932. Drunk cold with five parts water to one part pastis, it clouds to an opalescent yellow — the ritual aperitif at every Marseille terrace.
📍 Bar de la Marine · 15 Quai de Rive Neuve
Navettes
↳ Orange-blossom biscuits shaped like a small boat, baked since 1781 at Four des Navettes. Traditionally eaten at Candlemas, now sold year-round as the city's oldest confection.
📍 Four des Navettes · 136 Rue Sainte
Panisse
↳ Deep-fried chickpea flour fritters — crisp outside, creamy within — a staple Marseillais street snack that predates the city's Italian community and is now firmly woven into Provençal food culture.
📍 Chez Étienne · 43 Rue de Lorette · Le Panier

🌊 Claude Inspiration

Marseille is not quite like anywhere else in France, and certainly not like anywhere else in Europe. It is older than Paris by several centuries — founded by Greek sailors from Phocaea in 600 BC, it has been continuously inhabited ever since, absorbing wave after wave of migration until it became the most genuinely Mediterranean city on the continent: part Provençal, part North African, part Italian, wholly itself.

The instinct to compare it to other French cities is a mistake. It has none of the elegant detachment of Paris, none of Lyon's comfortable bourgeois certainty. It is rougher and more alive — a city that has never bothered to perform for visitors, which is one of the reasons visitors have always found it so compelling. The old port is not a backdrop; it is where things happen.

Give yourself time for the light. The particular quality of the light on the limestone and the sea in the early morning, or in the last hour before sunset when the Basilica of Notre-Dame de la Garde catches it and seems to float above the city on a column of gold — this is what the city is most proud of.
