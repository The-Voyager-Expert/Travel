Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → Avenue of Stars
1. Avenue of Stars
↳ The mile-long harbourfront promenade faces one of the world's most celebrated skylines. Bronze handprints of film legends line the walkway — Bruce Lee, Jackie Chan, Chow Yun-fat among a hundred others — while Victoria Harbour and the towers of Hong Kong Island rise directly across the water.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1.5 hrs
⚠️ Symphony of Lights runs nightly at 8:00pm — 44 buildings illuminate simultaneously across both shores.
📍 Salisbury Road · Tsim Sha Tsui
🚶 8 min · 🚕 4 min → Hong Kong Museum of Art
2. Hong Kong Museum of Art
↳ The city's largest art museum holds 17,000 works spanning 7,000 years — ancient Chinese bronzes, jade, and ceramics alongside the most comprehensive collection of contemporary Hong Kong art. The permanent galleries are free. The waterfront terrace extends to the harbour with views across to the Island skyline.

🚫 Closed Thursday

🚶 15 min · 🚕 8 min → M Plus Museum
3. M Plus Museum
↳ The museum of visual culture on the West Kowloon waterfront holds a permanent collection spanning art, design, architecture, and moving image. The 65-metre south facade doubles as a public LED artwork after dark. Permanent galleries are free; special exhibitions are ticketed separately.
📖 Wikipedia

📍 38 Museum Drive · West Kowloon Cultural District
🚕 25 min → Nan Lian Garden
4. Nan Lian Garden
↳ A Classical Tang-dynasty garden built to ancient geometric principles in Diamond Hill. Gilded timber pavilions, shaped bonsai pines, and Taihu rockery mountains surround a still lotus pond — extraordinary traditional craftsmanship set against the dense urban skyline. The adjacent Chi Lin Nunnery is free to enter.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 7:00am - 9:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
🆓 Free
📍 60 Fung Tak Road · Diamond Hill
🚶 14 min · 🚕 5 min → Kowloon Walled City Park
5. Kowloon Walled City Park
↳ The site of history's densest human settlement — a 2.7-hectare walled village that once housed approximately 50,000 people in 350 interconnected ungoverned buildings. The park preserves the original Qing-dynasty granite boundary stones, a scale model, and a photographic record of what stood here.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 6:30am - 11:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
⚠️ Exhibition Hall within the park: closed Wednesday.
📍 Tung Tau Tsuen Road · Kowloon City
🚕 15 min → hotel
Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → Star Ferry Pier · Tsim Sha Tsui
1. Star Ferry
↳ The green-and-white double-deck ferries have crossed Victoria Harbour for over a century, connecting Tsim Sha Tsui to Central in eight minutes over one of the world's most celebrated waterfronts. Every seat faces the Island skyline — the HSBC and Bank of China towers ahead. Time it for dusk.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 6:30am - 11:30pm
⏰ ~10 min
⚠️ Small flat fare — purchase tokens at the pier before boarding.
📍 Star Ferry Pier · Tsim Sha Tsui
🚢 10 min → Central Ferry Pier · 🚕 10 min → Peak Tram Lower Terminal
2. Victoria Peak · Sky Terrace 428
↳ The 428-metre summit delivers a panorama over Victoria Harbour, Kowloon, and the South China Sea. The Peak Tram — a funicular since the 19th century — climbs the steep forest face. Sky Terrace 428 is the highest public viewing platform. Plan for late afternoon: daylight to neon.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Sky Terrace 428 · Advance Ticket · 4.6⭐ · thepeak.com.hk
🏛️ Daily 8:30am - 10:00pm
⏰ ~2 hrs
⚠️ Peak Tram runs 7:00am - midnight — separate fare from Sky Terrace admission.
📍 Peak Road · The Peak
🚕 8 min · 🚶 20 min → PMQ
3. PMQ
↳ The former Police Married Quarters in Central — converted to a creative hub for 100 local design studios. Open terraces connect ground-floor retail to studios above, threading SoHo between Aberdeen Street and Hollywood Road. Stalls, independent restaurants, and pop-up exhibitions are entirely driven by local design.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 12:00pm - 11:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
🆓 Free
📍 35 Aberdeen Street · Central
🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → Tai Kwun
4. Tai Kwun
↳ The former Central Police Station compound — a walled precinct of 16 heritage buildings — reopened as a heritage and arts centre with a UNESCO Award of Merit. Original police station, magistracy, and prison now house galleries, restaurants, bars, and a continuous exhibition programme.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 11:00pm
⏰ ~1.5 hrs
🆓 Free
⚠️ Paid exhibitions within the complex require a separate ticket at the venue entrance.
📍 10 Hollywood Road · Central

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 35 min → Ngong Ping 360 · Tung Chung Cable Car Terminal
1. Tian Tan Buddha · Big Buddha
↳ The 34-metre outdoor bronze seated Buddha crowns the Ngong Ping plateau on Lantau Island at 520 m above sea level — the largest of its kind when completed in 1993. The Ngong Ping 360 cable car climbs 5.7 km over forested ridges in 25 minutes; 268 steps then lead to the statue's lotus throne.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Ngong Ping 360 Cable Car · 4.5⭐ · np360.com.hk
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~2 hrs
⚠️ Cable car suspended during high winds and rain. Book tickets in advance — queues at the terminal can be long.
📍 Tung Chung Cable Car Terminal · Lantau Island
🚶 10 min · 🚕 3 min → Po Lin Monastery
2. Po Lin Monastery
↳ The working Buddhist monastery at the foot of Tian Tan Buddha was founded in 1906. Three ceremonial halls — rooflines tiled in imperial yellow and gold — sit on a terrace above the Ngong Ping plateau, with the bronze Buddha visible above. The vegetarian dining hall serves lunch daily.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
🆓 Free
📍 Ngong Ping · Lantau Island
🚕 1h → Wong Tai Sin Temple
3. Wong Tai Sin Temple
↳ Hong Kong's largest Taoist temple in eastern Kowloon. The three-religion precinct — Taoist, Buddhist, and Confucian — centres on a red-and-gold shrine to Great Immortal Wong. Famous for kau cim: shake bamboo sticks until one falls, then have it interpreted by fortune tellers outside the main gate.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 7:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
🆓 Free
📍 2 Chuk Yuen Road · Wong Tai Sin
🚕 15 min · 🚶 12 min → Chi Lin Nunnery
4. Chi Lin Nunnery
↳ The Tang-dynasty-style Buddhist nunnery in Diamond Hill was rebuilt in 1998 from cypress wood without a single nail — one of the world's largest hand-crafted timber complexes. Ceremonial halls and lotus ponds step up the hillside; monks and nuns conduct daily rituals in the open halls.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 4:30pm
⏰ ~1 hr
🆓 Free
⚠️ Halls may close briefly during Buddhist ceremonies — check with staff on arrival.
📍 60 Fung Tak Road · Diamond Hill
🚕 20 min → hotel

🍮 Local Tastes
Dim Sum
↳ The Cantonese tradition of yum cha — small steamed and fried dishes served at round tables. Har gow, siu mai, cheung fun, turnip cake, and char siu bao define the Sunday morning ritual the city runs on.
📍 Tim Ho Wan · Luk Yu Teahouse · Crystal Jade
Dan Tat — Egg Tart
↳ A flaky or shortcrust shell with silky barely-set egg custard filling. Best eaten warm from the oven; sold at bakeries across every neighbourhood.
📍 Tai Cheong Bakery · Golden Gate Cake Shop · Hoover Cake Shop
Pineapple Bun — Bo Lo Bao
↳ A soft pillowy bun topped with a crackled sweet sugar crust resembling pineapple skin — no pineapple involved. Best split and filled with a cold butter slab at any cha chaan teng.
📍 Kam Wah Café · Lin Heung Tea House · Café de Coral
HK-Style Milk Tea
↳ Strong blended black tea strained through a silk stocking filter and topped with evaporated milk — thicker and more intense than any other milk tea; the backbone of the cha chaan teng.
📍 Lan Fong Yuen · Tsui Wah Restaurant · Café de Coral
Wonton Noodles
↳ Thin egg noodles in a clear broth topped with handmade wontons filled with whole prawns — the definitive bowl. The broth should be clear; the noodles al dente; the wontons plump.
📍 Mak's Noodle · Ho Hung Kee · Four Seasons Wonton Noodle

🌟 Claude Inspiration

Every night at 8pm, 44 buildings across both shores of Victoria Harbour ignite simultaneously. The Symphony of Lights runs for thirteen minutes — lasers sweep between skyscrapers, searchlights climb the air above Central, LEDs cascade down the ICC tower. By Guinness record, it is the world's largest permanent light and sound show. And it happens every single night, whether there are tourists or not.

Stand on the Tsim Sha Tsui promenade — the same Avenue of Stars — and watch it across the water. The spectacle is symmetrical to the view from Victoria Peak: the same skyline, the same harbour, reversed. This city rewards the second look. Everything here has a second look.
