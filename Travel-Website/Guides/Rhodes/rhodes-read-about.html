The largest of the Dodecanese islands has been a strategic prize for every Mediterranean power — and the city they built around it is still standing.

The island at the crossroads

Rhodes sits at the southeastern edge of the Aegean, roughly equidistant between mainland Greece to the north and the Turkish coast a mere twelve kilometers to the east. This position made it one of the most contested pieces of land in the Mediterranean. The island appears in the Iliad. It was a significant naval power in antiquity. The Knights of St. John fortified it into one of the most formidable strongholds in the medieval world. The Ottomans besieged it twice before taking it. The Italians colonized it. The Nazis occupied it. Today it is one of the most visited islands in Greece. The pattern is consistent: whoever controls the eastern Aegean sea lanes wants Rhodes.

The island measures about seventy-eight kilometers north to south, and the terrain changes noticeably as you move away from the main city. The northern coast is flat and developed, built for tourism. The interior is mountainous, covered with pine and cypress, and largely empty. The western coast is windier and wilder. The south is the most remote and least visited part of the island. Most visitors stay in or near Rhodes Town at the northern tip, which means most of the island remains relatively quiet even in high season.

The ancient city and the colossus

Before Rhodes Town, there were three ancient cities on the island: Lindos on the east coast, Kameiros on the west, and Ialyssos in the northwest. All three were founded by Dorian Greeks, and all three appear in the historical record from at least the eighth century BC. They were independent city-states that competed with each other until, in 408 BC, they made a rational decision: they pooled their resources, chose a site at the northernmost tip of the island, and built a single new capital. The result was ancient Rhodes, laid out on a Hippodamian grid plan — one of the earliest planned cities in the Greek world.

The new city prospered quickly. Rhodes controlled vital sea routes and exploited them intelligently, developing a sophisticated body of maritime law — the Rhodian Sea Law — that influenced commercial shipping codes for centuries afterward, including some that survive in modified form today. By the third century BC, Rhodes was one of the wealthiest cities in the Mediterranean.

The Colossus stood here. It was a bronze statue of the sun god Helios, built between 294 and 282 BC to celebrate the island's successful resistance against a Macedonian siege. Ancient sources put its height at roughly thirty meters — about the same as the Statue of Liberty from feet to crown — which would have made it the tallest statue in the ancient world. It stood for fifty-four years before an earthquake toppled it in 226 BC. The ruins lay on the ground for nearly nine hundred years before an Arab raider reportedly melted them down for scrap. The Colossus was one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, and nothing of it survives. We do not know exactly where it stood. The popular image of it straddling the harbor entrance is a Renaissance invention with no ancient authority behind it.

The Knights of St. John

The chapter of Rhodes's history that left the most visible mark on the island is also the most improbable. In 1309, the Knights Hospitaller — a crusading military order founded in Jerusalem in the eleventh century to care for sick pilgrims — were driven out of the Holy Land after its fall to the Mamluks and eventually purchased the island from a Genoese pirate who had seized it opportunistically. They held it for the next two hundred and thirteen years.

The Knights were simultaneously a religious order, a military force, a hospital organization, and a naval power. They spoke Latin and the languages of their home countries — the Order was organized into "tongues," or national divisions, with the largest contingents coming from France, Italy, Spain, and England. They were cosmopolitan in the medieval sense: defined by their religious vocation rather than by nationality, and more experienced at operating across cultural borders than most of their contemporaries.

The Knights rebuilt the ancient city as a medieval fortress-state — and the result survives almost intact.

What they built in Rhodes is remarkable. The medieval city — the Old Town that now draws tourists from around the world — was constructed largely by the Knights between the early fourteenth and early sixteenth centuries. They built the Palace of the Grand Masters, which served as the administrative headquarters and ceremonial heart of the Order. They built the Street of the Knights, lined with the inns of each national "tongue," where the different contingents lived and maintained their institutional identity. They built or rebuilt the circuit of walls that still encircles the Old Town — walls so well-constructed that they withstood a major Ottoman assault in 1480 and held for nearly another half century.

The Knights also expanded the Hospital, now the Archaeological Museum, into one of the largest medical facilities in the medieval world. At a time when most hospitals were little more than places to die, the Knights ran a genuine medical institution with trained staff, dietary protocols, and wards organized by type of illness. The building itself — a two-story structure around a central courtyard — is still one of the most impressive in the Old Town.

The Ottoman conquest

Suleiman the Magnificent besieged Rhodes in 1522 with a force that later accounts put at anywhere from a hundred thousand to two hundred thousand men. The Knights, numbering perhaps seven thousand including local Greek defenders, held out for six months. When they finally surrendered — having lost most of their garrison and faced with the certainty that no relief force was coming from Christian Europe — Suleiman allowed the surviving Knights to leave with their property, their weapons, and their dignity intact. It was an unusual arrangement for the period.

The Knights eventually settled in Malta, where they remained for nearly three centuries. In Rhodes, the Ottoman period lasted from 1522 until 1912, when Italian forces took the island during the Italo-Turkish War. The Ottomans made significant additions to the city — several mosques, a marketplace, a library — without fundamentally altering the medieval street plan. The Süleymaniye Mosque, built partly to commemorate the conquest, still stands in the Old Town, as does the Mosque of Ibrahim Pasha and several other Ottoman-era structures. The physical record of the island is genuinely layered: Greek antiquity, Byzantine period, medieval crusader architecture, and Ottoman additions all visible within a few blocks of each other.

The Italian period

When Italy took Rhodes in 1912, it acquired an island it intended to keep. Italian colonial policy in the Dodecanese was explicitly about demographic transformation: Italian families were encouraged to settle, Italian became the official language of administration and education, and major public works projects were undertaken to make the islands feel more Italian and less Greek or Ottoman.

In Rhodes Town, the Italians built an extensive new district outside the medieval walls — broad avenues, government buildings in a Mediterranean neo-classical style, a market hall — that is still called the New Town. They also restored or reconstructed several medieval buildings, including the Palace of the Grand Masters, which was heavily damaged by a nineteenth-century gunpowder explosion and rebuilt by the Italians into something closer to what they imagined it should have looked like than what it actually was.

The most striking Italian project was Thermes Kallithea, the spa complex built on the east coast between 1929 and 1938. Mussolini ordered it constructed on the site of ancient thermal springs, and Pietro Lombardi designed a complex of Moorish-influenced Art Deco buildings — domed rotundas, colonnaded arcades, terraced gardens — that have no obvious precedent elsewhere in Greece and sit in their bay like a proposition about what Mediterranean modernism could have been. It was used as a film location for The Guns of Navarone in 1961. It has been restored and is now open to visitors.

The medieval city

The UNESCO designation the Old Town received in 1988 recognizes it as the best-preserved medieval city in Europe. This is not hyperbole. The walls are essentially complete. The Street of the Knights is intact. The Hospital survives. The Palace of the Grand Masters, though heavily reconstructed, occupies its original footprint. The street plan within the walls is largely medieval. What makes Rhodes different from other well-preserved medieval sites is that the city inside those walls is still a living city — people live there, restaurants operate there, shops open and close there, laundry hangs from windows. The tourists move through a genuine inhabited place, which changes the character of the experience.

The city within the walls is also layered in a way that reflects the island's history. The Greek-speaking population of the Knights' era mostly lived in the Chora district, the southern part of the walled city, while the Knights occupied the Collachium, the northern section around the Street of the Knights and the Palace. During the Ottoman period, the Chora became home to significant Muslim and Jewish communities alongside the Greeks. The Jewish Quarter — still sometimes called the Ovriaki — occupied the eastern part of the old city, and the Kahal Shalom Synagogue, built in 1577 and restored in the twentieth century, is the oldest functioning synagogue in Greece. The Sephardic Jewish community of Rhodes was almost entirely deported by the Nazis in 1944; of the roughly 1,700 people taken, fewer than two hundred survived.

The walled city is layered across two thousand years — and the layers are all still there to read.

The island outside the walls

Rhodes without the Old Town is a different island. The road that follows the west coast passes Filerimos — the hilltop where an ancient city stood, then a Byzantine basilica, then a Franciscan monastery, now a complex of medieval and later buildings with views across to Turkey — and continues down past the excavated ruins of Ancient Kameiros, the only one of the three ancient Rhodian cities where serious archaeological work has been done. Kameiros was buried under a landslide in antiquity, which preserved it unusually well, and the ruins visible today give a reasonably clear picture of what a prosperous Hellenistic town looked like at street level.

Further south, Kritinia Castle sits on a promontory above the western coast — a Knights' fortification that is now an open ruin with nothing inside it except views: the Turkish coast to the east, the sea to the west, and the valley below. It is the kind of place that rewards arriving with no particular plan and staying longer than you meant to.

The Valley of the Butterflies, near the village of Petaloudes, is neither a valley nor, technically, home to butterflies. What gathers there in summer are Jersey tiger moths — thousands of them, clustering in the shade of the plane trees along a narrow stream bed to conserve moisture during their non-feeding adult stage. The visual effect is dramatic when the population is at its peak in July and August; outside that window, the site is pleasant woodland and nothing more. The name has been good for tourism.

What doesn't change

Rhodes has been a major tourist destination since the 1960s, and the infrastructure built for that tourism is now extensive — particularly along the east coast and in the New Town. The beach resorts are real resorts, with all that implies. Lindos, the most beautiful of the three ancient towns, is now entirely organized around visitors in high season.

What remains consistent about Rhodes is the light and the geography. The island is close enough to Turkey that the mountains there are visible from the eastern coast, which creates an unusual visual effect — you are looking at another country the way you might look at the far side of a lake. The light in the Aegean is distinctive: sharper and more contrasted than Atlantic light, with a particular clarity that the Impressionists associated with the south. The Old Town in the early morning, before the heat arrives and before the tourists, is a different place than the Old Town at noon. Both are real.
