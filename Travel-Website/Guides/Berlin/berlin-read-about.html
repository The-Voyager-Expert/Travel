Berlin doesn't ask you to admire it. It asks you to reckon with it — and that turns out to be more interesting.

Built on nothing in particular

Look at a map of Europe and find Berlin. It sits in the middle of a flat plain with no river worth defending, no mountain at its back, no natural harbor. Rome, Paris, London — every one of them was founded somewhere for a reason. Berlin was a pair of marshy trading settlements on a Spree River crossing, first recorded in 1237 under the name Cölln, its twin Berlin appearing in documents by 1244. They merged in 1432 with a combined population of roughly eight thousand. Nothing about the location says "great city."

What made it one was pure political will — and, repeatedly, immigration. After the devastation of the Thirty Years' War, Frederick William, the Great Elector, did something that would define Berlin's character for the next three centuries: he filled the city with outsiders. When Louis XIV expelled France's Huguenot Protestants in 1685, Frederick William issued the Edict of Potsdam and invited them in. By 1700, roughly thirty percent of Berlin's residents were French. Today you'll walk streets with French names in Mitte and see Huguenot heritage baked into Berlin's DNA — the city has always been rebuilt by people who arrived from somewhere else.

By 1701, Berlin was a kingdom's capital. By 1871, after Bismarck unified Germany, it was capital of the German Empire — and its population had exploded from 172,000 in 1800 to 826,000 by 1870. The grand boulevards of Mitte, the Berliner Dom, the old Reichstag — you're walking through the bones of that imperial ambition when you explore the center of the city today.

The golden twenties and the darkness underneath

The German Empire collapsed in November 1918, and what replaced it — the Weimar Republic — produced one of the most extraordinary decades any city has ever seen. By 1920, Berlin had absorbed its surrounding towns and hit 3.8 million people, the third largest city in the world. Walk into the Neue Nationalgalerie or the Berlinische Galerie and you'll see what came out of that moment: Otto Dix painting veterans begging on street corners, prostitutes under gas lamps, a city looking at itself without flinching. The movement even had a name — Neue Sachlichkeit, New Objectivity. It sounds like a bureaucratic term and it describes the most honest art being made anywhere in Europe.

Einstein was working here. Marlene Dietrich was performing here. Fritz Lang shot Metropolis in 1927. The Bauhaus — which fused fine art, craft, and architecture into a design language that still shapes everything from your phone to your chair — operated in this atmosphere. And hundreds of cabarets ran day and night, featuring near-nude performers and political satire sharp enough to draw blood. Christopher Isherwood moved to Berlin in 1929 and stayed until 1933, working as an English tutor and watching everything. His Goodbye to Berlin became Cabaret. If you visit the Clärchens Ballhaus or catch a performance at the Wintergarten Varieté, you're touching the last embers of that world.

The cabarets were the most honest places in the city — the only venues where what was really happening could be said out loud.

But the Golden Twenties were built on borrowed money, and the crash came. By December 1932, 630,000 Berliners were unemployed. The hyperinflation of 1923, the depression that followed Wall Street's collapse in 1929, the humiliation of the Versailles Treaty — these are the conditions in which the National Socialists found their audience. By January 1933, Hitler was chancellor. Isherwood left in May. The party was over, and what came next was something else entirely.

What the war did to the city

In 1933, approximately 160,000 Jews lived in Berlin — one-third of all Jews in Germany. Walk through the neighborhood of Scheunenviertel near Hackescher Markt today, with its beautifully restored courtyard passages and design shops, and understand what it was: a dense Jewish quarter. Nazi persecution began the moment Hitler took power. Deportations to death camps started in earnest in early 1943. By the time the war ended, roughly 1,200 Berliners had survived by hiding. The plaques you'll see set into the pavement outside apartment buildings across the city — small brass squares engraved with names and dates — are called Stolpersteine, stumbling stones. Each one marks someone who was deported. Once you notice them, you see them everywhere.

The Allied bombing campaign was comprehensive: British bombers dropped 46,000 tons of bombs on Berlin, Americans dropped 23,000 more. The Soviet advance in April 1945 added to the destruction. On April 30, Hitler died by suicide in a bunker beneath the Reich Chancellery garden — today that spot is an unmarked parking lot near the Holocaust Memorial, deliberately undistinguished so it doesn't become a shrine. On May 2, Berlin surrendered. The city was rubble.

The Allies divided what remained into four occupation sectors — American, British, French, and Soviet — and Berlin became the front line of the Cold War. When the Soviets blockaded West Berlin in 1948, cutting off road and rail access, the Western Allies supplied a city of two million entirely by air for nearly a year. You can see the history of that remarkable operation at the Allied Museum in Dahlem. The city held. The Soviets backed down. It was the first sign that Berlin's residents could outlast the forces trying to break them.

The wall

Stand at Checkpoint Charlie — the former American military crossing point at Friedrichstraße and Zimmerstraße — and you're at what was once the most famous border crossing in the world. It looks underwhelming now: a replica guardhouse in the middle of a busy intersection, surrounded by souvenir shops. That's intentional information. This was the crossing point for Allied personnel and foreign visitors, hemmed in by a bureaucratic theater of documents and delays. The absurdity of it was the point.

By 1961, approximately 3.5 million East Germans — twenty percent of the entire GDR population — had fled west through Berlin. The East German government estimated the drain was costing seven to nine billion dollars in lost productivity. On the night of August 12 to 13, they erected barbed wire along the border. Within days it was concrete. The Wall — 140 kilometers encircling West Berlin, 3.6 meters high, backed by a death strip of anti-vehicle trenches, beds of nails, trip wires, and 302 watchtowers — would stand for twenty-eight years. Over 100,000 people attempted to cross. About 5,000 succeeded. Between 136 and 200 were killed trying.

The Wall didn't just divide a city. It cut the same streets, the same neighborhoods, sometimes the same buildings in half — and forced both halves to pretend the other didn't exist.

West Berlin became a strange island — technically West German but geographically surrounded, economically subsidized, exempt from the West German military draft. That last detail mattered enormously: it made the city a magnet for conscientious objectors, artists, and radicals who didn't want to serve. Kreuzberg, enclosed by the Wall on three sides with rents near zero, became one of the most politically charged neighborhoods in Europe. Iggy Pop and David Bowie both lived here in the late 1970s, drawn by the creative lawlessness. Bowie recorded his Berlin Trilogy — Low, Heroes, Lodger — in a city that felt, as he said, like the edge of the world. You can walk past the apartment on Hauptstraße in Schöneberg where they both lived.

Meanwhile East Berlin was being rebuilt on Soviet principles: wide ceremonial boulevards, monolithic apartment blocks, a television tower at Alexanderplatz visible from everywhere — a deliberate assertion of socialist modernity. Walk Unter den Linden from the Brandenburg Gate toward Alexanderplatz and you're moving through the seam where those two cities meet.

November 9, 1989

The fall of the Wall began with a bureaucratic accident. On the evening of November 9, 1989, an East German spokesman named Günter Schabowski announced at a press conference that new travel regulations would allow East Germans to cross the border freely. Asked when, Schabowski — who hadn't been briefed on the intended rollout date — checked his notes and said: "immediately, without delay." The announcement went out live on East German television.

Within hours, crowds gathered at Wall checkpoints. Guards had no orders. They let people through. By midnight, Berliners were standing on the Wall itself, chipping at it with hammers. The Brandenburg Gate — closed since 1961, sitting in the death strip between East and West — reopened on December 22. Official demolition began the following June. Walk the East Side Gallery today: 1.3 kilometers of original Wall panels, painted by international artists in 1990, now protected as a heritage site. The longest remaining stretch of the Wall in the city. Standing in front of it, you can still feel the scale of what was here.

Reunification followed on October 3, 1990. But the physical wall came down faster than the one in people's heads. The Germans have a phrase for it — Mauer im Kopf, the wall in the head. As late as 1993, surveys showed only twenty-two percent of West Germans and eleven percent of East Germans felt truly unified. Decades of separate development — different economies, different expectations, different ways of being — don't dissolve overnight. That east-west tension, quieter now but still measurable in income gaps and voting patterns and neighborhood feel, is part of what you're walking through whenever you cross what used to be the border.

The ruins became the clubs

After the Wall fell, vast stretches of East Berlin sat empty — abandoned factories, decommissioned power plants, warehouses left derelict for decades, an entire apartment block here, a bombed-out shell there. The East German state had never bothered with them. Young people from across Germany and Europe moved in for almost nothing and started building something. What they built became one of the defining cultural exports of the late twentieth century.

Tresor opened in March 1991 in the underground vaults of the former Wertheim department store on Leipziger Straße — Berlin's largest Jewish-owned department store before the Nazis seized it in 1933, bombed to ruins, its basement intact for fifty years. When it became the birthplace of Berlin techno, it was literally a Jewish department store's crypt turned dancefloor. Tresor brought in Jeff Mills, Juan Atkins, and Robert Hood from Detroit — pioneers whose music had been shaped by that city's industrial collapse. Two cities rebuilt from ruin recognized each other across the Atlantic. The current Tresor is nearby in Mitte, still running, still worth a night if electronic music is your thing.

The ruins became the clubs. That's not a metaphor — it's the literal mechanism. Berlin parties in its own wreckage, and always has.

Berghain opened in 2004 in a former East German power plant from 1953 in Friedrichshain — its name a portmanteau of Kreuzberg and Friedrichshain, the two districts it straddles. It operates continuously from Saturday night through late Monday morning. No photography inside. In 2016, a German court officially designated it a cultural institution, giving it the same tax treatment as an opera house or concert hall. That ruling is the most Berlin thing that has ever happened: a court deciding that a notoriously selective nightclub in a Soviet-era power plant, running for forty-eight hours straight, belongs in the same legal category as the Philharmonie. It's not absurd. It's accurate.

What Berlin is now

Here's what most tourists miss about Berlin: the city isn't trying to impress you. Paris knows it's beautiful and performs accordingly. Rome is aware of its own weight. Berlin doesn't do that. It has 600-plus art galleries, more than 6,000 artists in residence, a UNESCO City of Design designation from 2005, and a startup scene that rivals London's — and it wears all of this casually, almost indifferently. The creative life of the city happens around you rather than for you, which is exactly what makes it worth being around.

A third of the city's area is forests, parks, rivers, and lakes. Locals spend summer weekends at the Müggelsee or the Wannsee with the same seriousness they bring to everything else. Potsdamer Platz — once the busiest intersection in pre-war Europe, then a death strip along the Wall, then an entire new quarter built from scratch after reunification — now feels slightly artificial, like a city built to prove that a city could be rebuilt there. It can be skipped. Museum Island, on the other hand, cannot: five world-class museums on an island in the Spree, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, anchored by the Pergamon Museum's reconstructed ancient architecture. Give it a full day.

Gentrification has arrived. Prenzlauer Berg is expensive now. Mitte is expensive. Kreuzberg is contested, with longtime Turkish residents who began arriving as guest workers in the 1960s now sharing the neighborhood with galleries and restaurants that have driven rents to levels their parents never saw. The cheapness that funded the creative explosion of the 1990s is eroding. The city keeps trying to preserve something of that spirit, with mixed results.

What hasn't changed is how the city handles its past. You won't find a single neighborhood in Berlin where history has been tidied away. The Holocaust Memorial — 2,711 concrete slabs of varying heights, covering a full city block near the Brandenburg Gate — was designed by architect Peter Eisenman specifically to disorient: there's no single correct path through it, no central viewing point, and as you walk deeper into it the city disappears. Go on your own, without a tour. Let it do what it was designed to do. Bullet holes in building facades throughout the former East have been left unrepaired on purpose. The Stolpersteine are everywhere. Berlin doesn't let you look away, and it doesn't apologize for that.

Before you go, here's the thing to carry with you through the city: every time Berlin was broken — the Thirty Years' War, the Weimar collapse, the firebombing, the Wall — it was rebuilt by people who moved in from somewhere else and had nothing to lose. The Huguenots. The postwar squatters. The Turkish guest workers. The artists from Detroit. The refugees from Syria. The city's instinct, whenever it finds itself in rubble, is to let strangers in and see what they build. That pattern is older than any of the landmarks you'll visit. It's the actual story of Berlin, and once you see it, you see it everywhere.
