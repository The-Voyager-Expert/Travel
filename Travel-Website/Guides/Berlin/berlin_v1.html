Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 2 min · 🚕 3 min → Brandenburg Gate
1. Brandenburg Gate
↳ Berlin's defining monument — a neoclassical gateway crowned by the Quadriga chariot. Once stranded in the Wall's death strip, it is now the city's symbol of reunification and the head of the Unter den Linden boulevard.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Pariser Platz · Mitte
🚶 3 min · 🚕 4 min → Reichstag Building
2. Reichstag Building
↳ Germany's parliament, wrapped around Norman Foster's mirrored glass dome. A spiral ramp climbs inside the cupola for a 360° view over the government quarter, while daylight pours straight down into the debating chamber below.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 11:59pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min

3. Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe
↳ Peter Eisenman's field of 2,711 grey concrete stelae rolls across a whole city block on an undulating floor. Walk in and the slabs rise overhead until the noise of the street drops away — disorienting by design. An underground information centre holds victims' names.
📖 Wikipedia

4. Gendarmenmarkt
↳ Widely called Berlin's most beautiful square, framed by the twin German and French Cathedrals with the colonnaded Konzerthaus between them. Recently reopened after a full restoration of its historic paving.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 17 min · 🚕 7 min → Berlin Cathedral
1. Berlin Cathedral
↳ The grand Hohenzollern court church on the Lustgarten, its green copper dome a landmark over Museum Island. A 270-step climb circles the dome's exterior gallery for a sweeping view across the river and the city's rooftops.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Monday - Saturday 9:00am - 7:00pm · Sunday 12:00pm - 7:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Am Lustgarten · Mitte
🚶 3 min · 🚕 4 min → Neues Museum
2. Neues Museum
↳ The home of the 3,300-year-old bust of Nefertiti, displayed alone in a domed hall. David Chipperfield's acclaimed rebuild wove the war-ruined shell back together, and the Egyptian and prehistory collections are world-class.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Sunday 10:00am - 6:00pm

3. Fernsehturm
↳ At 368 m, Germany's tallest structure — built by the GDR and still the needle on Berlin's skyline. A fast lift rises to the observation deck at 203 m, with a slowly revolving restaurant just above.
📖 Wikipedia

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 27 min · 🚕 10 min → Berlin Wall Memorial
1. Berlin Wall Memorial
↳ The most complete surviving stretch of the border, running 1.4 km along Bernauer Straße — preserved death strip, watchtower, and a section of wall standing in its full staggered depth. Open-air exhibits and a viewing platform explain how the city was cut in two.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 10:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
🆓 Free
📍 Bernauer Straße 119 · Gesundbrunnen
🚶 39 min · 🚕 13 min → Topography of Terror
2. Topography of Terror
↳ A documentation centre on the cleared site of the former Gestapo and SS headquarters. The exhibition traces the machinery of the Nazi police state, set beside one of the longest remaining stretches of outer Wall.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 8:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
🆓 Free
📍 Niederkirchnerstraße 8 · Kreuzberg
🚶 6 min · 🚕 4 min → Checkpoint Charlie
3. Checkpoint Charlie
↳ The best-known crossing point between East and West, where Soviet and American tanks once faced off. A replica guardhouse and the "You are leaving the American sector" sign mark the spot, ringed by open-air panels telling the border's story.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~20 min
📍 Friedrichstraße 43–45 · Kreuzberg
🚶 40 min · 🚕 13 min → East Side Gallery
4. East Side Gallery
↳ A 1.3 km run of the Wall along the Spree, painted by artists from around the world — the longest surviving section and the largest open-air gallery anywhere. Dmitri Vrubel's fraternal kiss of Brezhnev and Honecker is the signature image.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Mühlenstraße 3–100 · Friedrichshain

↳ Berlin's largest surviving royal palace, a baroque and rococo residence built for Queen Sophie Charlotte. The state apartments, the Porcelain Cabinet, and the formal gardens running down to the Spree make a full morning.

↳ The jagged, bomb-damaged spire left standing on Breitscheidplatz as an anti-war memorial — locals call it the "hollow tooth." Beside it, a modern octagon glows with deep-blue stained glass, and the ruined hall holds a small exhibition.

🍮 Local Tastes
Currywurst
↳ Berlin's signature street snack — sliced pork sausage under a dusting of curry powder and a tangy tomato-curry sauce, invented in the city after the war. Eaten standing up with a wooden fork and a side of fries.
📍 Curry 36 · Konnopke's Imbiss
Döner Kebab
↳ The grilled-meat-in-flatbread sandwich was popularised in its modern form by Berlin's Turkish community. The vegetable-piled version is now a city institution worth queuing for.
📍 Mustafa's Gemüse Kebap · Rüyam Gemüse Kebab
Eisbein
↳ The classic Berlin plate — a whole boiled, cured pork knuckle served with sauerkraut, pease pudding, and mustard. Hearty fare that the old-town taverns still do best.
📍 Zur letzten Instanz · Brauhaus Georgbräu

❗️ Heads Up
❗️ Reichstag Dome — No walk-ups
↳ The dome is free but entry needs a timed slot reserved in advance; same-day spots are very limited.
Workaround: Reserve online through the parliament's visitor service days ahead, or try the visitor centre opposite the building for a same-day slot.
❗️ Pergamon Museum — Closed
↳ The famous Pergamon is shut for a long renovation and its Altar wing will not reopen for years.
Workaround: The Neues Museum and the rest of Museum Island remain open.
❗️ Cash & Sundays — Old habits
↳ Plenty of cafés, imbiss stands, and smaller shops are cash-only, and most shops close all day Sunday.
Workaround: Carry some euro cash, and plan Sunday around museums, parks, and restaurants, which stay open.
🕊 Claude Inspiration

Berlin is the rare city where the route itself tells the story. Stand at the Brandenburg Gate and almost everything on Day 1 – the Reichstag, the Holocaust Memorial, the line of the vanished Wall — sits within a few hundred metres, because this single patch of ground was the seam where empire, dictatorship, division, and reunification all pressed against each other.

The thread that surprised me most is how often the city chose to keep its wounds visible instead of tidying them away. The Kaiser Wilhelm Church was left deliberately broken; the Topography of Terror is an exhibition on an empty lot where a building was erased on purpose; the East Side Gallery turned the instrument of division into the longest open-air painting on earth. Three stops, three different decades, the same instinct — remember in public.

And then Day 5 is the deliberate exhale. Frederick the Great named his Potsdam retreat Sans Souci — "without care" — and after four days of weighty history, taking the train out to a rococo palace above the vineyards feels exactly like the break he intended it to be.
