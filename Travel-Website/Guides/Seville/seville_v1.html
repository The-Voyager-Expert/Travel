Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 4 min · 🚕 2 min → Archivo General de Indias
1. Archivo General de Indias
↳ Spain's entire New World empire is catalogued here — 43,000 bundles of documents from Columbus's 1492 contracts to Lima's last dispatches. Juan de Herrera built it as a merchant exchange in 1598; the archive moved in 1785 and never left. Columbus's signatures and Magellan's route charts are in these shelves.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Saturday 9:30am - 5:00pm · Sunday 10:00am - 2:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday

🚶 9 min · 🚕 4 min → Torre del Oro
2. Torre del Oro
↳ The Almohad watchtower built in 1221 to anchor a chain stretched nightly across the Guadalquivir — no ship entered without permission. Gold-colored from the glazed tiles that once wrapped its walls. The naval museum inside holds nautical charts, navigational instruments, and scale models of the Indies galleons.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Casa de Pilatos
↳ A 16th-century Mudejar palace built after the Marquis of Tarifa's Jerusalem pilgrimage — the portico supposedly echoes Pontius Pilate's praetorium. Roman sculpture fills the ground floor. The garden courtyard — orange trees, azulejo fountains, carved stucco — is the most tranquil space in the city.

1. Palacio de las Dueñas
↳ The private palace of the House of Alba, opened to visitors only in 2016. Lemon trees shade the central courtyard; the gallery holds Goya portraits and family heirlooms across five centuries. The poet Antonio Machado was born here in 1875 — his room is preserved on the upper floor.
📖 Wikipedia

🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 8:00pm
⏰ ~1h

📍 Calle Dueñas 5 · La Macarena
🚶 11 min · 🚕 5 min → Palacio de la Condesa de Lebrija
2. Palacio de la Condesa de Lebrija
↳ A 16th-century palace filled with antiquities the Countess of Lebrija excavated herself from the Roman city of Italica. The ground floor is paved with genuine Roman mosaics removed in situ and relaid here; the upper floor holds amphorae, statuary, and Carthaginian coins assembled across a lifetime of digging.
📖 Wikipedia

🚶 14 min · 🚕 6 min → Real Fábrica de Tabacos
3. Real Fábrica de Tabacos
↳ Europe's largest 18th-century secular building, built 1728–1758 to house the royal tobacco monopoly and its 10,000 workers. Bizet set Carmen here knowing the factory defined the city's character. Now the University of Seville — the baroque facades, moat, and courtyards are open during academic hours.
📖 Wikipedia

🌅 The City That Refuses to Hurry

Seville operates on its own clock. The city that took 200 years to complete its cathedral did so deliberately — there is a civic pride here in doing things at the right pace, not the fast pace. The Alcázar has been under continuous use for over a millennium, which is to say: things that are built well in Seville tend to stay.

Walk the barrio early, before the heat rises and before the tour groups fan out from the Cathedral. The alleys between Santa Cruz and the Alcázar walls are genuinely quiet at 8:00am. Order a café con leche at a standing bar and watch the city start its day. Nobody is in a rush. The chalk menu on the wall hasn't changed in forty years and that, it turns out, is the whole point.

The Guadalquivir made this city and the city has never forgotten it — the light here is different, warmer, more golden than anywhere else in Spain, because the river reflects it back up into the streets. Stand on any bridge at sunset and you will understand immediately why painters have been coming here since the 16th century.
