Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 17 min · 🚕 5 min → Dominican Monastery
1. Dominican Monastery
↳ 14th-century Dominican complex just inside Ploče Gate. The Gothic-Renaissance cloister around a central garden is Old Town's calmest courtyard, and the small museum holds Titian and Lovro Dobričević masterpieces in one intimate room that cruise crowds consistently miss.

2. Cathedral of Assumption
↳ Baroque cathedral dominating Bunić Fountain square. The treasury holds a gold-and-enamel reliquary of St Blaise's skull and medieval textiles rarely found outside major European collections. The high altar painting is attributed to Titian's workshop.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Rector's Palace
↳ The Ragusan Republic's Gothic-Renaissance seat of government, now the City Museum. The rector governed for one month at a time and was barred from leaving the palace during his term. Period furniture, coins, and portraits bring the republic's history to life.
📖 Wikipedia

4. Stradun
↳ The main street of the republic — 300 m of polished limestone from Pile Gate to Luža Square. Morning and early evening are the best times, before cruise crowds or after they leave. The two Onofrio's Fountains mark each end.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 8 min · 🚕 4 min → City Walls
1. City Walls
↳ The 1,940-metre circuit along Dubrovnik's medieval fortifications is the city's single best experience. Views of the terracotta roofline and Adriatic from every angle. Enter at Ploče Gate, walk counterclockwise — exit near Pile Gate where the Franciscan Monastery waits.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~2h
📍 Ploče Gate · Old Town
🚶 3 min · 🚕 3 min → Franciscan Monastery
2. Franciscan Monastery
↳ One of Europe's oldest continuously operating pharmacies, established in the 14th century and still dispensing medicines today. The Gothic cloister columns are carved with 60 human heads. The museum holds original alchemical equipment and medieval manuscripts.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Buža Bar
↳ A hole cut in the city wall leads to cliff platforms overlooking the Adriatic. Locals jump off the 4-metre drop into crystal-clear water all afternoon while the bar serves cold drinks. The city wall looms above; the open sea stretches ahead.

4. Fort Lovrijenac
↳ St Lawrence Fortress on a 37-metre sea cliff west of Pile Gate, built in three months according to legend. The inscription above the gate reads 'Non bene pro toto libertas venditur auro.' The upper terrace gives the best external view of the City Walls.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 6 min · 🚕 3 min → Dubrovnik Cable Car
1. Dubrovnik Cable Car
↳ A gondola rises 778 m to Srđ Hill in three minutes, opening the definitive aerial view of the walled city — terracotta roofs, Adriatic, Lokrum Island. At the top, a Napoleonic fortress holds an exhibit on the siege and the highest-rated restaurant in Dubrovnik.
📖 Wikipedia

2. War Photo Limited
↳ A gallery devoted entirely to war photography. The permanent collection focuses on the siege of Dubrovnik and the Yugoslav Wars; rotating exhibitions cover current conflict zones worldwide. Founded by Wade Goddard, a former frontline photojournalist.

3. Sponza Palace
↳ Dubrovnik's only Gothic-Renaissance building to survive the great earthquake, Sponza served as customs house, mint, treasury, and armoury. The vaulted courtyard is freely accessible. The Memorial Room honours defenders killed in the siege — quietly devastating and free.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~30 min

🚶 4 min · 🚕 3 min → Old Town Harbour · 🚢 15 min → Lokrum Harbour
4. Lokrum Island
↳ A forested nature reserve 600 m offshore where peacocks roam freely. The Dead Sea — a saltwater lake connected to the Adriatic underground — offers sheltered swimming. A ruined Benedictine monastery, botanical garden, and rocky cove beaches within 20 min of the ferry.

🌊 The Republic That Invented Insurance

The Ragusan Republic — the city-state that built these walls — never had an empire, a conquered territory, or a standing army worth mentioning. What it had was paperwork. Dubrovnik created one of Europe's first public health systems, a quarantine law predating Venice's by decades, and a slave trade abolition statute in the early 15th century — over 400 years before the British Empire followed. It also invented marine insurance, keeping meticulous contracts in Sponza Palace's vaults. A city of 50,000 people that diplomated its way through Ottoman sultans and Venetian doges alike for five centuries by being too useful to bother with.

The inscription above Fort Lovrijenac's gate — 'Non bene pro toto libertas venditur auro' — translates as 'Freedom cannot be sold for all the gold in the world.' The republic put this on its own fortress, aimed inward at its own governors, as a constitutional reminder. That level of self-referential idealism, built into the walls in stone, is the thing that makes Dubrovnik different from a city that merely looks good.
