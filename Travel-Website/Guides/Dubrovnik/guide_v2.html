Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 20 min · 🚕 5 min → Fort Lovrijenac
1.Fort Lovrijenac
↳ A free-standing fortress perched on a 37-metre cliff outside the western city walls, guarding the sea approach to Ragusa since the 11th century. The ramparts give unobstructed views of the walls from outside their curve — the one angle the walls walk itself never gives.

2.Walls of Dubrovnik
↳ A 2-kilometre circuit along medieval fortifications enclosing the old city — walls up to 6 metres thick, reinforced with towers and bastions on three Adriatic sides. The full loop from Pile Gate to the eastern towers delivers every angle of the terracotta roofscape and the islands beyond.

3.Dubrovnik Cable Car
↳ A 4-minute aerial ride from near Ploče Gate to the summit of Mount Srđ at 405 metres — high enough to take in the entire walled city at once, the Elaphiti Islands dissolving south into the Adriatic, and on clear days the coast of Montenegro.

🚶 7 min · 🚕 3 min → Stradun
4.Stradun
↳ The 300-metre limestone-paved promenade running through the centre of Old Town from Pile Gate to Ploče Gate. Its uniform Baroque facades — rebuilt identically after the 1667 earthquake — create a street that feels deliberately composed rather than accumulated building by building across centuries.

🍮 Local Tastes
Rozata
↳ Dubrovnik's signature dessert — a silky caramel custard flavoured with rožulin, a rose liqueur unique to the old Republic of Ragusa. Lighter than crème brûlée and found only in Dubrovnik.
📍 Restaurant Kopun · Pantarul
Crni Rižot
↳ Black risotto made with cuttlefish or squid ink — rice stained dark, flavoured with the sea, and finished with olive oil. The defining rice dish of Dalmatia, ubiquitous on Dubrovnik menus.
📍 Restaurant Madonna · Restaurant Kopun
Peka
↳ Lamb, veal, or octopus slow-cooked under a heavy iron dome buried in embers — Croatia's most distinctive preparation. Must be ordered at least an hour in advance.
📍 Restaurant Kopun · Pantarul
Dingač
↳ Croatia's most prestigious red wine — a full-bodied PDO from Plavac Mali grapes grown on the steep southern slopes of the Pelješac Peninsula, the original Protected Designation of Origin for Croatian wine.
📍 D'Vino Wine Bar · Pantarul

🏛️ The City That Refused to be Sold

Above the entrance gate to Fort Lovrijenac, carved into the stone in Latin, is the motto of the Republic of Ragusa: Non bene pro toto libertas venditur auro — "Freedom is not to be well sold for all the gold." Ragusa spent five centuries using diplomacy, tribute payments, and commercial ingenuity to remain independent while surrounded by the Venetian and Ottoman empires. It paid tribute to both, allied with neither, and outlasted the arrangement until Napoleon dissolved the republic in 1808. The inscription was not aspirational — it described a policy the city had actually maintained.

Walking the walls of a city that survived that long on strategic caution, it is worth remembering that the limestone uniformity below was not always there. The earthquake of 1667 destroyed most of Old Town and killed between 2000 and 5000 people. What stands now — the identical facades, the measured Baroque proportions of Stradun — is a rebuild. The city decided to reconstruct itself as a coherent whole rather than recover building by building. That discipline is what gives the street its particular quality of feeling deliberately composed rather than merely old.
