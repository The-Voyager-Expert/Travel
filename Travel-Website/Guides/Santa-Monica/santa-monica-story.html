Santa Monica has always been where America comes to reinvent itself — whether as a health seeker in 1880, a movie stuntman in 1920, a bodybuilder in 1970, or a tech entrepreneur in 2010 — and the beach has absorbed all of it without blinking.

The Tongva Shore

The coastline that became Santa Monica was home to the Tongva people for thousands of years before European contact. The Tongva — also called the Gabrielino by the Spanish, after Mission San Gabriel — built a large village called Kuruvungna near the freshwater springs that made the area habitable. Those springs still flow today, surfacing in what is now University High School in West Los Angeles, where a small Tongva memorial garden marks the site. The location made sense: fresh water at the coast, the Santa Monica Mountains as a northern backdrop, and an ocean rich with kelp-bed ecosystems that supported sea mammals, fish, and shellfish.

Spanish missionaries established Mission San Gabriel in 1771, and within a generation the Tongva population had been drastically reduced by disease, forced labor, and the disruption of their food systems. The coastal land that would become Santa Monica was absorbed into large Mexican ranchos after secularization of the missions in the 1830s, and then sold to American settlers after California statehood.

The Health Resort and the Railroad War

Santa Monica was founded in 1875 by Senator John P. Jones of Nevada and businessman Archibald Young, who purchased a large land parcel and began selling lots to health-seekers drawn by Southern California's promotion as a therapeutic climate. The story of early Santa Monica is inseparable from the railroad wars that shaped Los Angeles in the 1870s and 1880s. Jones wanted Santa Monica to be the deep-water port for Los Angeles — he built a wharf and a railroad line, and briefly succeeded in making Santa Monica the dominant shipping point for the region. But the Southern Pacific Railroad, which controlled most transportation in California, eventually pushed port development to San Pedro instead, and Santa Monica's dreams of becoming a great commercial harbor died. What remained was the beach.

The consolation prize turned out to be better than the original plan. Without industrial development, Santa Monica's coastline remained accessible for recreation. Hotels, bathhouses, and tent cities for summer visitors appeared along the beach in the 1880s. The pier was built in 1909 — initially as a municipal sewer outfall that extended far enough into the ocean to keep sewage away from the swimming beach — and quickly became the focal point of resort life. The carousel on the pier dates from 1922 and is still running.

The End of Route 66

U.S. Route 66, established in 1926, ran 2,448 miles from Chicago to Santa Monica. It was the first continuously paved road connecting the Midwest to the Pacific Coast and became, especially during the Dust Bowl years of the 1930s, the road of last resort for migrant families heading west from Oklahoma, Texas, and Arkansas — the "Mother Road" that John Steinbeck named in The Grapes of Wrath. Several hundred thousand people made the journey west on Route 66 in the 1930s, and more than a few ended up in the agricultural valleys east of Los Angeles or in the labor camps that spread through the region.

The western terminus of Route 66 was originally at the intersection of Santa Monica Boulevard and Lincoln Boulevard. A sign now marks it at the end of the Santa Monica Pier, though the historical accuracy of that specific location is debated. What is not debated is the symbolic weight: for Dust Bowl migrants, for postwar travelers exploring an America newly interconnected by highways, for the counterculture road-trippers of the 1950s and 1960s, the endpoint of Route 66 was the Pacific Ocean. You drove until there was nowhere left to go, and then you arrived in California.

Muscle Beach and the Body Culture

In the 1930s, the Works Progress Administration built an outdoor gymnasium on the Santa Monica beach as part of its Depression-era public works program. The equipment was simple — parallel bars, rings, tumbling mats — but it attracted a community of acrobats, gymnasts, and strength athletes who began gathering there to train and perform. By the late 1940s, Muscle Beach had become an informal outdoor stage where visitors watched performers execute feats of strength and acrobatics that drew crowds every weekend.

The original Muscle Beach closed in 1959 after a series of incidents involving the community that had gathered there. The tradition migrated south to Venice Beach, where an outdoor weight training area was established and gradually became, in the 1970s, the epicenter of the bodybuilding movement that produced Arnold Schwarzenegger, Lou Ferrigno, and the culture documented in the 1977 film Pumping Iron. The Venice Muscle Beach that exists today — a serious outdoor gym with serious regular users — is the direct descendant of that WPA installation on the Santa Monica sand.

The Aviation Industry and the Creative Economy

Santa Monica's location — a flat coastal plain with predictable winds and year-round flyable weather — made it an early hub of California aviation. Douglas Aircraft Company established its plant in Santa Monica in 1921 and produced some of the most important aircraft of the 20th century here: the DC-3 (the plane that made commercial aviation economically viable, first flown in 1935) and a series of military aircraft that shaped World War II. At its peak, Douglas employed more than 44,000 workers at the Santa Monica plant. The factory is gone, but the Santa Monica Airport, which Douglas used for test flights, operated until 2028 and is now being converted to parkland.

The entertainment industry has always been adjacent to Santa Monica, given its proximity to the Hollywood studio system. But the city developed its own creative economy that was distinct from the studio world: a concentration of advertising agencies, design firms, architecture practices, and eventually tech companies that gave the area nicknamed "Silicon Beach" its character. The production companies, agencies, and startups that fill Santa Monica's office parks today represent a continuation of the creative industry tradition that has been part of the city since the 1920s.

Third Street Promenade and the Urban Comeback

Third Street, Santa Monica's main commercial corridor, went through a familiar American cycle in the mid-20th century: suburban competition drove customers away, the street declined, and by the 1970s it had been converted into a pedestrian mall in a common urban renewal move of that era. The first conversion — simply closing the street to cars — did not succeed in reviving the area. By the early 1980s, Third Street Promenade was struggling.

A second intervention in 1989 redesigned the promenade with new landscaping, street furniture, and active management by a property-based improvement district. The timing coincided with a broader urban revival in Santa Monica driven by rising property values, the influx of the entertainment and tech industries, and the general gentrification of the coastal Westside. Third Street Promenade became one of the busiest pedestrian shopping streets in California, and the challenge shifted from revival to management: keeping independent retail alive against national chains, managing the large homeless population that gathered in the area, and maintaining a street culture that served both tourists and residents.

The Place at the Edge

Santa Monica has a particular character that comes from being the western edge of the largest metropolitan area in the American West. Los Angeles expands in every direction except west — the Pacific stops it. Santa Monica, at that western limit, has accumulated the functions that end-of-the-line places accumulate: the destination for people who came the farthest, the concentration of the most conspicuous wealth, the gathering point for those who arrived seeking transformation and found the ocean.

The result is a place that is simultaneously very casual and very expensive, deeply health-conscious and deeply indulgent, politically progressive and economically exclusive. The Palisades Park bluffs above the Pacific Coast Highway carry joggers and meditators every morning and sunset-watchers every evening. The farmers' market on Arizona Avenue is one of the most prestigious in California, drawing chefs from restaurants across Los Angeles. The pier's Ferris wheel turns above water that was too polluted to swim in a few decades ago and is now clean enough for swimmers again. Walking Santa Monica, you are walking a city that has been many things and is still in the process of deciding what it wants to be next.
