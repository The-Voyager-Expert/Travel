A city that has spent most of its life being someone else's — Greek, then Savoyard, then Italian, then English by adoption, and only French for the last century and a half.

The name is Greek, and that's the first clue

Start with the word itself. "Nice" comes from Nikaia, a trading post that Greek sailors from Massalia (modern Marseille) planted here around the 4th century BC, and they named it for Nike, the goddess of victory, after winning a battle against the local tribes. So before Nice was French, before it was Italian, before it was anything you'd recognize, it was a Greek victory monument on the edge of the known world. That habit — of Nice being founded, funded, and shaped by people who weren't from here — turns out to be the whole story of the place, repeating itself for twenty-five centuries.

Walk the hill above the old town, the Colline du Château, and you're standing on the site of that original Greek and later Roman settlement. There's no castle up there anymore — it was demolished in 1706 — but the site still functions the way it always has: a high point from which whoever controls Nice keeps watch over the harbor and the coast road. The view is the point. It has always been the point.

The fact everyone gets wrong: Nice was Italian, not French, for most of its history

Here's the thing most visitors never learn, and it changes how you should look at everything in the old town. For roughly five hundred years — from the late 1300s until 1860 — Nice was not French. It belonged to the House of Savoy, the same dynasty that ruled Turin and, eventually, a unified Italy. Nice was Savoyard, then part of the Kingdom of Sardinia-Piedmont, an Italian-speaking, Italian-governed territory that happened to sit on the French side of the map. When Nice broke away from Provence in 1388 and placed itself under Savoy's protection, it was choosing Italy over France, and that choice held for centuries.

Nice only became French in 1860, and it happened almost as an afterthought to someone else's war. Napoleon III agreed to help Camillo di Cavour and the Kingdom of Sardinia fight Austria for the unification of Italy. Cavour's payment for that military help was two pieces of territory: Savoy and Nice, both ceded to France. A plebiscite followed — a vote stacked and rushed enough that historians still argue over how free it really was — and Nice became part of France. The city that would become the beating heart of the French Riviera joined France not out of French desire or French heritage, but as a transaction between an Italian statesman building a nation and a French emperor who wanted a war won.

Nice didn't become French because it wanted to be French. It became French because Cavour needed Napoleon's army and Nice was the price.

Vieux Nice is Italian, and once you know why, you can see it everywhere

This is why the old town doesn't look French. Walk through Vieux Nice and look at the buildings: the tall, narrow facades in ochre, terracotta, and burnt orange, the shuttered windows, the baroque churches stuffed with gilt and marble in a style that has nothing to do with the restrained French Catholic churches you'll find in Lyon or Paris. This is Piedmontese baroque, the architecture of Turin transplanted to the coast. The palette itself — those warm, saturated yellows and reds — is a Ligurian and Piedmontese habit, not a French one.

The food tells the same story, more bluntly, because food doesn't lie about where it came from. Socca, the chickpea-flour pancake cooked on giant copper pans and sold from hole-in-the-wall stalls, is essentially the same dish as farinata across the Italian border in Liguria — same batter, same technique, different name. Pissaladière, the onion tart topped with anchovies and olives, takes its name from pissalat, a Niçoise anchovy paste, but the dish itself is a cousin of Ligurian pizza traditions, built long before "Nice" and "France" belonged in the same sentence. When you eat in Vieux Nice, you are eating Italian coastal food that happens to be served under a French flag, because until 1860 that's exactly what it was.

The English built the seafront you're walking on

The Promenade des Anglais — the wide seafront boulevard that is now the single most photographed stretch of pavement on the Riviera — is named, plainly, for the English. In the 18th and 19th centuries, English aristocrats began wintering in Nice, drawn by a climate mild enough that doctors were prescribing it for consumption and other lung ailments. The city's exposure, tucked against the hills with the sea absorbing the cold, made it one of the first places in Europe marketed specifically as a health resort rather than a historic destination.

The English colony that formed here didn't just visit — they built. In the 1820s, a local reverend organized funding, much of it English money, to pave a proper coastal walkway so that wealthy winter visitors could stroll along the water without churning through mud. That walkway became the Promenade des Anglais, literally "the walkway of the English." Queen Victoria herself wintered in Nice multiple times later in the century, and her patronage cemented the city's reputation as the aristocratic escape from the fog and cold of northern Europe. Nice's most famous street exists because foreigners wanted somewhere pleasant to walk while they waited out the English winter.

That resort economy is also why Nice's grandest buildings are hotels and casinos rather than palaces or cathedrals. The Belle Époque architecture that lines the Promenade and fills the Musée Masséna's neighborhood — domes, wrought iron, pastel stucco — was built to sell a fantasy of leisure to people from somewhere else. Nice's high season, for most of its resort history, was winter, not summer. The city you experience as a beach destination today was designed first as a place to escape the cold, and only became a summer destination once air conditioning, sunbathing culture, and cheap flights rewired what a coastal vacation was supposed to look like.

Carnival: five centuries of the city letting loose before Lent

Nice's Carnival is one of the oldest recorded in the world, with references to Lenten celebrations in the city stretching back to the 13th century, long before Savoy, long before France, long before any of the borders that would later claim this coastline existed. The tradition as you'd recognize it today — parades, giant papier-mâché figures, flower battles along the Promenade — solidified in the 19th century, but the underlying instinct is much older: a fixed window each year when the ordinary rules relax before the discipline of Lent begins.

What's worth noticing is that Carnival, like everything else here, absorbed influence from every direction — Italian commedia dell'arte characters, French court pageantry, local folk tradition — and turned it into something specifically Niçoise. It's a good model for the city generally: nothing here is purely native. Everything is borrowed and then made local.

Matisse, Chagall, and the hill called Cimiez

Henri Matisse arrived in Nice in 1917 intending to stay a few weeks and ended up living here, on and off, for the rest of his life. He said the light convinced him — a particular clarity that comes off the water and through the hills, different from the light in Paris he'd grown up painting in. His years in Nice produced some of his most famous interiors, odalisques, and cut-outs, and the city holds his legacy properly: the Musée Matisse sits in Cimiez, the hill neighborhood above the center, in a 17th-century villa surrounded by olive groves, close to where Matisse is buried.

Marc Chagall settled nearby in the 1950s, and the Musée National Marc Chagall in Nice holds the largest public collection of his work anywhere, built specifically around his Biblical Message cycle. Two of the 20th century's most significant painters, neither French by birth, both chose this stretch of coast to spend their final, most settled years — which fits a pattern that should be familiar by now. Nice keeps attracting people who aren't from here and letting them define what the city becomes next.

But Cimiez was claimed long before the painters arrived. Above the modern center, the hill holds the remains of Cemenelum, the Roman town that once outranked the coastal settlement below it — an amphitheater, a bath complex, and the archaeological museum built around them. Rome governed this stretch of coast for centuries before Nice existed as a name anyone would recognize, and Cimiez's ruins are the plainest physical evidence that the story of this place didn't start with the Riviera glamour it's known for now. It started as a Roman provincial capital on a hill, doing the unglamorous administrative work of empire.

The same hill later became a magnet for exactly the kind of resort visitor Nice specialized in attracting: Queen Victoria stayed in Cimiez on her winter visits, at a hotel that's now converted apartments still bearing her name in the neighborhood's memory. Roman ruins, a English queen's winter retreat, and two of the twentieth century's greatest painters — all thirty seconds apart on the same hillside. That layering is Nice in miniature.

What actually happened here in 2016

On Bastille Day, July 14, 2016, a truck was deliberately driven into the crowd gathered on the Promenade des Anglais to watch the fireworks, killing 86 people. It remains one of the deadliest attacks in modern French history, and it happened on the exact stretch of seafront that generations of visitors have walked for pleasure. Nice has since rebuilt its relationship with that stretch of the Promenade thoughtfully — a memorial garden near the site, ongoing remembrance each July — without turning the whole boulevard into a monument to the day. If you walk the Promenade, know what it carries. The city does.

What to actually tell yourself before you land

Here's the idea to hold onto: nothing about Nice is original to Nice. The name is a Greek war memorial. The old town's colors and food are Ligurian and Piedmontese, inherited from five centuries as Italian territory. The seafront promenade was paved with English money by aristocrats fleeing their own winters. Even becoming French in the first place wasn't a choice the city made for itself — it was the collateral of a war between an Italian statesman and a French emperor, settled by a vote in 1860. The painters who made the city's cultural reputation in the 20th century, Matisse and Chagall, were transplants who arrived and simply never left.

None of that makes Nice fake. It makes Nice honest about something most old cities try to hide: that identity here was built by whoever showed up and decided to stay, layer after layer, for twenty-five hundred years. When you eat socca in a lane the color of terracotta, you're not tasting a French dish with a French history — you're tasting an Italian recipe served in a formerly Italian city that a formerly Italian dynasty ruled until a war it didn't start decided otherwise. That's not a footnote. That's the whole flavor of the place. Taste it as what it is.
