1. Getty Villa
↳ A museum dedicated to the arts and cultures of ancient Greece, Rome, and Etruria, housed in a recreated 1st-century Roman villa. The collection spans 44,000 objects — sculptures, mosaics, decorative arts, and manuscripts — acquired by J. Paul Getty beginning in 1945.

2. Malibu Lagoon State Beach
↳ A California State Park where Malibu Creek meets the Pacific, encompassing a protected coastal lagoon and a beach recognized as one of the premier longboard surf breaks in the world. Three surf points generate long right-hand waves that established the international surfing reputation of this coastline.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7

🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → Adamson House
3. Adamson House and Malibu Lagoon Museum
↳ A 1929 Moorish Spanish Colonial Revival residence designed by Stiles O. Clements for the Rindge family. Notable for its Malibu Potteries tile on floors, walls, fountains, and facades — the last major production run of the manufacturer founded by May K. Rindge. Designated a National Historic Landmark in 1977.
📖 Wikipedia

4. Point Dume State Beach and Preserve
↳ A 34-acre California state preserve on a volcanic headland rising 200 feet above the Pacific. A trail leads to the clifftop, a documented observation point for gray whale migration in winter and California sea lion haul-outs on the rocks below. The beach includes tide pools accessible via stairs.

🌊 On Malibu

Malibu is not a city in the conventional sense — it is 27 miles of Pacific coastline incorporated in 1991 primarily to give residents control over development. That tension between private land and public beach runs through everything here. The Rindge family spent decades trying to keep the public off what they believed was their private coast; their legal battles produced the estate system, the celebrity enclave, and eventually the Adamson House and its tile collection, which survived only because Rhoda Rindge built on the lagoon rather than the bluff.

The Getty Villa is a separate provenance entirely — an oil fortune translated into a replica Roman villa stuffed with antiquities — but it sits on the same coast for the same reason: the light, the views, the feeling that wealth and the Pacific belong together. Point Dume is what was here before any of that.
