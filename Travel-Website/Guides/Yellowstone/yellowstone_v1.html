Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 5 min · 🚕 2 min → Mammoth Hot Springs
1. Mammoth Hot Springs
↳ Terraced hot springs in brilliant white, gold, and rust hues cascade down the mountainside in an alien landscape. The Upper Terrace and Lower Terrace loop trails wind through the mineral-rich pools; the Mammoth Hotel sits in the middle of the thermal action. The formations shift and change constantly as thermal water deposits minerals year-round.

2. Norris Geyser Basin
↳ The hottest and most active geyser basin in Yellowstone. Echinus Geyser erupts reliably, shooting boiling water from a bright-blue pool; the Porcelain Basin trail passes dozens of steaming vents and brilliantly colored hot springs in an otherworldly landscape. Back Basin loops past Fountain Paint Pot and Morning Glory Pool. The Museum of the National Park Ranger (free, small) sits at the entrance.

🏛️ Open dawn to dusk year-round
⏰ ~2 hr

🚕 1 hr 10 min → Grand Prismatic Spring
3. Grand Prismatic Spring
↳ At 160 meters wide and 48 meters deep, the Grand Prismatic is the largest hot spring in the United States. The brilliant blue center deepens to orange, yellow, and rust at the edges — color from heat-loving bacteria (thermophiles). The overlook platform offers a close-up view; for the most iconic perspective (the rainbow-hued aerial shot), hike the Grand Prismatic Overlook Trail on the hillside opposite the spring (2.6 km round trip, 140m elevation gain).

Day 2

1. Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone
↳ The Yellowstone River has carved a stunning canyon with bright yellow, orange, and rust walls. The Lower Falls plunge 308 meters (102 feet higher than Niagara) in a thunderous cascade. Artist Point offers the most iconic view — the waterfall framed by the colorful canyon walls. The North Rim Trail (1.6 km round trip) and South Rim Trail (2.4 km loop) both offer spectacular viewpoints of the falls and canyon. Sunrise at Artist Point is unforgettable.

2. Hayden Valley
↳ A broad, open meadow where the Yellowstone River winds through sagebrush — one of the park's best wildlife viewing areas. Bison graze in massive herds; grizzly bears, wolves, and elk are regularly spotted, especially at dawn and dusk. The valley offers a glimpse of intact Yellowstone ecosystem. Pullouts along the road provide safe viewing points. Bring binoculars and patience.

🏛️ Open 24/7

📍 Hayden Valley · Inside Park
🚕 1 hr → Lamar Valley
3. Lamar Valley
↳ Called the "Serengeti of North America," Lamar Valley is the park's premier wildlife-watching destination. Massive herds of bison migrate across the wide-open sagebrush plain; elk, wolves (reintroduced in 1995), grizzly bears, and pronghorn antelope are regularly seen. The 6 km scenic road winds through the valley with multiple pullouts. Early morning (sunrise to 10am) and evening (5pm onward) offer the best chances. The Lamar Buffalo Ranch Historic Site (small museum, free) sits along the road.

🚌 Getting Around
🚗 Rental Car
Yellowstone requires a car to reach attractions. The park is 3,468 square miles with attractions spread across the interior. Most lodging (Mammoth Hot Springs) has rental car services or can arrange rentals. Drive times between stops are 45 minutes to 1 hour 45 minutes.
🅿️ Parking
Free parking at all trailheads and viewpoints inside the park. Parking lots are typically moderate to full during peak summer and early fall. Arrive early to secure a spot, especially at popular viewpoints like Artist Point and Grand Prismatic overlook.
⛽ Gas
Fill up at Canyon Village station or Mammoth service station. Gas is significantly more expensive inside the park than outside. No gas stations are reliably stocked during winter months — confirm availability before entering.
🛣️ Roads
The Grand Loop Road forms a figure-eight through the park. Roads are well-maintained but winding. Speed limits are typically 35–45 mph. Roads close seasonally due to heavy snow (winter closures are common October–April). Check Yellowstone.gov for current road conditions before arriving.
❗️ Heads Up
Park Entry Fee — The Yellowstone entrance fee is $35 per vehicle (valid for 7 days, covers all five park entrances). Individual passes ($20) and motorcycles ($30) also available. Purchase at any entrance station or online at recreation.gov.
Thermal Hot Spring Danger — Stay on marked boardwalks and trails around hot springs and geysers. Boiling water beneath thin mineral crusts can cause severe burns and death. At least 15 people have died from thermal burns since 1890. Do not leave children unattended near thermal features.
Wildlife Safety — Keep at least 25 meters (82 feet) from most wildlife and 100 meters (328 feet) from bears and wolves. The park has grizzly bears, which are dangerous when surprised. Make noise while hiking. Never approach or feed animals — bison, elk, and bears will attack if threatened or if cubs are nearby.
Elevation & Altitude — Mammoth Hot Springs sits at 2,239 meters (7,346 feet). The park's high elevation can cause altitude sickness. Drink water, avoid strenuous activity on the first day, and rest if experiencing headaches or shortness of breath.
Weather & Seasons — Summer (June–September) is the peak season with comfortable temperatures (15–24°C / 60–75°F) but crowded attractions. Snow is possible even in summer at higher elevations. Winter (November–March) brings heavy snow, closed roads, and brutal cold (–10°C / 14°F or lower). Spring (April–May) and fall (September–October) offer fewer crowds and wildlife viewing opportunities, though weather can be unpredictable.
Cell Service & Connectivity — Cell service is unreliable or nonexistent throughout most of the park. Mammoth Hot Springs has the best coverage of any park area. Download offline maps before arriving. Emergency services are available — dial 911 on any network.
Dining — The Mammoth Hotel has a restaurant on site. Canyon Village (1-hour drive) has a small café. Food options inside the park are limited and expensive. Pack snacks and water for full days in the park. Picnic supplies are available in Mammoth or nearby gateway towns (West Yellowstone is 1 hour away).
Park Lodging — Mammoth Hot Springs Hotel rooms (historic and modern) are competitive with outside hotels and have the advantage of zero drive time. Book well in advance (up to 13 months ahead). Winter lodging at Mammoth is limited but open year-round, unlike other park lodges.
