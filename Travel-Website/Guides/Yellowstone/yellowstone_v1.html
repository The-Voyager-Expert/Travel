1. Mammoth Hot Springs
↳ Terraced hot springs cascade down the mountainside in brilliant whites, golds, and rust hues. The Upper Terrace and Lower Terrace loops wind through mineral-rich thermal pools. Formations shift constantly as thermal water deposits minerals year-round.

2. Norris Geyser Basin
↳ The most active geyser basin in Yellowstone. Echinus Geyser erupts reliably, shooting boiling water from a bright blue pool. Porcelain Basin trails pass steaming vents and colored hot springs. Back Basin loops past Fountain Paint Pot and Morning Glory Pool.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 6:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~2 hr
📍 Norris Basin · Inside Park
🚕 1 hr 10 min → Grand Prismatic Spring
3. Grand Prismatic Spring
↳ The largest hot spring in the US at 160 meters wide. The brilliant blue center deepens to orange and yellow at the edges from thermophile bacteria. The overlook platform offers close views. The Grand Prismatic Overlook Trail offers the iconic aerial perspective · 2.6 km round trip · 140 m elevation.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Grand Prismatic Spring · Inside Park

↳ Small museum at Norris with exhibits on Yellowstone's geology, wildlife, and history. Features displays on geysers, thermal features, and the 1988 fires. A quick stop for learning about the park's natural forces and conservation efforts.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 1 hr → Grand Canyon of Yellowstone
1. Grand Canyon of Yellowstone
↳ The Yellowstone River has carved a stunning canyon with bright yellow, orange, and rust walls. The Lower Falls plunge 308 meters in a thunderous cascade. Artist Point offers the most iconic view. North Rim Trail · 1.6 km and South Rim Trail · 2.4 km loop offer viewpoints.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2 hr 30 min
📍 Canyon Village · Inside Park
🚕 20 min → Hayden Valley
2. Hayden Valley
↳ A broad meadow where the Yellowstone River winds through sagebrush. One of the park's best wildlife viewing areas. Bison graze in massive herds; grizzly bears, wolves, and elk are regularly spotted, especially at dawn and dusk. Pullouts along the road provide safe viewing points.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Hayden Valley · Inside Park
🚕 1 hr → Lamar Valley
3. Lamar Valley
↳ Called the Serengeti of North America, Lamar Valley is the park's premier wildlife watching destination. Massive herds of bison migrate across the wide sagebrush plain. Elk, wolves, grizzly bears, and pronghorn antelope are regularly seen. The 6 km scenic road winds with pullouts for viewing.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2 hr
📍 Lamar Valley · Inside Park
🚕 30 min → Lamar Buffalo Ranch
4. Lamar Buffalo Ranch
↳ Historic homestead in Lamar Valley built in 1907 to study and breed bison. The small museum features exhibits on the buffalo ranching era and wildlife management. The site sits in an excellent wildlife viewing area where elk, bison, and sometimes wolves can be observed from the grounds.
📖 Wikipedia

🚌 Getting Around
Rental Car
Yellowstone requires a car to reach attractions. The park spans 3,468 square miles with attractions spread throughout. Most lodging at Mammoth Hot Springs has rental car services. Drive times between stops range from 45 minutes to 1 hour 45 minutes.
🅿️ Parking
Free parking at all trailheads and viewpoints. Parking lots are typically moderate to full during peak summer and early fall. Arrive early to secure a spot at popular viewpoints.
⛽ Gas
Fill up at Canyon Village or Mammoth service stations. Gas is significantly more expensive inside the park than outside. No gas stations are reliably stocked during winter months.
🛣️ Roads
The Grand Loop Road forms a figure-eight through the park. Roads are well-maintained but winding. Speed limits are typically 35–45 mph. Roads close seasonally due to heavy snow · winter closures common October–April.
❗️ Heads Up
Park Entry Fee — $35 per vehicle · valid for 7 days · covers all five park entrances. Individual passes $20 · motorcycles $30. Purchase at entrance stations or recreation.gov.
Thermal Safety — Stay on marked boardwalks around hot springs and geysers. Boiling water beneath thin mineral crusts causes severe burns. Do not leave children unattended near thermal features.
Wildlife Safety — Keep at least 25 meters from most wildlife and 100 meters from bears and wolves. Make noise while hiking. Never approach or feed animals.
Elevation — Mammoth Hot Springs sits at 2,239 meters. High elevation can cause altitude sickness. Avoid strenuous activity on the first day.
Weather — Summer · June–September · peak season · 15–24°C but crowded. Snow possible even in summer. Winter · November–March · heavy snow and closed roads. Spring · April–May and fall · September–October offer fewer crowds and best wildlife viewing.
Cell Service — Cell service is unreliable throughout most of the park. Mammoth Hot Springs has the best coverage. Download offline maps before arriving.
Dining — Mammoth Hotel has a restaurant on site. Canyon Village · 1 hour drive · has a small café. Food options inside the park are limited and expensive. Pack snacks and water.
