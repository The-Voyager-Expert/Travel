Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 5 min → Mammoth Hot Springs
1. Mammoth Hot Springs
↳ Brilliant white travertine terraces built by 56 active hot springs, cascading down the mountainside in sculpted tiers. The mineral-laden water deposits calcium carbonate as it cools, continuously reshaping the landform. The boardwalk threads past the Minerva Terrace, Palette Spring and the Grand Terrace, the park's most photogenic geothermal display.

2. Norris Basin
↳ The hottest geothermal basin in Yellowstone, home to colourful hot springs, mudpots and geysers. The Porcelain Basin boardwalk passes vibrant pools like the brilliant blue Chapel Pool and acidic springs that paint the ground in yellows and oranges. Back Basin holds the Steamboat Geyser, one of the most powerful geysers in the world.

🚕 30 min → Grand Prismatic
3. Grand Prismatic Spring
↳ The largest hot spring in the USA and the third-largest in the world, a 370-foot-diameter crater of impossibly vivid blue water that shifts to orange and yellow at the edges, the temperature gradient creating distinct colour zones. The colours come from heat-loving bacteria and minerals suspended in the 199-degree water. From the boardwalk below, the colours are muted; the Grand Prismatic Overlook trail above reveals the full rainbow.

4. Fountain Paint Pot
↳ A short, easy boardwalk loop past a variety of geothermal features in the Lower Geyser Basin — bubbling mudpots, geysers including the predictable Fountain Geyser, and hot springs in a 1-mile circuit. The geothermal forces here are active and dynamic; mudpot shapes and geyser behaviour shift with the seasons, so every visit reveals something new.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 75 min → Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone
1. Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone
↳ A 1,200-foot-deep chasm carved by the Yellowstone River with golden cliffs and two major waterfalls — the Upper Falls (109 feet) and the thundering Lower Falls (308 feet). The Artist Point viewpoint captures the full drama of the canyon's descent into the valley below. This is Yellowstone's most photographed landscape, a landscape of impossibly warm rocks set against dark forest.

2. Hayden Valley
↳ A broad, open grassland in the heart of the park where the Yellowstone River meanders through willow groves and sagebrush, one of the best places in North America to see wildlife. Bison herds graze openly, elk gather at dawn and dusk, and during early morning or evening hours grizzly bears fish the river. The valley floor is relatively flat and walkable; multiple pullouts along the Grand Loop Road offer viewing.

3. Lamar Valley
↳ Often called the "Serengeti of North America," this expansive valley in the northeast of the park is prime habitat for wolves, grizzly bears, bison, elk and pronghorn. The wolves reintroduced here in 1995 have fundamentally changed the ecosystem; their presence is now a guaranteed draw. Early morning and late afternoon are prime hours. Soda Butte Creek cuts through; multiple pullouts and short walks offer views across the valley floor.

4. Roosevelt Arch
↳ A stone gateway arch at the park's North Entrance, completed in 1903 during the Roosevelt presidency. Inscribed with the words "For the Benefit and Enjoyment of the People," it is Yellowstone's most iconic entrance and a symbol of the conservation movement that led to the creation of America's first national park in 1872. The arch frames the road north to Mammoth and is a popular photo stop.

🌋 Geysers as Clocks

Old Faithful erupts roughly every 90 minutes, and that regularity made it the most famous geyser in the world. But what most visitors don't realise is that geysers are not pressure-relief valves in any conventional sense. They're thermal oscillators — water falling through cracks in the rock heats up, forms steam, builds pressure, forces an eruption, then drains and begins refilling. The cycle repeats with ballistic precision because the geometry of the chamber and the geothermal heat gradient stay constant.

Yellowstone's geysers are concentrated in the Geyser Hill area near Old Faithful because a particular combination of geology — silica-lined chambers, the right depth to the water table, and reliable heat flow from below — creates the precise conditions for oscillation. Move a few hundred metres away and the conditions shift slightly; the water cools too fast or steam escapes too readily, and you get hot springs instead. The Norris Basin geysers are hotter and more unpredictable because the water table is shallower and the heat gradient is steeper. Mammoth Hot Springs, by contrast, is so hot and the water column is so shallow that geysers can't form at all — instead the water deposits minerals as it cools, building those iconic travertine terraces. Every thermal feature on the Grand Loop is a different solution to the same underlying problem: moving heat from depth to the surface.
