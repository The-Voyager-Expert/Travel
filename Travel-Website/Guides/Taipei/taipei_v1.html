Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 15 min · 🚕 5 min → Huashan 1914 Creative Park
1. Huashan 1914 Creative Park
↳ A former tobacco factory repurposed into Taipei's most vibrant arts campus — red-brick warehouses, sunlit courtyards, and exhibition halls hosting indie markets, installations, and pop-up performance spaces year-round. Weekend streets fill with local designers and food vendors.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 No. 1 Section 1 Bade Road · Zhongzheng District
🚶 25 min · 🚕 8 min → Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall
2. Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall
↳ A white-marble monument anchoring one of Asia's grandest ceremonial squares — the main hall faces the National Concert Hall and National Theater across a vast axial plaza. The hourly changing of the guard draws crowds for its precise choreography; the underground museum covers Taiwan's 20th-century history.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
🆓 Free
📍 No. 21 Zhongshan South Road · Zhongzheng District
🚕 20 min → Elephant Mountain
3. Elephant Mountain
↳ Four rocky peaks rising directly from the edge of Xinyi District — a 20-minute climb to granite outcrops where Taipei 101 fills the centre of the frame. At sunset the tower turns gold, then blue, then erupts in light. One of the city's unmissable views, open day and night.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Xiangshan MRT Exit 2 · Xinyi District
🚶 10 min · 🚕 5 min → Taipei 101
4. Taipei 101
↳ For a decade the world's tallest building — its segmented pagoda silhouette defines Taipei's skyline. The 89th-floor observatory gives a 360° view over the river basin, the Yangmingshan volcanic massif, and the north coast. The 101-tonne golden damper sphere is an attraction in itself.
📖 Wikipedia

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 15 min → Grand Hotel Taipei
1. Grand Hotel Taipei
↳ A 14-storey Tang-dynasty-style palace built on a hill — commissioned as a state guesthouse in the 1950s and still one of Asia's most photographed buildings. The main lobby is a sequence of lacquered red columns, gilded ceilings, and carved dragons. The hilltop grounds are open to all visitors.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 No. 1 Section 4 Zhongshan North Road · Zhongshan District
🚕 15 min → National Palace Museum
2. National Palace Museum
↳ One of the world's great collections — 700,000 objects spanning 8,000 years: Shang bronzes, Song porcelain, imperial jade, and the Jadeite Cabbage, a palm-sized carving that draws its own queue. The collection was evacuated from Beijing's Forbidden City and never returned.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~2 hrs

🚕 20 min → Beitou Thermal Valley
3. Beitou Thermal Valley
↳ A steaming jade-green lake of highly acidic hot-spring water — one of only two places on Earth with Radon Spring water. The vivid sulphur-blue surface and mist rolling off the valley floor make this Taipei's most otherworldly sight. A short boardwalk loops the lake rim.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Sunday 9:00am - 5:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~45 min

4. Xiaoyoukeng
↳ The geothermal field of Yangmingshan National Park at 800 metres — fumaroles, sulphur vents, and acid-bleached slopes. Boardwalks wind through the steam; on a clear day the view south over Taipei is extraordinary. The park's most dramatic volcanic terrain.
📖 Wikipedia

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚝 20 min → Longshan Temple
1. Longshan Temple
↳ Longshan Temple is one of Taipei's oldest places of worship, founded in 1738 by settlers from Fujian province. It is dedicated primarily to Guanyin, the Buddhist goddess of mercy, and houses dozens of additional Taoist and folk religion deities. The nearby Huaxi Street Night Market is a short walk away.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 6:00am - 10:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
⚠️ Active place of worship — observe ritual etiquette
📍 No. 211 Guangzhou St · Wanhua District
🚕 15 min → Dihua Street

↳ Taipei's oldest commercial street — preserved Baroque shophouses from the Japanese colonial era still selling dried goods, medicinal herbs, and fabric along a 19th-century trading district.

🍮 Local Tastes
Xiao Long Bao
↳ Steamed soup dumplings with 18 folds per piece — a gossamer skin and a burst of hot broth on the first bite. Tradition: take a small bite off the side, let the broth cool, then add ginger and black vinegar in a soup spoon.
📍 Din Tai Fung
Beef Noodle Soup
↳ Braised beef shin and tendon in a dark soy-and-spice broth over thick wheat noodles — the city's most contested comfort food. The red-braised style is the standard: deep and sticky, with star anise and broad bean paste in the base.
📍 Yongkang Beef Noodle
Scallion Pancake
↳ A flaky lard-laminated flatbread rolled with chopped scallions and pan-fried to order — Taipei's street-corner breakfast. Best with an egg cracked on and folded inside. At any breakfast stall from 6am.
📍 Breakfast stalls citywide · open from 6:00am
Pineapple Cake
↳ A buttery shortbread shell around pineapple jam — Taiwan's signature souvenir sweet. Bakeries along Zhongshan North Road stock the best artisan versions; airport shops are a distant second.
📍 Bakeries on Zhongshan North Road
Oyster Vermicelli
↳ Glass noodles braised in thick sweet-potato-starch gravy with fresh oysters, topped with garlic, black vinegar, and cilantro. Eat standing at the counter — the Ximending stall is the city benchmark.
📍 Ay-Chung Flour-Rice Noodle · Ximending

Taichung · 1 hr
↳ Taiwan's second city — Rainbow Village is a neighbourhood painted in exuberant murals by a retired soldier, Miyahara is an ice cream parlour in a restored early-20th-century ophthalmologist's office, and Fengjia Night Market is the island's largest.
🎫 book at: THSR or Omio
Tainan · 1 hr 25 min
↳ Taiwan's oldest city and culinary capital — colonial-era forts, Qing-dynasty shrines, and a street-food culture denser than anywhere else on the island. Eat along Haian Road and the alleys around Chihkan Tower; the coffin bread and milkfish congee are morning staples.
🎫 book at: THSR or Omio
Hualien · 2 hrs
↳ Gateway to Taroko Gorge — a 19-km marble canyon with turquoise water, suspension bridges, and walking tunnels cut through cliffs. Travel by TRA; Hualien is not on the THSR network. Go early — gorge road access is managed after rainfall.
🎫 book at: TRA or Omio

🌋 The City That Lives on the Side of a Volcano

Most people arrive in Taipei thinking it's a city. It is, but it's also an encircled basin at the foot of Yangmingshan, a live volcanic massif where fumaroles hiss in the highland meadows and hot-spring water runs through the pipes of buildings that would otherwise be apartments in any other city. Day 2's route makes this visible: grand palace on a hill, then imperial collection in the mountain foothills, then an actual geothermal lake in a valley, then sulphur vents at 800 metres. The city progressively peels away.

The thread running through both days is the weight of contested identity — a tobacco factory turned art park, a memorial hall whose meaning is actively debated, a collection evacuated from one country and never returned. Taipei carries more unresolved history per square metre than almost any city its size. The food, at least, is not contested: the xiao long bao count is sacred, the beef noodle broth is non-negotiable, and the scallion pancake stall opens before sunrise whether anyone is watching or not.
