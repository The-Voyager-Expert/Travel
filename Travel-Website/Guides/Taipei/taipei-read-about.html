Taipei is the city where an alternative version of Chinese civilization played out in real time — and where that alternative is visible on every block, in every meal, in the fact of the place itself.

The island at the edge of everything

Taiwan sits 160 kilometers off the southeastern coast of China, separated by a strait that is shallow enough to have been a land bridge as recently as ten thousand years ago. The people who came across that bridge — the Austronesian aboriginal peoples who still make up about 2.5 percent of Taiwan's population — were the island's first inhabitants, and their presence here predates any Chinese settlement by thousands of years. The Taiwanese government formally refers to them as "indigenous peoples," and their languages, traditions, and claim to this land have been present throughout every colonial and political overlay that followed.

The Han Chinese arrived in significant numbers beginning in the seventeenth century, primarily from Fujian province across the strait — the Hokkien-speaking settlers who became the majority population and whose language, Taiwanese Hokkien, is still spoken widely alongside Mandarin today. The Dutch had a fort on the island (Fort Zeelandia, near present-day Tainan) from 1624 until 1662, when they were expelled by Zheng Chenggong — Koxinga — a Ming loyalist who used Taiwan as a base for resisting the Qing dynasty that had just taken the mainland. The Qing eventually incorporated Taiwan in 1683 and governed it, with varying degrees of attention, until 1895.

Then Japan won the First Sino-Japanese War, and Taiwan changed hands in the Treaty of Shimonoseki. For fifty years, Taiwan was a Japanese colony. What this means for the city you're walking through is more legible than you might expect.

The Japanese city underneath

Walk through the Zhongzheng district near the Presidential Office Building, or along the streets around National Taiwan University in Daan, and you'll notice something: the trees are old, the streets are wide, and the remaining older buildings have a specific character — brick, with tiled roofs and covered walkways — that looks nothing like mainland China and exactly like Meiji-era Japan. That is because it is. The Japanese colonial administration built Taipei as a model modern city, with planned streets, public buildings in Western neoclassical style as filtered through Japanese taste, and the kind of tree-lined boulevards that Japanese urban planners were deploying across their empire.

The Presidential Office Building — which you can see from the end of Ketagalan Boulevard — was built in 1919 as the Governor-General's Office. It is a brick Renaissance-Baroque building of a type that could stand comfortably in Tokyo or Osaka, and it still functions as Taiwan's seat of executive government. The red-brick campus of National Taiwan University was built during the Japanese period. The Zhongshan Hall, where Japan formally surrendered Taiwan to the Republic of China in 1945, is a Japanese-built civic auditorium that has since hosted Taiwanese presidential inaugurations.

The Japanese built a modern city here. What the people who lived in it did with it is more interesting than the buildings themselves.

The Japanese colonial period is complicated territory in Taiwan. The colonists were not benevolent — there were uprisings, suppressions, and forced assimilation campaigns — but the infrastructure they built and the educational system they introduced left a different mark than comparable colonial periods elsewhere in Asia. A generation of Taiwanese intellectuals was educated in Japan; some of them became fluent in Japanese culture while remaining deeply Taiwanese in identity. The tension between those two things is still audible in how older Taiwanese discuss the period, which is with considerably more ambivalence than outright condemnation.

What arrived in 1949

On October 1, 1949, Mao Zedong proclaimed the People's Republic of China in Beijing. Two months later, Chiang Kai-shek and the remnants of the Nationalist government — the Republic of China — retreated to Taiwan with approximately two million people: soldiers, government officials, intellectuals, artists, and the families of all of them. They brought with them the conviction that they were the legitimate government of all China, temporarily displaced, and that they would return to the mainland once the Communist government collapsed.

They also brought the contents of the National Palace Museum.

Between 1933 and 1949, the curators of the Palace Museum in Beijing had been packing and moving China's imperial art collection — more than 600,000 objects accumulated over centuries by Chinese emperors — to keep it out of Japanese hands during the war. When the Nationalists retreated to Taiwan, they took the best of it with them: roughly 700,000 objects, packed in crates, transported across the strait, stored in a mountain tunnel in central Taiwan for years before a purpose-built museum was constructed in Taipei in 1965. The National Palace Museum on Shilin's edge of the city now holds the largest collection of Chinese imperial art in the world — larger than what remained in Beijing, larger than anything outside Taiwan.

This is the detail that changes how you see the museum. You are not looking at Taiwanese art. You are looking at the portable record of Chinese imperial civilization, rescued from the mainland, preserved here by the people who lost a civil war. The museum is a claim about legitimacy expressed in bronze vessels and jade carvings and Song dynasty paintings. It is one of the stranger and more poignant institutions you will visit anywhere.

The long authoritarian winter

Chiang Kai-shek governed Taiwan under martial law from 1949 until his death in 1975 — the longest period of martial law in modern history. The period is known in Taiwan as the White Terror. Political opposition was suppressed ruthlessly; thousands of people, mostly Taiwanese intellectuals and suspected Communists, were imprisoned or executed. The terror was not abstract. It reached into families, into universities, into the medical and legal professions. The people who lived through it remember it, and their children remember them remembering it.

The Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall — the enormous white marble plaza in the center of Taipei, with the octagonal hall at its end — was built after his death and is one of the most contested public spaces in Taiwan. For decades it was simply a memorial to a founding father. In recent years, as Taiwan's democracy has deepened, the question of what to do with it has become a live political issue: remove it, repurpose it, rename it, contextualize it? The hall still stands. The changing of the guard ceremony still happens every hour. The debate continues.

What Chiang's son and successor Chiang Ching-kuo began in the 1980s — a gradual loosening of political controls, the lifting of martial law in 1987, the legalization of opposition parties — culminated in 1996 in Taiwan's first direct presidential election. It was also, not coincidentally, the moment when China conducted missile tests in the Taiwan Strait in an attempt to intimidate voters. The voters returned the incumbent president with an increased majority. Taiwan's democracy, unlike most, can date itself to a specific election on a specific day while Chinese missiles flew overhead.

The food as a kind of history

Taiwanese food is one of the world's great unheralded cuisines, and understanding it means understanding the layers that produced it. The base is Hokkien cooking from Fujian province — seafood-forward, light on spice, built around clear broths and fresh ingredients. On top of that: the Hakka cooking of a second wave of mainland immigrants, sturdier and saltier, built for agricultural labor. On top of that: the Japanese colonial influence, visible in the oyster omelets at night markets, the beef noodle soup that probably descends from the Japanese ramen tradition filtered through mainlander Chinese technique, the taro desserts that appear in both Japanese and Taiwanese sweets.

Then 1949: two million mainlanders arrived, and they brought the regional cuisines of every province of China with them. Sichuan restaurants, Shanghainese soup dumplings, Cantonese roast meats, Shanxi noodles, Yunnan mushroom dishes — all of them arrived in Taiwan with the Nationalist retreat and have been developing here, in isolation from the mainland, for seventy-five years. The beef noodle soup that Taipei's restaurants compete ferociously to perfect is, at its best, a product of this particular history: mainland technique, Taiwanese ingredients, Japanese influence on broth clarity, decades of local refinement.

The night market is not a tourist attraction. It is where Taipei eats dinner, has always eaten dinner, and will continue eating dinner after the tourists have gone.

The night markets — Shilin, Raohe, Ningxia — are where this food culture is most alive and least performed. Go to Raohe Street Night Market on a weekday evening. Push through the Songshan Temple gate at the east end and walk the length of the market, which takes about fifteen minutes and involves turning down multiple offers of grilled squid, oyster vermicelli, scallion pancakes, and sesame-paste noodles. Stop at the iron egg stall near the middle — the black, slightly rubbery eggs braised repeatedly in soy until they're more concentrated than anything an egg normally tastes like. Eat one. Then go back and find the pork pepper bun vendor, the one with the clay pot oven, who has been there for decades. The pepper and scallion pork inside the bread, pulled from the oven and eaten standing on the street — this is Taipei eating, which is to say eating quickly, in a small space, surrounded by other people doing the same thing, every bite better than you expected.

The democracy you can walk through

Taipei is a democratic capital in a way that is specifically visible. The Legislative Yuan — Taiwan's parliament — sits in the city center, open to visitors; the debates inside it are sometimes very loud. The Presidential Office can be visited on weekends. Political protests happen regularly and are policed with a restraint that visitors from authoritarian states find startling.

The 2014 Sunflower Student Movement, in which students occupied the Legislative Yuan for three weeks to oppose a trade agreement with China, is recent enough that most Taipei residents over thirty remember exactly where they were. The occupation ended without violence, the government negotiated, and the students went home. This is not how occupations of parliaments usually end. The 2024 presidential election — in which the incumbent ruling party won its third consecutive term, with the main opposition demanding closer ties with Beijing — is an ongoing conversation happening in the streets, the media, and the restaurants where people argue over whether the trajectory is right.

Walking through Taipei, you are in the middle of that argument. The city is not performing democracy for visitors. It is living it, with all the mess and noise that entails. Coming from most other places in Asia — or from much of the rest of the world — this is noticeable and specific and worth paying attention to.

The alternative history you're standing in

Here is what to carry with you when you land: Taipei is what Chinese civilization looks like when it was not subjected to the Cultural Revolution.

The Cultural Revolution (1966–1976) on the mainland deliberately destroyed temples, burned classical books, smashed traditional art, and attacked Confucian education as feudal. In Taiwan, the same period saw the opposite: the Nationalist government, positioning itself as the true heir of Chinese culture, actively preserved and promoted traditional opera, classical calligraphy, Confucian ritual, and the religious practices — Buddhism, Taoism, folk religion — that the mainland was suppressing. The temples you visit in Taipei — Longshan Temple, Xingtian Temple, Xingji Temple — are still alive as religious institutions, crowded with worshippers, thick with incense, organizing the social and spiritual life of their neighborhoods. They are not museums.

The National Palace Museum holds the imperial art. The temples hold the living religion. The night markets hold the food traditions. The Japanese buildings hold the colonial history. The Chiang Kai-shek Memorial holds the authoritarian past. The Legislative Yuan holds the democratic present. And the strait between Taiwan and the mainland holds, still, the question that has organized the last seventy-five years of this island's existence.

When you walk through Taipei, you are walking through the most vivid answer to a hypothetical: what would Chinese civilization have become if the Communist Party had not won the civil war? The answer is not simple, and it is not entirely comfortable, and it is not finished being written. But it is here, in this city, available to anyone who pays attention. The Japanese gate next to the dumpling shop next to the democratic parliament next to the temple that has been operating without interruption since the Japanese colonial period — all of it present, all of it active, all of it Taipei in a way that nowhere else on earth quite is.
