Two cities on opposite banks of a river, held together by bridges that have been blown up and rebuilt, occupiers who came and left, and hot springs no one has ever been able to destroy.

Two cities, one name

Stand on the Chain Bridge and look both ways. That's the whole story of Budapest in a single turn of your head. To the west: Buda, climbing limestone hills that rise straight from the river, medieval ramparts and the castle silhouette on the ridge above. To the east: Pest, perfectly flat, spreading across the Hungarian plain in a grid of grand boulevards that goes on until it runs out of city. These weren't the same place until 1873, when three separate municipalities — Buda, Óbuda, Pest — were merged by administrative decree. The seam is still there. You'll feel it every time you cross the Danube.

The Danube narrows here to about 230 meters — wide enough to be a serious river, narrow enough to be bridged. That's the whole reason this city exists. The Romans understood it first. They founded Aquincum on the western bank in AD 41, built it into the capital of the province of Pannonia Inferior, and held it until Rome withdrew in the early fifth century. You can still walk through the ruins north of the city center. The site was too valuable to stay empty for long.

What followed — Mongol invasion in 1241, a Renaissance flowering under King Matthias Corvinus whose library was considered among Europe's greatest, then Ottoman occupation from 1541 for over 150 years — left the city repeatedly emptied and rebuilt. When Habsburg forces retook Buda in 1686, the Christian population had collapsed from perhaps a thousand people to fewer than a hundred. The city was essentially rubble. This would not be the last time.

The springs beneath the city

Go to the Rudas Baths on a weekday morning, before the tourists arrive. Walk into the main octagonal pool — the original Ottoman pool, five hundred years old, stone on all sides, light coming down through the domed ceiling in narrow shafts the way it was designed to. Sit in the water. This is why the site has been continuously occupied for two thousand years. Budapest sits above a geological fault system that feeds 125 thermal springs into the city, producing seventy million liters of hot water every day at temperatures reaching 58 degrees Celsius. You can bomb the buildings, topple the government, reroute the economy — and the water still comes up. The Ottomans who built the Rudas in the sixteenth century understood this. It's why they built here. It's why everyone has always built here.

Budapest is the only major European capital that sits above a thermal spring system of this scale. "Spa capital of Europe" is the official tourism line — but go to the Rudas on a Tuesday morning and you'll understand it's not marketing.

The later baths are grander. Skip the Gellért if you want the authentic experience — it's beautiful but it knows it's beautiful. Go to the Széchenyi in City Park instead, the yellow Neo-Baroque palace opened in 1913 with outdoor pools large enough to swim laps in. On winter mornings the steam rises off the heated water into the cold air and old men play chess on floating boards. That's not performance. That's Tuesday. Going to the baths in Budapest is not something residents do for tourists. It's what they do on Saturday morning. You're just allowed to come along.

The compromise that built a city

Almost everything you'll photograph in Budapest — the Parliament, the Opera House, Heroes' Square, Andrássy Avenue — was built in a forty-year sprint between 1867 and 1910. To understand why, you need one piece of political history: the Austro-Hungarian Compromise of 1867. The deal transformed the Habsburg Empire into a Dual Monarchy, giving Hungary co-equal status with Austria. Budapest became a co-capital alongside Vienna overnight. The Hungarians' response was to build a city that looked the part — fast, and bigger than strictly necessary.

Walk to the Pest bank of the Danube at dusk and look north. The Hungarian Parliament Building, completed in 1904, is the third-largest parliament building in the world — Neo-Gothic, with a central dome modeled on St. Paul's in London and two symmetrical wings so that neither political chamber could claim precedence over the other. It is lit from below at night and it is genuinely, almost embarrassingly beautiful. The Hungarians didn't build it to be modest.

Then walk Andrássy Avenue from the Opera House to Heroes' Square. The avenue was modeled on the Champs-Élysées, lined with Neo-Renaissance mansions, and it anchors the Millennium Monument at its far end — built in 1896 to mark the thousandth anniversary of the Magyar conquest of the Carpathian Basin. Directly beneath the avenue runs the M1, which opened the same year: the first underground railway on the European continent, still operating with its original small cars. The avenue, the underground, Heroes' Square — it's all one UNESCO World Heritage Site. They built a lot in forty years.

The Jewish quarter and its ghosts

Walk into the 7th district and you're walking into a neighborhood that holds two completely contradictory things at the same time. In 1900, Budapest's Jewish population made up 23.6 percent of the city — one of the largest concentrations in Europe. The community shaped the intellectual and cultural life of the city so thoroughly that detractors called it "Judapest." The center of that community was here, in the 7th district, where the Dohány Street Synagogue still stands — the second-largest synagogue in the world, built in Moorish Revival style between 1854 and 1859, its twin onion-domed towers still the dominant feature of the streetscape.

Go into the garden behind the synagogue. There's a mass grave there, and a weeping willow sculpture in metal with the names of the dead on its leaves. The synagogue was used as a military barracks during the war. The Jewish quarter was enclosed as a ghetto in the final months of German occupation in 1944. Of Budapest's roughly 250,000 Jewish residents before the war, between 20 and 40 percent did not survive.

Two blocks from the ghetto memorial, Szimpla Kert — the ruin bar that launched a genre — opens every night in a derelict courtyard. The 7th district holds both things at once. You don't have to resolve that. You just have to notice it.

In the early 2000s, those same crumbling courtyards and abandoned buildings — too contested to redevelop easily — became something else. Szimpla Kert opened in 2002 in a derelict factory building: a bar, gallery, and social space built inside a ruin rather than in spite of one. The "ruin bar" became Budapest's most exported cultural invention. Other cities have copied the format; none has quite matched the original context, which is a neighborhood that carries genuine weight and has decided, somehow, to keep living in it.

The siege and what it left behind

On certain blocks in the 7th and 8th districts, look closely at the facades. The pockmarks you're seeing in the stucco are bullet holes. They are not maintained as monuments. Nobody put a plaque on them. They're just not patched yet. That's what eighty years of subsequent history looks like on a building.

The Siege of Budapest ran from December 24, 1944 to February 13, 1945 — fifty-one days, one of the longest and bloodiest urban battles of the Second World War in Europe. Over 38,000 civilians died. The retreating German forces destroyed every bridge across the Danube as they left, including the Chain Bridge, which had been the city's first permanent crossing when it opened in 1849. The Parliament was damaged. The Royal Palace on the Buda hill was gutted. A city built in forty years of confident expansion was mostly rubble in fifty days.

Then it was rebuilt — under Soviet-backed communist rule, which brought its own complications, including the suppression of the 1956 uprising that killed more than three thousand people and drove two hundred thousand into exile. The Chain Bridge was restored in 1949, exactly a hundred years after it first opened. The Parliament was repaired. The Royal Palace went up in a modified form historians still debate. The center held because the structure was too solid to erase. Hungary transitioned to democracy peacefully in 1989; Soviet forces departed by 1991. Budapest has a particular relationship with resilience. It's had a lot of practice.

The music the city made

Three composers whose names you may know came out of Budapest in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, and they're worth knowing in sequence. Franz Liszt, born in 1811, was the greatest piano virtuoso of his era — and he founded the Budapest Academy of Music in 1875. His name is on the building, on the main concert hall, on the street it sits on. Béla Bartók arrived at the Liszt Academy in 1907, taught there for decades, and spent his spare time making field recordings of folk music across Hungary and Central Europe, feeding those rhythms directly into chamber music and orchestral work that still sounds like no one else. Zoltán Kodály did the same work in parallel. Between the three of them, they built a tradition of Hungarian music that is identifiable anywhere in the world.

The cafés that shaped this world are still there too. The New York Café opened in 1894 as an around-the-clock salon — open day and night, papers available to anyone who sat down, a standing offer of paper and pen. Writers and journalists used it as an office. The poet Ferenc Molnár once threw the café's keys into the Danube to prevent it from ever closing. The New York is still there, restored to its full Baroque excess of gilded columns and frescoed ceilings. You can sit in it and order coffee. The keys, presumably, are still in the river.

A city built to be walked

Here's what most people miss about Budapest: the scale is human. The grand boulevards of Pest are wide but not alienating. The historic center is genuinely walkable. The Buda hills are twenty minutes from the Chain Bridge. Walk up to the Citadella on Gellért Hill — it's a twenty-minute climb from the river — and you can see the whole city at once: the Parliament lit on the Pest bank, the castle on the Buda ridge, the bridges strung between them, the Danube going silver in both directions. It's one of the better urban views in Europe, and it costs nothing and requires no reservation.

The food will surprise you if you arrive with low expectations, which most visitors do. Hungarian cuisine has no international profile to speak of. This is a marketing failure, not a culinary one. Eat goulash soup in a bread bowl at the Central Market Hall. Try lángos from a street stall. The serious restaurants — Budapest has been named among the fifty best food cities globally — are excellent and, by Western European standards, still affordable.

Before you board: every time this city was taken apart — by the Mongols, the Ottomans, the Nazis, the Soviets — the hot springs kept running beneath the rubble, and someone came back. The buildings you'll walk past were rebuilt by people who had just lost everything and decided to make them grander than before. Once you understand that about Budapest, you see it in the architecture, in the baths, in the bullet holes that haven't been plastered over. The persistence isn't incidental. It's what the city is made of.
