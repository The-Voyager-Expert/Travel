Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 8 min · 🚕 3 min → Hungarian Parliament Building
1.Hungarian Parliament Building
↳ The neo-Gothic masterpiece built 1885–1904 dominates the Danube embankment and defines the Budapest skyline. The third-largest parliament building in the world — and arguably the most beautiful of any — houses the Crown of Saint Stephen and the Hungarian coronation regalia in the Dome Hall. All visits are guided.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Hungarian Parliament Building — Guided Tour · 4.8⭐ · parlament.hu
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
⚠️ Timed entry — tickets sell out; book online before visiting
📍 Kossuth Lajos tér 1-3 · Lipótváros
🚶 12 min · 🚕 4 min → St. Stephen's Basilica
2.St. Stephen's Basilica
↳ Hungary's largest church, its dome equal in height to Parliament. The observation terrace at 65 metres delivers panoramic views across Pest, the Danube, and the Buda Hills. St. Stephen's mummified right hand is displayed in the treasury.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ St. Stephen's Basilica — Panorama Tower · 4.6⭐ · bazilika.biz
🏛️ Monday - Saturday 9:00am - 6:45pm · Sunday 1:00pm - 6:45pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Szent István tér 1 · Lipótváros
🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → Hungarian State Opera House
3.Hungarian State Opera House
↳ One of the most beautiful neo-Renaissance opera houses in Europe, designed by Miklós Ybl and opened in 1884. Franz Joseph I attended the inaugural performance in 1884. Guided tours reveal the gilded foyer, frescoed ceiling, and the third-largest stage in Europe by volume.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Hungarian State Opera House — Guided Tour · 4.7⭐ · opera.hu
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
⚠️ Evening performance tickets available at opera.hu
📍 Andrássy út 22 · Terézváros
🚶 25 min · 🚕 8 min → Heroes' Square
4.Heroes' Square
↳ Created for Hungary's millennial celebrations in 1896. Twin colonnades frame bronze statues of the seven founding Magyar chieftains, topped by the Archangel Gabriel at 36 metres. The walk down Andrássy Avenue from the Opera is itself a procession through one of Europe's finest urban boulevards.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Hősök tere · Városliget
🚶 8 min · 🚕 3 min → Vajdahunyad Castle
5.Vajdahunyad Castle
↳ Built for Hungary's 1896 Millennium Exhibition as a miniature history of Hungarian architecture — Romanesque chapel, Gothic battlements, Renaissance loggia, Baroque domes united at City Park lake. Originally a temporary exhibit in wood, rebuilt in stone after the public refused to let it be demolished.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Vajdahunyad sétány · Városliget

Day 2

1.Buda Castle
↳ The Royal Palace complex on Castle Hill houses the Hungarian National Gallery — Gothic winged altarpieces to 20th-century masters. The terrace commands the most celebrated view of the Pest embankment, the Chain Bridge, and the Parliament.
📖 Wikipedia

2.Matthias Church
↳ The 14th-century Gothic coronation church rebuilt by Frigyes Schulek in the 1890s. The diamond-patterned Zsolnay tile roof is Budapest's most photographed architectural detail. Franz Joseph I was crowned King of Hungary here in 1867.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Matthias Church · 4.6⭐ · matyas-templom.hu
🏛️ Monday - Friday 9:00am - 5:00pm · Saturday 9:00am - 12:00pm · Sunday 1:00pm - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Szentháromság tér 2 · Castle District
🚶 3 min · 🚕 3 min → Fisherman's Bastion
3.Fisherman's Bastion
↳ Seven white fairy-tale towers built 1895–1902, each representing one of the Magyar tribes that conquered the Carpathian Basin in 896. The outer terrace offers Budapest's most famous panorama — the Parliament, Chain Bridge, and the full Pest skyline from the Buda bank.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
⚠️ Upper towers require a paid ticket during summer

1.New York Café
↳ Opened in 1894 in the New York Palace — one of the most extraordinary interiors in Europe. Gilded columns, frescoed ceilings, crimson drapes, and tiered marble galleries in continuous operation for 130 years. A reservation is essential; walk-in seating is very limited.

2.Great Synagogue
↳ The largest synagogue in Europe, seating 3000 worshippers. The Moorish Revival twin towers define the Jewish Quarter skyline. The complex includes the Jewish Museum, Holocaust Memorial Room, and the Raoul Wallenberg Memorial Garden — a weeping willow whose leaves bear victims' names.
📖 Wikipedia

3.Gellért Hill
↳ The 235-metre hill rising from the Danube is Budapest's finest panoramic viewpoint, topped by the Liberation Monument and the Citadella fortress. The 40-minute climb rewards with the most sweeping view of the city, both banks, and the full curve of the Danube.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Gellért-hegy · District I

4.Vigadó tér
↳ The ornate neo-Romantic concert hall rebuilt in 1865 by Frigyes Feszl is one of Budapest's grandest 19th-century façades. The riverside square is the start of the Danube Corso — the city's most elegant waterfront promenade, framing the Chain Bridge and Buda Castle.
📖 Wikipedia

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 18 min · 🚕 6 min → Margaret Island
1.Margaret Island
↳ A 2.5-kilometre car-free park island in the middle of the Danube — shaded promenades, a rose garden, medieval Dominican convent ruins, and a Japanese garden. At the southern tip a musical fountain performs hourly water-and-light shows, and the 1911 Art Nouveau water tower rises over the open-air theatre.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Margitsziget · District XIII
🚕 15 min → Aquincum
2.Aquincum
↳ The excavated capital of Roman Pannonia Inferior, once home to 40,000 people. Its ruin garden — among the largest Roman archaeological parks in Hungary — preserves paved streets, bathhouses, and a market square, and the museum holds mosaics, frescoes, and the reconstructed Aquincum water organ.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Aquincum Museum & Archaeological Park · 4.6⭐ · aquincum.hu
🏛️ Tuesday - Sunday 10:00am - 6:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Szentendrei út 135 · Óbuda
🚕 13 min → Gül Baba's Tomb
3.Gül Baba's Tomb
↳ The 16th-century octagonal türbe of Gül Baba, a Bektashi dervish who died in Buda in 1541 — the northernmost Islamic pilgrimage site in the world. Set in a hilltop rose garden on Rózsadomb, it centres on a quiet colonnaded courtyard with a wide view across the Danube to Pest.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Sunday 10:00am - 6:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~45 min

1.Eger Castle
↳ The hilltop fortress that held off a 40,000-strong Ottoman army in the 1552 Siege of Eger with barely 2,000 defenders. The Dobó István Castle Museum inside holds Gothic and Renaissance halls, casemate tunnels, and the ruins of the medieval cathedral, above views of the Baroque town.

2.Eger Minaret
↳ A 40-metre red-sandstone minaret from around 1596 — the northernmost surviving Ottoman minaret in the world. Its mosque was demolished in 1841, but the tower was spared; a tight spiral of 97 steps climbs to a balcony at 26 metres for a panorama over Eger's rooftops and the castle.
📖 Wikipedia

3.Dobó Square
↳ Eger's Baroque heart — a broad pedestrian plaza named for the castle's defender István Dobó, whose bronze statue commands the centre. The twin-towered Minorite Church, a curved-façade masterpiece by Kilián Ignác Dientzenhofer, dominates the eastern side, framed by the Town Hall and pastel merchant houses.

🍮 Local Tastes
Gulyás
↳ Hungary's national dish — a paprika-red beef and potato soup cooked with onions, caraway, and sweet paprika. Every restaurant in Budapest serves it; the test is the colour: deep red from real paprika, not tomato paste.
📍 Menza · Café Kör · Stand25 Bisztró
Halászlé
↳ The Danube fisherman's soup — a fiery paprika-red broth of freshwater fish and hot paprika. Two schools: Baja style with pasta, Szeged style strained smooth. Either version is exceptional.
📍 Halászkert · Dunacorso
Lángos
↳ Hungary's definitive street food: a disc of deep-fried dough served hot with sour cream and grated cheese, or garlic butter. Sold from stands at markets and near tourist sites. The Ferenciek tere area has reliable options.
📍 Ferenciek tere · Belváros
Dobos Torta
↳ Invented by József C. Dobos in 1884: thin sponge layers with chocolate buttercream, topped with a disc of hard caramel. Budapest's most iconic cake, available at every serious pastry shop.
📍 Gerbeaud · Café Central
Kürtőskalács
↳ Chimney cake — sweet dough wound on a spit, baked until golden, rolled in sugar and walnut or cinnamon. Crisp outside, soft inside. A Transylvanian tradition ubiquitous on Budapest streets; eat immediately.
📍 Váci utca · Belváros · Vörösmarty tér

✨ Claude Inspiration

Budapest is two cities that decided to share a river — and have been in productive argument ever since. Buda has the castles, the hill, the ancient defensive logic. Pest has the parliament, the boulevards, the cafés, the synagogue, the energy. The Chain Bridge between them is not just infrastructure; it is the thesis of the city. Every great day here crosses it at least once.

What this guide deliberately avoids — the thermal baths, the ruin bars, the river parties — are fine, but they flatten Budapest into a weekend-break destination. What it gives instead is the city that earned the name Pearl of the Danube: the Parliament at dawn seen from the Buda bank, the New York Café before the tour groups arrive, the Great Synagogue in the quiet of a weekday morning, the long walk down Andrássy Avenue from the Opera to Heroes' Square that passes through a century of architectural ambition in forty minutes on foot.

The Danube cruise on Day 3 evening is optional but worth it. The city looks different from the water — both banks at once, the illuminated Parliament reflected in the river, the Chain Bridge overhead. Budapest at night from the Danube is one of Europe's great sights, and it costs less than dinner.
