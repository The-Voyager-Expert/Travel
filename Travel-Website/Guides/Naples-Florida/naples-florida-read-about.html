A town built by and for people who already had everything — and how that made it genuinely strange.

The accidental paradise

Naples, Florida exists because a Kentucky newspaper publisher named Walter Haldeman visited the southwest coast of Florida in 1887 and decided it reminded him of the Bay of Naples in Italy. It did not, particularly. The bay here is actually a long shallow estuary, the climate is subtropical rather than Mediterranean, and there were no ancient ruins or Vesuvius looming over anything. But Haldeman was a man with money and a romantic streak, and he named the place Naples, and the name stuck.

What Haldeman had actually found was something more interesting than a Bay of Naples imitation: a strip of Gulf Coast so flat, so empty, and so spectacularly difficult to reach that it would remain essentially undeveloped for another seventy years. The Tamiami Trail — the road that finally connected this coast to Miami — wasn't completed until 1928, and even then Naples was a fishing village with a pier, a few tin-roofed houses, and an overwhelming quantity of mosquitoes. The millionaires didn't arrive in force until after World War II, when air conditioning made the summers survivable and someone figured out how to drain enough swamp to build golf courses.

Where the money went

The thing that makes Naples different from other wealthy Florida towns — from Palm Beach, from Boca Raton, from the gilded stretches of the Miami waterfront — is what the money built here. In Palm Beach, wealth built spectacle: mansions designed to be seen from the road, social seasons reported in the press, a culture of display that goes back to Henry Flagler and hasn't stopped. In Naples, the money went the other direction.

The mansions in Port Royal don't face the street. They face the water, behind walls, behind hedges, behind a deliberate architectural silence.

Port Royal, the neighborhood at the southern tip of the city where the most expensive homes sit, is a masterclass in invisible wealth. You can drive its streets and see almost nothing — hedges twelve feet high, gates with no nameplates, driveways that curve away into landscaping so dense it could be jungle. The houses behind those hedges are enormous, many of them worth tens of millions, but the aesthetic is refusal. The point is that you cannot see them. This is money that has decided it has nothing to prove, and the result is a neighborhood that looks, from the road, like an unusually well-maintained botanical garden.

Fifth Avenue South, Naples' downtown, carries the same DNA but translates it into something more accessible. The storefronts are tasteful. The restaurants are good and expensive. The galleries show work that is polished without being challenging. Nothing is cheap and nothing is loud and nothing, if you're paying attention, is accidental. The street was redesigned in the 1990s after decades of decline, and the redesign was done with the same philosophy that governs Port Royal: quality expressed through restraint.

The swamp next door

The most interesting thing about Naples is what sits immediately beside it. Drive twenty minutes east from Fifth Avenue and you're in the western Everglades — not a park in the manicured sense but an actual subtropical wilderness, one of the most biologically complex ecosystems in North America, and one that very nearly didn't survive the twentieth century.

The Everglades were systematically drained starting in the early 1900s. The Army Corps of Engineers channelized rivers, built levees, and rerouted water to make South Florida developable. It worked — the coasts boomed — but it also destroyed roughly half the original Everglades and brought the ecosystem to the edge of collapse. The Florida panther, which once ranged across the entire Southeast, was reduced to a population of about twenty animals, all of them in the swamps east and south of Naples. The wood stork colonies crashed. The water table dropped.

What you see when you visit Corkscrew Swamp Sanctuary or the Rookery Bay Reserve is what remains, and also what's coming back. The restoration effort — still ongoing, still underfunded, still fighting against development pressure — has been the largest environmental engineering project in American history. When you walk the boardwalk at Corkscrew through a stand of bald cypress trees that are seven hundred years old, you're in a forest that predates Columbus, surrounded by the sound of birds that almost weren't there anymore.

Naples is the only wealthy American town where the wilderness is not a backdrop. It is the thing the town was built on top of, and it keeps reminding you.

The beach that matters

Naples Pier is the most photographed thing in town, and it deserves it. The pier extends a thousand feet into the Gulf of Mexico, and the sunsets from the end of it are legitimately extraordinary — the Gulf faces due west here, and the horizon is flat water all the way to the sky. On any given evening there will be fifty or a hundred people at the end of the pier, watching the sun go down, and the mood is not performative tourism but something closer to a shared civic ritual. People who live here do this regularly. It doesn't get old.

The beach itself — the stretch of white sand that runs north and south from the pier — is what makes Naples' reputation. The sand is quartz, not coral, which means it stays white and doesn't heat up in the sun the way darker sand does. You can walk barefoot on it at noon in July without pain, which sounds like a small thing until you've burned your feet on a beach in the Carolinas or the Mediterranean. The water is calm, warm, and clear in a way that the Atlantic side of Florida can't match. Naples Beach is a Gulf beach, and the Gulf here is essentially a bathtub — shallow, protected, and body-temperature from May through October.

The arts nobody expected

Naples has a cultural infrastructure that doesn't match its size. The town's permanent population is around twenty thousand, but it supports a philharmonic, a professional repertory theater, a ballet company, and enough galleries to fill a midsize city. This is what happens when a community is built by retirees who had careers in New York, Chicago, and Boston: they bring their cultural expectations with them, and they have the money and time to fund institutions.

The Artis-Naples campus — which houses both the Baker Museum and the Naples Philharmonic — is the most visible expression of this. The philharmonic plays a full season, the museum rotates serious exhibitions, and the campus itself is the kind of glass-and-steel cultural building that usually belongs to a city twenty times Naples' size. It exists because enough people wrote enough checks, and because the alternative — retiring to a place with no orchestra — was apparently unacceptable.

The result is a town that has better classical music than most American cities, better theater than anywhere else in Florida outside Miami, and an art market that sustains dozens of working galleries. Whether this constitutes genuine cultural life or a very expensive form of recreation is a question Naples residents argue about, usually over good wine.

The quiet part

What you won't find in Naples is edge. There is no nightlife to speak of. The restaurant scene is excellent but not experimental — you eat extremely well here, but nobody is reinventing anything. The architecture is uniformly handsome and uniformly safe. The politics are conservative in the way that enormous concentrations of private wealth tend to produce. The town is overwhelmingly white and overwhelmingly older, and it does not pretend otherwise.

None of this is a criticism exactly. Naples is a town that has decided what it wants to be and has executed that vision with remarkable consistency. The streets are clean, the landscaping is immaculate, the service everywhere is professional, the beach is perfect. If what you want from a place is beauty without friction, Naples delivers that better than almost anywhere in America.

But walk the boardwalk at Corkscrew at dawn, when the cypress trees are breathing fog and an anhinga is drying its wings on a dead branch and the silence is so deep you can hear your own heartbeat — and you'll understand that the real Naples isn't the one on Fifth Avenue. The real Naples is the swamp that was here first, that is still here, and that will be here long after the hedges in Port Royal have gone to seed. The town had the good fortune to be built next to something genuinely wild, and the good sense — eventually, after decades of not doing so — to protect it.

That's the thing to carry with you. Naples is a beautiful place because of the mangroves, not despite them.
