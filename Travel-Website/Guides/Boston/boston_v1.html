Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 15 min · 🚕 5 min → Boston Common & Public Garden
1. Boston Common & Public Garden
↳ The oldest public park in the United States — 50 acres of lawns, the Frog Pond skating rink, and Swan Boats gliding through the willow-lined lagoon of the adjacent Public Garden. The two green spaces form the living heart of the city and the southern gateway to the Freedom Trail.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 4 Charles St · Downtown
🚶 7 min · 🚕 3 min → Beacon Hill
2. Beacon Hill
↳ The Federal-era neighbourhood of red-brick rowhouses, gas-lit cobblestone lanes, and iron boot scrapers polished by residents. Acorn Street — the most photographed street in New England — is one block of 19th-century city life compressed into a single frame: steep cobbles, purple-paned windows, and black shutters.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Acorn St · Beacon Hill
🚶 18 min · 🚕 5 min → Old North Church
3. Old North Church
↳ The oldest surviving church in the city and the most famous in America — the steeple from which sexton Robert Newman hung two lanterns, signalling Paul Revere that the British were crossing the Charles by sea. The interior still holds the original box pews numbered by family.
📖 Wikipedia

4. USS Constitution
↳ "Old Ironsides" — the oldest commissioned warship still afloat, undefeated in 42 sea battles. British cannonballs bounced off her oak hull. Sailors in dress uniform lead the free tour of the gun decks and captain's quarters; the adjacent museum covers her construction and three wars at sea.

2. Fenway Park
↳ The oldest Major League Baseball park still in use. The guided tour leads through the press box, the Red Sox dugout, and onto the field itself, then up to the top of the 37-foot Green Monster left-field wall — the most iconic structure in all of American sport.

3. View Boston
↳ The city's only observation deck, on floors 50–52 of the Prudential Tower, 800 feet above the city. On clear days the view sweeps from the Blue Hills to the south, over the harbour to the airport, and north to New Hampshire. The sky terrace on the 52nd floor is the highest open-air vantage point in New England.

4. Trinity Church
↳ The Romanesque masterwork of Henry Hobson Richardson, ranked the most important building in American architecture in the American Institute of Architects' definitive survey. Its reflection in the face of the Hancock Tower directly across Copley Square is one of the great urban compositions in American cities.

🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 8 min · 🚕 4 min → Faneuil Hall
1. Faneuil Hall
↳ The colonial meeting house where Samuel Adams rallied the town against British taxation — the Cradle of Liberty. The Great Hall retains its original wood-beamed interior and a painted portrait of Daniel Webster mid-speech; admission is free. Quincy Market rotunda wraps the block outside.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
🆓 Free
📍 4 South Market St · Downtown
🚶 10 min · 🚕 4 min → Paul Revere House
2. Paul Revere House
↳ The oldest surviving structure in downtown Boston — a timber frame house built around 1680, home to Paul Revere from 1770 to 1800. Revere left from here on April 18, 1775, for his midnight ride. The rooms hold period objects including Revere family silver; the courtyard holds his original signal lanterns.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Harvard Yard
↳ The 22-acre core of the oldest university in the United States, founded in 1636. Massachusetts Hall — built 1720 — is still in use. The John Harvard statue is a triple fiction: a sculptor's idealised face, the wrong John Harvard, and the wrong founding date — yet it remains the most photographed object on campus.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7

🚕 22 min · 🚇 40 min → Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum
4. Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum
↳ A Venetian palazzo built in 1903 housing Isabella Stewart Gardner's personal collection — Titian, Rembrandt, Sargent, and Vermeer arranged exactly as her will specifies, which forbids any rearrangement. Thirteen works were stolen in the unsolved 1990 art heist; the empty frames still hang where the paintings were.
📖 Wikipedia

🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 5 min · 🚕 4 min → Old State House
1. Old State House
↳ Boston's oldest surviving public building, completed 1713. On March 5, 1770, soldiers fired on civilians below the balcony — the Boston Massacre. On July 18, 1776, the Declaration of Independence was first read publicly in Massachusetts from that balcony to a crowd on State Street.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Old State House — revolutionaryspaces.org
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 206 Washington St · Downtown
🚶 3 min · 🚕 3 min → Old South Meeting House
2. Old South Meeting House
↳ The 1729 meeting hall where 5,000 Bostonians gathered on December 16, 1773, and voted to return three tea ships to Britain. When Governor Hutchinson refused, Samuel Adams gave the signal from this pulpit — the Boston Tea Party began. The hall's original box pews and galleries survive intact.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Old South Meeting House — revolutionaryspaces.org
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~45 min
📍 310 Washington St · Downtown
🚶 9 min · 🚕 5 min → Massachusetts State House
3. Massachusetts State House
↳ Charles Bulfinch's 1798 Federal capitol. The gold dome was sheathed in copper by Paul Revere's company in 1802, then gilded in 1874. A pine cone sits atop — symbol of the Maine timber trade when Maine was still a Massachusetts district. Free tours cover the Hall of Flags and Senate Chamber.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Monday - Friday 8:45am - 5:00pm
🚫 Closed Saturday & Sunday

🚶 16 min · 🚕 7 min → Boston Public Library
4. Boston Public Library
↳ The first large free municipal library in the United States, founded 1848. The McKim building houses Bates Hall — a 218-foot barrel-vaulted reading room open to anyone — and John Singer Sargent's mural cycle painted over 28 years. The courtyard is modeled on the Palazzo della Cancelleria in Rome.

🚉 ARRIVE Salem: 🚶 11 min · 🚕 5 min → Peabody Essex Museum
1. Peabody Essex Museum
↳ Founded 1799 as the East India Marine Society — sea captains pooling objects from every port they visited. Now a world-class art museum with 1.8 million objects. Its centerpiece: Yin Yu Tang, a 200-year-old Chinese merchant house dismantled in Anhui Province and reassembled inside, 16 rooms intact.
📖 Wikipedia

🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → Witch House
2. Witch House
↳ The only surviving Salem structure with direct ties to the 1692 witch trials. Judge Jonathan Corwin lived here and presided at the Court of Oyer and Terminer, which sent 19 people to the gallows. The accused were brought here for pretrial examinations. The 17th-century house is preserved by the City of Salem.

✨ Claude Inspiration

What makes this city remarkable in the English-speaking world is that it has kept its revolutionary geography almost intact. The 2.5-mile Freedom Trail threads through sites that are not reconstructions or museums of themselves, but the actual buildings where the events happened: the Old South Meeting House where Samuel Adams called the Tea Party; the Old State House from whose balcony the Declaration of Independence was first read aloud in Massachusetts; the Granary Burying Ground where Paul Revere, John Hancock, and Samuel Adams are still buried under the same headstones visitors can touch.

The USS Constitution's status struck me most in research. She is not a restored ship. She is not a replica. She is the same vessel, with the same naval commission, that last fired her guns in anger more than two centuries ago — and she is still crewed by active-duty US Navy sailors who live on board. The world's oldest commissioned warship, still in service. The sailors give you the tour.

The pairing of Day 2 surprised me too. The Museum of Fine Arts has one of the world's great Impressionist collections — and is a short walk from Fenway Park. Within 90 minutes you can go from standing in front of a Monet haystack to standing in the Red Sox dugout and looking up at the Green Monster. No other American city puts those two things so close together.
