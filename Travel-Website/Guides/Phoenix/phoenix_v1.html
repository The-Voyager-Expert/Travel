Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 7 min → Heard Museum
1. Heard Museum
↳ Founded in 1929, the Heard holds 40,000+ objects documenting American Indian art from the Southwest. Twelve galleries include the 437-piece Barry Goldwater Hopi kachina collection and the American Indian Veterans National Memorial. Free guided tours run daily at 10:30am, noon, 1pm, and 2:30pm.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 4:00pm
⏰ ~2 hrs
📍 2301 N Central Ave
🚕 12 min → Desert Botanical Garden
2. Desert Botanical Garden
↳ Set in Papago Park since 1939, the garden holds 50,000+ plants across 4,000 taxa — one of the world's largest desert collections. A third are native; 139 species are rare or threatened. Five trails wind through towering saguaros and Dale Chihuly glass sculptures. Arrive before 9am.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 7:00am - 8:00pm
⏰ ~2 hrs
📍 1201 N Galvin Pkwy · Papago Park
🚕 14 min → Camelback Mountain – Echo Canyon Trailhead
3. Camelback Mountain – Echo Canyon Trail
↳ Tallest peak within the city at 2,704 feet. The Echo Canyon Trail is 2.6 miles out-and-back with 1,522 feet of gain on Precambrian red sandstone, requiring steel handrails near the summit. Fully exposed, no shade. The 360° summit panorama covers the entire Valley of the Sun.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2.5 hrs
📍 4925 E McDonald Dr · Echo Canyon Trailhead
🚕 20 min → Musical Instrument Museum
4. Musical Instrument Museum
↳ The largest musical instrument museum in the world, opened April 2010. Five geographic galleries display 6,800+ instruments from nearly 200 countries. Wireless headphones activate at each exhibit, playing live video of local musicians. The Experience Gallery lets visitors play instruments from around the globe.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~2 hrs
📍 4725 E Mayo Blvd · Desert Ridge
🚕 22 min → hotel

🍮 Local Tastes
Sonoran Hot Dog
↳ A bacon-wrapped hot dog in a bolillo bun topped with pinto beans, tomato, onion, mayonnaise, mustard, and cotija cheese. Found at neighborhood stands on the west side of the city.
📍 La Pasadita · West Side
Prickly Pear
↳ The magenta fruit of the Opuntia cactus, harvested throughout the Sonoran Desert. Appears on menus as lemonade, margaritas, sorbets, and glazes. Sweet with a mild watermelon-like tartness.
📍 Desert Botanical Garden
Chiltepin Chile
↳ A tiny wild chile native to the Sonoran Desert. Arizona is the only US state with a wild native chile. Sharply hot with a quick fade; used as a finishing spice in salsas and on cocktail rims.
📍 Barrio Café · Grand Avenue
Mesquite-Grilled Meats
↳ Mesquite wood is endemic to the Sonoran Desert. Its smoke is dense and slightly sweet, distinct from oak or hickory. Used for carne asada and whole chicken at local taquerías and restaurants.
