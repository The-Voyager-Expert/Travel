Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 4 min · 🚕 2 min → Palacio Real
1.Palacio Real
↳ The largest functioning royal palace in Western Europe — 3,418 rooms, 50 open to visitors. The Throne Room with its Tiepolo ceiling, the Royal Armoury, the Royal Pharmacy, and halls of Flemish tapestries and portraits by Velázquez and Goya.

🚶 2 min · 🚕 2 min → Galería de las Colecciones Reales
2.Galería de las Colecciones Reales
↳ A 40,000 m² underground gallery below the Royal Palace. Eight centuries of Spanish royal collections — Charles V's armour, Philip II's tapestries, royal carriages, Velázquez and Caravaggio canvases — descending past the stone foundations of the medieval Alcázar.

🚶 3 min · 🚕 2 min → Almudena Cathedral
3.Almudena Cathedral
↳ Madrid's cathedral took over a century to build, consecrated by Pope John Paul II. The neo-Gothic exterior faces the Royal Palace; inside, the Gothic crypt and the brightly frescoed main nave make an unlikely combination. The paid dome climb gives city views over the Royal Palace gardens.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 8:30pm
⏰ ~45 min
🆓 Free
📍 Plaza de la Almudena · Centro
🚶 13 min · 🚕 5 min → Plaza Mayor
4.Plaza Mayor
↳ Madrid's great ceremonial square — 129 × 94 metres of uniform Habsburg arcades, completed in the early 17th century. Built for bullfights and royal proclamations; nine gateway arches open onto it. The equestrian statue of Philip III at the centre is by Giambologna and Pietro Tacca.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min

🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 22 min · 🚕 10 min → Prado Museum
1.Prado Museum
↳ Spain's national painting museum — Velázquez's Las Meninas, Goya's Black Paintings, Bosch's Garden of Earthly Delights, and 8,000 works spanning five centuries. The most essential visit in Madrid. Three hours minimum; four is more comfortable.
📖 Wikipedia

🚶 8 min · 🚕 3 min → Thyssen-Bornemisza
2.Thyssen-Bornemisza
↳ The former private collection of the Thyssen-Bornemisza dynasty — 1,000 works from Van Eyck to Basquiat, Caravaggio to Hopper. Where the Prado goes deep into the Spanish masters, Thyssen goes wide across seven centuries of European painting in a single neoclassical palace.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 28 min · 🚕 10 min → Reina Sofía
1.Reina Sofía
↳ Spain's national museum of 20th-century art and the permanent home of Picasso's Guernica — the vast canvas depicting the bombing of a Basque town. Beyond Guernica: major works by Miró, Dalí, and the Spanish Surrealists in a converted 18th-century hospital.

🚕 12 min → Temple of Debod
2.Temple of Debod
↳ A 2nd-century BCE Egyptian temple given to Spain by Egypt after the Nubian monuments rescue campaign, dismantled from the Nile and rebuilt stone by stone in Parque del Oeste. Standing in a shallow reflecting pool facing the Sierra de Guadarrama, it makes the finest sunset viewpoint in the city.
📖 Wikipedia

🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 14 min · 🚕 6 min → Círculo de Bellas Artes
1.Círculo de Bellas Artes
↳ The 1926 Beaux-Arts cultural palace on Gran Vía and Alcalá, with one of Madrid's finest rooftop terraces seven floors up. The Azotea gives 360° views: the Gran Vía canyon, the Metrópolis building, the dome of the Cibeles palace, and the Sierra on clear days.

2.CentroCentro Mirador
↳ The Palacio de Cibeles — Madrid's city hall — is a Beaux-Arts steel and granite structure built in the early 20th century. The Mirador observation deck in the central tower gives 360° views over Retiro, the Paseo del Prado corridor, the Almudena dome, and north to Guadarrama.

3.Retiro Park
↳ 125 hectares of parkland opened to the public in the 19th century and UNESCO-listed as part of the Paseo del Prado corridor. The Estanque Grande lake, framed by the colonnaded Alfonso XII monument, is where Madrileños rent rowboats on Sunday mornings. The Palacio de Cristal is currently closed for renovation.

1.Cathedral of Toledo
↳ The primatial cathedral of Spain, begun in the 13th century on the site of the city's main mosque. Inside: El Greco's Disrobing of Christ and the extraordinary Transparente altarpiece, lit by a hidden skylight punched through the vault. Stands in the same rank as Cologne and Chartres.

2.Alcázar of Toledo
↳ The stone fortress dominating Toledo's highest point — Roman origins, rebuilt as an imperial palace by Charles V, destroyed and rebuilt in the Civil War. Now Spain's Army Museum, with the panoramic terrace over the Tagus gorge as the main reason to visit.

↳ A medieval church that exists for one reason: El Greco's The Burial of the Count of Orgaz, painted in the 16th century and still in the apse where it was commissioned. The 15-foot canvas is a masterpiece — densely observed nobility below, a visionary celestial scene above.

❗️ Heads Up
❗️ Palacio Real — State Ceremony Closures
↳ Closes without notice for state ceremonies — even with a confirmed timed ticket. Check the official website the morning of your visit.
Workaround: Book with free cancellation so you can shift. Galería de las Colecciones Reales next door is the strong backup.
❗️ Reina Sofía — Closed Tuesday
↳ Closed every Tuesday — an unusual closure for a museum at this international level.
Workaround: Plan for Monday or Wednesday through Sunday. Wednesday and Saturday mornings are least crowded for the Guernica room.
❗️ Temple of Debod — Interior Cap
↳ Only 30 visitors inside at once. Walk-up queues fill quickly on summer weekends. Exterior and reflecting pools are always accessible.
Workaround: Reserve a timed slot through the city's official reservations page before your trip. Free and takes under two minutes.
🏛️ The Weight of Layers

The Galería de las Colecciones Reales is the strangest museum in Madrid — not for what it contains, but for where it is. You enter from street level next to the Palacio Real and descend five floors into the bedrock beneath the city, past the stone foundations of the original medieval Alcázar wall, into a vast underground space that runs beneath three successive buildings constructed on the same ground over a thousand years. The experience of moving between Baroque tapestries and medieval stone — under three millennia of successive construction — is unlike anything I have encountered in a European museum.

And then, the Temple of Debod: an Egyptian temple from the second century BCE disassembled stone by stone from the Nile bank, shipped to Spain, and rebuilt on a hill overlooking a modern European capital — because Spain helped UNESCO save Nubian monuments from rising dam waters. Both facts are simultaneously true. Standing between them at sunset — the Sierra de Guadarrama orange behind the temple, the city spread below — the mind does something it rarely does at European monuments: it genuinely doesn't know what time it is.
