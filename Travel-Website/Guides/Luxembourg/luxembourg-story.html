A fortress city that outlasted every army sent to take it, then quietly became the richest country in Europe by hosting the institutions designed to make armies unnecessary.

The Gibraltar of the North

Luxembourg City exists because of a rock. In 963 AD, Count Siegfried of Ardennes acquired a small Roman fortification called Lucilinburhuc — "little fortress" — perched on the Bock promontory above the Alzette River. The promontory was a natural strongpoint: sheer sandstone cliffs on three sides, a river valley below, and a commanding view of every approach for miles. Siegfried built a castle on it. Over the next nine centuries, every major power in Europe would try to take it from his successors.

The Spanish came first, then the French, then the Austrians, then the French again, then the Prussians. Each occupier dug deeper into the rock. The Bock Casemates — seventeen miles of tunnels and galleries carved into the cliff face — were built in layers by successive armies, each one extending the network left by the last. By the eighteenth century the fortress was considered one of the strongest in Europe, and the city had earned the name that stuck: the Gibraltar of the North.

What you walk through today is what survived demolition. When the Treaty of London granted Luxembourg independence in 1867, one of the conditions was that the fortress had to be dismantled. The great powers could not agree on who should control it, so they agreed that no one would. The demolition took sixteen years. Even so, the engineers could not destroy all of it — the tunnels went too deep into the rock, and some of the fortifications were load-bearing for the city above. The casemates that remain are the portions that were literally too embedded in Luxembourg to remove.

The gorge

The feature that made Luxembourg defensible is the same feature that makes it beautiful. The Alzette and Pétrusse rivers carved deep valleys through the sandstone plateau, splitting the city into distinct levels. The upper town — the Ville Haute — sits on the plateau where the original fortress stood, and this is where the government buildings, the cathedral, and the Grand Ducal Palace are. The lower town — the Grund — lies at the bottom of the gorge, pressed against the river, with its own churches and houses and a quality of light that differs from the plateau above because the cliff walls filter the afternoon sun differently.

The Chemin de la Corniche runs along the edge of the cliff between the two levels. It has been called Europe's most beautiful balcony, which is the sort of claim that invites skepticism until you actually walk it. The path follows the old fortification wall, and from it you look down into the Grund — the abbey, the river, the houses with their slate roofs — and across to the Kirchberg plateau where the glass towers of the European institutions rise. The view holds the entire history of the city in a single frame: the medieval, the pastoral, and the institutional, separated by geology.

The elevator from the Ville Haute to the Grund drops you sixty meters and six centuries in twenty-three seconds.

There is a public elevator — the Pfaffenthal Panoramic Elevator — that connects the upper and lower towns. It is glass-walled and free. The ride takes twenty-three seconds and covers a vertical drop of roughly sixty meters. It is, in its way, a perfect piece of Luxembourg engineering: practical, quiet, and designed to solve a problem that has existed for a thousand years without anyone making too much fuss about it.

The money

Luxembourg is the second-richest country on earth by GDP per capita, and the wealth is not abstract. You see it in the condition of the streets, in the public transit system that the country made entirely free in 2020, in the quality of the restaurants, and in the Kirchberg plateau — the district northeast of the old town where the European Investment Bank, the European Court of Justice, and the European Court of Auditors have their headquarters. The buildings are enormous and sleek and made of the kind of glass and steel that communicates institutional confidence.

The numbers behind the wealth are genuinely strange. Luxembourg has a resident population of roughly 660,000 — about the size of Portland, Oregon. But approximately 200,000 people commute into the country every day from France, Belgium, and Germany to work. The population does not quite triple during business hours, but it comes close. The country has more jobs than residents. The cross-border commuters drive on three-lane highways that were built to handle traffic volumes that would be ambitious for a country ten times the size.

The financial sector accounts for much of this. Luxembourg is the largest investment fund center in Europe and the second-largest in the world after the United States. It became this by being small, stable, multilingual, and strategically willing to write regulatory frameworks that attracted capital. Whether this constitutes shrewd governance or something less flattering depends on whom you ask. What is not in dispute is that it worked.

The languages

Luxembourg has three official languages: Luxembourgish, French, and German. In practice, most Luxembourgers speak all three plus English, and many speak Portuguese as well — the country has a large Portuguese community dating to labor migration in the 1960s and 1970s. Road signs are in French. Laws are written in French. Schools teach in German in the early years and transition to French later. Luxembourgish — a Franconian dialect that became a written language only in the twentieth century — is the language of daily life, of family, of parliament, and increasingly of national identity.

This is genuinely different from how multilingualism works elsewhere in Europe. Belgium has three language communities that maintain careful separation and occasionally threaten to split the country. Switzerland has four linguistic regions that operate with significant autonomy. Luxembourg is not divided by language — it is united by polyglotism. A single person switches between languages depending on context, and no one finds this remarkable. The European institutions chose Luxembourg in part because this was already how the city operated.

The size of the place

The entire country is smaller than Rhode Island. You can drive from the northern border to the southern border in under an hour. The Mullerthal region — called Little Switzerland for its narrow gorges and moss-covered rock formations — is a thirty-minute drive from the capital. Vianden Castle, one of the finest medieval castles in Europe, is an hour north. The Moselle wine region, where Luxembourg produces its small quantities of Riesling and Crémant, runs along the eastern border with Germany.

The smallness is not a limitation. It is the point. Luxembourg works because it is small enough to govern well, small enough that the institutions know each other, small enough that the entire country functions with a kind of administrative intimacy that larger nations cannot achieve. The tradeoff is that nothing here is anonymous. Everyone is someone's colleague, neighbor, or classmate. The Grand Duke walks through the old town. The prime minister lives in a normal house. The country operates at a human scale that most of its European neighbors abandoned generations ago.

Occupations and independence

Luxembourg has been independent since 1839, but this fact understates the contingency of the country's existence. Before independence, the territory was ruled by Burgundians, Habsburgs, Spanish Habsburgs, French revolutionaries, the Dutch, and the Prussians, in roughly that order. The Congress of Vienna in 1815 made it a Grand Duchy in personal union with the Netherlands. The Treaty of London in 1839 split it — the western, French-speaking half became the Belgian province of Luxembourg; the eastern half became the independent country.

The twentieth century tested the independence twice. Germany occupied Luxembourg in both World Wars. In the second occupation, the Nazis attempted to annex the country outright, imposing German as the sole language and conscripting Luxembourgish men into the Wehrmacht. The forced conscription — the Zwangsrekrutéiert — sent roughly 12,000 young men to the Eastern Front, of whom more than 3,000 did not return. The memory is not distant. There are families in Luxembourg today whose grandfathers were forced to fight for an army that was occupying their country.

Luxembourg exists because the great powers couldn't agree on who should have it — and then it got rich making sure the question would never be asked again.

The General Strike of 1942, when Luxembourgers refused to comply with German conscription orders, is one of the defining moments in the national story. The Nazis responded with executions. The country remembers this every year. It is the sort of history that a small nation holds very close, because when you are the size of Rhode Island and your neighbor has an army of millions, resistance is not a gesture — it is an existential act.

What the fortress became

Luxembourg is the answer to a question that the twentieth century spent decades trying to resolve: what do you do with a fortress when the wars are over? The answer, it turns out, is that you fill it with the institutions designed to prevent the next one. The European Coal and Steel Community — the direct ancestor of the European Union — established its headquarters in Luxembourg in 1952. The choice was deliberate. Luxembourg was small enough to be unthreatening, central enough to be convenient, and multilingual enough to function. The country that had been fought over for nine centuries became the administrative seat of the project to make fighting obsolete.

The Kirchberg plateau, where the EU institutions now stand, was farmland within living memory. The transformation is complete. Glass and steel where there were once fields, and behind the glass, the bureaucratic machinery of a continental peace that has now lasted longer than any in European history. The casemates below the old town are a museum. The fortification walls are a walking path. The garrison buildings are restaurants.

Walk the Corniche in the late afternoon, when the light comes in low across the Alzette valley. Below you, the Grund. Above you, the plateau. Ahead, if you look northeast, the Kirchberg towers catching the sun. The city was built to withstand siege after siege. Now it hosts the institutions designed to make sieges impossible. And it has gotten extraordinarily wealthy doing it. That is the Luxembourg story — a fortress that found a better use for itself.
