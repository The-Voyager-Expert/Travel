Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 1 min · 🚕 2 min → St. Pierre Cathedral
1. St. Pierre Cathedral
↳ Geneva's Old Town crowns out at this part-Romanesque, part-Gothic cathedral where John Calvin preached the Reformation. Climb the 157 steps of the north tower for a sweeping panorama over the rooftops, the lake and the Jet d'Eau, then drop into the vast archaeological site beneath the nave.

2. Place du Bourg-de-Four
↳ Geneva's oldest square sits on a former Roman marketplace at the heart of the Old Town. Ringed by pastel townhouses, an 18th-century fountain and café terraces spilling across the cobbles, it's the natural place to pause between the cathedral and the Bastions.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7

🚶 6 min · 🚕 4 min → Reformation Wall
3. Reformation Wall
↳ Set against the old city ramparts in Parc des Bastions, this 100-metre monument honours the Protestant Reformation with giant statues of Calvin, Farel, Bèze and Knox. The leafy park behind it has shaded lawns and Geneva's famous giant outdoor chess boards.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Promenade des Bastions 1 · Les Bastions
🚶 12 min · 🚕 6 min → Jardin Anglais
4. Jardin Anglais
↳ This lakefront garden at the foot of the Old Town is best known for the Flower Clock — some 6500 plants forming a working timepiece with the world's longest second hand. A wrought-iron bandstand, fountain and the lake's edge make it a classic Geneva stroll.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Quai du Général-Guisan · Rive
🚶 8 min · 🚕 5 min → Jet d'Eau
5. Jet d'Eau
↳ Geneva's signature landmark blasts a column of water 140 metres into the sky from a pier on the lake — visible from across the city. Walk out along the stone jetty for the full roar and spray, and a rainbow on sunny days.

1. CERN
↳ The world's largest particle-physics laboratory, home to the Large Hadron Collider, sits on the city's edge in Meyrin. Its free Science Gateway visitor centre — Renzo Piano-designed tubes beside the landmark Globe of Science and Innovation — runs hands-on exhibitions on the building blocks of the universe.

2. Palais des Nations
↳ The European headquarters of the United Nations occupies the vast inter-war Palais in Ariana Park, where it took over from the League of Nations. Guided tours walk you through the grand Assembly Hall and council chambers ringed by member-state flags, with lakeside lawns roamed by peacocks.

3. Broken Chair
↳ A 12-metre wooden chair with one shattered leg towers over Place des Nations, opposite the UN. Daniel Berset's sculpture stands as a global protest against landmines and cluster bombs — one of Geneva's most photographed and most pointed works of art.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~15 min

4. Geneva Botanical Garden
↳ Twenty-eight hectares of gardens sweep down toward the lake just north of the UN — rock gardens, an arboretum, glasshouses and a small animal park. Free to enter, it's a quiet green counterpoint to the international quarter.

Lausanne · 38 min

Montreux · 1 hr 3 min
↳ The Swiss Riviera resort on the lake's eastern end, with palm-lined promenades and the lakeside Château de Chillon a short ride beyond — one of Europe's best-preserved medieval castles.
🎫 book at: SBB or Omio
Annecy · France · 1h 30 min
↳ A French Alpine town on a glacial lake so clear it is simply called the Venice of the Alps — a medieval canal quarter, a lakeside château, pedalos and paragliders overhead, and one of the most scenic waterfronts in France. SNCF TER trains cross the border direct.
🎫 book at: sncf-connect.com or Omio
Bern · 1 hr 51 min
↳ Switzerland's capital, a UNESCO-listed medieval Old Town wrapped in a bend of the Aare — six kilometres of sandstone arcades, the Zytglogge clock tower and the Bear Park.
🎫 book at: SBB or Omio
Lyon · France · 2h
↳ France's gastronomic capital — bouchon bistros, the UNESCO traboules network of Renaissance covered passages, the Vieux-Lyon old town, and Fourvière basilica above the Saône. TGV Lyria and SNCF TER both make the cross-border run.
🎫 book at: sncf-connect.com or Omio

❗️ Heads Up
❗️ Jet d'Eau — Maintenance shutdown
↳ The fountain shuts for maintenance for about a month each November, and the pier closes whenever the jet isn't running.
Workaround: Check sig-ge.ch before walking out; the lakefront view of the spot works any time.
❗️ Palais des Nations — Passport required
↳ Every visitor shows a valid passport — Schengen ID also accepted — at the Pregny gate, and the one-hour tours sell out.
Workaround: Book online at ungeneva.org and arrive 30 minutes early with ID.
❗️ CERN — Free but timed · closed Mondays
↳ Science Gateway admission is free but needs advance registration, and the whole site is closed every Monday.
Workaround: Reserve a slot at visit.cern and plan CERN for Tuesday to Sunday; tram 18 runs to the door.
🕰️ Geneva Keeps Time

What struck me building this is how relentlessly Geneva is a city about measuring things. The Flower Clock in the Jardin Anglais isn't just decoration — it's a civic monument to the watchmakers who made the city rich, ticking in living petals beside the lake. A ten-minute walk away, the Jet d'Eau began life as a pressure-relief valve for the watchmakers' workshops before it became a spectacle. The same instinct for precision runs from the artisan benches of the Old Town all the way out to CERN, where physicists measure particles in fractions of a nanosecond.

There's a quieter thread connecting the two days, too. The Reformation Wall and St. Pierre's pulpit mark Geneva as a city that took ideas seriously enough to remake itself around them five centuries ago; later, the Palais des Nations and the Broken Chair show the same impulse pointed outward — Geneva as the place where the world comes to argue toward something better. Old Town on Day 1, the world's business on Day 2: a remarkably small city for how much it has tried to keep time on the rest of us.
