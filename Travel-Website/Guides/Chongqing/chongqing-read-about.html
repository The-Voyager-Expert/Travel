The most populous city you've never thought about — a mountain metropolis at the confluence of two rivers, where geography forced a civilization to build differently, and where the fog saved China from Japan.

A city that geography made impossible, then made inevitable

Chongqing sits at the junction of the Yangtze and Jialing rivers in a canyon of mountains so steep that the city's neighborhoods are stacked vertically rather than spread horizontally. There are elevators inside buildings that open onto streets at different floors. There is a metro line — Line 2, the Liziba station — that passes directly through the middle of a residential tower between the sixth and eighth floors: the train runs through the living room of the building. This is not an engineering stunt. It is what you do when there is no flat land.

The population of Chongqing municipality is around 32 million, making it one of the most populous administrative units on earth, though the urban core is smaller than that figure suggests. The municipality encompasses a vast area of mountains and valleys; the actual dense city is something like the size of a large European capital compressed into terrain that would defeat most urban planning. The result is a skyline of extraordinary verticality — towers jammed onto ridgelines, bridges spanning gorges between neighborhoods, cable cars linking hillsides that would otherwise require an hour's drive to connect.

Most Western visitors come on their way to or from a Three Gorges cruise, see a few hours of neon-lit night markets, and leave thinking Chongqing is a generic Chinese megacity. This is an error that the city's geography alone should correct: there is no other city on earth that looks like this, and the cityscape is not accidental. It is the result of a specific set of rivers and mountains and a civilization that had to figure out how to live in them.

Why the fog matters more than you think

Chongqing's nickname is the "Fog City" — Wùdū in Chinese. The fog is real: the basin geography traps moisture from the rivers, and the city spends a significant portion of the year under low cloud or actual fog. In winter, you may go days without seeing the sun. This atmospheric condition shaped the city's history in ways that are still visible.

In 1938, after Japan had captured Beijing, Shanghai, and Nanjing and driven the Nationalist government of Chiang Kai-shek deep into the interior, the Republic of China relocated its wartime capital to Chongqing. The choice was partly strategic — the gorge terrain made overland invasion extremely difficult — and partly meteorological. Japanese bombing runs relied on clear visibility. Chongqing's legendary fog kept the bombers away for months at a time, and even when they came, the smoky haze and mountain terrain made accurate targeting nearly impossible.

Chongqing held. China held. The fog bought the time that the rest of the war required.

The city was nevertheless bombed heavily during 1940 and 1941 — one of the longest strategic bombing campaigns in the Second World War, largely unknown in the West. Incendiary bombs were particularly effective on the dense wooden structures of the old city. The tunnels that residents dug into the sandstone cliffs as bomb shelters still exist; some are now used as wine cellars, storage, or underground restaurants. You can eat hot pot in a tunnel that sheltered families from Japanese incendiaries in 1941. Nobody makes much of this. It is simply where the restaurant is.

The rivers, the gorges, and what was drowned

The Yangtze that flows through Chongqing is not the docile tourist river of postcards. Upstream of the city, particularly through the Three Gorges — the Qutang, Wu, and Xiling gorges stretching east toward Hubei province — it is a force that carved some of the most dramatic terrain in Asia. The gorge walls rise hundreds of meters above the water. For centuries, wooden tracking boats were hauled upstream through the rapids by teams of men — "trackers" — pulling ropes along paths cut into the cliff faces. The image is in every nineteenth-century account of travel in China: the ropes taut, the men bent horizontal, the roar of the water below.

The Three Gorges Dam, completed in 2006, changed the river permanently. The reservoir behind the dam raised the water level in the gorges by up to 175 meters. More than a million people were relocated as towns and villages disappeared under the rising water. The famous hanging coffins of the Ba people — wooden caskets wedged into cliff faces by a Bronze Age culture that believed the higher you were buried the closer you were to heaven — are now largely submerged or inaccessible. Cities that had stood for centuries went under. The gorges themselves are still spectacular, but what you see from a cruise ship is not what travelers saw before 2003. That version of the river is gone.

The White Crane Ridge — an underwater rock formation inscribed with water-level records dating back to the Tang dynasty, the longest hydrological record in Chinese history — was preserved by encasing it in a pressurized underwater museum below the surface of the reservoir. You can visit it by descending through a shaft and peering through portholes at the inscriptions on the submerged ridge. The museum is extraordinary and melancholy in equal measure.

The heat, the spice, and why they belong together

Chongqing hotpot is not the same as Sichuan hotpot, though outsiders frequently conflate them. Chongqing's version is older, descended from the dockworkers and river men who ate communally from a shared pot on the riverbanks. The broth is a dark red sea of dried chilies and Sichuan peppercorns floating in beef tallow, and the heat level has no upper bound unless you specifically ask for a milder version. You dip raw ingredients — tripe, beef, lotus root, tofu skin, vegetables — into the boiling broth and fish them out when they're cooked.

The Sichuan peppercorn is the ingredient that makes this cuisine distinctive: it produces a numbing sensation on the tongue — má — that is distinct from chili heat (là). The two together are called málà, and the combination creates a palate condition in which you can eat things far hotter than you normally could, because the numbness precedes the burn. The food science here is genuinely interesting: the peppercorn acts on a different receptor than capsaicin, creating a kind of sensory interference that allows escalation.

The local explanation for why Chongqing food is so spicy is the climate: the damp mountain fog creates a body cold that only heat can counteract. This may or may not be medically accurate. It is deeply believed.

A hotpot meal in Chongqing typically runs two to three hours. You order in rounds, the broth gets more concentrated as ingredients cook into it, and beer — usually Chongqing Beer, a local lager — is consumed in quantities that make sense given the ambient heat. Tables of strangers end up talking to each other. This is partly the alcohol and partly the shared experience of the same extreme food. Hotpot creates temporary communities in the way that very few dining experiences do.

The city that doesn't explain itself

Walking through the Ciqikou ancient town — a preserved Ming-dynasty district now heavily commercialized but still structurally intact — you can see what pre-industrial Chongqing looked like: narrow lanes, wooden shopfronts, steep stone stairways connecting levels of the hillside. Most of the shops sell tourist goods now, but the architecture is real and the topography is unmistakable. Chongqing has always been a city of stairs. The official count of public staircases in the city has at one point been listed in the thousands.

The Hongya Cave — a multi-story stilt-house complex built into the cliffside above the Jialing River, now an entertainment and dining complex — is the image of Chongqing that appears on every travel poster, and it earns its prominence. At night, illuminated in red and gold, stacked up the cliff face with the river below and the bridges spanning beyond, it looks like a city that decided the mountain was a building material. This is, in a sense, exactly correct. Chongqing didn't compromise with its terrain. It incorporated the terrain into the architecture and called it done.

There is a neighborhood — Liziba, near the metro station where the train passes through the building — where the city's spatial logic becomes almost hallucinatory. You exit the metro at what appears to be the middle of a building, step outside onto a street that is actually a bridge spanning a gorge, look down to see another neighborhood below and up to see another above, and realize that "street level" in Chongqing is not a fixed concept but a relative one. The city occupies multiple vertical planes simultaneously. A shop that appears to be on the ground floor is, from a different direction, on the eighth.

What Chongqing is becoming

Chongqing is a major industrial and manufacturing center — one of the largest in China — and has been since the wartime relocations of the 1930s, when factories moved inland to escape Japanese occupation. It is a car-manufacturing hub: Changan Automobile, one of China's largest automakers, is headquartered here. The city is not primarily a tourist destination in the way that Xi'an or Guilin are; it is a working city of enormous scale that happens to have extraordinary physical geography and a food culture that the rest of China acknowledges as one of the country's best.

The tourists who come are increasingly Chinese rather than international. Young Chinese travelers have discovered Chongqing's photogenic verticality — the stacked neighborhoods, the neon bridges, the fog-shrouded towers — and made it a major destination for the kind of travel photography that populates Chinese social media. The city has leaned into this: the light shows on the Hongya Cave and the riverside skyscrapers are calibrated for the phone camera. This is not cynical. It reflects a genuine engagement with what the city actually looks like, which is unlike anything else.

The thing that changes how you see it

Before you go, here is what to carry: Chongqing does not care if you understand it. Most great cities perform themselves for visitors — Paris is staged, Rome is curated, Barcelona has been optimized. Chongqing is simply itself, at enormous scale, on terrain that makes no concessions, feeding you food that will test your limits without apology. The metro runs through the apartment building because that was the most direct route. The restaurant is in the bomb shelter because the tunnel was available. The hotpot broth is volcanic because that is what the climate requires. The city makes decisions based on its own logic and does not explain them.

This quality — the city as a system operating on principles it doesn't feel the need to translate — is rare and worth paying attention to. Once you stop expecting Chongqing to perform legibility and simply let it be what it is, the two rivers, the thousand bridges, the fog dissolving around the ridge-line towers at dusk — it becomes one of the most visually extraordinary urban environments on earth.

The fog lifted for the Japanese bombers sometimes. The city held anyway.
