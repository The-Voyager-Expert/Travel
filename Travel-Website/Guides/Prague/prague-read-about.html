Prague looks like a fairy tale and has the history of a place that kept getting thrown out of windows. Both things are true, and one explains the other.

The golden age under Charles IV

The name Charles Bridge, Charles University, Charles Square — Charles IV is everywhere in Prague because he essentially built the city you're walking through. He became King of Bohemia in 1346 and Holy Roman Emperor in 1355, and he had the instincts of someone who understood that great cities require deliberate investment. He modeled Prague's expansion on Paris. He founded Charles University in 1348 — the first university in Central Europe — which is still operating today on the Old Town side of the river. He ordered the construction of the New Town adjacent to the Old Town, more than doubling the city's settled area.

He also commissioned Charles Bridge, laying the first stone himself on July 9, 1357, at exactly 5:31 in the morning. That specificity is real: Charles IV chose the time because the date and time formed a numerical palindrome — 1-3-5-7-9-7-5-3-1 — which he believed would protect the structure. The bridge took 45 years to complete. It's still standing.

Under Charles IV, Prague was the intellectual and political capital of Central Europe. When he died on November 29, 1378, the city he left behind was — for one brief window — among the most significant in the world. The Czechs call this the Golden Age of Bohemia. Then the next century arrived, and things got complicated.

Jan Hus and the wars that followed

Jan Hus is probably the most important Czech who ever lived, and most visitors to Prague walk past his memorial in Old Town Square without knowing who he was. He was born around 1369, earned his degree from Charles University, and by 1402 was preaching at the Bethlehem Chapel — a few blocks south of Old Town Square, still standing — to congregations that packed the place. He preached in Czech, which was itself a statement. He attacked the sale of indulgences and papal corruption, and he drew on the English theologian John Wycliffe. The church was not pleased.

At the Council of Constance in 1415, he was given a guarantee of safe conduct by the Holy Roman Emperor Sigismund. He went anyway. The guarantee wasn't honored. On July 6, 1415, he was burned at the stake for heresy. He refused to recant. He said he would accept correction only from Scripture. A 2015 Czech Radio survey voted him the greatest hero of the Czech nation — greater than any king, general, or president. That tells you something about what the Czechs think matters.

The Hussite Wars that followed ran from 1419 to 1434. The opening event was the First Defenestration of Prague — on July 30, 1419, Hussite protesters threw royal officials from the windows of the New Town Hall, on Charles Square, killing them. The military commander Jan Žižka then led forces that defeated four papal crusades, using wagon fortifications and early firearms in ways that rewrote medieval tactics. The movement eventually split and the radical wing was crushed. But the tradition of dramatic exits through Prague's windows was just getting started.

The second defenestration and what it started

On May 23, 1618, Protestant Bohemian nobles confronted Catholic regents at Hradčany Castle — Prague Castle, on the hill above the river — over the suppression of Protestant worship on royal lands. The confrontation ended the way confrontations in Prague seem to end: two Catholic regents, Count Vilém Slavata and Count Jaroslav Bořita of Martinice, plus their secretary Philip Fabricius, were thrown from a third-floor window. A fall of approximately seventy feet.

All three survived. Catholics claimed the Virgin Mary caught them. Protestants said they landed in a dung heap. Both explanations circulated simultaneously, each side using the identical fact to confirm what it already believed. That's a remarkably modern dynamic for 1618.

The same window, the same drop, the same survival. Catholics and Protestants used identical facts to confirm opposite conclusions.

This Second Defenestration is the opening event of the Thirty Years' War, which killed millions and remade the map of Europe. The Protestant side lost the decisive Battle of White Mountain in 1620, Prague was forced back to Roman Catholicism, and in 1621 the severed heads of twenty-seven executed anti-Habsburg rebels were displayed on the Old Town Bridge Tower — the Gothic gate at the Old Town end of Charles Bridge that you'll walk under. Stand under it and look up. The heads were up there for ten years.

The Baroque overlay

After 1620, the Habsburgs re-Catholicized Prague in stone. The medieval street plan survived — Prague's Old Town is still the same tangle of lanes it was in the fourteenth century — but the city's visual character was methodically overlaid with Baroque. The copper domes, the dramatic church facades, the ornate pillar monuments in public squares: almost all of that dates from the century after the Catholic victory at White Mountain. It was deliberate. Architecture as theology.

The most concentrated example is Charles Bridge itself. The bridge was built under Charles IV, but the thirty Baroque statues lining its parapets were added between 1683 and 1714 — a devotional program of Catholic saints installed on what was then Prague's only river crossing, the route through which all trade between east and west moved. The medieval engineering underneath, the Baroque piety on top, the tourists now: the bridge holds its whole history in one 516-meter span. The original stone statues have been in the National Museum's Lapidarium since 1965. What you're walking between are high-quality replicas, which is worth knowing.

Kafka's city

Franz Kafka was born in Prague in 1883, in a German-speaking Jewish family, and spent nearly his entire life here, dying in 1924. He wrote exclusively in German despite living in a Czech-speaking city — his prose was notably pure, which scholars attribute partly to writing in a language not native to his surroundings. He worked as an insurance officer at the Worker's Accident Insurance Institute from 1908 onward, which left him afternoons for writing. He remained largely unknown in his native city during his lifetime.

The Prague he inhabited was specific: a small German-Jewish enclave operating within a Czech-speaking city under the Austro-Hungarian Empire. His fiction's sense of spatial entrapment, institutional opacity, and identity without stable ground is legible as the experience of that community — a minority within a minority. He asked his friend Max Brod to destroy his manuscripts. Brod didn't. The Trial, The Metamorphosis, The Castle were published after his death.

Walk through the Old Town — particularly the narrow lanes between Old Town Square and the river — and you start to feel what Kafka was writing about. The streets double back. The building you're looking for is always one more turn away. The labyrinth isn't a metaphor here; it's the street plan. The German-Jewish world Kafka described was effectively erased within twenty years of his death. What replaced it, in a deep irony, is a city that has turned his face into its primary tourist brand. His image is on merchandise throughout the Old Town. He would probably have found this appropriate.

The twentieth century

Czechoslovakia was occupied by Germany from March 15, 1939, until liberation in May 1945. Prague came through World War II with its historic core largely intact — unlike Warsaw, Dresden, or Cologne, it wasn't leveled. That survival is why you're here: the coherent cityscape spanning Romanesque, Gothic, Baroque, and Art Nouveau across a relatively compact area is the direct result of the city not being bombed flat.

The communist government that took power in 1948 ran things for forty years. In January 1968, Alexander Dubček became First Secretary of the Communist Party and launched what he called "socialism with a human face" — press freedom, economic reform, reduced secret police authority. By August, the reform journal Literární listy was reaching 300,000 readers. On the night of August 20–21, 1968, approximately 165,000 troops and 4,600 tanks from the Soviet Union, Bulgaria, Poland, and Hungary rolled in. Romania and Albania refused to participate. The invasion killed 72 people and injured more than 700. Dubček was replaced, censorship reinstated, and twenty years of what the regime called "normalization" followed.

By August, Literární listy was reaching 300,000 readers. The tanks arrived that night.

The Velvet Revolution ran from November 17 to November 28, 1989. Riot police suppressed a student demonstration on the 17th. By November 20, approximately 500,000 people were in the streets. The revolution's symbol was the jingling of keys — the unlocking of doors, and an implicit dismissal of the regime. ("Go home," essentially, in the form of a sound.) The Communist leadership resigned November 24. Václav Havel was elected President on December 29. Alexander Dubček — whose reforms the Soviets had crushed twenty-one years earlier — was elected speaker of parliament the day before. The country dissolved peacefully into the Czech Republic and Slovakia on December 31, 1992.

The clock and the river

The Astronomical Clock on the Old Town Hall has been running since 1410 — the oldest working astronomical clock in the world. It was designed by the horologist Mikuláš of Kadaň and the astronomer Jan Šindel of Charles University. The astronomical dial is a mechanical astrolabe showing the positions of the Sun and Moon. The calendar dial was added around 1490. During the Prague uprising of May 7–8, 1945, Nazi gunfire destroyed the wooden sculptures and the calendar dial. After repairs, the clock resumed operation in 1948. A 2018 restoration stripped out the electric mechanism installed that year and put back the original 1860s mechanical system.

Don't watch it on the hour from directly in front — you'll be twelve people deep and see nothing. Find a position on the square's edge, slightly elevated if you can, and watch the crowd watching it. The mechanism itself is genuinely extraordinary: a clock designed in 1410 that is still doing what it was built to do. That's what's worth registering.

The Vltava runs 431 kilometers before joining the Elbe at Mělník. Prague is crossed by 21 bridges. The river was the city's drinking water source until 1912. It has flooded catastrophically at irregular intervals — the 2002 flood was the worst in modern memory, causing serious damage to the historic districts and Charles Bridge. Bedřich Smetana composed his symphonic poem Vltava in its honor — the most famous piece of Czech music, and worth listening to before you go. In Jewish folklore, the river's clay was the material for the Golem of Prague, the artificial being that sixteenth-century Rabbi Loew supposedly animated to protect the Jewish quarter. The quarter he was protecting is largely gone; the legend survived.

What Prague wants you to understand

Most visitors see the Old Town, the Castle, Charles Bridge, the Clock, and leave. That's a perfectly reasonable trip and the sights are genuinely worth seeing. But Prague rewards going further — both physically and historically. The Vinohrady and Žižkov neighborhoods east of the New Town are working districts with good restaurants and no particular tourist identity. Holešovice, the old industrial area north of the center, has become the city's gallery and studio district. The hill of Vyšehrad, where the legendary Duchess Libuše stood and prophesied a great city, is quiet enough to hear the wind.

Here's what to carry with you: Prague has been through things that should have broken it — a Catholic military occupation that killed a quarter of Bohemia's population, four decades of communist rule, a Soviet invasion, the systematic erasure of its German-Jewish intellectual class. Each time, the city absorbed the damage and kept going. It kept the medieval street plan. It kept the bridge. It kept the clock running. And it came out of 1989 with Václav Havel — a playwright — as president, and the man the Soviets had tried to silence twenty years earlier as speaker of parliament. That specific outcome, that particular elegance, is very Prague.

The fairy tale exterior is real. So is everything underneath it. When you're standing on Charles Bridge at dawn before the crowds arrive, or looking at the Clock from the edge of the square, or walking the lanes of the Old Town that Kafka walked — you're standing in a place that has been maintained, with great stubbornness, for seven hundred years. Once you understand that, you stop seeing the tourist surface and start seeing what it's protecting.
