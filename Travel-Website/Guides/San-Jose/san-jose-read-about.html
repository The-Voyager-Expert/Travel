The city that started it all and gets credit for none of it — and why that's exactly the point.

The first city in California, twice

San Jose has a habit of being first and being forgotten about it. In November 1777, Spanish colonists under José Joaquín Moraga founded El Pueblo de San José de Guadalupe on the banks of the Guadalupe River — the first civilian town in California, meaning the first settlement that wasn't a mission or a military presidio. Its entire job was to grow food for the soldiers stationed in San Francisco and Monterey. That's the founding character of this place: not a fort, not a church, but a farm town built to feed everyone else.

Then, in 1850, when California entered the Union, San Jose became its first state capital. The legislature met here for two sessions, earned the nickname "the Legislature of a Thousand Drinks" for reasons you can probably guess, and then decamped for Vallejo within a year. San Jose has been California's first pueblo and its first capital, and if you asked most Americans to name either fact they'd guess Sacramento or Los Angeles. Sacramento got the permanent capitol. Los Angeles got the fame. San Jose got back to work.

The valley that grew fruit before it grew chips

Here is the fact almost nobody visiting for a tech conference knows: the flat basin you're standing in, filled with office parks and freeway interchanges, was until the 1950s the largest fruit-producing region on earth. They called it the Valley of Heart's Delight. By the late 1800s the Santa Clara Valley was carpeted in more than 100,000 acres of apricot, cherry, prune, and almond orchards, and for the better part of a century this was the place that packed and shipped more fruit than anywhere else in the world — 39 canneries running at once, blossoms in spring turning entire hillsides white and pink.

Then, starting in the mid-1950s, bulldozers came for the orchards. Not gradually — fast. Developers leveled trees for tract housing, then for the low concrete buildings that would become semiconductor plants, and within twenty years the valley's identity flipped completely. Today fewer than 5,000 of those orchard acres remain, scattered and mostly ornamental. When you drive past a business park with a name like "The Orchard" or "Blossom Hill Road," that's not branding nostalgia. That's the actual, literal thing that used to be there.

The valley didn't lose its orchards to make room for Silicon Valley. For about a decade, it was both at once — apricot trees on one side of the road, wafer fabs going up on the other.

Where the name actually came from

"Silicon Valley" is younger than you'd think — the term wasn't printed until 1971, when journalist Don Hoefler used it in the trade paper Electronic News. But the thing it names starts in 1956, when William Shockley, one of the co-inventors of the transistor at Bell Labs, moved west and opened Shockley Semiconductor Laboratory in Mountain View, a few miles from where you're staying. He was a visionary and, by every account, a miserable manager. Within a year, eight of his best engineers — later nicknamed "the traitorous eight" — walked out and started Fairchild Semiconductor, where they built the first practical silicon transistors and the first commercial integrated circuit. Fairchild alumni went on to found Intel, AMD, and dozens of other companies. Nearly every chipmaker in the valley can trace its lineage back to that one walkout.

San Jose's own contribution predates all of it. In 1954, IBM opened a research lab at 99 Notre Dame Avenue in San Jose specifically to invent a faster way to store data than punch cards or tape. The team, led by engineer Reynold Johnson, built the IBM 350 — the world's first hard disk drive, fifty spinning 24-inch platters stacked in a cabinet the size of two refrigerators, storing what would today be considered a rounding error's worth of data. It shipped in 1956. Every hard drive, every SSD's conceptual ancestor, every "save" icon shaped like a floppy disk — all of it traces back to a building in San Jose that most people driving past today have no idea was there.

A capital that works instead of performs

San Jose is the largest city in the Bay Area by population — bigger than San Francisco — and by a wide margin its least famous. That's not an accident of geography. It's a consequence of what the place was built to do. San Francisco was built to be looked at: hills, fog, a skyline made for postcards. San Jose was built to work. Its downtown doesn't perform the way San Francisco's does, and the city has spent decades being described, a little unfairly, as "no there there" — a line actually written about Oakland by Gertrude Stein, but that gets pinned on San Jose constantly because it fits the stereotype people want to believe.

What San Jose actually has is the valley's largest concentration of the people who build the things everyone else's cities eventually use. It calls itself, with a straight face and reasonable justification, the Capital of Silicon Valley. The tech headquarters cluster in the suburbs around it — Cupertino, Mountain View, Santa Clara — but San Jose is the city that houses, feeds, and moves the workforce that makes all of that run. That's a less glamorous kind of importance than a skyline. It's also the more durable kind.

Japantown that almost wasn't the last one

Walk through the blocks of Japantown north of downtown and you're standing in one of only three surviving historic Japantowns left in the United States — the others are in San Francisco and Los Angeles. San Jose's is the youngest of the three, and locals will tell you, with some pride, that it's also the most intact: unlike the other two, it was never leveled by urban renewal and rebuilt as something more polished for outsiders. What you're walking through is close to what was actually there a century ago.

It survived something worse than renewal, though. During World War II, the federal government forcibly removed and incarcerated the Japanese American families who built this neighborhood, along with more than 100,000 other Japanese Americans on the West Coast. Most of San Jose's Japantown families lost their homes and businesses outright. That the neighborhood exists at all today is because enough of those families came back after the war and rebuilt it block by block, on the same streets, with nothing handed to them. The temples, the produce markets, the small family-run restaurants you'll walk past — those are not a historical reenactment. They're the second act of a story that was supposed to end during the war and didn't.

The house that never stopped

Then there's the Winchester Mystery House, which belongs to no category at all. Sarah Winchester, heiress to the Winchester rifle fortune, bought an eight-room farmhouse here in 1885 and kept building onto it, by most accounts continuously, until she died in 1922. What resulted is a 24,000-square-foot sprawl with 160 rooms, 10,000 windows, 2,000 doors, staircases that climb into ceilings, and doors that open onto blank walls two stories up. There was no master architect and no blueprint. The popular legend — that she was building endlessly to confuse the vengeful spirits of everyone killed by a Winchester rifle — is probably more folklore than fact, but nobody has ever produced a better explanation for why a wealthy widow spent thirty-eight years and an unknown fortune on a house with no logical floor plan.

It's the strangest thing in a city not otherwise known for strangeness, and that's worth sitting with for a second. San Jose is the practical city, the working city, the city that invented the disk drive and grew the fruit and rebuilt Japantown by hand — and then it also has this one enormous, illogical, obsessive monument to a single person's private grief, sitting in the middle of the suburbs like nothing about it is unusual at all.

What to actually understand before you arrive

Every famous city sells you a version of itself before you get there — Paris sells romance, New York sells ambition, San Francisco sells its own skyline. San Jose doesn't sell anything, because for most of its history nobody outside the valley was buying. That turns out to be the real story here: this is what a city looks like when it spends two and a half centuries just doing the actual work — feeding the coast, briefly running the state, growing the world's fruit, then inventing the hardware that quietly runs the rest of the planet — without ever pausing to build the mythology to match.

San Jose has been first at almost everything that mattered and famous for almost none of it. That's not an oversight. It's the whole personality of the place.

So the thing to carry with you here isn't a fact about a building or a founder. It's this: you are standing in the one place in America that has been quietly right about the future longer than anywhere else, and it has never once needed you to notice.
