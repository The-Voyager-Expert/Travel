🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 1 min · 🚕 1 min → Duomo di Como
1. Duomo di Como
↳ Como’s Gothic-Renaissance cathedral, begun in 1396 and crowned in 1740 — one of the last great cathedrals of Italy, its marble facade flanked by seated statues of Pliny the Elder and the Younger, both born in Como.

2. Funicolare Como–Brunate
↳ A wood-panelled funicular running since 1894, climbing over 500 m in seven minutes to the hill village of Brunate for a sweeping balcony view across the lake, the rooftops of Como, and the Alps beyond.

3. Tempio Voltiano
↳ A neoclassical rotunda on the lakefront, built in 1927 for the centenary of Alessandro Volta’s death, holding the original instruments and batteries of the Como-born scientist who gave his name to the volt.
📖 Wikipedia

↳ A grand neoclassical villa of 1797 by Simone Cantoni, fronted by a free lakeside park of fountains, statues and old trees with paths along the water; the villa interior is under restoration, but the gardens stay open.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 10 min → Cernobbio
1. Cernobbio
↳ A 15th-century lakeside village built around the grounds of Villa d'Este — now a grand hotel whose terraced garden reaches the water. The narrow lanes of the old centre lead to a small piazza and a waterfront promenade with the village's characteristic view south across the lake toward Como.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Piazza Roma · Cernobbio
🚕 35 min → Villa Carlotta
2. Villa Carlotta
↳ A neoclassical villa on the Tremezzo waterfront with an eight-hectare botanical garden terraced above the lake. The rooms hold sculptures by Canova and Thorvaldsen; the garden's wisteria pergolas and camellias lead to terraces facing the Bellagio promontory across the water.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 7:30pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Via Regina 2 · Tremezzo
🚕 10 min → Villa del Balbianello
3. Villa del Balbianello
↳ An 18th-century villa on a narrow wooded promontory, reached by boat taxi from Lenno pier. Cardinal Durini built the loggia terrace in 1787 for its unobstructed 270-degree view of the lake; the FAI-owned villa later served as the final scene in Casino Royale and the meadow in Star Wars Episode II.
📖 Wikipedia

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 35 min → Sacro Monte di Ossuccio
1. Sacro Monte di Ossuccio
↳ A UNESCO World Heritage pilgrimage route of 14 Baroque chapels on a wooded crag 200 m above Ossuccio, built 1635–1710. Each chapel holds polychrome terracotta scenes of the Mysteries of the Rosary; the path ends at a sanctuary terrace overlooking Isola Comacina.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Via Sacro Monte · Ossuccio
🚕 10 min · 🚢 15 min → Varenna
2. Varenna
↳ A medieval village on the eastern shore, its narrow lanes of yellow and terracotta facades descending to the water. The Passeggiata degli Innamorati — the Lovers' Walk — is a lakeside path cut into the cliff face between the ferry landing and the village beach, with open views across to Bellagio.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Piazza San Giorgio · Varenna
🚶 25 min · 🚕 10 min → Castello di Vezio
3. Castello di Vezio
↳ A 12th-century tower castle on a limestone spur 240 m above Varenna, reached by a 25-minute footpath. The ramparts give a panorama over the Bellagio fork where Lake Como splits, both arms stretching south, and the Alps north. The castle maintains a falconry display with live birds of prey.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Via al Castello · Perledo
🚶 25 min · 🚕 10 min → Villa Monastero
4. Villa Monastero
↳ A 12th-century Cistercian convent converted into a villa, now owned by the Province of Lecco. The terraced botanical garden runs along the eastern waterfront — 14 themed sections with loggias, palms and citrus, descending to the lake with views west toward Bellagio.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~1 hr 15 min

🔋 The Town That Powers Your Phone

Three of today’s stops quietly circle the same man. Alessandro Volta was born in Como in 1745, and the city has never let him go: the Tempio Voltiano on the lakefront is a temple built to house his actual batteries, and up at Brunate — where the funicular drops you — a stone lighthouse, the Faro Voltiano, was raised in 1927 to mark a century since his death. Every time a device reports its charge in volts, it is using a unit named after a man whose home cathedral you can see from his temple’s steps.

It is a small lake town with an outsized footprint. The same Como that gave the world the volt also gave it Pliny the Elder and the Younger — both seated in marble on the Duomo’s facade — and, for a few centuries, much of Europe’s finest silk. For one day, a guide that fits in your pocket sits in the city that helped define how the pocket-sized things get powered.
