Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 28 min → citadel gate
1. Machu Picchu Citadel
↳ The Inca citadel built c.1450 under Emperor Pachacuti — 200 acres of temples, palaces, and agricultural terraces at 2430m between two Andean peaks. Constructed without mortar, each stone shaped to fit its neighbor by hand. Abandoned within a century; the Spanish never found it.

1. Huayna Picchu
↳ The 2693m granite peak above the north end of the citadel — the summit in every iconic Machu Picchu photograph. Circuit 3A includes the Huayna Picchu trail: a 400m vertical climb on Inca-carved steps rewarded with a vertical view down over the entire citadel and the Urubamba horseshoe.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Inca Bridge
↳ A retractable drawbridge set into a cliff ledge above a 600m drop — the citadel's western entrance, pullable to block the only approach from that side. A 25-minute walk from the Guardian's House along a narrow Inca road carved into the rock face with no railings above the valley.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~1 hr

3. Aguas Calientes
↳ The rail-access town hemmed in by near-vertical jungle walls where the Urubamba bends. The Mercado Artesanal along Avenida Imperio de los Incas sells Andean textiles, ceramics, and silver; the Pachacutec Monument plaza at the far end of the avenue marks the edge of town where the thermal pool road begins.

🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 28 min → Montana Machu Picchu
1. Montana Machu Picchu
↳ A 3082-metre peak rising south of the citadel, requiring a separate Circuit 1A ticket limited to 800 visitors per day. The summit trail climbs through Inca-terraced cloud forest — a 5-to-7-hour trek reaching a panoramic viewpoint where the citadel, the Urubamba horseshoe, and the Andean ranges spread below.

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 20 min → Jardines de Mandor
1. Jardines de Mandor
↳ A botanical garden and waterfall complex tucked 9 kilometres west of Aguas Calientes along the Urubamba railway. The path winds through orchid-hung cloud forest past three cascades before reaching the main 30-metre falls — one of the few places near the citadel that rewards a full morning with few other visitors.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 6:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~2 hr
📍 Mandor valley · Aguas Calientes
🚕 20 min → Manco Capac Plaza
2. Manco Capac Plaza
↳ The main square of Aguas Calientes marked by a bronze statue of the Inca founder Manco Cápac at the upper end of Avenida Pachacutec. Ringed by craft stalls, pisco bars, and the roar of the Vilcanota River behind the market, it is the social centre where the town gathers at nightfall.

1. Ollantaytambo Archaeological Site
↳ The best-preserved Inca town still inhabited on its original 15th-century street plan, and the fortress that repelled a Spanish cavalry assault in 1537. The site rises in six massive agricultural terraces to the unfinished Temple of the Sun, built from fitted granite blocks hauled from quarries across the Urubamba.

2. Pinkuylluna Granaries
↳ A cliff face of Inca storehouses carved into the mountain directly above Ollantaytambo, reachable by a 30-minute hike up stone steps from the main plaza. The granaries exploit the mountain wind to preserve maize through temperature swings. The trail gives the best overhead view of the Inca street grid below.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Ollantaytambo Town
↳ The only Inca settlement still occupied on its original layout — a grid of stone-lined irrigation channels and compound walls laid out by Pachacuti in the 15th century. Water channels run along every block; residents still live inside the canchas behind the original doorways facing the plaza.

Day 5
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 28 min → Inti Punku
1. Inti Punku
↳ The Sun Gate, the final pass on the Inca Trail and the original approach to the citadel. A 2km walk from the citadel entrance crosses a ridge at 2730 metres, framing the classic dawn view of the entire site in one stone portal — the same vantage point Inca pilgrims reached after four days on the trail.
📖 Wikipedia

🍮 Local Tastes
Trucha a la Plancha
↳ Rainbow trout from the Urubamba River — grilled whole or in fillets with native papa amarilla and Andean herbs. The river runs alongside Aguas Calientes and is prized for its cold, fast-flowing water.
📍 Mapacho · Toto's House
Chicha de Jora
↳ Fermented yellow-corn chicha brewed in earthenware — the Inca's sacred beverage, used in ceremonies at the citadel. Still made by small chicharías in the Cusco region; served sour and lightly fizzy.
📍 Indio Felíz · Inka Wasi
Choclo con Queso
↳ Andean giant corn — kernels the size of marbles, starchy and sweet, served warm with a slab of queso fresco. Sold by vendors at the bus terminal in the early morning before the first ascent.
📍 Bus terminal vendors · Aguas Calientes
Rocoto Relleno
↳ A whole rocoto pepper stuffed with spiced minced beef, olives, and egg, baked under melted cheese. One of the few spicy dishes in highland Andean cooking; a signature of the Cusco region.
📍 Chef House · Inka Wasi

🌄 The Stones That Breathe

Every stone at Machu Picchu is unfixed. There is no mortar — only the weight of one stone against another, shaped so precisely that a knife blade cannot slip between them. The Inca used a technique called ashlar: stones hammered against each other until they fit perfectly, then stacked dry. In an earthquake-prone mountain corridor where Spanish colonial churches crumbled for centuries, the Inca walls kept standing. They don't resist earthquakes; they move with them — flexing and resettling, no external binding required.

The site was used for roughly 80 years. Built in the 1450s under Emperor Pachacuti, abandoned in the 1530s as Pizarro's forces consolidated control of the Andes. In that time, Machu Picchu appears in no Spanish colonial record — the conquistadors never found it. Four centuries passed before Hiram Bingham III arrived in 1911 and declared it "discovered." By then, local farmers had been growing crops on its terraces all along. The lost city was never quite lost.
