A granite city on a saddle between two peaks, built by people who had no iron, no wheel, and no written language — and abandoned before anyone with those things ever saw it.

Not a Lost City

The phrase "lost city of the Incas" has been selling books and tour packages for over a century, but it was never accurate. When Hiram Bingham arrived in July 1911, a local farmer named Melchor Arteaga led him up the ridge for a small fee. At the top, two Quechua families were living among the terraces, growing crops in the same soil the Inca had cultivated four centuries earlier. Bingham "discovered" Machu Picchu the way Columbus "discovered" America: by being led there by someone who already lived nearby.

What Bingham actually did was introduce the site to the English-speaking academic world. He photographed it, mapped it, shipped roughly 46,000 artifacts to Yale (Peru spent decades getting them back — the last batch arrived in 2012), and wrote a bestseller. The discovery was real in the sense that it changed the site's future. But the idea that this city was hidden or forgotten belongs to the mythology, not the history.

A Century of Use, Then Silence

Construction began around 1450 under the Inca emperor Pachacuti, the same ruler who transformed Cusco from a modest settlement into the capital of the largest empire in pre-Columbian America. Machu Picchu was likely a royal estate — part retreat, part administrative center, part sacred site. The population at its peak was probably around 750 people, most of them support staff: farmers, cooks, weavers, priests.

The Inca used it for roughly a century. By the 1570s, the site was empty. Why they left is still debated — smallpox brought by the Spanish, civil war between rival Inca factions, or simply the collapse of the supply chain that kept a mountaintop city fed. The Spanish never found it, which is almost certainly why it survived. They were thorough destroyers of Inca sacred architecture everywhere else. They dismantled Cusco stone by stone, built a cathedral on top of the Qoricancha sun temple, and smashed every Intihuatana pillar they could reach. This one they never reached.

Engineering Without the Wheel

You will hear guides say that no mortar holds these walls together, and that is true but understates what is happening. The stones are cut so precisely that you cannot fit a knife blade between them. This technique — ashlar masonry — requires each block to be ground against its neighbor until the surfaces lock. No iron tools. No draft animals hauling material. No wheel. Just bronze chisels, stone hammers, sand as an abrasive, and an amount of coordinated human labor that is difficult to imagine from where you are standing.

The agricultural terraces are not decorative. They are a layered irrigation and erosion-control system — topsoil over gravel over sand over large stones — that channels rainwater downhill through internal drainage layers, preventing the entire site from sliding off the mountain. Roughly sixty percent of Machu Picchu's construction is underground, and that invisible infrastructure is the reason the visible part is still here. The Intihuatana stone — the carved granite pillar near the highest point — is an astronomical instrument calibrated to mark the equinoxes. The Temple of the Sun has a window aligned to catch the first light of the winter solstice. Everything here has a function. The Inca did not build for show.

The Altitude

Machu Picchu sits at 2,430 meters — about 7,972 feet. That is lower than Cusco at 3,400 meters, which helps. Most itineraries route you through Cusco first for acclimatization, and if you can handle Cusco you can handle this. But "lower than Cusco" is still high enough to give you a headache, shorten your breath on the steep paths, and make the staircases feel twice as long as they look.

The coca tea served at every hotel in the Sacred Valley is not a souvenir experience. It is medicine. The locals have been chewing coca leaves at altitude for thousands of years because it works — it eases nausea, opens the airways, and takes the edge off the fatigue. Drink it. The altitude pills are optional. The tea is not.

Two Climbs, Two Views

Huayna Picchu is the steep, dramatic peak that appears in every photograph — the shark-fin mountain rising behind the ruins. The climb takes about an hour up, roughly the same down. The path is narrow, the steps are carved into exposed rock, and there are sections where you are pulling yourself up by a cable bolted into the cliff face. The view from the top looks down into the ruins and across the Urubamba gorge. It is the most photographed angle on the planet for a reason. Permits are capped at 400 per day, split into two entry windows, and sell out weeks in advance.

Machu Picchu Mountain — Montaña — is the larger peak on the opposite side. The trail is longer, about ninety minutes up, and less dramatic step by step. But the summit gives you the overhead view that Huayna Picchu cannot: the entire citadel laid out below you like a blueprint, the terraces, the plazas, the relationship between the buildings and the landscape all visible at once. Fewer people climb it. If you want to understand what Pachacuti built, this is the better vantage point.

Huayna Picchu gives you the postcard. Montaña gives you the blueprint.

Aguas Calientes

The town at the base — officially renamed Machu Picchu Pueblo, though everyone still calls it Aguas Calientes — is honest about what it is. It exists to serve the site. The main street is a corridor of restaurants, tour agencies, and souvenir shops. The hot springs that give the town its name are lukewarm concrete pools fed by a sulfur spring. They are mediocre. But the setting is extraordinary: a narrow valley floor hemmed in by cloud-forest mountains, the Urubamba River roaring through the middle, and no road in or out. You arrive by train or on foot. There is something clarifying about a town you cannot drive to.

Walking In vs. Riding In

The Inca Trail is a four-day, 43-kilometer trek through the Andes that ends at the Sun Gate — the same entrance the Inca used. You walk through cloud forest, over a 4,200-meter pass called Dead Woman's Pass, past ruins that no bus tour will ever reach, and arrive at Machu Picchu from above at dawn, the way Bingham did. It requires a permit booked months in advance, a licensed guide, and a level of fitness that the brochures understate. It is one of the great walks in the world.

The train from Ollantaytambo is the alternative, and it is spectacular in its own right. The route follows the Urubamba River downstream through the Sacred Valley, the landscape shifting from dry highlands to cloud forest as you lose altitude. The glass-roofed cars on the Vistadome service let you watch the mountains close in overhead. You arrive in Aguas Calientes in about ninety minutes. It is not the same experience as walking in. Nothing is. But it is not a lesser one — it is a different one, and the valley it takes you through is worth seeing from a train window.

The Crowds and the Rules

Machu Picchu receives roughly 4,500 visitors per day. The Peruvian government has responded with timed entry circuits, mandatory guides for the first portion of your visit, and route restrictions that keep you moving in one direction. You cannot wander freely the way travelers did twenty years ago. The infrastructure struggles with the volume — trail erosion, stone wear, drainage stress — and the restrictions exist because this place almost loved itself to death.

Go early. The first entry circuit, starting at six in the morning, gives you the site in low-angle light with the smallest crowds. Follow the rules. Stay on the marked paths. Do not touch the stones — the oils from human hands accelerate weathering on surfaces that have survived five centuries of rain and earthquakes. The fact that you need a ticket, a guide, and a time slot to visit a ruin on a remote mountaintop in Peru is itself a kind of tribute. Five hundred years after the Inca walked away, the world is still trying to figure out how to take care of what they left behind.
