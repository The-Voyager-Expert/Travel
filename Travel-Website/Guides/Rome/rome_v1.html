Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 18 min · 🚕 7 min → Colosseum
1. Colosseum
↳ The largest amphitheatre ever built, opened in 80 AD and once seating roughly 50,000 spectators for gladiatorial games. Its travertine arches and partly restored arena floor remain the defining image of imperial Rome. The skip-the-line ticket also covers the Roman Forum and Palatine Hill within 24 hours.

2. Roman Forum & Palatine Hill
↳ The political and religious heart of ancient Rome — temples, basilicas, triumphal arches and the Senate house spread along the Sacra Via. The adjoining Palatine Hill holds the ruins of imperial palaces and looks down over the whole Forum. Entry is included with the Colosseum combined ticket.

3. Capitoline Hill
↳ The smallest of Rome's seven hills and its ancient citadel, crowned by Piazza del Campidoglio — the geometric square Michelangelo designed in the 1530s, with its star-patterned pavement and a replica of the bronze equestrian Marcus Aurelius at the centre. The flanking palaces house the Capitoline Museums.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Piazza del Campidoglio · Campitelli
🚶 3 min · 🚕 3 min → Victor Emmanuel II Monument
4. Victor Emmanuel II Monument
↳ Known to Romans as the Vittoriano, this vast white-marble monument to Victor Emmanuel II, first king of a united Italy, towering over Piazza Venezia with colonnades, fountains and bronze quadrigas. The Altare della Patria and its terraces are free; a glass lift climbs to a panoramic rooftop over the historic centre.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 38 min · 🚕 14 min → Vatican Museums & Sistine Chapel
1. Vatican Museums & Sistine Chapel
↳ One of the world's greatest museum complexes — kilometres of galleries holding the Raphael Rooms, the painted halls and classical sculpture amassed by the popes. The route ends in the Sistine Chapel beneath Michelangelo's ceiling and Last Judgment. Book a timed entry; lines without one can run for hours.

2. St. Peter's Basilica
↳ The largest church in the world and the centre of Catholicism, raised over the tomb of St. Peter. Inside are Michelangelo's Pietà, Bernini's bronze baldachin and the soaring dome Michelangelo engineered. A separate ticket climbs the cupola for one of Rome's finest panoramas over St. Peter's Square.

3. Castel Sant'Angelo
↳ Built as Emperor Hadrian's mausoleum in the 2nd century, then turned into a papal fortress, prison and refuge linked to the Vatican by an elevated passage. A spiral ramp winds up past papal apartments and the armoury to the Angel's Terrace, crowned by a bronze archangel and wide views over the Tiber.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 7 min · 🚕 4 min → Pantheon
1. Pantheon
↳ The best-preserved building of ancient Rome, rebuilt by Hadrian around 126 AD as a temple to all the gods and later a church. Its coffered concrete dome — still the largest unreinforced concrete dome on earth — is open at the centre through a 9-metre oculus that lights the marble interior.

2. Piazza Navona
↳ Rome's grandest Baroque square, laid out over the oval of Domitian's ancient stadium. At its centre stands Bernini's Fountain of the Four Rivers, framed by Borromini's church of Sant'Agnese in Agone and ringed by café terraces, street artists and palaces.

3. Trevi Fountain
↳ The most theatrical fountain in Rome, completed in 1762 where three roads meet. Nicola Salvi's design sends Ocean on his shell chariot bursting from the façade of Palazzo Poli over crashing travertine reefs. Tradition says a coin tossed over the shoulder guarantees a return to Rome.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Piazza di Trevi · Trevi
🚶 10 min · 🚕 5 min → Spanish Steps
4. Spanish Steps
↳ A sweeping 135-step staircase of 1725 rising from Piazza di Spagna to the twin-towered church of Trinità dei Monti. At the foot sits Bernini's boat-shaped Barcaccia fountain; the terraces are a classic spot to sit and watch the city, especially at sunset.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 18 min · 🚕 7 min → Circus Maximus
1. Circus Maximus
↳ The largest chariot-racing track ever built — 620 meters long and once holding 250,000 spectators. The full oval between the Palatine and Aventine hills is still legible as a free open-air park; best understood by standing at the far end and looking back at the Palatine looming above.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Piazza del Circo Massimo · Aventino
🚶 14 min · 🚕 6 min → Orange Garden
2. Orange Garden
↳ A terraced park on the crest of Aventine Hill planted with bitter orange trees and centered on a Pietro Lombardi fountain. The north terrace delivers an unbroken panorama over the Tiber, the dome of St. Peter's, and the sweep of Rome's rooftops — one of the city's finest views, almost never crowded.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 7:00am - 9:00pm
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Via di Santa Sabina · Aventino
🚶 6 min · 🚕 3 min → Aventine Keyhole
3. Aventine Keyhole
↳ Through the keyhole of the Knights of Malta priory gate, St. Peter's Basilica appears perfectly centered at the end of a hedge-lined avenue — a composition staged by Piranesi in the 18th century. The bronze gate stands in Piazza dei Cavalieri di Malta; free to see, costs nothing and takes a minute.
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~15 min
📍 Piazza dei Cavalieri di Malta · Aventino
🚶 18 min · 🚕 7 min → Trastevere
4. Trastevere
↳ Rome's most intact medieval quarter on the right bank of the Tiber — cobblestone lanes, golden churches, ivy-covered facades. The center is Piazza di Santa Maria in Trastevere, anchored by a 12th-century basilica with gilded mosaics that light the square at night.
📖 Wikipedia

1. Orvieto Cathedral
↳ One of Italy's most dramatic Gothic buildings — a golden facade of mosaics, reliefs, and carved marble built over three centuries. The interior holds the Cappella di San Brizio, frescoed by Signorelli in the 1490s; his Last Judgment directly influenced Michelangelo's Sistine ceiling a generation later.

2. Pozzo di San Patrizio
↳ A 53-meter well commissioned by Pope Clement VII after the 1527 Sack of Rome. Sangallo engineered a double-helix staircase — two spiral ramps never crossing — so donkeys with full and empty water jugs could ascend and descend without meeting. The descent is 248 steps.

🏺 Threads Across the Ages

Two names quietly tie the first three days together. Hadrian: the emperor who rebuilt the Pantheon on Day 3 also raised, across the river, the drum-shaped mausoleum that became Castel Sant'Angelo on Day 2 — two of the same man's buildings, eighteen centuries apart. Michelangelo: his hand appears on all three days without ever being the headline — the geometry of the Capitoline square on Day 1, the dome of St. Peter's on Day 2, and the Sistine ceiling closing the Vatican galleries.

Day 4 adds a third thread: Piranesi. The 18th-century engraver who obsessively documented ancient Rome also designed the hedge-lined alley that makes the Aventine Keyhole work — a framed view so precisely composed it feels like a stage set. He drew the ruins on Day 1 and engineered a sight on Day 4, the same mind straddling antiquity and the Enlightenment.

On Day 5, Orvieto adds one more: Luca Signorelli, whose Last Judgment frescoes in the Cappella di San Brizio are the direct source Michelangelo studied before painting the Sistine ceiling. The ceiling that anchors Day 2 has a prequel an hour from Rome, waiting in a Gothic cathedral on a tuff-rock cliff. Rome rarely tears down — and the things it exported keep turning up just around the corner.
