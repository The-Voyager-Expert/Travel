You're arriving in a city that collapsed from a million people to twenty thousand and somehow came back — and the past here isn't behind you, it's underneath you.

The founding story they told themselves

The Romans pegged their city's birthday to April 21, 753 BC — a date a scholar named Varro reverse-engineered from king lists and genealogies with more confidence than the evidence really supported. Nobody believes it anymore. What the archaeology actually shows is slower and more interesting: hilltop settlements on the Capitoline going back to around 1700 BC, gradually merging into a real city by 600 BC under Etruscan influence.

But pay attention to the founding myth, because it tells you something you'll see everywhere. Romulus kills his twin, names the city after himself, then immediately opens the gates to fugitives and exiles from across the region. Come as you are. That's not a fairy tale — it's a policy statement. Rome built its power by absorbing outsiders, not by keeping them out. When you stand in the Forum and try to make sense of the layers — Greek columns, Etruscan engineering, Egyptian obelisks, Christian churches — you're looking at what that policy produced over two thousand years.

The infrastructure under your feet

By the first century AD, Rome held at least a million people. No Western city would match that again until London in the 1800s. Think about what that means in a world without electricity or pumps: you need to move water, move waste, move food, all by gravity and engineering.

When you toss a coin into the Trevi Fountain, the water catching it has traveled from springs twenty-one kilometers outside the city through the Aqua Virgo — an aqueduct Augustus built in 19 BC that still works. The great sewer that the Etruscan kings dug to drain the swamp between the Palatine and Capitoline hills still discharges into the Tiber. You're walking over infrastructure that has been running, continuously, for more than two millennia.

The Pantheon's dome is 44 meters across — still the largest unreinforced concrete dome on earth, nearly two thousand years after it was poured. Step inside and look up: that oculus is the only light source, and rain falls straight through it onto the slightly convex floor, which drains through holes the original builders cut.

The Colosseum is the building you think you know, but stand in front of it and look at the pockmarked exterior — thousands of holes in the stone where bronze clamps once held the blocks together. Those weren't battle damage. They were made by Romans, centuries later, prying out the bronze for reuse. The building was a quarry for a thousand years. Travertine from the Colosseum went into St. Peter's Basilica. The Romans have a saying for that: "What the barbarians didn't do, the Barberini did" — a jab at the pope who stripped the Pantheon's bronze for Bernini's altar canopy.

When the city emptied

In 410 AD, the Visigoths sacked Rome — the first time the city had fallen to a foreign enemy in eight hundred years. The sack lasted three days. In 455, the Vandals came and stayed for fourteen (their name entered the language because of what they did). By 476, the last Western emperor — a teenager named Romulus Augustulus — was deposed by a Germanic chief who didn't even bother claiming the title. The empire was done.

But what really killed the city was quieter. During the Gothic Wars of the 530s, the Ostrogoths cut the aqueducts. Without water, the baths closed, the mills stopped, sanitation collapsed. The population that had been a million fell to perhaps twenty thousand. Walk through the Forum today and remember: for most of the medieval period, this was called the Campo Vaccino — the Cow Field. Cattle grazed among the broken columns. The ground level had risen ten meters above the ancient street, burying everything.

That's the thing to hold in your head as you navigate the modern city. You are always ten meters above where Rome actually happened.

The church that kept the lights on

What saved Rome from becoming an abandoned ruin was the papacy. The Bishop of Rome effectively ran the city when there was no emperor left to do it. The great basilicas — St. Peter's, St. John Lateran, Santa Maria Maggiore — were anchors in a city that was otherwise emptying out. And the pilgrims who came to visit them were the economy.

In 1300, Pope Boniface VIII declared the first Jubilee Year: visit the principal basilicas, get full remission of sins. The response overwhelmed the city — up to two million pilgrims descended on a town of maybe twenty-five thousand permanent residents. That single year locked in Rome's economic identity for the next seven centuries. You'll feel it the moment you arrive. The entire center is calibrated to receive visitors — the wide piazzas, the fountains at every turn, the restaurants and hotels layered into every block. This infrastructure didn't grow by accident. It grew because Rome has been a pilgrimage destination since before the word "tourist" existed.

When the papacy relocated to Avignon under French pressure in 1309 and stayed for sixty-seven years, Rome nearly died again. The papal court — thousands of clerks, cardinals, lawyers, merchants — was the city's engine. Without it, buildings went abandoned and the population cratered. The poet Petrarch visited in 1337 and found a city of ruins haunted by its own past. The popes came back, eventually, but the scar took generations to heal.

Art as a power move

Nearly everything you'll line up to see in Rome — the Sistine Chapel, St. Peter's, the Raphael Rooms — exists because Renaissance popes treated art as a weapon. Julius II is the one to know. A warrior pope who personally led armies in the field, he commissioned Michelangelo (then thirty-three and deeply reluctant) to paint the Sistine ceiling, hired Raphael to paint The School of Athens, and made the fateful call to demolish the original St. Peter's and build a new one from scratch. That project took 120 years and outlived a dozen popes.

The selling of indulgences to fund St. Peter's was one of the triggers of Luther's 95 Theses. The most famous building in Christendom helped split Christendom in two.

Stand in front of St. Peter's and you're looking at a building that changed the trajectory of Western religion — not through theology but through its construction budget. And when you visit the Sistine Chapel, skip the ceiling for a moment and look at the far wall: Michelangelo's Last Judgment, painted twenty-five years later by the same artist, now in his sixties, angry and disillusioned. The difference between the ceiling and the wall is the difference between a young genius performing and an old one settling scores.

The boulevard that buried the evidence

If you walk from Piazza Venezia toward the Colosseum, you'll take the Via dei Fori Imperiali — a wide, tree-lined boulevard that feels like it was always there. It wasn't. Mussolini built it in 1932 by demolishing an entire medieval neighborhood: homes, churches, centuries of lived history, bulldozed to create a parade route connecting his balcony at Piazza Venezia to the Colosseum. He rode a horse down it on opening day.

The paradox is real: the demolition exposed the Imperial Fora — the forums of Caesar, Augustus, Trajan — which had been buried for centuries. But it also cut them apart with a four-lane highway and made a unified archaeological park impossible. Mussolini wanted ancient Rome as a stage set, not as a place to understand. You're walking on his propaganda every time you take that road. Most tourists do, and most don't know.

Why they can't finish the metro

Here's the fact that explains more about modern Rome than anything else: the city sits on fifteen to twenty meters of hidden layers — Imperial, Republican, medieval, Renaissance — stacked on top of each other. Any time someone digs, they hit something. And by law, they have to stop and excavate.

The Metro C line has been under construction for roughly two decades. Every tunnel bore, every station excavation, turns up another find — a second-century military barracks under one station, residential rooms with intact frescoes under another, ancient wells, everyday objects (hairpins, knives, oil lamps in shapes you wouldn't put on a mantelpiece). Two stations opened in December 2025 with the archaeological finds built into the station design as permanent exhibits. You can ride the metro and see Roman artifacts displayed behind glass on the platform. No other city does this, because no other city has this problem.

Paris had Haussmann, who organized the medieval tangle into a legible grid. London had the Great Fire and the Blitz, which created blank canvases for planning. Rome has had neither. Every era just got built on top of the last one, and the layers keep breaking through. You'll see ancient columns embedded in the walls of Baroque churches. You'll eat dinner next to the Pantheon. You'll jog past the Circus Maximus — where 250,000 people once watched chariot races — because that's where Romans go for a morning run.

The thing to carry with you

Before you go, here's what to hold onto: Rome is the only major city in Europe that never got a clean page. It was never burned flat and rebuilt on a rational plan. It was never reorganized by a visionary planner. It just kept going — collapsing, being looted, losing ninety-eight percent of its population, getting rebuilt by whoever showed up next, and never once clearing the rubble from the last version before starting the new one.

The word for a manuscript that's been scraped and rewritten is palimpsest — the old text bleeds through the new. Once you see Rome that way, you can't unsee it. The medieval church with Roman columns holding up the nave. The Renaissance palazzo built on top of an ancient theater. The modern apartment building with an Etruscan wall in the basement. Every layer is still there, just underneath. You're not visiting a city that was great once. You're walking on every version of it, all at the same time.
