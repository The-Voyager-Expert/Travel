A city that collapsed from a million people to twenty thousand and somehow came back — and why the past here is not behind you but underneath you.

The myth and the archaeology

The traditional founding date of Rome is April 21, 753 BC. This figure was calculated by the Roman scholar Marcus Terentius Varro in the first century BC, working backward through king lists and genealogies with a confidence that the available evidence did not quite warrant. The Romans accepted Varro's date and celebrated it as a civic birthday for centuries. Modern archaeologists largely dismiss it as a retrospective construction — a story the Romans told themselves about their origins rather than a record of what actually happened.

What actually happened, the evidence suggests, was a gradual coalescence. Hilltop settlements on the Capitoline Hill date to around 1700 BC. By roughly 600 BC, these clusters had merged into an organized urban center, probably under Etruscan influence. The myth of Romulus — who killed his twin Remus in a quarrel over the city's location, then named it after himself, then invited fugitives and exiles from across the region to populate it — encodes something real about how Rome actually worked: as a place that absorbed outsiders rather than excluding them, that built its strength from heterogeneity rather than purity. This was not an accident of character. It was Roman policy from the beginning, and it explains more about what Rome became than any founding date does.

The Republic was established around 509 BC, after the Romans expelled their Etruscan king Tarquinius Superbus. Two annually elected consuls replaced the king. The Senate, originally a council of one hundred men selected by Romulus from the most noble families, became the institution that held power across the centuries of expansion that followed.

A million people, one city

At its peak in the first and second centuries AD, Rome held at least one million inhabitants. No other city in the Western world would reach this size again until London in the nineteenth century. Feeding and watering a population of that scale in the ancient world required an infrastructure of engineering that had no parallel anywhere on earth.

Eleven aqueducts served Rome during the imperial period, delivering an estimated one million cubic meters of water per day — roughly one cubic meter per person. The Aqua Virgo, built by Augustus in 19 BC, still supplies the Trevi Fountain today, fed by springs 21 kilometers from the city. The Cloaca Maxima, the great sewer built originally by the Etruscan kings to drain the marshy valley between the Palatine and Capitoline hills, still discharges into the Tiber. The Roman road network eventually stretched approximately 80,000 kilometers, all distances measured from a Golden Milestone that Augustus erected in the Forum. "All roads lead to Rome" was a literal engineering fact.

Augustus claimed to have found Rome a city of brick and left it a city of marble. The Colosseum was begun by the Emperor Vespasian around 70 AD on the drained lake of Nero's private pleasure palace, completed and inaugurated by Titus in 80 AD. It is 189 meters long, 48 meters high, and seated between 50,000 and 80,000 spectators. The Pantheon, rebuilt by Hadrian between 118 and 125 AD, carries a dome of 44.4 meters — still the world's largest unreinforced concrete dome, nearly two thousand years after it was poured. The building was consecrated as a Christian church in 609 AD, which is the reason it survives intact while almost everything around it is rubble.

Trajan's Column, completed in 113 AD, carries a continuous spiral frieze 200 meters long depicting Trajan's Dacian Wars — a story told in stone around a pillar, like a film reel frozen in marble.

The collapse

In 410 AD, the Visigoths under Alaric sacked Rome — the first time the city had fallen to a foreign enemy in eight hundred years, since the Gauls in 387 BC. Saint Jerome, writing from Bethlehem, expressed the shock of the age: "The city which had taken the whole world was itself taken." The sack lasted three days. In 455, the Vandals sacked the city for fourteen days — their name entering the language as a synonym for what they did to it. In 476, the last western emperor, a teenager named Romulus Augustulus, was deposed by a Germanic chieftain named Odoacer, who sensibly did not bother to declare himself emperor. The Western Roman Empire was over.

What happened next was slower and in some ways worse than the sacks. The Gothic Wars of 535 to 554 AD — a prolonged Byzantine reconquest of Italy — involved repeated sieges of Rome and the deliberate cutting of the city's aqueducts by the Ostrogoths in 537. Without the aqueducts, the mills stopped, the baths closed, the sanitation failed. Famine followed. The population that had been at least one million at the imperial peak fell to perhaps 100,000 by the end of the fifth century, then to somewhere between 20,000 and 50,000 in the sixth and seventh centuries. A reduction of 95 to 98 percent.

The Forum Romanum, once the political, commercial, and religious heart of the ancient world, filled gradually with sediment and rubble. By the medieval period it was known as the Campo Vaccino — the Cow Field. Cattle grazed among the broken columns. The ground level rose by as much as ten meters above the ancient street, burying the monuments under centuries of accumulation. The Colosseum became a quarry. Its travertine and marble were stripped away; the bronze clamps holding the stone together were pried out, leaving the thousands of holes you see today. Travertine from the Colosseum went into the construction of St. Peter's Basilica. A Roman saying arose: "What the barbarians didn't do, the Barberini did" — a reference to Pope Urban VIII, who stripped the bronze from the Pantheon's portico for Bernini's baldachin in the new basilica.

The papacy and the city's survival

What kept Rome alive through the early medieval period was the Church. The papacy owned vast properties, maintained the great basilicas, distributed charity, and drew pilgrims whose spending constituted the core of the city's economy. The Bishop of Rome effectively governed the city in the absence of imperial authority. The great early Christian basilicas — St. Peter's, St. Paul Outside the Walls, St. John Lateran, Santa Maria Maggiore — served as anchors of the urban fabric when everything else was emptying out.

Pope Boniface VIII declared the first Jubilee Year in 1300, offering full remission of sins to all pilgrims who visited the principal basilicas. The response was extraordinary: up to two million pilgrims may have visited Rome during 1300, in a city that probably held only 20,000 to 30,000 permanent inhabitants. The economic shock of that influx established Rome's role as the preeminent destination for Western Christendom. The Jubilee became a recurring institution — held every 25 years — that has continued ever since.

The Avignon Papacy (1309–1377) nearly undid it. After a catastrophic confrontation between Pope Boniface VIII and the French king, the papacy relocated to Avignon under French pressure and stayed there for 67 years under seven successive French popes. For Rome, the effect was devastating. The papal court — with its thousands of clerks, cardinals, lawyers, merchants, and hangers-on — was the city's primary economic driver. Its removal triggered depopulation, building abandonment, and accelerated deterioration. The poet Petrarch, visiting Rome in 1337, described a city of ruins haunted by its own greatness. Pope Gregory XI finally left Avignon in September 1376 and arrived in Rome in January 1377. He died the following year, triggering the Western Schism, during which two and briefly three rival popes simultaneously claimed the throne. The institution that had kept Rome alive was at war with itself for another generation.

The popes who rebuilt it

The Renaissance popes used art and architecture as instruments of power, and Rome was the canvas. Pope Julius II, who reigned from 1503 to 1513, was the most aggressive patron in papal history — a warrior pope who personally led armies in the field and treated cultural commissions as weapons of political authority. In 1506 he made the decisive call to demolish the ancient Constantinian St. Peter's Basilica, which was structurally failing, and build an entirely new one. He laid the foundation stone on April 18, 1506. He hired Donato Bramante to design it; Bramante was so aggressive in tearing down the old basilica to clear the site that he earned the nickname Bramante Ruinante — Bramante the Wrecker.

Julius commissioned Michelangelo — then 33 years old and deeply reluctant — to paint the ceiling of the Sistine Chapel, a project that ran from 1508 to 1512. He commissioned Raphael to paint the four Stanze of the Vatican Palace, including the Stanza della Segnatura, which contains The School of Athens — arguably the defining image of Renaissance humanism. Construction of the new St. Peter's proceeded through the reigns of multiple popes and the work of multiple architects: Raphael, Antonio da Sangallo the Younger, and finally Michelangelo, who redesigned the dome and worked on it from 1546 until his death in 1564. The dome was completed by Giacomo della Porta in 1590. The basilica was finished and consecrated in 1626 — 120 years after Julius laid the foundation stone.

The selling of indulgences to fund St. Peter's was one of the triggers of Luther's 95 Theses. The most famous building in Christendom helped split Christendom in two.

In May 1527, mutinous Imperial troops — German Landsknechte and Spanish soldiers, largely unpaid after their commander was killed in the assault — stormed and sacked Rome. An estimated 6,000 to 12,000 people were killed. The city's population, which had recovered to perhaps 55,000, collapsed to around 10,000. Pope Clement VII fled through the Passetto di Borgo — a covered corridor connecting the Vatican to Castel Sant'Angelo — where he was besieged for months before paying a ransom of 400,000 ducats to secure his release. The Sack of Rome is generally treated as the terminus of the Italian High Renaissance. The artists and scholars who had gathered in Rome dispersed to Venice, France, and Spain.

Mussolini's Rome

Benito Mussolini's Fascist regime was self-consciously obsessed with ancient Rome. The Fascist symbol — the fasces, a bundle of rods around an axe — was borrowed from Roman imagery of authority. Mussolini's ambition was to make Rome a capital that physically expressed the continuity between the Roman Empire and his regime. This required demolishing the medieval and Renaissance city to expose the ancient monuments, which could then serve as stage sets for Fascist pageantry.

The most consequential of his urban projects was the Via dell'Impero — now the Via dei Fori Imperiali — inaugurated on April 9, 1932. A broad, straight avenue connecting Piazza Venezia to the Colosseum, it was built by clearing more than 40,000 square yards of densely inhabited urban fabric: the medieval neighborhood of Via Alessandrina, 746 families' homes, three ancient churches. Mussolini rode a horse down the new avenue to open it.

The paradox of the demolition was that it exposed the Imperial Fora — the forums of Julius Caesar, Augustus, Nerva, and Trajan, along with Trajan's Markets — which had been buried and built over for centuries. But it also severed them from each other with a four-lane highway, damaged them for decades with exhaust and vibration, and foreclosed the possibility of a proper unified archaeological park. Mussolini was using authentic antiquity as propaganda for a regime that bore almost no resemblance to the Republican values it claimed to inherit. He stripped the ancient city of its context in order to use it as a backdrop.

The living palimpsest

Rome today has approximately 2.74 million residents and receives roughly 35 million visitors a year. The Colosseum received 12.2 million visitors in 2023 — the most visited paid site in the world. The Trevi Fountain became so overwhelmed that the city implemented a two-euro entry fee for close-up access beginning in February 2026, in a year when an estimated ten million people visited a fountain that fits perhaps five hundred people comfortably.

None of this is the most interesting thing about the city. The most interesting thing is structural. Rome is the only major European capital where construction almost always halts for archaeology. The city sits atop approximately fifteen to twenty meters of hidden patrimony — a continuous underground accumulation of Imperial, Republican, medieval, and Renaissance layers. Any building project triggers mandatory surveys; significant finds trigger legally protected excavation periods that can last years. The Metro C line, a third metro line in planning and partial construction for approximately two decades, is expected to be completed in 2034 at a total cost approaching three billion euros. The Piazza Venezia station alone is estimated at 755 million euros. Finds have included a second-century military barracks, residential rooms with intact frescoes and mosaics, ancient wells, and thousands of everyday objects — hairpins, knives, phallic oil lamps. In December 2025, two new stations opened with the archaeological finds incorporated into the station design as permanent exhibits.

This is what distinguishes Rome from every other city in Europe. In Paris, Haussmann's boulevards organized the medieval fabric into a legible modern city. In London, the Great Fire and the Blitz created opportunities for planning. Rome has had neither. Every era has simply been layered over the previous one, and the layers keep breaking through. Ancient columns are embedded in the walls of Baroque churches. The Pantheon is surrounded by café tables. The Circus Maximus, which once held 250,000 spectators for chariot races, is a public park where Romans jog in the morning. Traffic rumbles past the ruins of Trajan's Forum. The Colosseum is visible from a McDonald's.

The word for a manuscript that has been scraped and reused is palimpsest. The original text bleeds through the new. Rome is the palimpsest that never stops being written.
