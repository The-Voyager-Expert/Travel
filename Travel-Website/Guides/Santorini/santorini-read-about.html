You are standing on the rim of a volcano that destroyed one of the most sophisticated civilizations of the ancient world, and the view is so beautiful that it is easy to forget what you are actually looking at.

Before the Eruption

Santorini — the ancient name was Thera, and many Greeks still use it — was a single roughly circular island in the southern Aegean for most of recorded prehistory. A volcanic island, yes, but an active and fertile one. The Bronze Age civilization that settled it, the Minoans (originally from Crete) or a Minoan-influenced Aegean culture, built a city at Akrotiri on the island's southern tip sometime around 2000 BCE. By 1700 BCE it was a prosperous trading port, importing copper from Cyprus, olive oil from Crete, and grain from Egypt. Its residents lived in multi-story buildings with indoor plumbing, plastered walls, and frescoed rooms. They were literate, cosmopolitan, and, by the standards of the Bronze Age world, comfortable.

Then, sometime around 1600 BCE — the precise date remains debated, with estimates ranging from 1645 to 1500 BCE — the volcano erupted. The Minoan eruption was one of the largest volcanic events in the past 10,000 years. Geologists estimate it ejected 30 to 60 cubic kilometers of material, roughly equivalent to hundreds of times the 1980 Mount St. Helens eruption. The explosion was heard as far away as Scandinavia. The ash fall reached Egypt and the Black Sea. The eruption triggered tsunamis that struck the coasts of Crete and the eastern Mediterranean with waves estimated at 35 to 150 meters high. The caldera you look across from every terrace in Fira and Oia is the scar that eruption left -- and the layered cliff face along the caldera wall, banded in red and black volcanic deposits, is a visible cross-section of the event itself.

What the Volcano Left Behind

When the eruption's central magma chamber emptied, the roof of the volcano collapsed inward, creating the caldera — the drowned crater — that gives Santorini its extraordinary geography. The caldera is roughly 11 kilometers wide and 400 meters deep. The arc of the present island (and the smaller islands of Thirasia and Aspronisi that complete the ring) is the rim of that collapsed volcano. The cliffs you stand on in Fira and Oia, dropping 300 meters to the water, are the interior wall of a volcano. The dark water below you is the filled caldera.

The islands visible in the center of the caldera — Nea Kameni and Palea Kameni — are new volcanic islands that emerged from eruptions in historical times. Nea Kameni formed in 1707 CE and has been geologically active since. The Santorini volcanic field is not extinct. The most recent significant eruption was in 1950. Walking down to the black lava surface of Nea Kameni (a common boat excursion from Fira) is a reminder that the landscape you are admiring was made by a process that is still ongoing.

Akrotiri and the Pompeii of the Aegean

The city buried at Akrotiri was not discovered until 1967, when Greek archaeologist Spyridon Marinatos began systematic excavations. What he found was extraordinary: a city preserved under volcanic ash much as Pompeii was preserved under the ash of Vesuvius, but 1,500 years older. Multi-story buildings still standing. Ceramic storage jars intact in their ground-floor rooms. Wooden furniture carbonized but in place. And the frescoes — the paintings on the walls of the upper floors — astonishingly well preserved.

The Akrotiri frescoes are some of the finest surviving examples of Bronze Age painting in the world. The famous Spring Fresco, which covered three walls of a single room with red lilies growing from red and black rock, established that the Minoans painted with a naturalism and color sense that would not be matched in European art for another thousand years. The Flotilla Fresco depicts a harbor festival with decorated boats and spectators; it is the earliest surviving landscape painting. The Boxing Boys — two children facing off — shows that the Aegean Bronze Age cultures practiced boxing as sport, not just combat.

Critically, no human remains have been found at Akrotiri. The residents apparently had enough warning to evacuate before the eruption destroyed the city. They left behind their furniture, their storage jars, their wall paintings — but they themselves escaped. Where they went, and what happened to them afterward, is one of archaeology's open questions. The excavation site is open to visitors today under a modern protective roof structure -- you walk on elevated walkways above the streets of the buried city, looking down into rooms where the frescoes once hung and storage jars still sit where they were left 3,600 years ago.

The Atlantis Question

Plato described Atlantis in two dialogues, the Timaeus and the Critias, written around 360 BCE. The story is of a great island civilization in the Atlantic Ocean, beyond the Pillars of Hercules (the Strait of Gibraltar), that sank into the sea in a single day and night of catastrophe. Plato attributed the story to Egyptian priests who had passed it to the Athenian statesman Solon around 600 BCE.

The theory that the Minoan eruption of Thera was the historical basis for the Atlantis myth was proposed in the 1960s and has been debated ever since. The parallels are suggestive: a sophisticated island civilization, destroyed suddenly by a catastrophic event that involved both volcanic eruption and massive flooding. The problems are also real: Plato places Atlantis in the Atlantic, not the Aegean; he dates the story to 9,000 years before Solon, not 900 years; and the island he describes is far larger than Santorini. Most classical scholars treat Atlantis as a philosophical fiction that Plato invented to make a political argument about the hubris of great powers. The Theran connection remains a theory — compelling and unprovable. Standing at the caldera rim, though, the theory gains a physical weight that no scholarly paper can convey -- the drowned crater, the sheer cliffs, the islands still steaming at the center. Whatever Plato intended, the landscape itself tells a story of a world that vanished in a day, and you are standing on the edge of the evidence.

Byzantine Towers, Venetian Castles, and Ottoman Rule

After the Bronze Age catastrophe, Santorini was resettled in the Archaic and Classical periods by Dorian Greeks from the Peloponnese. Ancient Thira, the city they built on the rocky Mesa Vouno headland between the black sand beach of Perissa and the modern capital of Fira, is one of the better-preserved ancient Greek cities in the Cyclades, with temples, a gymnasium, a theater, and inscriptions still readable on stone. The site is reachable by road or by a steep footpath and is rarely crowded even in high season.

The medieval history of Santorini is a series of conquests: Byzantine, then Venetian (the island was granted to Marco Sanudo's Duchy of the Archipelago after the Fourth Crusade in 1204), then Ottoman (1579). The Venetian period left the island's characteristic kastelia — the fortified hilltop settlements, of which the one at Pyrgos Kallistis is the best preserved — where the medieval town clustered around a central tower for defense against pirate raids. The streets of Pyrgos, climbing to the ruined castle at its crown, give you the clearest sense of what Santorini looked like before tourism reshaped its economy.

Volcanic Wine and the White Architecture

The volcanic soil of Santorini produces one of the most distinctive wines in the world. Assyrtiko grapes, grown on the island for at least 3,500 years, thrive in the pumice-heavy soil that drains rapidly and forces roots deep in search of water. The vines are trained in a technique called kouloura — basket-shaped coils that protect the fruit from the fierce summer winds — and some individual vines are more than 200 years old, surviving phylloxera (which wiped out most European vineyards in the late 19th century) because the volcanic soil is inhospitable to the root louse. Santorini Assyrtiko is high-acid, mineral-driven, and capable of aging — a serious wine made by people who have been growing grapes in a volcanic caldera for millennia.

The white architecture that makes Santorini so visually distinctive is not ancient. The whitewashed cube houses with their domed churches and blue doors are largely a 20th-century phenomenon, standardized after Greek tourism authorities encouraged the look in the 1970s and 1980s. The domed churches are genuinely old — Greek Orthodox Christianity has been the dominant religion here since Byzantine times — but the particular blue-dome-white-wall aesthetic that fills every photograph of the island is partly a creation of the tourism era. This does not make it less beautiful; it just means you are looking at a place that has consciously curated its appearance for visitors, and doing so with considerable skill.

What You Are Really Seeing

When you stand on the caldera edge in Oia at sunset — surrounded by thousands of other people doing the same thing, phones raised, jostling for position — it is worth pausing to think about what you are actually looking at. The caldera is not a scenic bay. It is a wound in the earth, 400 meters deep, created when a volcano consumed an entire civilization and then collapsed into itself. The cliffs are the interior wall of that collapsed chamber. The islands in the center are new volcanoes, still building.

The Bronze Age city buried at Akrotiri was sophisticated enough to have indoor plumbing and art on its walls — and it vanished so completely that it was not rediscovered for 3,500 years. The people who painted the Blue Monkeys and the Flotilla Fresco had no idea they were living on borrowed time. The wine you drink at dinner comes from vines planted in the ash that buried them. The view you came to see was made by the catastrophe that destroyed them. Santorini is one of the most beautiful places in the world, and it is beautiful because of one of the most violent events in the history of human civilization. Keep both things in mind as you watch the sunset — it makes the wine taste different.
