2. Cours Mirabeau
↳ Aix's grand plane-tree avenue, laid out in 1651 — a canopy of shade over fountains, cafés, and golden hôtels particuliers. The social spine of the city and the best place to feel its rhythm.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Place d'Albertas
↳ A jewel-box cobbled square from 1745, framed on three sides by matching Baroque façades and a single central fountain — modelled on the Parisian hôtel courtyards and used today for summer chamber concerts.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7

4. Aix Cathedral
↳ A cathedral built across fifteen centuries — Roman columns, a 5th-century baptistery, a Romanesque cloister, and Gothic and Baroque naves side by side. Don't miss Nicolas Froment's Burning Bush triptych.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:40am - 6:30pm
⏰ ~45 min

5. Atelier de Cézanne
↳ Paul Cézanne's last studio on the Lauves hill, kept exactly as he left it in 1906 — coats, bottles, skulls and ladders that recur in his canvases, lit by the great north window he built for the work. Entry is by timed, small-group ticket — reserve ahead on the official Cézanne sites.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 9 Avenue Paul Cézanne · Les Lauves

🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 5 min · 🚕 4 min → Musée Granet
1. Musée Granet
↳ Aix's finest art museum, installed in the 17th-century priory of the Knights of Malta. A dedicated room holds eight Cézanne canvases alongside works by Ingres, Rembrandt, and Rubens, plus Celto-Ligurian sculptures from the oppidum of Entremont. Tickets at the door or on the official site.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Sunday 12:00pm - 6:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~1.5 hrs
📍 Place Saint-Jean-de-Malte · Quartier Mazarin
🚶 1 min · 🚕 3 min → Église Saint-Jean-de-Malte
2. Église Saint-Jean-de-Malte
↳ Provence's first Gothic church, begun in 1272 by the Knights of Malta and consecrated in 1346 — a single nave of austere elegance rising 34 metres to its bell tower, pierced by early Gothic lancet windows. The sacristy houses the stone sarcophagi of the Counts of Provence.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 12:00pm · 3:00pm - 6:00pm
⏰ ~25 min
🆓 Free
📍 Place Saint-Jean-de-Malte · Quartier Mazarin
🚶 4 min · 🚕 5 min → Fontaine des Quatre-Dauphins
3. Fontaine des Quatre-Dauphins
↳ Four leaping dolphins carved in 1667 by sculptor Jean-Claude Rambot mark the crossing of the Mazarin grid — the quietest, most aristocratic square in Aix, surrounded by 18th-century hôtels particuliers. Worth a slow circuit of the surrounding streets before walking north.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~20 min
📍 Place des Quatre-Dauphins · Quartier Mazarin
🚶 15 min · 🚕 8 min → Pavillon de Vendôme
4. Pavillon de Vendôme
↳ A baroque pleasure pavilion built in 1665 for the Duke of Vendôme, framed by sculpted atlantes and a formal French garden. Inside, the rooms hold a museum of 17th- and 18th-century decorative arts — Provence's finest surviving classical maison de plaisance, a world away from the tourist centre.
📖 Wikipedia

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 6 min · 🚕 4 min → Parc Jourdan
1. Parc Jourdan
↳ Aix's most characteristic public garden, laid out in 1932–35 south of the centre: a lower level of classical French parterres and a reflecting pool linked by a double staircase to a romantic upper garden. The park holds Philippe Solari's bust of Emile Zola and Bottinelly's bust of Frédéric Mistral.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Chemin de l'Université · Centre-Ville
🚕 13 min → Oppidum d'Entremont
2. Oppidum d'Entremont
↳ Entremont was the hilltop capital of the Celtic-Ligurian Salyes from around 175 BC until Rome's consul Gaius Sextius Calvinus destroyed it in 123 BC. Excavated ramparts, a street grid, and a warrior sanctuary — where enemy skulls were displayed — survive across the 3.5-hectare plateau park.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Wednesday - Monday 9:00am - 12:00pm · 2:00pm - 6:00pm
🚫 Closed Tuesday
⏰ ~1 hr
🆓 Free
📍 960 Avenue Fernand Benoît · Puyricard
🚕 11 min → Fondation Vasarely
3. Fondation Vasarely
↳ Vasarely designed this building in 1973–76: sixteen hexagonal cells clad in alternating black-and-white circles and squares that animate optically as you move. Inside, 44 monumental 6-by-8-metre kinetic works fill the seven largest cells — each commissioned for this space, inseparable from the architecture.
📖 Wikipedia

🚶 28 min · 🚕 9 min → Thermes Sextius
4. Thermes Sextius
↳ The springs here led Rome to found Aquae Sextiae in 123 BC — the waters that became Aix-en-Provence. The thermal fountain on Cours Sextius marks the ancient source; the spa building at No. 55 stands on the exact footprint of the 1st-century baths, with vaulted Roman pools preserved beneath the lobby floor.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~25 min

🎨 A City That Painted Itself

Almost everything on this one-day route is a thing Cézanne actually painted or walked. He grew up a few doors off the Cours Mirabeau, drank at its cafés, was baptised in Saint-Sauveur, and spent his last four years in the studio on the Lauves looking south toward Montagne Sainte-Victoire — the mountain he painted more than sixty times. Aix is rare among cities in that you can stand inside the source material: the light on the limestone, the plane-tree shade, the ochre walls are the same palette he spent a lifetime trying to fix on canvas.

One small thing I loved: the calisson, the city's almond sweet, is cut to that pointed-oval shape said to echo a smile — legend traces it to a 15th-century royal wedding feast held in Aix. A town that turned its own happiness into a confectionery shape feels of a piece with a painter who couldn't stop painting the hill at the end of his street.
