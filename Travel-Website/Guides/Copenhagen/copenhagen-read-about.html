The most livable city in the world spent five hundred years as a trading port, a Viking capital, and a recurring target — and none of that made it hard.

A merchant city from the start

Copenhagen's name comes from the Danish Købmandshavn — merchant's harbor — and the trading instinct has never left. The city began as a fishing village on the strait between Denmark and Sweden, occupying the most strategically useful piece of coastline in northern Europe: whoever controlled this narrow passage collected tolls from every ship moving between the Baltic and the North Sea. For three centuries, Denmark collected those tolls, and the money built the palaces, the canals, the university, and the fleets that made Copenhagen one of the wealthiest cities on the continent.

What that history left behind is a city with an innate pragmatism about commerce and pleasure. Copenhagen has never been puritanical — the Scandinavian stereotype of earnest minimalism is a late arrival here. The city that built Tivoli Gardens in 1843 (before Disneyland by a century, and still one of the best amusement parks in the world) is a city that understood, early, that people need somewhere beautiful to go when the work is done.

The fires that cleared the slate

Medieval Copenhagen burned twice, in 1728 and again in 1795, and the fires swept through most of the old town both times. What this means for you, walking the city today, is that Copenhagen does not have the dense medieval street plan that cities like Bruges or Prague preserved intact. The rebuilding happened fast, in the Georgian and Neoclassical styles of the late eighteenth century, which is why the city center has a particular coherence — broad streets, regular facades, buildings that belong to the same era rather than six different ones crowded against each other.

The one exception is Nyhavn. The canal was dug in 1671 to connect the harbor to the city center, and the seventeenth-century townhouses that line it somehow survived the fires. Those painted facades — deep red, ochre, electric blue, dusty yellow — are the image that defines Copenhagen in the global imagination. They were built as warehouses and taverns for sailors. Hans Christian Andersen lived at number 20, and later at number 67, and later at number 18, which suggests he was either very attached to the canal or perpetually dissatisfied with his landlords. The canal is now all restaurants and tourist boats. It is still beautiful.

The fires didn't erase Copenhagen's history. They just reset it to a cleaner version of itself, and the city has been renovating ever since.

Hans Christian Andersen and the city he loved and resented

Andersen was born in Odense, not Copenhagen, but he spent most of his adult life in the capital trying to be taken seriously there. He arrived at fourteen, mostly illiterate, the son of a shoemaker, and spent years failing at acting and singing and ballet before he found the form — the fairy tale — that made him one of the most widely translated authors in history. The Little Mermaid, The Ugly Duckling, The Snow Queen, Thumbelina — these are stories written by a man with an outsider's acute sensitivity to rejection and belonging, set in a world of magic precisely because the real world had been unkind to him.

The Little Mermaid statue sits in the harbor on a rock at Langelinie — smaller than you imagine, about the height of a seated adult — and it has been attacked, decapitated, draped in paint, and hauled away so many times that the version you see is not the original. It is nevertheless one of the most visited monuments in Europe. What it represents is not a tourist attraction. It represents the longing of someone who doesn't quite belong anywhere for the world they can see but not fully enter. Andersen knew this feeling personally.

You can visit his rooms at the Copenhagen City Museum, where his belongings include a coil of rope he always carried in case of a hotel fire. He was not paranoid — he was a man who had survived difficulty by being prepared for it. That seems like the right context for reading his work.

The canal city that wasn't

Copenhagen was supposed to become a canal city in the seventeenth century — Christian IV, the most architecturally ambitious of the Danish kings, dug Nyhavn and planned a whole new district of canals on the artificial island of Christianshavn, across the harbor from the old town. If you cross the bridge to Christianshavn, you see what he managed: a grid of canals lined with Dutch-style townhouses, built by Dutch engineers, inspired by Amsterdam. It is the most un-Danish part of Copenhagen and the most charming.

At the center of Christianshavn is the Church of Our Saviour, with its external spiral staircase corkscewing 90 meters up the tower — you can climb it, and the view from the top is the best in the city. But the neighborhood is also home to Freetown Christiania, which has occupied the abandoned military barracks here since 1971 and still operates as a semi-autonomous community of about 900 people, with its own laws, its own economy, and its own relationship to the Danish state that can be described, charitably, as negotiated. Pusher Street, where cannabis has been openly sold for decades, has closed and reopened several times depending on the political climate. The rest of Christiania is murals, workshops, music venues, and a genuine experiment in communal living that has lasted longer than almost any comparable experiment anywhere in the world. Whether it works is a matter of what you think "working" means.

The food thing is real

Copenhagen became the center of the global food world sometime around 2010, when Noma — René Redzepi's restaurant in a converted warehouse on the Christianshavn waterfront — was declared the best restaurant in the world for the first time. The restaurant closed in 2024, but by then it had done its work: a generation of chefs trained there, or were influenced by what was called New Nordic cooking, and the city is now dense with serious restaurants in a way that would have seemed improbable thirty years ago.

What New Nordic actually means, stripped of the hype: cooking with what grows in the region, in the season it grows, prepared with technique rigorous enough to reveal rather than disguise. Fermentation. Foraging. Coastal ingredients. The aesthetic is spare — small plates, earthy presentations, flavors that don't announce themselves loudly. It is the opposite of French classical cooking in every surface quality, and yet it takes technique just as seriously.

You don't need to eat at a Michelin-starred restaurant to taste what this produced. The food culture has propagated downward through the city: the smørrebrød at a decent lunch spot is better than it was twenty years ago, the bakeries are serious about their bread, the coffee has improved out of recognition. A city that decides to care about food tends to care about it at every level eventually.

Cycling as infrastructure, not lifestyle

Copenhagen has more bicycles than people — approximately 650,000 bikes for a population of 600,000 — and the city has spent decades building the infrastructure to match. The cycle tracks here are not painted lines on the edge of a road. They are separated lanes, maintained in winter, signaled separately from traffic, designed for the kind of everyday cycling that is not sport or recreation but transport. People cycle to work in heels, in suits, carrying children and groceries, in the rain.

This is worth understanding before you arrive: cycling in Copenhagen is not a tourist activity. It is how the city moves. The infrastructure exists because the city decided, sometime in the 1970s in response to the oil crisis, that car dependency was a problem to be solved rather than a fact to be accepted. Forty years of consistent political will and infrastructure investment later, Copenhagen has the lowest car-to-population ratio of any major European capital. The air quality is different. The noise level is different. The relationship between the city and its residents is different when you're not separated from the street by a windshield.

Rent a bike on the first day. Not because it's charming. Because it's how Copenhagen actually works.

What the city gets right

Copenhagen ranks near or at the top of every livability index, and after a few days you stop wondering why. The public spaces are genuinely good — not grimly functional but designed to be pleasant to be in. The harbor has been cleaned to the point where people swim in it in summer, in the center of the city, with lifeguard stations and diving boards. The libraries are beautiful. The social housing is mixed into the city rather than segregated to the edges.

None of this is accidental or inevitable. It is the result of a long series of political choices about what public money is for, sustained across decades and governments of different parties. It is also the result of a civic culture that takes the quality of shared space seriously — that treats a badly designed public bench or a poorly maintained playground as a failure worth being annoyed about.

What this means for you as a visitor is that Copenhagen is genuinely pleasant to be in, at a level that goes beyond the tourist surfaces. The city works. It is clean in a way that is not antiseptic. It is orderly in a way that is not unfriendly. Spend a few days here and you may find yourself having the thought that most cities are this way by choice rather than by nature.

The thing to carry with you

Here is what to take with you when you leave: Copenhagen has been a merchant city, a Viking capital, a colonizer, a Nazi-occupied city that organized one of the most successful rescue operations of Jewish citizens in the war, a near-bankrupt city in the 1990s, and now the food capital of the world. It has rebuilt itself from fire twice and from political catastrophe more times than that. Each time, the instinct was not to preserve what was lost but to build something better in its place.

That instinct is still running. The harbor is cleaner than it has been in a century. The bikes have the right of way. The restaurants are better than they were last year. Copenhagen treats improvement as a project in progress rather than a destination already reached, and that is an unusual way for a city to feel about itself. Once you notice it, you notice it in everything.
