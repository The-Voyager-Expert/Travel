1. Griffith Observatory
↳ An Art Deco landmark on the south slope of Mount Hollywood, free to enter, with sweeping views over the LA basin, the Hollywood Sign, and a domed planetarium. The terraces at sunset are the classic Los Angeles panorama.

2. TCL Chinese Theatre
↳ The 1927 movie palace on Hollywood Boulevard, built as a fantasy Chinese pagoda and still hosting premieres. Its forecourt holds nearly 200 hand- and footprints of film legends pressed into the concrete, free to wander among the Walk of Fame stars.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 20 min → Getty Center
1. Getty Center
↳ Richard Meier's travertine-clad campus on a Brentwood hilltop, reached by a cable-hauled tram. Free to enter, it pairs European paintings, the Central Garden, and some of the best views in the city. Admission is free; only parking is paid, and a timed entry is reserved online.

🚕 18 min → Santa Monica Pier
2. Santa Monica Pier
↳ The 1909 pier marking the western end of Route 66, with Pacific Park's solar-powered Ferris wheel, an aquarium under the deck, and wide ocean views. The boardwalk and beach below it are the postcard image of the LA coast.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 6:00am - 10:00pm

3. Venice Canals
↳ A quiet pocket of footbridges and waterways dug in 1905 to recreate Venice, Italy, and now lined with bungalows and gardens. A slow loop along the canal walkways is one of the most charming, low-key strolls on the Westside.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 12 min → LACMA
1. LACMA — LA County Museum of Art
↳ The largest art museum in the western United States, spanning seven buildings on Wilshire Boulevard's Miracle Mile. The outdoor installation Urban Light — 202 restored cast-iron street lamps — stands at the entrance and is the most photographed artwork in Los Angeles.
📖 Wikipedia

🚫 Closed Wednesday
⏰ ~2 hr
📍 5905 Wilshire Blvd · Miracle Mile
🚕 15 min → Natural History Museum
2. Natural History Museum of LA County
↳ A 1913 Beaux-Arts building in Exposition Park housing nearly 35 million specimens, from a full-scale dinosaur hall to a live butterfly pavilion. The rotunda's trio of mounted elephants is one of the most recognizable museum interiors in the city.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:30am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 900 Exposition Blvd · Exposition Park
🚕 20 min → Watts Towers
3. Watts Towers
↳ Seventeen interconnected steel and mortar spires built entirely by hand by Italian immigrant Simon Rodia between 1921 and 1954, encrusted with broken tile, glass, and seashells. The tallest tower reaches 99 feet and the complex is one of the most remarkable works of outsider art in the world.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Thursday - Saturday 10:00am - 4:00pm · Sunday 12:00pm - 4:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday - Wednesday
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 1727 E 107th St · Watts
🚕 25 min → Angels Flight
4. Angels Flight Railway
↳ A funicular railway first built in 1901 and billed as the world's shortest, climbing a single steep block between Hill Street and Bunker Hill. The two orange-and-black cars — Olivet and Sinai — have been a Downtown landmark for over a century and still carry passengers the 298-foot climb.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 6:45am - 10:00pm
⏰ ~20 min
📍 350 S Grand Ave · Bunker Hill · Downtown

The Broad — Free Timed Tickets Sell Out
↳ Admission is free but needs a timed-entry ticket; monthly batches release on the last Wednesday and go fast.
Workaround: Reserve online the moment the monthly batch drops, or arrive at opening for the standby line.
Getty Center — Free Entry But Paid Parking
↳ Admission is free, but there is no street parking and the garage charges a fee; the hilltop is reached by tram.
Workaround: Reserve free timed entry online; parking is cheaper after 3pm and on Saturday evenings.
Griffith Observatory — Closed Mondays
↳ The building closes every Monday and nearby parking fills at sunset and on weekends.
Workaround: Visit Tuesday through Sunday and arrive before sunset or take a rideshare; the grounds stay open.
✨ Claude Inspiration

What struck me building this is how vertical Los Angeles really is, despite its reputation for sprawl. Both days are built around a climb to a vantage point — Griffith Observatory on the slope of Mount Hollywood, the Getty on its Brentwood ridge — and from each you can trace the whole basin out to the ocean. The city reads completely differently from above than it does stuck on a freeway.

There's also a neat thread between two of the stops: the same architect-as-showman impulse that gave 1927 Hollywood its fantasy Chinese pagoda runs straight through to Gehry's stainless-steel concert hall eight decades later. LA has always treated its buildings as part of the entertainment — worth keeping in mind as you move from the Walk of Fame to Grand Avenue.
