The city that was built by people who were told to go away and decided, instead, to build something better than where they came from.

A city that chose itself

Melbourne was not planned. In 1835, a group of settlers from Van Diemen's Land — present-day Tasmania — arrived at Port Phillip Bay and bought land from the Wurundjeri people of the Kulin nation through a treaty the colonial government refused to recognize. The government sent an official to tell them to leave. They didn't leave. Within two years the settlement had a name — Melbourne, after the British prime minister — and within fifteen years, after gold was discovered at Ballarat in 1851, it was one of the fastest-growing cities on earth.

That origin story — settling illegally, getting told to go, staying anyway, and then discovering gold — explains something about the city's personality. Melbourne has always had a chip on its shoulder and a high opinion of its own taste, and both of these things are, depending on the moment, its most irritating and most appealing qualities. The rivalry with Sydney is real and completely sincere on both sides. Sydney got the harbor and the Opera House and the international profile. Melbourne got the culture, the coffee, the football, and the food. Melburnians will tell you they got the better deal. They might be right.

What the gold rush built

The buildings in the CBD that stop you on the street — the Royal Exhibition Building in Carlton, the State Library on Swanston Street, the block-long Victorian terrace facades in East Melbourne, the ornate shopping arcades off Collins Street — were all built in the 1880s, when Melbourne was officially the second-largest city in the British Empire after London, and its citizens were determined to build something that looked the part. The gold from Ballarat and Bendigo funded it. The architects were mostly trained in Britain and Europe and arrived with ambitions that outscaled anything that had previously existed in Australia.

The State Library of Victoria, which opened in 1856, has a domed reading room that you should sit in even if you don't need a book. The dome is 35 meters across and lit from above, and the concentric rings of desks beneath it are still used by students and researchers every day. The Royal Exhibition Building is the only Victorian-era building in Australia to hold UNESCO World Heritage status. It was built in eleven months for the 1880 Melbourne International Exhibition, which drew over a million visitors to a city that had only existed for 45 years. The ambition involved is almost comic. The building has outlasted everything it was meant to announce.

The city built itself to impress visitors who mostly weren't coming yet, and then became genuinely impressive while waiting for them to arrive.

Federation Square and the question of identity

When Federation Square opened in 2002 — a deconstructivist design by Lab Architecture Studio, all fractured stone and glass angles, built over the Yarra River railway yards — the response in Melbourne was mixed to the point of being a cultural event in itself. Half the city thought it was hideous. Half thought it was exactly what Melbourne had been waiting for. Both groups went down to look at it. Both groups kept going back. It is now the meeting point, the place where every public gathering in the city happens, the answer to the question "where should we meet?" — which is what a central public square is supposed to be, regardless of what anyone thinks about the architecture.

Federation Square marks the shift from colonial Melbourne — the gold-rush city that built Victorian grandeur to prove itself — to contemporary Melbourne, which is less interested in proving anything and more interested in being genuinely good at things. It faces Flinders Street Station across Swanston Street: the ornate Edwardian building with the famous clocks under the dome, the one that appears in every photograph of the city. You meet people under the clocks at Flinders. It's been the standard for a century. If you want to understand Melbourne in two images, those two buildings facing each other across the intersection is it: the thing the city built to announce itself, and the thing it built when it stopped caring what you thought.

The lanes

The city grid was laid out in 1837 by Robert Hoddle, who drew wide north-south and east-west streets and narrow service lanes between them. The lanes were for deliveries and rubbish. By the 1990s they had become something else: the laneways that now carry Melbourne's reputation — Hosier Lane, Centre Place, Degraves Street, AC/DC Lane — filled with street art, espresso bars, dumpling restaurants, record shops, cocktail bars in converted loading docks.

Hosier Lane is the most famous: a cobblestone alley off Flinders Lane where the walls have been legally available for street art since the 1990s, and where the work turns over constantly, new layers going over old ones, so that what you see today will be partially or entirely different from what was there last month. The best of it is genuinely extraordinary — large-scale, technically sophisticated work by artists from Melbourne and internationally. The worst of it is someone's tag over someone else's tag. Go anyway. The combination of quality work, the physical texture of the lane, and the knowledge that the whole thing is impermanent and ongoing is unlike anything you'll find in a gallery.

Degraves Street, nearby, is where the Melbourne coffee culture is most concentrated and most itself. The cafe at the end of the lane, the espresso standing up at the counter, the understanding between the barista and the regular that nothing needs to be explained — this is not performance, it's infrastructure. Melbourne takes its coffee the way other cities take their public transit: a basic service that should work perfectly and usually does, and whose failure is taken as a civic emergency.

The Yarra and what lives beside it

The Yarra River runs through the city from the Dandenong Ranges to Port Phillip Bay, and for most of Melbourne's history it was used primarily as an industrial drain. The river is brown — chocolate brown, always, because the geology upstream releases fine clay that never fully settles — and Melburnians have been making jokes about it since the city was founded. "The river that flows upside down," because the mud is on top. "Only river in the world that flows with its bottom up." The jokes are affectionate. The river is genuinely muddy and genuinely beloved.

The South Bank along the river is where the institutions are: the National Gallery of Victoria, which holds the best art collection in Australia, including an Ian Potter Centre devoted entirely to Australian art; the Arts Centre Melbourne, where the main theatres are; the Melbourne Recital Centre. Walk the river path from Federation Square to South Melbourne on a Sunday and you get a picture of what the city looks like when it's relaxed: families cycling, rowers on the water, the MCG dome to the east, the Botanic Gardens to the south.

Sport as civic religion

The Melbourne Cricket Ground — the MCG, always called "the G" — is the largest stadium in the Southern Hemisphere and the most important building in Melbourne that most visitors don't go inside. It seats 100,000 people. On AFL Grand Final day, every seat is filled. On Boxing Day, when the Test cricket starts, the crowd fills the lower levels and spills into the stands. The ground was built for the 1956 Melbourne Olympics and has been expanded and rebuilt so many times since that almost nothing of the original structure remains. It has been a test cricket ground since 1877 — the first Test match ever played was here — and the ghosts are felt if not seen.

AFL — Australian Rules Football — is not a sport Melbourne invented, but it is a sport Melbourne owns. The league's headquarters are here. Eight of the eighteen clubs are from Melbourne. The game itself, played on an oval field with 18 players per side, involves kicking and handballing and marking — catching a kicked ball on the full, which earns a free kick — and produces a kind of athletic spectacle that is genuinely unlike anything in international sport. Go to a game if there's one on. Sit anywhere. The stadium acoustics on a packed day are something you hear with your whole body.

The thing about Melbourne's weather

You have been warned. The four-seasons-in-one-day thing is not a joke Melburnians make to manage expectations — it is an accurate meteorological description of what Port Phillip Bay does to the local climate. A warm morning can produce a hot afternoon can produce a cool-change thunderstorm by 4pm. The "cool change" — a rapid southerly shift in the wind that drops the temperature 15 degrees in an hour — is a specific Melbourne event with a specific name that locals track on weather apps and mention in conversation the way New Yorkers mention hurricane warnings. Pack layers regardless of the forecast. Trust nothing.

What this produces, paradoxically, is a city intensely attuned to weather — obsessively attentive to the sky, alert to shifts, opportunistic about good days in a way that cities with reliably fine weather don't need to be. Melbourne takes a warm afternoon in winter personally, as if it's a gift. That quality — the appreciation of something good precisely because you know it won't last — runs through the city's relationship with a lot of things.

The thing to carry with you

Here's what changes how you see Melbourne: every wave of people who arrived here — the settlers who wouldn't leave in 1835, the gold-rush immigrants who came from China and Ireland and California and stayed, the postwar European migrants who set up the espresso bars and delicatessens and trattorie in Carlton and Fitzroy, the Vietnamese community that made Richmond's Victoria Street into a food destination, the communities from the Horn of Africa and South Asia and Southeast Asia who are reshaping the outer suburbs right now — all of them arrived somewhere inhospitable or distant or unfamiliar and built what they needed rather than accepting what was there. The lanes and the coffee and the food and the art and the football and the architecture are all downstream of that impulse.

The Wurundjeri people, on whose land the city was built without consent or treaty, have been here for at least 40,000 years. The city's full accounting with that fact is ongoing and incomplete. But the version of Melbourne that everyone visits — the multicultural, food-obsessed, culturally ambitious, mildly self-congratulatory city that genuinely deserves most of its reputation — was built by people who arrived with nothing, were told in various ways that they didn't belong, and built something anyway. Once you see that pattern, you see it in everything: the cafe in the alley, the mural on the wall, the stadium on the hill. This city is made of arrivals.
