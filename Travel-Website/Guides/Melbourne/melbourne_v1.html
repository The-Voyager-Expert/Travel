Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 9 min · 🚕 4 min → Flinders Street Station
1. Flinders Street Station
↳ Melbourne's oldest railway station, completed in 1910. The Edwardian Baroque ochre facade and clocks above the entrance — "meeting under the clocks" — have been the city's most iconic rendezvous point for a century. The dome caps the view south from Swanston Street.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Cnr Flinders & Swanston Streets · CBD
🚶 3 min · 🚕 3 min → Federation Square
2. Federation Square
↳ Opened in 2002 on a 3.2-hectare site at the symbolic heart of the city. The angular facade of zinc, sandstone, and glass covers the rail corridor below and frames an open forecourt used for every major public gathering in the city. Houses ACMI and the Ian Potter Centre NGV Australia.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Cnr Flinders & Swanston Streets · CBD
🚶 7 min · 🚕 4 min → NGV International
3. NGV International
↳ Australia's oldest and most visited art gallery. The Great Hall ceiling by Leonard French — 4,096 hand-cut glass panels forming a luminous canopy — is one of the largest in the world. The permanent collection spans 75,000 works; Rembrandt, Tiepolo, and Monet holdings are the strongest in Australia.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~2 hrs
🆓 Free
⚠️ Major ticketed exhibitions run alongside the permanent collection — check the gallery website for the current programme.
📍 180 St Kilda Road · Southbank
🚶 12 min · 🚕 5 min → Royal Botanic Gardens Melbourne
4. Royal Botanic Gardens Melbourne
↳ Established in 1846 on the south bank of the Yarra. These 38 hectares hold over 50,000 plant specimens from 10,000 species. Guilfoyle's Volcano — a restored 19th-century ornamental reservoir designed as a garden folly — is the most photographed feature. Free guided walks depart daily at 11:00am.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 7:30am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
🆓 Free
📍 Birdwood Avenue · South Yarra
🚶 8 min · 🚕 4 min → Shrine of Remembrance
5. Shrine of Remembrance
↳ Victoria's principal war memorial, built in 1934. The inner sanctuary holds the Stone of Remembrance; a single ray of sunlight falls through the roof aperture at 11:00am on 11 November — the exact minute of the armistice. The rooftop terrace commands the finest city panorama from the gardens.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~45 min
🆓 Free
📍 Birdwood Avenue · Domain
🚶 30 min · 🚕 8 min → hotel
Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 18 min · 🚕 7 min → Parliament House
1. Parliament House
↳ Victoria's Parliament has met here since 1856 — the year the colony introduced the secret ballot to the world. The Neoclassical Corinthian colonnade on Spring Street is one of the city's grandest facades. Free guided tours visit both legislative chambers when Parliament is not sitting.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Monday - Friday 9:30am - 3:30pm
🚫 Closed Saturday & Sunday
⏰ ~45 min
🆓 Free
⚠️ Tours run only when Parliament is not sitting. Check the official website before visiting.
📍 Spring Street · Parliament Quarter
🚶 10 min · 🚕 5 min → State Library Victoria
2. State Library Victoria
↳ Founded in 1854 and one of the first free public libraries in the world. The La Trobe Reading Room — a five-tier octagonal gallery beneath a 35-metre concrete dome — is the city's most extraordinary interior. The collection includes Ned Kelly's armour and the Burke and Wills expedition diary.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Monday - Thursday 10:00am - 9:00pm · Friday - Sunday 10:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 328 Swanston Street · CBD
🚶 12 min · 🚕 5 min → Royal Exhibition Building
3. Royal Exhibition Building
↳ The only surviving purpose-built 19th-century exhibition hall still in active use — Australia's first UNESCO World Heritage Site. Completed in 1880, it hosted the inauguration of Australia's Commonwealth Parliament in 1901. The Great Hall rises 60 metres to the dome; its gardens are free to enter.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Royal Exhibition Building Dome Promenade · 4.7⭐ · museumsvictoria.com.au
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
⚠️ Interior access is by the 60-minute guided Dome Promenade tour only; the exterior and Carlton Gardens are free and open any time.
📍 9 Nicholson Street · Carlton
🚶 3 min · 🚕 3 min → Melbourne Museum
4. Melbourne Museum
↳ Australia's largest museum, opened in 2000. Six galleries cover human minds, Australian ecosystems, Aboriginal cultural heritage, and Victoria's history. The Forest Gallery — a living rainforest inside a glass enclosure with 8,000 plants and free-roaming birds — is one of the most unusual spaces in any museum.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Melbourne Museum Admission · 4.5⭐ · museumsvictoria.com.au
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~2 hrs
📍 11 Nicholson Street · Carlton
🚶 25 min · 🚕 8 min → Melbourne Cricket Ground
5. Melbourne Cricket Ground
↳ The MCG — capacity 100,024 — is the world's largest cricket ground. The first Test match in cricket history was played here in 1877. The ground hosted two Olympic Games and AFL Grand Finals watched by over 100,000 spectators. The National Sports Museum occupies the Great Southern Stand.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ MCG Tour & National Sports Museum · 4.8⭐ · mcg.org.au
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 3:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
⚠️ Tours depart every 30 min on non-event days. No tours on AFL match days, cricket days, or concert days. Check the official website before visiting.

1. Luna Park Melbourne
↳ An amusement park on the St Kilda foreshore open since 13 December 1912. The Scenic Railway — a timber roller coaster from 1912 — is the oldest continually operating roller coaster in the world. The park's iconic laughing-face entrance is a registered heritage structure.

2. St Kilda Beach
↳ Melbourne's most visited beach, stretching along Port Phillip Bay 6 kilometres south of the CBD. The sandy foreshore has a promenade, a historic kiosk dating from 1931, and views across the bay toward the Mornington Peninsula.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7

🚶 8 min · 🚕 3 min → Acland Street
3. Acland Street
↳ The main commercial strip through St Kilda, associated since the post-war years with Central European Jewish immigrants who established its pastry shops and cake counters. Independent restaurants and cafes now line the full length of the street.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Acland Street · St Kilda
🚶 10 min · 🚕 4 min → St Kilda Pier
4. St Kilda Pier
↳ A 150-metre timber pier extending into Port Phillip Bay with a kiosk at its end dating from 1904. The breakwater at the base of the pier is home to around 1,200 Little Penguins — the world's smallest penguin species — which return to their burrows each evening after feeding at sea.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
⚠️ The Little Penguins return to the breakwater at dusk — arrive at sunset for the best sighting. Viewing is free; do not approach or use flash photography.

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 10 min · 🚕 4 min → Old Melbourne Gaol
1. Old Melbourne Gaol
↳ A bluestone prison operating from 1845 to 1924, where 135 people were executed, including bushranger Ned Kelly in 1880. The original cell blocks, gallows, and Kelly's armour are on display. The building is listed on the Victorian Heritage Register.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 377 Russell Street · CBD
🚶 12 min · 🚕 5 min → Hosier Lane
2. Hosier Lane
↳ A cobblestone CBD laneway between Flinders Street and Flinders Lane, directly opposite Federation Square. Since the late 1990s it has become Melbourne's most documented street art destination, with murals covering both walls from ground to roofline under a de facto free-to-paint policy.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Hosier Lane · CBD
🚶 8 min · 🚕 4 min → Melbourne Town Hall
3. Melbourne Town Hall
↳ The civic administrative centre of Melbourne, constructed between 1867 and 1870 on the corner of Swanston and Collins Streets. The Classical Revival facade and the Grand Organ — one of the largest in the Southern Hemisphere with 4,452 pipes — are both listed on the Victorian Heritage Register.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Monday - Friday 9:00am - 5:00pm
🚫 Closed Saturday & Sunday
⏰ ~30 min
🆓 Free
📍 90–130 Swanston Street · CBD
🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → Chinatown Melbourne
4. Chinatown Melbourne
↳ Centred on Little Bourke Street between Exhibition and Swanston Streets, Melbourne's Chinatown is one of the oldest continuous Chinese settlements outside Asia, established during the Gold Rush of the 1850s. The precinct holds 19th-century buildings, restaurants, herbalists, and the Chinese Museum at 22 Cohen Place.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Little Bourke Street · CBD

🍮 Local Tastes
Flat White
↳ Melbourne's defining coffee: a double ristretto beneath a thin layer of microfoam — smaller and more espresso-forward than a latte, with no dry foam. Every serious café in the city does one well.
📍 Patricia Coffee Brewers · Market Lane Coffee · Industry Beans
Melbourne Dim Sim
↳ A distinctly local creation: a large steamed or fried dumpling of minced pork and cabbage in thick pastry, popularized by South Melbourne Market since the 1940s. Steamed with soy sauce is the canonical order.
📍 322 Coventry Street · South Melb
Chicken Parma
↳ Victoria's unofficial state dish: a crumbed chicken breast topped with napolitana sauce and melted cheese, served alongside thick-cut chips in every pub. The parma is a Melbourne institution — ask the hotel concierge for the best nearby.
📍 1 Swanston Street · CBD

✨ The City That Invented Itself

Melbourne has no obvious reason to exist. Sydney has one of the most spectacular natural harbours on earth and was founded by the British as a matter of imperial policy. Melbourne was founded in 1835 by a sheep farmer who paid a handful of trinkets to Indigenous elders for a land deal the colonial government refused to recognize — and then built a city on it anyway. Within twenty years, the Gold Rush had made it one of the wealthiest cities in the world. Within fifty, it had electrified its trams, built a free public library, and introduced the secret ballot to the world. The city essentially willed itself into existence through sheer competitive energy, and it has never entirely stopped competing with Sydney since.

What makes Melbourne genuinely distinctive is its relationship with culture as a form of civic argument. The coffee obsession — Melbourne pioneered the flat white, the single-origin espresso bar, the café as a room you sit in for two hours — is not a trend but a 30-year project. The laneway street art, which began as illegal graffiti on the walls of forgotten service lanes, is now a municipal policy: the City of Melbourne funds the walls, defends the artists from prosecution, and markets Hosier Lane to the world. The Australian Rules Football Grand Final at the MCG, watched by 100,000 people inside the ground and millions more on television, is a public holiday for the city. Everywhere you go in Melbourne, someone has turned a hobby into a religion, and the city has decided this is worth celebrating.
