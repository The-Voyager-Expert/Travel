A single dynasty built this city over seven centuries, and then the twentieth century nearly destroyed it twice — once by bombs, once by ideology. What you walk through today is a city that rebuilt itself on purpose, choosing tradition not out of ignorance but out of defiance.

738 Years of One Family

The Wittelsbach dynasty ruled Bavaria from 1180 to 1918. That is not a typo. One family, unbroken, for 738 years — longer than the Habsburgs held Austria, longer than any ruling house in European history. Every major building you will see in Munich's Altstadt exists because a Wittelsbach decided to build it. The Residenz, which grew room by room over four centuries into the largest city palace in Germany. The Theatinerkirche, commissioned in 1663 to celebrate the birth of an heir. The Frauenkirche, whose twin onion domes became the city's symbol and whose architect was told the towers could be no taller than the tallest existing structure in town — so he capped them with those distinctive copper domes to stay just under the limit.

You feel the Wittelsbachs most clearly at the Residenz. It is not one building but a complex of ten courtyards and 130 rooms, each generation adding its own wing in whatever style was fashionable at the time — Renaissance, Baroque, Rococo, Neoclassical. The Antiquarium, built in the 1570s, is the largest Renaissance hall north of the Alps, and it was built for one purpose: to house the duke's collection of Roman busts. That tells you everything about what the Wittelsbachs wanted Munich to be. Not just a capital. A rival to Rome.

Marienplatz — the City's Stage

Every city has a central square, but Marienplatz has been Munich's stage in a way that is hard to overstate. The golden column at its center, the Mariensaule, went up in 1638 to celebrate the end of Swedish occupation during the Thirty Years' War. The Neues Rathaus — the enormous neo-Gothic city hall that dominates the north side — was built between 1867 and 1909, and its Glockenspiel draws crowds at 11 a.m. and noon every day to watch 32 mechanical figures reenact two stories from Munich's history: a jousting tournament from 1568 and the Schafflertanz, a coopers' dance that supposedly celebrated the end of a plague.

But this square has also been the stage for Munich's worst moments. Hitler held rallies here. The square was the symbolic heart of the movement that called itself National Socialism, and the Feldherrnhalle on the nearby Odeonsplatz was where the Beer Hall Putsch ended in gunfire in November 1923. Munich was designated the "Capital of the Movement" by the Nazi regime. The city does not hide this. The documentation center at the former Brown House site on Brienner Strasse presents the history without flinching, and the stumbling stones — small brass plaques set into sidewalks — mark the last known addresses of deported residents. You will step on them without noticing, until you notice one, and then you see them everywhere.

The Beer Hall as Social Institution

The Hofbrauhaus is the most famous beer hall on earth, and it carries a stain that the city has chosen to address head-on rather than erase. Adolf Hitler gave one of his earliest major public speeches in the Hofbrauhaus festival hall on February 24, 1920, presenting the Nazi Party's 25-point program to a crowd of nearly 2,000. The building itself dates to 1589, when Duke Wilhelm V founded the royal brewery because he was tired of importing expensive beer from Einbeck. That origin — a duke who wanted cheaper beer — is more Munich than anything else you will hear.

Munich still drinks in the Hofbrauhaus. Not to forget what happened there, but because six centuries of daily life do not yield the floor to twelve years of horror.

The beer hall tradition is genuine. The Augustiner-Keller, the Paulaner am Nockherberg, the Lowenbraukeller — these are not themed restaurants. They are social infrastructure. The long wooden tables are designed so strangers sit together, and the one-liter Mass is designed so you stay long enough to talk to them. Bavarians have a word for this: Gemutlichkeit, which translates poorly as "coziness" but really means a shared agreement to slow down and be present. The beer gardens work on the same principle. The Englischer Garten's Chinesischer Turm beer garden seats 7,000 people. The Hirschgarten seats 8,000. These are not novelty venues. They fill on any warm evening.

Nymphenburg and the Olympic Park

Nymphenburg Palace sits at the western edge of the city, and its scale tells you what the Wittelsbachs thought of themselves by the late seventeenth century. The main building stretches over 600 meters from wing to wing. The park behind it covers 200 hectares. Max Emanuel, who commissioned the expansion, had just been appointed governor of the Spanish Netherlands and wanted a summer residence that matched his ambitions. The result is a palace that could hold Versailles' gaze without blinking.

At the opposite end of the city, the Olympic Park represents a different kind of ambition. Built for the 1972 Summer Olympics, the park's tent-like acrylic roof — designed by Frei Otto and Gunter Behnisch — was meant to project a new image of Germany: open, transparent, democratic. The architecture succeeded. What followed did not. On September 5, 1972, Palestinian militants from the Black September organization took eleven Israeli athletes and coaches hostage in the Olympic Village. A botched rescue attempt at the military airfield in Furstenfeldbruck ended with all eleven hostages dead, along with five of the eight attackers and one German police officer. The event changed global security permanently. Every metal detector you walk through at an airport, every credential check at a stadium — the thread traces back to Munich, 1972. The memorial at Connollystrasse 31 in the Olympic Village is quiet and plain, which is exactly right.

Industry, Gardens, and the Eisbach Wave

BMW's headquarters sit directly next to the Olympic Park, in a building shaped like a four-cylinder engine. The company was founded in Munich in 1916 as a manufacturer of aircraft engines — Bayerische Motoren Werke, Bavarian Motor Works — and its blue-and-white logo represents a spinning propeller against a Bavarian sky, or at least that is the story the company has told since the 1920s (the colors actually come from the Bavarian state flag, and the propeller story was a later marketing invention). What matters is that BMW, Siemens, Allianz, and Munich Re all chose to stay headquartered here. Munich is the wealthiest city in Germany, with the lowest unemployment rate of any major German city, and that wealth is visible in ways both obvious and subtle — the pristine facades, the well-kept parks, the fact that nothing looks neglected.

The Englischer Garten is larger than Central Park in New York and Hyde Park in London. It was created in 1789 by an American-born physicist named Benjamin Thompson, who had entered the service of the Bavarian elector and was later made Count Rumford. His idea was radical for the time: a public park, open to everyone, not just the aristocracy. It worked. On a summer afternoon, you will find office workers on their lunch break, university students reading on the grass, and — at the southern entrance where the Eisbach canal passes under a small bridge — surfers. The standing wave on the Eisbach is about one meter high and has been surfed illegally since the 1970s. The city legalized it in 2010. A line of surfers waits on the bank at all hours, and each rider gets the wave for about thirty seconds before peeling off to let the next person in. It is the most Munich thing imaginable: orderly, precise, and quietly eccentric.

Twenty Minutes from Dachau

The Dachau concentration camp memorial is a twenty-minute train ride from Munich's central station. That proximity is the point. Dachau was the first concentration camp the Nazi regime opened, on March 22, 1933 — less than two months after Hitler became chancellor. It operated for twelve years. More than 41,500 people died there. The camp sits on the edge of the town of Dachau, a place that had been known for its artists' colony and its Renaissance castle before 1933 and has been known for only one thing since.

The memorial is unsentimental. The barracks have been reconstructed to show the conditions. The crematorium is intact. The inscription at the entrance — "Arbeit macht frei" — is the same lie that greeted prisoners. What strikes you most is the flatness of the grounds, the way you can see from one end to the other, the way there is nowhere to hide. Munich does not put distance between itself and Dachau. The S-Bahn runs there. School groups visit. It is part of the city in the same way that the Residenz and the beer gardens are part of the city, because pretending otherwise would be its own form of dishonesty.

Freistaat Bayern

Bavaria still officially calls itself a Freistaat — a Free State — and the distinction is not ceremonial. Bavarians will tell you, with varying degrees of seriousness, that they are Bavarian first and German second. The dialect is different. The food is different. The Catholic identity is different from Protestant northern Germany. When the German constitution was ratified in 1949, Bavaria was the only state that voted against it — not because it opposed democracy, but because it wanted more autonomy. It joined anyway, because the constitution's terms specified that it would take effect with a two-thirds majority of the states regardless.

Munich is simultaneously the most traditional and the wealthiest city in Germany, and it sees no contradiction between the two.

That stubbornness is the key to Munich. You see it in the Oktoberfest, which has run every year since 1810 and still requires that only Munich breweries serve beer in the tents. You see it in the building codes that prevent any structure in the city center from rising above the Frauenkirche's towers. You see it in the dress code: Lederhosen and Dirndl are not costumes here. People wear them to work on Fridays during Oktoberfest, and to weddings, and to the opera. This is a city with a BMW plant and a medieval Christmas market, a subway system and a royal palace, a tech industry and a standing rule that beer gardens must close by 10:30 p.m. to respect the neighbors. It holds all of it at once, and if you ask a Munchner how, they will look at you as if the question makes no sense.
