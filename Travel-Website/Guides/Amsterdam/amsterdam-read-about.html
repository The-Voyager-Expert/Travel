You're arriving in the wealthiest city the seventeenth century produced — and almost all of it is still standing. Start there.

The dam in the river

Look at the name. Amsterdam is a dam — a dam across the Amstel river, built after the All Saints' Flood of 1170 to keep the water at bay. The word is baked into the city's name in a way that still makes geographic sense: the center grew around the point where the river was blocked, and for centuries you could only reach it by crossing water. That hasn't changed as much as you'd think.

The growth is almost comically fast once you see it on a timeline. A thousand people in 1300. Thirty thousand by 1564. Two hundred thousand by 1660 — larger than London, larger than Rome, second in Europe only to Paris. You can walk across the center of that 1660 city in about twenty minutes. All of it is still here.

The city that fell so Amsterdam could rise

The thing that made Amsterdam is a catastrophe that happened somewhere else. In 1585, the Spanish army took Antwerp — then the commercial capital of northern Europe. Protestant merchants, skilled craftsmen, and large concentrations of money had about five minutes to decide where to go. Most went north to Amsterdam. The commercial center of a continent migrated coastward in a generation, and Amsterdam was ready for it.

What followed is called the Dutch Golden Age, and the numbers strain credulity even now. By 1670, Dutch merchant ships totaled 568,000 tons — roughly half the entire European fleet. A thousand Dutch ships entered the Baltic every year, controlling grain and timber. The VOC, the Dutch East India Company founded in 1602, was by most reckonings the first publicly traded multinational corporation in history. The Amsterdam stock exchange, created the same year to finance it, is the ancestor of every securities market you've ever heard of. The Bank of Amsterdam, opened in 1609, kept interest rates lower than anywhere in Europe. All of this built those canal houses you're about to spend a week photographing.

Amsterdam became the wealthiest city on earth — not because it had resources, but because it sat between everyone who did.

The city's real genius was the people it let in. It had no mines, no great agricultural plains, no natural resources to speak of. What it had was a harbor and a deliberate policy of tolerating people that other states had expelled. The Sephardic Jews expelled from Spain in 1492 and Portugal in 1496 brought merchant networks spanning the Mediterranean. Huguenots pushed out of France after 1685 brought craft skills and capital. Flemish Protestants fleeing the Spanish Netherlands brought everything the southern cities had built. Amsterdam's famous tolerance wasn't pure enlightenment — it was also smart commerce. The merchants who ran this city understood that talent expelled elsewhere was talent available here. They were right.

The canal belt

Walk the Herengracht on a quiet morning before the tour boats start and you're looking at what the Golden Age actually built. In the early seventeenth century, Amsterdam's engineers dug a concentric ring of four major canals around the medieval center — by hand, over decades, proceeding from west to east across the city. The Grachtengordel, the canal belt, was finished in the 1660s. It's a UNESCO World Heritage Site now, for reasons you'll understand immediately.

The three main canals tell you the social geography at a glance. The Herengracht is the Gentlemen's Canal — the governing merchant class built here, and the Golden Bend stretch near the center is still among the priciest addresses in Europe. The Keizersgracht, the Emperor's Canal, is the widest. The Prinsengracht, the Prince's Canal, runs along the outer edge, past the Westerkerk and the building at number 263 where a family hid for 761 days.

Look at the canal houses and you'll start noticing things that don't initially make sense. The facades are narrow — rarely more than nine meters wide — because the city taxed street-width frontage, so the houses grew tall and thin, pushing back into deep lots. The staircases inside are nearly vertical (you'll notice this at your hotel), so every house was built with iron hooks and hoisting beams extending from the roofline: furniture went up by rope. Look closely and you'll see that most facades lean slightly forward — deliberately, so that hoisted goods cleared the windows below. The gables at the top — stepped, neck-shaped, bell-curved, classical — were required to be individual, to distinguish neighbors within the unified streetscape. Over 100 kilometers of canals, 90 islands, 1,500 bridges, 1,550 monumental buildings. The whole thing is still here.

The canal ring was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2010. It earned it.

Rembrandt, Vermeer, and the art of a prosperous republic

The same decades that dug the canals produced the Dutch Golden Age in painting, and the two things are connected more directly than they might seem. The merchant class that built those Herengracht mansions was the market for art — not a church commissioning altarpieces, not a court ordering royal portraits, but a prosperous middle class that wanted pictures for their walls. They commissioned portraits of themselves, paintings of their ships, still lifes of the things that made them rich: tulips, spices, pewter. The demand was broad and commercial, and it produced something unprecedented in European art history.

Go to the Rijksmuseum and walk to the back of the main gallery. The room at the end was built specifically for one painting: The Night Watch, completed by Rembrandt in 1642. It is nearly four meters tall and nearly five meters wide. It is technically a group portrait of a civic militia company — a routine commission. What Rembrandt did with it was create a scene of orchestrated drama using light and shadow at a scale no one had attempted. Stand in front of it for ten minutes before you read anything about it. You'll feel why it matters before you understand why.

Rembrandt died in poverty in 1669, his house on the Jodenbreestraat sold to pay debts — that house is now the Rembrandt House Museum, worth a few hours. He is buried in the Westerkerk on the Prinsengracht. Johannes Vermeer, his greatest contemporary, never lived in Amsterdam — he spent his life in Delft — but his work is inseparable from the world the Golden Age made. He died in 1675 leaving debt and scattered paintings and a name so forgotten that several of his works were attributed to other artists. The Milkmaid at the Rijksmuseum is his. It hangs near the Night Watch and is much smaller than you expect.

The house on Prinsengracht

Book your Anne Frank House tickets before you leave home. They sell out weeks in advance, and there's no good alternative to having them.

On July 6, 1942, Otto Frank and his family climbed the stairs behind the bookcase on the third floor of Prinsengracht 263 and didn't come back down for 761 days. Otto had been running a pectin and spice company from the building; the concealed annex behind it had been prepared over the preceding weeks by employees who knew what was coming. Four more people joined them — the van Pels family and Fritz Pfeffer. Eight people lived in the Secret Annex, sustained by a small network of helpers, among them Miep Gies.

Germany had invaded the Netherlands on May 10, 1940. Amsterdam's Jewish population at the time was roughly 80,000. More than 100,000 Dutch Jews were eventually deported and murdered — 56,521 at Auschwitz, 34,082 at Sobibor. The Netherlands lost three-quarters of its Jewish population, the highest proportion of any Western European country under Nazi occupation.

On February 25, 1941, Amsterdam's non-Jewish workers staged a general strike to protest the first major Nazi roundup of Jewish men — unprecedented in occupied Europe. About 300,000 people stopped work for something being done to people who were not them. It was crushed within two days.

A statue of a dockworker on Jonas Daniël Meijerplein commemorates that strike. Most visitors to the Anne Frank House don't make it there. You should.

The group in the annex was discovered on August 4, 1944, and deported. Otto Frank was the only survivor. Miep Gies salvaged the diary from the floor of the annex after the arrest and returned it to him when he came back. The Diary of a Young Girl appeared in Dutch in 1947. The museum opened May 3, 1960. The building was built in 1635. The Franks were from Frankfurt. The history of the Netherlands and of the twentieth century runs through this address in ways that are impossible to fully account for.

The museums

The Rijksmuseum is the kind of museum that recalibrates your expectations of what a national collection can be. Founded in 1798, moved to Amsterdam in 1808, installed in its current building — designed by Pierre Cuypers, who also designed Centraal Station — in 1885, then closed for a ten-year, €375 million renovation before reopening in 2013. It holds a million objects spanning eight hundred years; about eight thousand are on display, including more than two thousand paintings from the Dutch Golden Age. Budget at least three hours. Don't miss the Dutch naval paintings if you want to understand what the Golden Age actually looked like from the water.

The Van Gogh Museum is a five-minute walk on the Museumplein. Van Gogh was not from Amsterdam — born in North Brabant, painted in Paris and Arles and Saint-Rémy, died in Auvers-sur-Oise in 1890, never sold a painting in his lifetime. The museum holds 200 paintings, 400 drawings, and 700 letters — the world's largest collection — because his sister-in-law Johanna van Gogh-Bonger spent decades after his death organizing and championing the work. Without her, it scatters. The Yellow House, The Potato Eaters, the Sunflowers series, nine self-portraits, and the letters that make him the most documented artist in history are all here. Don't skip the letters.

The bicycle and what it means

You'll notice the bikes immediately — there are more bicycles in Amsterdam than people. The city has roughly 850,000 residents and over a million bikes. About a third of all trips are made by bicycle; two-thirds of daily urban movement runs on two wheels. The cycle paths are red-surfaced, 515 kilometers of them, and the etiquette is that pedestrians don't walk on them and cyclists don't yield to anyone. Learn this fast.

The cycling culture isn't ancient. In the 1970s Amsterdam faced the same pressure toward car-centric planning that reshaped most Western cities. What changed the trajectory was the 1973 oil crisis, a grassroots movement called Stop de Kindermoord — "Stop the Child Murder," a response to rising traffic fatalities among children — and a series of deliberate policy decisions to restrict car access and invest in cycling infrastructure. Car access was cut through "knips," physical road interruptions that forced drivers onto longer routes while keeping cycle paths direct. It worked: cycling's share of trips rose 40 percent over twenty years.

Watch the cyclists at any intersection for ten minutes and you'll understand the democracy of it. Politicians, students, grandmothers, businesspeople in suits — all on the same red paths, all moving at the same pace, nobody in a helmet. The canal belt, built in the seventeenth century for a city that moved by boat, turns out to be a nearly perfect cycling city: the ring of water controls where cars can go, and the cycle paths run alongside the water. Twelve to fifteen thousand bicycles are dredged from the canals each year by municipal barges. What happens to them afterward is not the city's most pressing question.

What the city has become

Before you go, here's what to carry with you: Amsterdam was built by people who had been told to leave somewhere else. The Jews expelled from Spain. The Huguenots pushed out of France. The Flemish Protestants fleeing the Spanish Netherlands. Amsterdam's regents made a calculation that talent expelled elsewhere was talent available here, and the city that welcomed the exiles became the wealthiest in the world. Then it stayed open. The 180 nationalities represented among its residents today are the long tail of a policy that's been running for four hundred years.

Once you know that, you see it everywhere. The canal houses were built narrow because foreigners couldn't hold too much land, but the city needed them anyway. The stock exchange was invented here because traders spread across multiple nations needed a shared way to manage risk. The February Strike happened because the people of Amsterdam had a four-hundred-year habit of betting on each other's survival. The city that attracts twenty million tourists a year and complains about all of them is the same city that built its identity on the idea that outsiders make the place.

The 180 nationalities among Amsterdam's residents have roots in a policy that's been running for four hundred years. It hasn't resolved the contradiction between openness and overcrowding. Go while it's still wrestling with it.

Walk the Jordaan on a quiet afternoon. Get out to the canal belt before the tour boats start. Stand in the February Strike memorial square. Spend time with the letters at the Van Gogh Museum. The VOC is gone, the merchant ships are gone, the world that built those houses is four centuries away — but the logic that built it, the logic of the open door, is still legible in every canal, every gable, every borrowed face you'll see on the street.
