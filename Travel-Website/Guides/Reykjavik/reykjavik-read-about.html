The world's northernmost capital sits on a volcanic island in the North Atlantic — and it knows exactly what it is.

The first settler and what he found

When the Norse chieftain Ingólfr Arnarson sailed into a bay on Iceland's southwest coast in 874 and saw steam rising from the ground, he named the place Reykjavik: Smoky Bay. The steam was from geothermal vents, not fires — hot water and gas pushing up through the volcanic rock that this island is made of. He built his longhouse there, and Reykjavik has been continuously inhabited ever since, which makes it simultaneously one of the oldest European settlements in the North Atlantic and one of the youngest capital cities in Europe. It wasn't formally recognized as a trading town until 1786, and it remained a modest fishing village for most of the centuries in between.

The steam Ingólfr saw is still rising. Reykjavik's entire heating system runs on it. The pipes that carry geothermal hot water under the city's streets are among the reasons Iceland runs almost entirely on renewable energy: nearly a hundred percent of the country's electricity comes from geothermal and hydroelectric sources. The hot water in your hotel taps comes from the same geological process that Ingólfr stood in front of twelve hundred years ago, watching and naming. Iceland is the place where the Mid-Atlantic Ridge breaks the surface, where the North American and Eurasian tectonic plates are pulling apart at the rate of two centimeters a year, and the whole island is basically the byproduct of that slow geological argument.

A republic founded on a parliamentary tradition older than almost any other

In 930, the Norse settlers who had spread across Iceland established the Althing — a national assembly that met annually at Þingvellir, a dramatic rift valley about forty kilometers east of Reykjavik where the tectonic plates have pulled apart visibly. The Althing is one of the oldest parliaments in the world, predating the English Parliament by three centuries. It was not a democracy in any modern sense — power belonged to the chieftains who could afford to attend — but it was a genuinely collective governance structure, a place where disputes were adjudicated by law rather than pure force, where decisions required consensus among competing factions.

Go to Þingvellir before or after you've been to the parliament building in central Reykjavik. The modern Althing, which has sat in a modest nineteenth-century building just off Austurvöllur square since 1881, is one of the least imposing legislative buildings in Europe — deliberately so. The contrast with Þingvellir, where you can stand in the rift valley and look at the raw geology of a continent pulling itself apart while understanding that people stood in this same place and argued about law for over a thousand years, makes both places more interesting.

At Þingvellir in the year 1000, the Althing resolved a potential civil war by a remarkable compromise: Iceland would officially convert to Christianity, but pagans could continue their practices in private. This was religious tolerance, in the North Atlantic, a thousand years ago.

Iceland came under Norwegian rule in 1262, then Danish rule in 1380, and didn't regain full independence until 1944 — when, while Denmark was under German occupation, Icelanders held a referendum and voted to dissolve their union with the Danish crown. They restored the Althing and declared a republic on June 17, the birthday of the nineteenth-century independence leader Jón Sigurðsson. The Danes, occupied and unable to object, couldn't stop them. The timing was not accidental.

Hallgrímskirkja and the argument it makes about scale

The church that defines Reykjavik's skyline — the concrete spire of Hallgrímskirkja rising seventy-four meters above the city — was designed by Guðjón Samúelsson and took forty-one years to complete, from the first sketches in 1937 to the final consecration in 1986. When you stand in front of it, you understand immediately what Samúelsson was looking at when he designed it. The stepped colonnades that flank the tower are basalt columns — the same hexagonal formations you see at Svartifoss waterfall in the south, at the Giant's Causeway in Northern Ireland, at hundreds of volcanic sites where lava cooled slowly in the right conditions. He took the geology of Iceland and turned it into a church.

Reykjavik spreads out in every direction below the observation deck at the top: the colorful tin-roofed houses, the harbor, the flat silver expanse of the bay, the mountains that ring the city on the landward side. On a clear day you can see Snæfellsjökull, the glacier-capped volcano on the Snæfellsnes peninsula that Jules Verne used as the entrance to the center of the earth in his 1864 novel. It sits about a hundred and fifteen kilometers to the northwest, visible from the church tower, looking exactly like the entrance to something.

How a fishing village became a literary capital

Iceland has more writers, publishers, and books per capita than almost any country on earth. More books are published in Iceland per person per year than anywhere else, a fact Icelanders will tell you with a satisfied lack of surprise. The tradition goes back to the medieval sagas — the prose narratives written in Iceland in the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries that documented the settlement of Iceland, the explorations of Leif Erikson, the feuds and honor-killings and complicated inheritances of the founding Norse families.

The sagas are unlike anything else in medieval literature. They're not courtly romances or religious allegories — they're close to journalism, written in a flat, declarative style that reports events without much moralizing, that treats women and slaves and Greenlandic Inuit as subjects worth describing, that contains genuine psychological complexity alongside the expected catalogue of violence. Njáls saga, which chronicles a decades-long blood feud in the Icelandic south, runs to three hundred pages and contains characters you can still find modern equivalents of at any dinner party.

The Saga Museum in Reykjavik attempts to bring this world to life with wax figures and recorded narration, and it is somewhat campy in the way that wax museums tend to be. Go anyway. The Reykjavik City Library has a permanent exhibition on the saga manuscripts that is quieter and more useful for understanding what you're actually dealing with when you read one of these texts — vellum pages covered in an Icelandic hand that was standardized across the country before standardized spelling existed almost anywhere else.

The financial crash and what it revealed

In October 2008, Iceland's three major banks collapsed simultaneously. Kaupthing, Glitnir, and Landsbanki had grown to hold assets worth ten times Iceland's entire GDP — a reckless over-expansion driven by a decade of deregulation and the specific confidence of a small country that had suddenly discovered it could play in international financial markets. When the global credit crisis hit, the banks failed within days of each other. The krónur lost half its value against the euro overnight. Iceland technically went bankrupt.

What happened next was unusual. The Icelandic government, under massive international pressure to bail out the banks and guarantee foreign depositors, largely refused. The banks were allowed to fail. The people who had lent money to Icelandic banks, including hundreds of thousands of British and Dutch depositors who had been drawn by high interest rates on Icesave accounts, were largely left to seek restitution through the bankruptcy proceedings rather than through taxpayer guarantees. Iceland imposed capital controls, accepted an IMF loan, prosecuted and jailed the bankers involved (an almost unique outcome in post-2008 Western democracies), and proceeded to recover faster than almost anyone expected.

The Icelanders did not hold a polite inquiry. They formed a "pots and pans revolution" and banged cookware outside the parliament until the government resigned. Then they elected a new parliament with a significantly higher proportion of women and people who had not previously been politicians.

The protest movement also spawned an experiment in direct democracy: a crowd-sourced constitutional revision process in which ordinary Icelanders participated in drafting a new constitution via social media. The new constitution was never formally adopted — the political establishment found ways to stall it — but the process itself was watched by the rest of the world as a possible model for something. Walking around Reykjavik today, you're in a city that crashed harder than almost anywhere else in 2008 and rebuilt itself without the political convulsions that crashed harder-and-recovered-slower places experienced. There's something in the national character about this — the same quality that made settlement in this particular spot seem reasonable in the first place.

Light, dark, and what they do to the city

In June, the sun sets after midnight and rises before four. The quality of light at eleven p.m. in Reykjavik in midsummer — a low, amber, endlessly angled glow that makes the colored houses on Skólavörðustígur look like they're lit from within — is not replicated anywhere at a reasonable hour of the day elsewhere on earth. The famous midnight sun is not a gimmick. It genuinely rewrites your sense of time: you eat dinner at nine because you're not hungry until nine, you stay out until two because it still feels like evening, you sleep badly because your body can't believe it's night.

In December, the sun rises around eleven and sets around three. The city runs on something between determination and caffeine. The Northern Lights are visible from within Reykjavik on clear dark nights, though they're better the further you get from the city's light pollution. The geothermal pools — Laugardalslaug in the east end, the neighborhood pools scattered through the suburbs — are at peak social function in winter, when sitting outside in ninety-degree water while snow falls around you transitions from novelty to genuine necessity.

The Icelanders treat their pools the way other cultures treat bars or coffee shops: as the primary venue for unhurried social conversation. Don't sit in the hot pot and look at your phone. Sit in the hot pot and talk to whoever is sitting next to you. This will feel strange and then it will feel completely natural, which is the arc of most things in Iceland.

What Iceland is actually made of

Here is the thing to carry with you before you board: Iceland is not an ordinary place wearing an extraordinary hat. It is extraordinary all the way down. The geology is active — there are volcanoes here that have disrupted European air travel within living memory, glaciers that are visibly retreating, geysers that erupt on schedules that have been reliable for centuries. The culture is old in the specific way that oral traditions stay old — the sagas are not academic texts, they're stories that Icelandic schoolchildren still know. The political history is built on a thousand years of self-governance by people who had very little state apparatus to rely on and developed, in response, an unusual degree of civic directness.

Reykjavik the city is small enough to hold in your head after two days — you can walk the old center in an afternoon, and most of what's worth seeing is within a kilometer of Hallgrímskirkja. But Iceland is not Reykjavik. It's the ring road, and the lava fields, and the glaciers at the edge of the world, and the hot water rising through the cracks in the ground the way it was rising when Ingólfr arrived and looked at the steam and named it Smoky Bay. The city is the entry point. The island is the whole argument.
