Bruges was once the richest city in northern Europe. Then the water receded, the money left, and the medieval city that remained was too poor to tear itself down. That turned out to be the best thing that ever happened to it.

THE CITY THAT FROZE

You walk into Bruges and something feels off. Not wrong, exactly, but suspended. The stepped gables along the canals look like they belong in a painting from the 1400s, and they do, because most of them are from the 1400s. The cobblestones, the brick facades, the church towers crowding the skyline. Almost every medieval trading city in Europe tore itself apart and rebuilt in the centuries that followed. Bruges never did. It could not afford to.

In the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries, Bruges was the commercial capital of northern Europe. It sat on the Zwin, a tidal inlet that connected the city to the North Sea, and through that narrow channel flowed more wealth than any city north of the Alps had ever seen. English wool arrived by ship. Flemish weavers turned it into the finest cloth on the continent. Italian bankers set up offices on the canals. The word "bourse," the European term for a stock exchange, comes from a family of innkeepers in Bruges named Van der Beurze, whose house on Vlamingstraat became the meeting point for international merchants.

Then the Zwin silted up. Slowly, over the course of the fifteenth century, the channel that had made Bruges rich filled with sand. Ships could no longer reach the city. The merchants moved to Antwerp. The bankers followed. By 1500, Bruges was a backwater, and it would remain one for the next four hundred years. The population shrank. The grand houses emptied. The cloth halls fell quiet. And because no one had the money to demolish the medieval buildings and put up something modern, they simply stayed.

The greatest cities destroy themselves through success. They grow rich, tear down the old, and build the new. Bruges grew poor, and kept everything.

WOOL, CLOTH, AND THE HALLEN

The Belfry is the first thing you see when you step into the Markt, and it tells you everything about what Bruges used to be. It is not a church tower. It is not a palace tower. It is a commercial tower, built to watch over trade, store the city's treasury, and ring its market bells. The 83-metre tower was the beating heart of a mercantile city, and the Hallen beneath it were the cloth halls where Flemish textiles were bought and sold.

The cloth trade made Bruges. English sheep produced the raw wool. Flemish artisans, working in cities like Bruges, Ghent, and Ypres, wove it into fabric that was sold across Europe and into the Mediterranean. At the trade's peak, Bruges was processing more wool and moving more money than London. The Markt was the marketplace. The Belfry was the watchtower. The Hallen were the trading floor. Everything you see in that square was built to serve commerce, not God and not a king.

The civic buildings on the Burg, the second great square just a few minutes' walk away, tell the other half of the story. The Gothic City Hall, the oldest in the Low Countries, is where the city governed itself. Bruges was not ruled by a feudal lord in any meaningful sense. The merchants ran it. The guilds ran it. The wealth that flowed through the Zwin gave the city the leverage to negotiate its own charter, set its own laws, and build institutions that answered to trade, not to a throne.

THE FLEMISH PRIMITIVES

Jan van Eyck moved to Bruges in 1432 and spent the rest of his life here. He came because this was where the money was. He and the painters who followed him, Hans Memling chief among them, did not invent oil painting from scratch, but they perfected it. They figured out how to layer translucent glazes of oil paint to create depth, luminosity, and detail that no one in Europe had achieved before. The jewels in a Van Eyck painting look wet. The fur looks like you could touch it. The faces look like they are about to speak.

The Groeninge Museum holds the proof. Van Eyck's work hangs alongside Memling's and Gerard David's and a handful of other painters who are grouped under the label "Flemish Primitives," a name that makes no sense because there is nothing primitive about them. They were the most technically advanced painters in fifteenth-century Europe, and they were in Bruges because Bruges had the patrons, the wealth, and the international connections to support them. When the money left, the painters left too. But the paintings stayed, because no other city wanted to buy them from a place that could no longer afford to ship them.

THE CANALS AND THE BEGIJNHOF

The Reie runs through Bruges the way the canals run through any Low Countries city. They were not decorative. They were infrastructure: highways for cargo, drainage systems for the flat land, and the reason the city existed where it did. The Rozenhoedkaai, the most photographed spot in the city, is pretty now, but it was a working quay where goods were loaded and unloaded.

South of the center, the city softens. The Begijnhof is a walled courtyard of white houses surrounding a green lawn and a stand of old trees. It was founded in 1245 as a community for beguines, lay religious women who lived communally but took no permanent vows. They worked, they prayed, they answered to no husband and no convent. The community lasted until the twentieth century. Benedictine nuns live there now. You walk through the gate and the city noise stops. It is one of the quietest places in Belgium.

Beyond the Begijnhof, the Minnewater sits at the edge of the old city. The name translates roughly as "Lake of Love," which is a tourist invention. It was a commercial harbor, the inner port where barges turned around. But the willows hang over the water now, the swans drift across it, and the commercial past is easy to forget.

CHOCOLATE AND BEER

Belgium's two great edible exports converge in Bruges with a density that borders on absurd. There are more chocolate shops per square metre in the city center than anywhere else in the country, and Belgium already has more chocolate shops per capita than any country on earth.

Belgian chocolate is different from Swiss or French chocolate for a specific reason: Belgian law requires a minimum cocoa butter content that is higher than most other countries mandate. The result is a smoother, more meltable product. The praline, the filled chocolate shell, was invented in Brussels in 1912 by Jean Neuhaus, and Belgian chocolatiers have been refining the format ever since. In Bruges, the concentration is so thick that you can walk from one shop to the next without losing sight of either.

The beer runs just as deep. De Halve Maan, the last working brewery inside the city walls, has been brewing on the same site since 1856. In 2016, they built a three-kilometre underground pipeline from the brewery to a bottling plant outside the city because they could not get enough tanker trucks through the medieval streets. The Trappist tradition is a short drive away. Westvleteren, often called the best beer in the world, is brewed by monks at the Abbey of Saint Sixtus, about thirty kilometres from Bruges. You cannot buy it in stores. You call a phone line, reserve a case, and drive to the abbey gate to pick it up.

They built a pipeline under a medieval city because the streets were too narrow for beer trucks. That is Belgium in a single sentence.

THE LIVING MUSEUM PROBLEM

The 2008 film "In Bruges" gave the city a cultural moment it had not had in centuries. Colin Farrell's character, a hitman hiding out in the city, spends most of the movie complaining about how boring it is. The joke worked because it played on something real: Bruges is extraordinarily beautiful and extraordinarily quiet, and those two qualities exist in tension.

The population of the historic center has been declining for decades. Young Belgians move to Ghent or Brussels or Antwerp, cities with jobs and nightlife and universities. The people who remain in the center are increasingly outnumbered by the tourists who visit it. On a summer day, the Markt and the streets around it are packed. The horse carriages circle. The boat tours fill. The waffle stands multiply. After six in the evening, the center empties out. The restaurants serve dinner to visitors. The shops close. The cobblestones go quiet.

This is the paradox of preservation. A city that was frozen by poverty is now sustained by the tourism that poverty made possible. The medieval buildings survived because no one could afford to replace them. Now they survive because millions of people pay to look at them. Whether that makes Bruges a living city or an open-air museum depends on what time of day you visit and how far from the Markt you walk.

THE ACCIDENT THAT SAVED IT

Every great medieval trading city in Europe faced the same choice as it grew wealthier in the centuries after the Renaissance. London rebuilt after the Great Fire and kept rebuilding. Amsterdam tore down its walls and expanded. Paris was gutted by Haussmann. Antwerp industrialized. The pattern is universal: wealth destroys the old city and builds a new one in its place.

Bruges never had that wealth. The Zwin silted up, the trade routes shifted, and the city sat in its own medieval shell for four centuries with no money and no reason to change. By the time tourism rediscovered it in the late nineteenth century, what visitors found was a complete medieval city, intact in a way that no other city in northern Europe could match. The buildings were there because demolition costs money. The canals were there because filling them in costs money. The street plan was there because no one had the capital or the ambition to redesign it.

You can walk the same streets that Van Eyck walked. You can stand in the same Markt where the cloth merchants traded. You can look up at the same Belfry that has been watching over the city since the thirteenth century. None of this was planned. None of it was preserved out of reverence or foresight. It was preserved by accident, by the simple fact that a city that lost everything could not afford to lose anything more. Bruges is what happens when a city's worst disaster turns out to be its only salvation.
