Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 5 min · 🚕 2 min → Plaza Independencia
1. Plaza Independencia
↳ Gateway between the 18th-century Ciudad Vieja grid and the modern city center, anchored by the equestrian José Artigas monument above his mausoleum. Surrounded by Palacio Salvo, Teatro Solís, and Palacio Estévez — Montevideo's symbolic threshold between colonial and modern Uruguay.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Plaza Independencia · Ciudad Vieja
🚶 1 min · 🚕 1 min → Palacio Salvo
2. Palacio Salvo
↳ Designed by Mario Palanti, this Art Deco tower held the title of tallest building in South America for over a decade after it was built. Its 26 floors fuse neo-Gothic ornament and Palladian massing in a way that defies category — the building's silhouette remains the most recognizable in all of Uruguay.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~20 min
📍 Plaza Independencia 848 · Ciudad Vieja
🚶 3 min · 🚕 1 min → Teatro Solís
3. Teatro Solís
↳ Montevideo's flagship opera house has been the cultural nerve center of the Río de la Plata for more than a century. The neoclassical facade conceals a 1,200-seat horseshoe hall where Caruso, Pavlova, and Piazzolla have all performed — and where a full season of opera, ballet, and orchestral concerts runs year-round.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Sunday 11:00am - 5:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~25 min
📍 Buenos Aires 652 · Ciudad Vieja
🚶 6 min · 🚕 2 min → Barrio Sur
4. Barrio Sur
↳ Historic enclave south of Ciudad Vieja where candombe drumming took root in the colonial era. The barrio hosts comparsa rehearsals, pasajes painted with Afro-Uruguayan murals, and the living cultural memory that makes Montevideo's Carnival the longest in the world.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Barrio Sur · south of Ciudad Vieja
🚶 7 min · 🚕 2 min → MAPI
5. MAPI
↳ The only museum in Uruguay dedicated entirely to pre-Columbian and indigenous art, spread across three floors of a restored colonial palace. Andean ceramics, Amazonian weaving, and Patagonian rock-art casts ground the colonial story of the region in the millennia that preceded it.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Sunday 11:00am - 5:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 5 min · 🚕 2 min → Cabildo
1. Cabildo
↳ This Italianate colonial hall served as the seat of Montevideo's first civic government and the birthplace of Uruguay's first Constitution. Renovated and reopened as a museum, it now exhibits silver altar pieces, colonial documents, and portraits that trace the transition from Spanish colony to independent republic.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Museo del Carnaval
↳ Uruguay's Carnival runs over 40 days — the world's longest — and this museum tells the story of Afro-Uruguayan roots, murga traditions, and drumming comparsas that fill the streets each February. Feathered headdresses, masks, and vintage photographs pack the galleries.

3. Castillo Pittamiglio
↳ Humberto Pittamiglio was an alchemist, architect, and self-described devotee of Hermes who spent over 50 years constructing this Rambla castle as a personal monument to esoteric thought. Every corbel, column, and window encodes a symbol — the castle is equal parts home, laboratory, and lifelong obsession.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~20 min
📍 Rambla República de México 1251 · Parque Rodó

4. La Rambla
↳ A 22-kilometer continuous promenade hugging the Río de la Plata, La Rambla is where Montevideo exhales. Locals jog, fish, sip mate, and watch the sunset from the same concrete balustrades that face Buenos Aires across the water — unreachable but always visible on a clear afternoon.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Rambla República de México · Parque Rodó sector
🚶 5 min · 🚕 2 min → Parque Rodó
5. Parque Rodó
↳ Named for writer José Enrique Rodó, this 26-hectare park offers rowboats on a lake, an open-air Teatro de Verano stage, and a weekly craft fair under century-old trees. On weekends it fills with families, musicians, and mate circles — a living counterpoint to the formal plazas of Ciudad Vieja.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr

1. MNAV
↳ Uruguay's national art museum occupies a purpose-built pavilion inside Parque Rodó. The permanent collection centers on Joaquín Torres García — whose universal constructivist grids hang here in original scale — alongside Pedro Figari's candombe scenes and rotating international exhibitions. Entry is always free.
📖 Wikipedia

🚫 Closed Monday

2. Fortaleza del Cerro
↳ A hilltop fortress built to guard Montevideo Bay from Portuguese encroachment, sitting atop the city's highest natural point with 360° panoramic views of the harbor, the Río de la Plata, and the city skyline. The taxi ride up Cerro hill is the most dramatic vantage point in the entire city.

3. Palacio Legislativo
↳ This neoclassical granite palace is clad in 12 types of Uruguayan marble and anchors the western edge of the city's legislative district. Free guided tours run on weekdays through the ceremonial chambers, the Senate hall, and the soaring Hall of Lost Steps.

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 15 min → El Prado & Rosedal
1. El Prado & Rosedal
↳ Montevideo's oldest park follows a European landscape plan with canopy-covered avenues, ponds, and over 800 rose varieties in the celebrated Rosedal. The rose garden remains the most serene corner of the city — a place to slow down before the stadium energy of the afternoon.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Parque El Prado · El Prado
🚶 10 min · 🚕 3 min → Jardín Botánico
2. Jardín Botánico
↳ Uruguay's national botanical garden spans 60 hectares around a cast-iron Art Nouveau greenhouse, a French formal garden, and an arboretum with native and exotic trees. Entry is free, and early-morning walks before the heat arrives catch the garden at its quiet, photogenic best.
🏛️ Daily 7:30am - 7:00pm
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Av. 19 de Abril 1181 · El Prado
🚶 30 min · 🚕 8 min → Estadio Centenario
3. Estadio Centenario
↳ Built in record time for the inaugural FIFA World Cup, the Centenario hosted all 18 matches of the first tournament — including Uruguay's final victory over Argentina. The Museo del Fútbol inside traces the full arc of Uruguayan football, from Olympic gold through the legendary Maracanazo.

1. Barrio Histórico
↳ Colonia's compact grid of cobblestone streets, whitewashed colonial walls, and ruins is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The entire quarter can be walked in a morning, but the layers of Portuguese and Spanish heritage reward lingering — this is one of the most intact colonial townscapes in South America.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Barrio Histórico · Colonia del Sacramento
🚶 3 min · 🚕 2 min → Calle de los Suspiros
2. Calle de los Suspiros
↳ Colonia's most photographed street runs one block between the harbor and the convent ruins, lined with low colonial houses whose window boxes brush the original cobblestones. The "Street of Sighs" earns its name — the atmosphere delivers fully.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~20 min
📍 Calle de los Suspiros · Barrio Histórico
🚶 3 min · 🚕 2 min → Faro de Colonia
3. Faro de Colonia
↳ Built on the foundations of a demolished convent, the lighthouse rises from the highest point of the Barrio Histórico and offers sweeping panoramic views over the Río de la Plata and the terracotta rooftops below. A small fee buys the climb and the widest view in Colonia.
📖 Wikipedia

4. Ruinas San Francisco
↳ The ruined walls of a Franciscan convent, destroyed in war and never rebuilt. The remaining stone foundations and tower walls frame a quiet open plaza — among the oldest surviving European structures anywhere in Uruguay.

🍮 Local Tastes
Chivito
↳ Uruguay's national sandwich: thin beef escalope, ham, egg, mozzarella, tomato, mayo, and black olives layered into a fluffy bun. Found at virtually every café and parrilla in the city.
📍 Café Brasilero · La Corte Restaurant · any corner café
Asado
↳ Uruguay's wood-fired beef ritual. Blood sausage, short ribs, and thin-sliced matambre are the local treasures — eaten communally at long parrilla tables over several slow hours.
📍 Mercado del Puerto · Ciudad Vieja
Mate
↳ Uruguay's national drink: bitter yerba mate sipped through a metal bombilla from a gourd, carried everywhere by locals. Accepting an offer from a stranger is a social gesture — take one sip, hand it back.
📍 Tienda Inglesa supermarket · any branch

✨ Claude Inspiration

🇵🇹 Founded Portuguese, Now Spanish

Colonia del Sacramento was founded by Portugal on land Spain claimed as its own — and the city changed hands between the two empires multiple times before Uruguay's independence resolved the dispute. Walking the Barrio Histórico on Day 5, you walk on Portuguese cobblestones laid inside a Spanish-speaking country. The dual heritage is visible in the street grid: the Portuguese quarter's irregular organic lanes end where the Spanish colonial grid begins.

⚽ Uruguay Won the World Cup at Home

The inaugural FIFA World Cup was played entirely in Montevideo — every single match, including the final. Uruguay beat Argentina in the Estadio Centenario you'll visit on Day 4. Uruguay remains the smallest country ever to host and win the World Cup in the same tournament. The stadium was built in record time for the occasion and has never hosted a more consequential match since.
