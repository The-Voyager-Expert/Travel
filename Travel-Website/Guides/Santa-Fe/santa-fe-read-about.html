The oldest state capital in the US — a high-desert city built from adobe and shaped by three flags, a Pueblo revolt, and a century of artists who came and never left.

The Oldest Capital

Santa Fe is the oldest state capital in the United States, founded by Spanish colonists in 1610 — a decade before the Pilgrims reached Plymouth Rock. The city was laid out according to Spanish colonial law, with a central plaza and a Palace of the Governors on the north side that still stands on that exact footprint today. At 7,000 feet in the Sangre de Cristo Mountains, it was a deliberate choice: high enough to command the surrounding terrain, cool in summer, close to the trading routes that connected Mexico City to the northern frontier of New Spain.

Before the Spanish arrived, the site and surrounding land had been occupied by Pueblo peoples for centuries. The Tewa-speaking people who lived at Ogapoge — near the present-day Plaza — were displaced when Governor Pedro de Peralta established the colonial capital. The tension between the Spanish colonial government and the Pueblo communities would define the city's early history and erupt in one of the most consequential events in North American history.

The Pueblo Revolt of 1680

In August 1680, Pueblo leader Popé of Ohkay Owingeh organized a coordinated uprising across the Rio Grande valley. Twenty-one Spanish missionaries were killed on the first day. Governor Antonio de Otermín was besieged in the Palace of the Governors for nine days before retreating south with the surviving Spanish settlers. For twelve years, the Pueblo people controlled their homeland. It was the only successful Native American revolt against a European colonial power to last longer than a few years.

The Spanish returned in 1692 under Governor Diego de Vargas, retaking Santa Fe in a negotiated reconquest. The relationship between the two cultures that emerged after 1692 was subtly different from what came before — less overtly coercive, more syncretic. Pueblo religious practices were tolerated to a degree they had not been before the revolt. The architecture that defines Santa Fe today — the flat-roofed adobe compound — is itself a synthesis, combining Spanish colonial building techniques with the multi-story Pueblo construction that had existed here for a thousand years.

Three Flags Over the Plaza

Santa Fe has been governed by Spain, Mexico, and the United States — three sovereign governments in 240 years. New Mexico became Mexican territory when Mexico gained independence from Spain in 1821, and the Santa Fe Trail — the commercial route connecting Missouri to New Mexico — opened that same year. American merchants arrived within months, beginning the slow commercial integration of New Mexico into the eastern United States that would precede formal annexation.

In 1846, during the Mexican-American War, General Stephen Kearny marched 1,700 soldiers up the Santa Fe Trail and took the city without firing a shot. New Mexico became a U.S. territory in 1850 and a state in 1912 — the last of the contiguous 48 states to be admitted. The Palace of the Governors flew the Confederate flag briefly in 1862 during the Battle of Glorieta Pass, sometimes called the Gettysburg of the West. Union forces won. The Confederate push into New Mexico — an attempt to reach California's gold fields — was permanently stopped.

The Artists Arrive

In the early 20th century, the Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Railway began actively marketing New Mexico to eastern tourists, commissioning paintings and posters that portrayed the Southwest as a romantic, ancient landscape. Artists followed the marketing. In 1917, a group of painters who had come to Taos — 70 miles north — established the Taos Society of Artists, the first significant art colony in the American West. Santa Fe developed its own scene simultaneously, and by the 1920s Canyon Road had become a gathering point for painters, sculptors, and writers who wanted to work in a place that felt unlike anywhere in industrial America.

Georgia O'Keeffe visited New Mexico for the first time in 1917 and returned repeatedly before settling in Abiquiú, 60 miles north of Santa Fe, in 1949. She was drawn to the same qualities as the other artists: the quality of desert light, the isolation, the physical grandeur of the plateau landscape. Her paintings — bone-white skulls against cobalt sky, the red hills of Ghost Ranch, close-cropped flowers rendered as abstract color fields — became the visual language through which much of America understood the Southwest.

A City Built from Adobe

Santa Fe is unusual among American cities for having a legally enforced architectural style. In 1957, the city adopted a historic district ordinance requiring that new construction and major renovations in the old town conform to one of two styles: Pueblo Revival or Spanish Colonial Revival. The effect is that the entire downtown is visually coherent in a way that almost no American city is — warm earth tones, flat roofs, rounded parapets, vigas (wooden roof beams) protruding from exterior walls.

Adobe — sun-dried mud brick — is both the traditional building material and the visual signature of the city. It is an excellent material for the high desert climate: thick walls absorb heat during the day and release it slowly at night, moderating the wide temperature swings of the plateau. The Palace of the Governors is adobe. The Cathedral Basilica of St. Francis of Assisi, a Romanesque Revival structure that looks stone but is faced with golden sandstone, stands in deliberate contrast to the surrounding adobe blocks — Archbishop Lamy's signal that the Catholic Church was a European institution planted in new soil.

The Art Market Today

Santa Fe has one of the highest concentrations of art galleries per capita of any city in the United States, and arguably in the world. Canyon Road alone has more than 80. The Santa Fe Indian Market, held every August on the Plaza since 1922, is the largest and oldest juried Native American art show in the world — more than 1,000 artists from over 220 tribes, with sales exceeding tens of millions of dollars over the two-day weekend.

The Portal program — where Native American artists sell handcrafted work under the shaded portal of the Palace of the Governors — is also juried and limited to enrolled members of New Mexico tribes. The artists who sit along that portal represent an unbroken tradition of craft that predates the city itself. It is, in that sense, the most historically continuous thing in Santa Fe: the people who were here before the Spanish, selling work to visitors, on the same ground.

Meow Wolf and the Next Chapter

In 2015, the Meow Wolf artist collective — founded in 2008 by a group of young Santa Fe artists who were barely making rent — opened the House of Eternal Return inside a former bowling alley. The installation, funded in part by George R.R. Martin, became an immediate cultural phenomenon and invented the immersive art experience as a commercially viable form. The collective has since opened venues in Las Vegas, Denver, and Washington D.C., but the Santa Fe original remains the only one in the city where it began, in the high-desert capital that has been attracting artists with unusual visions since the railroad first brought them here a century ago.
