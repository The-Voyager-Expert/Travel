Two thousand years of occupation, reinvention, and argument with Madrid — and the scars from all of it are still visible if you know where to look.

Barcino

When you walk across Plaça de Sant Jaume — the open square in the heart of the Gothic Quarter where the Catalan government and the Barcelona City Hall face each other across a few meters of paving stones — you're standing on the site of the Roman forum. The Romans placed it here between 15 and 13 BC, under Augustus, calling the place Barcino. Two thousand years later, the civic center of Barcelona hasn't moved an inch.

Go looking for the Roman walls. Fragments of the third-century fortifications are embedded in medieval buildings throughout the Gothic Quarter — look for the big stone blocks incorporated into the bases of later structures, open to the street. And when you're in the area, find the Temple of Augustus: four Corinthian columns from the first or second century AD, one of the best-preserved Roman temples in the Iberian Peninsula, sitting inside a medieval courtyard you reach through an unmarked door off a narrow alley on Carrer del Paradís. Most visitors walk past it entirely. Don't.

The city changed hands constantly after the Romans. Visigoths in 414, who briefly made it their court. Moors in 717, who converted the cathedral to a mosque and ruled for eighty-five years. Louis the Pious, son of Charlemagne, in 801. By 878, a count named Wilfred the Hairy had established the hereditary dynasty that would rule Catalonia for four centuries. The origin myths of Catalan nationhood begin with him, and Catalonia has been telling those origin myths to itself ever since — most insistently when someone else is telling a different story.

The language and the question it carries

You'll hear Catalan everywhere in Barcelona. Not Spanish — Catalan, which is its own language with its own grammar, its own literature, and its own eight-hundred-year history. It's not a dialect of Castilian; it's a separate Romance language, closer to Occitan than to Spanish. About 95 percent of people in Barcelona understand it. Around 72 percent can speak it. The fact that you're visiting a city where 95 percent of people understand a language that was banned within living memory is worth sitting with.

The story of how Catalan gets suppressed and comes back is the story of Barcelona. In 1716, after Catalans backed the losing side in the War of Spanish Succession, Philip V abolished Catalonia's separate institutions and imposed Castilian. The Catalans mark that defeat every September 11 as their national day — the Diada Nacional — and the wound hasn't healed in three hundred years. Under Franco, who took power in 1939, Catalan was banned from schools, public administration, and publishing. By the early 1940s, newspapers and books in Catalan had nearly disappeared. The language survived in private homes and in churches — and, visibly, at Camp Nou.

FC Barcelona's motto is "Més que un club" — More than a club. Under Franco, it meant exactly that: the stadium was one of the only public spaces where Catalan identity could be expressed. Singing in Catalan, waving the senyera — this was resistance, in front of a hundred thousand people at a time.

Franco died in 1975. Within two years, more than a million people marched in Barcelona for Catalan autonomy, which was granted within a month. The independence movement has pulsed ever since: a million people in the streets again in 2010 when Spain's Constitutional Court struck down provisions in Catalonia's autonomy statute. In 2017, a disputed referendum ended with Spanish police beating voters at polling stations, the regional president fleeing into exile in Brussels, and a crisis that still isn't resolved. You're visiting a city in the middle of a long argument. That context is part of what you're seeing.

The Gothic Quarter and the city beneath it

Don't let the name mislead you. The Barri Gòtic is partly medieval, partly Roman, and partly early twentieth-century romantic reconstruction — architects in the 1920s and 1930s moved buildings around to create a more satisfying "medieval" streetscape. Some of what looks authentically thirteenth-century is a 1920s pastiche. This doesn't make it less interesting. It makes it a useful lesson in how cities construct their own history.

The thing to do is go underground — literally. The Barcelona City History Museum, the MUHBA, has excavated beneath the Gothic Quarter and built walkways over the remains of the original Roman city, six meters below street level. You walk on glass floors over preserved streets, workshops, winemaking facilities, and laundry rooms from two thousand years ago. It's one of the great small museum experiences in Europe, and most visitors skip it for the cathedral. Don't.

In the call — the medieval Jewish quarter, a maze of narrow lanes near the Plaça de Sant Jaume — archaeologists found in 1996 what may be one of the oldest surviving synagogues in Europe. The medieval Jewish community here was one of the largest in Catalonia until the pogrom of 1391 and the subsequent expulsions. The cathedral keeps a flock of thirteen white geese in its cloister — one for each year of Saint Eulàlia's age when she was martyred, says the legend. Its neo-Gothic facade was added between 1882 and 1913. In Barcelona the medieval and the nineteenth-century idea of the medieval are often standing right next to each other, and the neo-Gothic facade is the more technically accomplished of the two.

The grid and the man who designed it

When you walk the Eixample — the grid neighborhood north of the Gothic Quarter — you're walking a plan that Madrid forced on Barcelona over Barcelona's explicit objection. In 1860, a Catalan engineer named Ildefons Cerdà submitted an expansion plan for the city. The central government in Madrid approved it. Barcelona's own city council had chosen a different design by a local architect. Madrid overrode them. Barcelonins still know this. They still care.

What Cerdà actually built is one of the great acts of urban planning in history. The Eixample is a strict grid: standard streets 20 meters wide, major avenues at 30 and 60 meters, city blocks 113.3 meters on each side with the corners cut at 45 degrees. Those chamfered corners — creating an octagonal space at every intersection — are the Eixample's signature, visible from any aerial view of the city. Cerdà's original social vision was radical: blocks built on only two sides, interior gardens open to all classes, mixed-income housing throughout. Developers built on all four sides and filled the interiors. The bourgeoisie moved in. The Eixample is now one of the most desirable addresses in Barcelona. Cerdà would recognize the streets. He wouldn't recognize what happened inside the blocks.

Walk the Passeig de Gràcia — the main avenue — and look at the buildings on the block between Carrer d'Aragó and Carrer del Consell de Cent. Three of the greatest buildings of Catalan Modernisme stand here within a short walk of each other: Casa Lleó Morera, Casa Amatller, and Casa Batlló. The block is nicknamed the Manzana de la Discordia — the Block of Discord — for the competition implied by putting three masterworks side by side. Casa Batlló is Gaudí's.

Gaudí

On June 7, 1926, Antoni Gaudí was struck by a tram not far from the Sagrada Família. He was poorly dressed, carrying no identification. The people who found him assumed he was a vagrant and took him to a hospital for the poor. He died three days later without being recognized. He is buried in the crypt of the church he spent the last decade of his life sleeping beside. He was 73.

The Sagrada Família started in 1882 under a different architect. Gaudí took it over in 1883 and redesigned it from the foundations. From 1915 until the tram, he worked on almost nothing else. When he died, less than a quarter was built. A fire in 1936 during the Spanish Civil War destroyed many of the original plans. Construction continued, guided by reconstructions and evolving interpretations of what he intended.

In February 2026, the central Tower of Jesus Christ reached structural completion at 172.5 meters — now the tallest church in the world, surpassing the Ulm Minster's record that had stood since 1890. Stand inside on a sunny morning and you'll understand why people keep coming even while it's unfinished: the nave columns branch upward like trees, the light through the stained glass moves through the interior in a spectrum that shifts across the day. Gaudí designed the tower to stand one meter shorter than Montjuïc hill. He believed man should not surpass what God made.

The Sagrada Família has been under construction for 144 years. It will likely be finished within the decade. That it is already one of the most visited buildings in Europe — despite being incomplete for its entire existence — says something about what Gaudí achieved.

Walk from the Sagrada Família down the Passeig de Gràcia and stop at Casa Batlló: an ordinary 1877 building that Gaudí, between 1904 and 1906, covered in polychrome ceramics and stone, reshaped the balconies until they read as skulls and bones, and tiled the roof in a curve that evokes a dragon's back. A few blocks further is Casa Milà — La Pedrera, the Quarry, the nickname its neighbors gave it mockingly — built from scratch between 1906 and 1912, with a self-supporting stone facade, no load-bearing walls inside, and a rooftop terrace where the chimneys stand like helmeted warriors. Go up to the roof. Then walk up the hill to Park Güell, commissioned as a garden city that never happened — only two of sixty planned houses were built — and now a public park with mosaic terraces and views across the city that Gaudí designed as living space for sixty families who never came.

The sea, recovered

If you'd stood at the Barcelona waterfront in 1985, you'd have seen factories, warehouses, rail yards, and shantytowns. A coastal railway line physically separated the city from the Mediterranean. The beaches were polluted. Barcelona, a city on the sea, had its back turned to it for the better part of two centuries.

The 1992 Olympics reversed this by design. The coastal railway was buried. Industrial buildings were demolished. Shantytowns were cleared. About three kilometers of beach were created north from Barceloneta through the former industrial district of Poblenou — much of the sand imported from Egypt. The Olympic Village was built on former factory land and converted to apartments afterward. Two skyscrapers — the Torre Mapfre and a Norman Foster communications tower — created a new skyline against the Mediterranean. Urban planners still study the transformation as a model for what a sports event can do if the city commits to it as urban regeneration, not just entertainment.

The neighborhood of Barceloneta is older than any of this by two centuries. Philip V built a citadel to watch over Barcelona after conquering it in 1714, and to do it he demolished the La Ribera neighborhood. The displaced families were resettled in the 1750s on a triangular spit of reclaimed land, in a neighborhood specifically designed with wide straight streets so troops could move through it quickly. The people evicted to build a military installation that oppressed them were put in a neighborhood designed for their continued surveillance. For two centuries Barceloneta was fishermen and metalworkers. Since 1992 it's been the most visited beach neighborhood in Spain, with all the strain that implies.

What Barcelona has always known about food

Skip La Boqueria, or at least go in knowing what it is. The market on La Rambla has been there in some form since 1217, and the permanent covered building dates to 1840 — a real market with real history. It is also now, in the words of Ferran Adrià, more a tourist attraction than a working market. The produce near the entrance is often expensive and arranged for photography. The chefs who cook seriously in Barcelona shop at the Mercat de Santa Caterina in the Sant Pere neighborhood — the one with the undulating mosaic roof designed by Enric Miralles, completed in 2005 — or at the neighborhood markets in Gràcia. Go to one of those instead and eat at the bar.

Catalan food has a distinct identity that's easy to miss if you're eating near the Rambla. The starting point is pa amb tomàquet: bread rubbed with ripe tomato, drizzled with olive oil, salt. It's on the table at nearly every meal. It's better than it sounds. Escalivada is roasted aubergine and peppers. Crema catalana is a caramelized custard with a claim — fiercely defended — to predate the French crème brûlée. The tradition is closer to the French Mediterranean than to Andalusian tapas, which is part of why the cheap tapas bars near the waterfront taste wrong. On Carrer de Blai in the Poble Sec neighborhood, a Basque tradition has been transplanted: pintxos bars where individual bites on bread are displayed along the counter and you pay by counting your toothpicks. Go there for an early evening drink.

Ferran Adrià's restaurant elBulli, an hour north of Barcelona in the coastal town of Roses, was named the best restaurant in the world five times in the 2000s. It's where molecular gastronomy — using scientific techniques to transform ingredients into unexpected forms — was invented and refined. It closed in 2011. Its influence on how serious cooking is thought about globally is not overstated by saying it changed everything.

The city it has become

Before you go, here's the thing to carry with you: Barcelona has been in the middle of something for two thousand years. The Romans were building a colony. The Moors were administering a province. The Counts of Barcelona were assembling a Mediterranean empire. Cerdà was designing a city that Madrid forced on them. Gaudí was building a church that still isn't done. The 1992 Olympics were creating a waterfront that hadn't existed. The independence movement was holding a referendum that no one recognized. The city has never been finished, never been settled, never been fully decided.

That's what makes it feel alive in a way that cities with more resolved histories don't. The Roman forum is still the civic square. The Cerdà grid that Madrid imposed has become the neighborhood Barcelonins love most. Gaudí's unfinished church is now the tallest in the world. The beaches that didn't exist in 1985 are the city's most recognizable image. Every time Barcelona was told what it was by someone else, it built something they hadn't planned on.

Keep looking for the unmarked door off the narrow alley. That's usually where the real thing is.
