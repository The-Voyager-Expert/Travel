The largest city in the Western Hemisphere that most visitors still fly over on their way somewhere else — and why that is a serious mistake.

A city that built itself

São Paulo does not have a founding myth that sells itself easily. There is no harbor, no dramatic geography, no imperial edict that made this the obvious place for a city. What there is: a Jesuit mission on a plateau above a bend in the Tietê River, founded in 1554 by two priests who wanted a stable base to reach inland tribes. For more than two centuries after that, it was a backwater — a staging post for bandeirantes, the colonial-era raiders who pushed into the interior to enslave indigenous people and hunt for gold, and then came home to this unremarkable hill town.

The city only became a city because of coffee. In the second half of the nineteenth century, the interior plateau of São Paulo state turned out to be ideal for cultivating it, and the wealth that followed — the fazendas, the railways, the immigrant workers brought in to replace enslaved labor after abolition in 1888 — poured into the city at the center of the web. By 1900, São Paulo was growing faster than almost anywhere on earth. By 1950, it had passed Rio de Janeiro to become Brazil's largest city. Today it holds somewhere between twelve and twenty million people depending on how you draw the line, sprawling across a metropolitan area roughly the size of Connecticut.

You feel this history in the skyline before you feel it in any monument. São Paulo has almost no protected historic core — it built over itself too fast and too many times. What it has instead is layer upon layer of economic ambition made concrete: Art Deco towers from the 1930s hemmed in by Brutalist blocks from the 1960s hemmed in by glass curtain walls from the 2000s. The city is an argument about growth, and it never stops.

The building on Paulista that looks like it's floating

Walk up Avenida Paulista — the avenue that runs along the top of the plateau ridge, the spine of the city's financial and cultural life — and about halfway down on the left you'll see a building that appears to be hovering over the street on two red concrete columns. This is MASP: the Museu de Arte de São Paulo. The building was designed by the Italian-Brazilian architect Lina Bo Bardi, completed in 1968, and is one of the most radical museum buildings anywhere in the world.

Bo Bardi's idea was to create a free public space underneath the museum itself. The ground-floor plaza — the Belvedere — runs the full width of the block and is open to anyone who wants to walk through. On Sundays it fills with an antiques market. On weekday evenings it fills with skateboarders. On protest days it fills with the city. The building was designed to be used by the people who walk past it, not only by the people who pay to go inside.

Bo Bardi wanted the collection suspended in space so visitors would feel like they were looking at the paintings, not at the walls.

Inside, the collection is extraordinary — Raphael and Rembrandt and Degas and van Gogh and Portinari, displayed on clear glass easels so the paintings float in the gallery space with no wall behind them. Bo Bardi wanted visitors to see through the paintings to the paintings behind them, to understand them as objects in space rather than objects on walls. It is slightly disorienting and completely right.

What makes MASP worth understanding before you arrive is that it was never a neutral institution. Bo Bardi was building it during Brazil's military dictatorship, in a city that was simultaneously booming economically and being crushed politically. The building's openness — its insistence on the public ground floor, its refusal of the monumental staircase that usually signals "this is a place for important people" — was a political statement as much as an architectural one. You walk under it every time you walk past it. That's the point.

The immigrants who made São Paulo what it is

São Paulo is, in terms of ethnic diversity, unlike any other city in the Americas. Between roughly 1880 and 1930, the Brazilian government and the state of São Paulo actively recruited immigrants from Europe and Asia to work the coffee fazendas — Italians, Japanese, Spanish, German, Lebanese, Syrian, Polish. They came by the millions. What happened when the coffee economy mechanized and those laborers moved to the city is visible everywhere you look.

The Liberdade neighborhood, southeast of the historic center, is the largest Japanese community outside Japan. Not a tourist recreation: an actual neighborhood where the signage is in Japanese, the bakeries sell pão de queijo alongside taiyaki, and the supermarkets stock ingredients for both weeknight Japanese home cooking and weekend churrasco. São Paulo has more Japanese-Brazilians than any city in Japan outside Tokyo.

The Italian influence saturates the food culture in a way that is easy to underestimate. The city's most beloved daily bread is pão na chapa — soft sandwich rolls griddled flat with butter, descended directly from the rolls Italian immigrants brought. The neighborhood of Bixiga, just below Paulista, is still the center of Italo-Paulistano identity, with Italian restaurants that have been operating for three and four generations. The city's most famous street food, the pastel — thin-walled deep-fried pastry pockets sold at every municipal market — came from Japanese-Brazilian adaptation of Chinese spring rolls via the immigrant communities of the interior.

None of this is presented as heritage. It is just how people live.

The art in the streets, and what it says

In the 1980s and 1990s, as the military dictatorship ended and São Paulo grappled with extreme inequality, crime, and civic neglect, the city produced one of the most significant street art movements in the world. The style that emerged — pixação — is not graffiti in the way New York understood graffiti. It is a form of angular, tag-based lettering developed specifically to be written on the vertical surfaces of high-rise buildings, sometimes stories above ground, in locations that required genuine physical courage to reach. It is not decorative. It is territorial, oppositional, and illegible to outsiders by design.

Beco do Batman, in the Vila Madalena neighborhood, is where you see the other side of this: a long alley that has become a rotating gallery of enormous murals, some of the most technically accomplished large-scale spray work anywhere. Artists from São Paulo and from around the world paint here, and the walls are never static — a mural from six months ago may be painted over by the time you arrive. The alley is free, it is always open, and it is always changing.

Vila Madalena is where São Paulo's artists and intellectuals live. Beco do Batman is where they leave proof.

Walk the surrounding blocks too. The murals spill out of the alley into the streets, onto the sides of buildings, onto shuttered storefronts. The neighborhood has the energy of a place that is still figuring out what it wants to be, which in São Paulo is a compliment.

The Mercadão, and why markets matter here

The Mercado Municipal de São Paulo — the Mercadão — is a covered market built in 1933, a Neo-Gothic hall of stained glass and vaulted ceilings full of produce, spice stalls, dried fish, cured meats, and a dozen competing empadas. It serves a metropolitan area of twenty million people and it operates at a scale that makes ordinary supermarkets feel like convenience stores.

Go for the mortadella sandwich. This requires an explanation: a specific stall on the upper level of the Mercadão, Hocca Bar, serves a sandwich that is a single mortadella slice larger than your face, folded into a bread roll. It is not refined food. It is the food of a market city that has been eating lunch fast since the 1930s, and it is perfect for exactly what it is.

But the deeper reason to understand the Mercadão is what it represents about how São Paulo feeds itself. With that many people in that concentrated a space, food distribution becomes a logistical achievement of the same order as the city's infrastructure. The municipal market system — the Mercadão is the anchor, but there are dozens of smaller ones across the city — is how São Paulo has always managed the gap between the produce arriving from the interior and the twenty million mouths that need to eat it.

Inequality made visible

São Paulo is one of the most economically unequal cities in the world, and it does not hide this. The favelas — here called favelas like Rio, not comunidades — sit directly adjacent to some of the most expensive real estate in South America. You can stand on the terrace of a glass-and-steel apartment in Itaim Bibi and look directly at the rooftops of Paraisópolis, one of the largest favelas in the city, the two communities separated by a wall that is, in several places, literally shared.

The helicopters are a marker of this. São Paulo has one of the largest private helicopter fleets of any city on earth — estimates put it above New York and Los Angeles — because the wealthy figured out that they could simply fly over the traffic that the city's inequality produces. The sound of rotors is normal background noise in the neighborhoods around Paulista. You get used to it faster than you expect.

This is not something to observe from a safe distance. It is the condition of the city, present in every neighborhood you walk through, in the contrast between the restaurant where you are eating and the street vendor outside it, in the way public space is claimed or not claimed by different bodies. São Paulo does not ask you to be comfortable with this. It asks you to see it.

What São Paulo wants you to understand

Before you land, here is the thing to carry with you: São Paulo is not a city that exists to be visited. It has never organized itself around tourism, never positioned itself against Rio the way American cities position themselves against New York. It is a production city — it produces things, makes things, builds things, feeds things — and its pleasures are the pleasures of a city where most people are actually doing something.

This means the restaurants are real restaurants, not showrooms. The art scene is serious and argues with itself. The music — samba, pagode, funk carioca adapted into its own local form, the jazz club scene around Vila Madalena that runs until four in the morning — is music that people actually listen to, not music staged for visitors. The city's energy comes from the fact that it is not performing for you.

Once you feel that, you start to understand the skyline differently. All those towers, all that concrete ambition, all those layers of economic history built over each other — it is not chaos. It is what a city looks like when twenty million people showed up from all over the world and had to make something work. That's São Paulo. Everything else follows from that.
