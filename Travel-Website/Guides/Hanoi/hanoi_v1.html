Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → St. Joseph's Cathedral
1. St. Joseph's Cathedral
↳ Hanoi's principal Catholic church, built in 1886 in neo-Gothic style modelled on Notre-Dame de Paris. Twin bell towers rise above the Old Quarter, and the interior holds original stained-glass windows imported from France. One of the few intact French colonial landmarks in the city.

2. Ngoc Son Temple
↳ An 18th-century Confucian and Taoist temple on a small island in Hoan Kiem Lake, reached by the iconic red-painted Huc Bridge. The temple honours the 13th-century military hero Tran Hung Dao and the scholar Van Xuong. Inside, a preserved giant softshell turtle from the lake is displayed.

🚶 12 min · 🚕 5 min → Hoa Lo Prison Museum
3. Hoa Lo Prison Museum
↳ A French colonial prison built in 1896, later used to hold American prisoners of war who nicknamed it the "Hanoi Hilton." The surviving gatehouse and cell blocks preserve original shackles, solitary confinement chambers, and a guillotine room — a stark document of both colonial and wartime history.
📖 Wikipedia

4. Hanoi Opera House
↳ A 1911 Beaux-Arts opera house modelled on the Palais Garnier in Paris, with a columned facade, wrought-iron balconies and a grand staircase. One of the finest examples of French colonial architecture in Southeast Asia. Currently closed for renovation — visit for the exterior and the surrounding Trang Tien plaza.

🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 15 min → Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum
1. Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum
↳ A grey granite mausoleum on Ba Dinh Square where Ho Chi Minh declared independence on 2 September 1945. The embalmed body lies inside a glass case in a darkened chamber. The surrounding complex includes his stilt house, the Presidential Palace gardens, and the One Pillar Pagoda.

2. Imperial Citadel of Thang Long
↳ A UNESCO World Heritage Site and the political centre of Vietnam for over a thousand years. Archaeological layers from the 7th to 19th century lie beneath the grounds — royal palaces, dragon-patterned ceramics and military bunkers from the Vietnam War are all preserved within the same compound.

3. Hanoi Flag Tower
↳ A 33-metre hexagonal watchtower built between 1805 and 1812, the oldest surviving structure of the Nguyen dynasty citadel. It survived French colonial demolition and American bombing intact — one of the few Hanoi landmarks to do so. The Vietnamese flag has flown from the top since 1945.

4. Temple of Literature
↳ A Confucian temple complex founded in 1070 and home to Vietnam's first national university since 1076. Five walled courtyards lead to the Great Success Sanctuary. In the third courtyard, 82 stone stelae on tortoise sculptures record doctoral graduates spanning 1484 to 1780.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 25 min · 🚕 10 min → Long Bien Bridge
1. Long Bien Bridge
↳ A 1.68 km steel cantilever bridge across the Red River, built between 1899 and 1902 by the French firm Daydé & Pillé. Bombed repeatedly during the Vietnam War and patched each time, it still carries trains, motorbikes and pedestrians. Walking across gives views of the river, banana-island farms, and the skyline.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Tran Nhat Duat · Hoan Kiem

2. Quan Thanh Temple
↳ An 11th-century Taoist temple dedicated to Huyen Thien Tran Vu, the guardian deity of the north — one of four temples built to protect ancient Thang Long. The main hall houses a 4-tonne bronze statue of Tran Vu cast in 1677, seated on a serpent with his feet resting on a tortoise.

3. Vietnamese Women's Museum
↳ A national museum across four floors documenting women's roles in Vietnamese history — resistance fighters in the American and French wars, artisans across 54 ethnic groups. The marriage and birth customs gallery and street-vendor photography collection are standouts.

4. Thang Long Water Puppet Theatre
↳ Vietnam's premier water puppet venue, performing a thousand-year-old art form that originated in the Red River Delta rice paddies. Puppeteers stand waist-deep behind a bamboo screen, controlling wooden figures on a pool-stage while live musicians play traditional instruments.

🐉 Notes from the Ascending Dragon

Hanoi's ancient name was Thang Long — the Ascending Dragon — given by King Ly Thai To in 1010 when he moved the capital here and claimed to see a golden dragon rising from the Red River. A thousand years later the city still moves at two speeds: motorbikes weaving through the 36 guild streets of the Old Quarter, and old men bent over chessboards on the shore of Hoan Kiem Lake.

Three days trace the whole arc. Day 1 stays within walking distance of the lake — a French Gothic cathedral, a Confucian temple on an island, a colonial prison turned war museum, and an opera house modelled on the Palais Garnier. Day 2 crosses to the wide Soviet-era avenues of Ba Dinh, where a thousand years of imperial history are compressed into a single morning: the mausoleum of Ho Chi Minh, the citadel where emperors ruled, the flag tower that survived every war, and the temple that housed Vietnam's first university in 1076. Day 3 starts at the far end of a bomb-scarred steel bridge and works back through a Taoist temple, a museum unlike any other, and finishes where Hanoi always finishes — watching wooden puppets dance on water.
