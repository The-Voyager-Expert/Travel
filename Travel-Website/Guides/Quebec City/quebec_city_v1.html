Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 2 min · 🚕 2 min → Terrasse Dufferin
1. Terrasse Dufferin
↳ The 671-metre boardwalk promenade along the St. Lawrence clifftop, built in 1879 on the foundations of the Cape Diamond Redoubt. Named after the Governor General who championed Quebec City's walls, it offers panoramic views over the Lower Town, the St. Lawrence, and the city of Lévis on the opposite shore.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1h
📍 Terrasse Dufferin · Vieux-Québec
🚶 1 min · 🚕 2 min → Château Frontenac
2. Château Frontenac
↳ According to Guinness World Records, the most photographed hotel in the world. Built between 1893 and 1924, the château-style towers have defined the Québec City skyline. The hotel hosted the 1943 and 1944 Québec Conferences between Churchill, Roosevelt, and Mackenzie King. Lobby open to the public.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1h
📍 1 rue des Carrières · Vieux-Québec
🚶 12 min · 🚕 5 min → La Citadelle
3. La Citadelle
↳ An active Canadian Forces garrison — the largest British fortification ever built in North America. Construction began in 1820 on fortifications dating to 1693. The summer Changing of the Guard runs daily at 10:00am with the Royal 22nd Regiment. The museum holds regimental history and 18th-century military artifacts.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~1.5h

🚶 8 min · 🚕 4 min → Fortifications of Québec
4. Fortifications of Québec
↳ The only remaining intact fortified city north of Mexico — 4.6 km of walls and gates enclosing Old Quebec, a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The four main gates — Saint-Louis, Saint-Jean, Kent, and Prescott — remain standing. The promenade along the walls connects all four gates with views over Upper and Lower Town.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1h
📍 Porte Saint-Louis · rue Saint-Louis · Vieux-Québec

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 15 min · 🚕 5 min → Observatoire de la Capitale
1. Observatoire de la Capitale
↳ A 360° observation deck on the 31st floor of the Marie-Guyart building — the tallest building in Québec City. Panoramic views reach from Old Quebec and the St. Lawrence to the Laurentian Mountains. The Parliament Building, the walled city, and the Lévis ferry terminal are all visible.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Funiculaire du Vieux-Québec
↳ A cable-operated incline railway linking Terrasse Dufferin at the top of the Cap-Diamant cliff with rue du Petit-Champlain in the Lower Town — a 64-metre descent. In operation since 1879, the glass-walled cabin descends in two minutes with views across the Lower Town rooftops.
📖 Wikipedia

🚶 1 min · 🚕 2 min → Escalier Casse-Cou
3. Escalier Casse-Cou
↳ The oldest stairway in Canada, connecting Terrasse Dufferin to Rue du Petit-Champlain since 1635. The name refers to the dangerously steep pitch of the original steps. The 59-step stone descent features market stalls and public art at each landing. The Funiculaire runs parallel one minute away.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~15 min
📍 59 rue du Petit-Champlain · Vieux-Québec
🚶 1 min · 🚕 2 min → Rue du Petit-Champlain
4. Rue du Petit-Champlain
↳ The oldest commercial street in North America — a pedestrianized cobblestone lane at the foot of the Cap-Diamant cliff. Artisan boutiques, glassblowers, and restaurants line the narrow street beneath trompe-l'œil murals on the clifftop walls. The street connects directly to Place Royale at its southern end.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1h
📍 Rue du Petit-Champlain · Vieux-Québec
🚶 4 min · 🚕 3 min → Place Royale
5. Place Royale
↳ The spot where Samuel de Champlain built his first trading post in 1608 — the birthplace of French civilization in North America. Notre-Dame-des-Victoires Church, built in 1688, anchors the south side of the square. The surrounding stone buildings are among the oldest on the continent.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Place Royale · Vieux-Québec

🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 20 min → Montmorency Falls
1. Montmorency Falls
↳ At 83 metres, the highest waterfall in the province — 30 metres taller than Niagara Falls. The park includes a suspension bridge above the falls, a cable car to the summit, and cliff-edge walking paths. In winter, the mist freezes into a massive ice cone — the sugarloaf — used for traditional ice climbing.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~1.5h

🚕 20 min → Basilique Sainte-Anne-de-Beaupré
2. Basilique Sainte-Anne-de-Beaupré
↳ The most visited pilgrimage site in Canada — 1.5 million pilgrims annually. The Romano-Byzantine basilica was completed in 1976, the fifth church on this site since 1658. The twin granite towers and richly decorated interior house hundreds of ex-votos — crutches and braces left by pilgrims claiming miraculous cures.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~1h

3. Île d'Orléans
↳ An 18-km island in the St. Lawrence, 10 minutes from Québec City by bridge. First mapped by Cartier in 1535, the island's six villages retain 17th-century stone farmhouses among the oldest rural architecture in North America. Roadside stands sell strawberries, cider, and maple products along the 67-km circuit road.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~2h

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 10 min · 🚕 4 min → Plaines d'Abraham
1. Plaines d'Abraham
↳ Site of the 1759 battle between Wolfe and Montcalm that shaped the future of Canada. Today 103 hectares of parkland in the heart of Québec City, part of the Parc des Champs-de-Bataille — the most visited national historic site in Québec. The Martello towers and interpretive centre explain the military history.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1.5h
📍 Parc des Champs-de-Bataille · Vieux-Québec
🚕 30 min → Village Touristique Wendake
2. Village Touristique Wendake
↳ The only Huron-Wendat nation community in North America open to visitors. The Onhoüa Chetek8e site demonstrates pre-contact Wendat life — bark longhouses, canoe-building, and traditional cooking led by community members. The adjacent Saint-Charles-Borromée Church, built in 1730, holds 18th-century Wendat art.
📖 Wikipedia

Poutine
↳ French fries topped with fresh cheese curds and brown gravy — curds must squeak when bitten. Born in rural Québec in the late 1950s, now omnipresent from street counters to fine dining. Chez Ashton serves the canonical version since 1969.
📍 Chez Ashton · multiple locations · Vieux-Québec
Tourtière
↳ Québécois meat pie — ground pork, veal, and beef spiced with cloves, cinnamon, and allspice under a double-crust flaky pastry. A Christmas Eve staple across Québec for generations, it appears year-round on traditional menus.
📍 Aux Anciens Canadiens · 34 rue Saint-Louis · Vieux-Québec
Cipaille
↳ A multi-layered meat pie with moose, deer, rabbit, and partridge alternated with pastry layers and slow-cooked in a deep casserole. The dish traces to 17th-century New France and is one of the defining dishes of traditional Québec cuisine.
📍 Aux Anciens Canadiens · 34 rue Saint-Louis · Vieux-Québec
Tire d'érable
↳ Maple taffy pulled on snow — boiled maple syrup poured onto packed snow in a thin ribbon, then rolled onto a popsicle stick before it sets. The definitive sugar shack experience, enjoyed alongside maple-glazed ham and cretons.
📍 La Bûche · 49 rue Saint-Louis · Vieux-Québec
Cretons
↳ A smooth, spreadable pork pâté — ground pork slow-cooked with onions, spices, and lard until it sets into a rustic spread. Served cold on toast as part of a traditional Québécois breakfast alongside baked beans and maple syrup.
📍 La Bûche · 49 rue Saint-Louis · Vieux-Québec
