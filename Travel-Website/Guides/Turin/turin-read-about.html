The city that built Italy, built the cars, built the chocolate — and then quietly let Rome and Milan take the applause.

The Capital Nobody Remembers

Here is a fact that surprises almost everyone who visits: Turin was Italy's first capital. Not Rome. Not Florence. Turin. From 1861 to 1865, the newly unified Kingdom of Italy was governed from a chamber inside Palazzo Carignano, a curved Baroque building on a quiet piazza where you can still walk into the room where the first Italian parliament sat. The desks are arranged in a hemicycle. The acoustics are terrible. And from this impractical room, a country was stitched together from dozens of kingdoms, duchies, and papal states that had spent centuries pretending the others did not exist.

The unification movement — the Risorgimento — was not born in a battlefield. It was born in Turin's cafes. Cavour, the prime minister who engineered the diplomatic chess game that made Italy possible, held his meetings at Caffè Fiorio on Via Po. The aristocrats and intellectuals who debated whether Italy should even be a single country did so over cups of coffee and hot chocolate in rooms that still have their original mirrors and marble tables. When you sit in one of these cafes today, you are sitting where a country was argued into existence.

The Savoy Grid

Walk through Turin's center and you will notice something immediately: the streets are straight. They meet at right angles. The arcades run for kilometers in continuous covered walkways. This is not an accident, and it is not Roman — it is Savoy. The House of Savoy ruled Piedmont for over eight hundred years, and they rebuilt Turin as their showcase capital with a geometric precision that makes the city feel more like a northern European court than a Mediterranean town.

Palazzo Reale, the royal palace at the head of Piazza Castello, is where the Savoys lived and governed. It is grand without being overwhelming — the apartments are rich but measured, more mahogany and silk than gold leaf. Palazzo Carignano, a block south, is the architectural opposite: a facade of undulating brick designed by Guarino Guarini in 1679, so theatrical it looks like the building is breathing. The Savoys built both. They had range.

The arcades are the Savoy legacy you will use most. Over eighteen kilometers of covered porticoes line the main streets, which means you can walk from the train station to the river without ever stepping into rain or sun. The Savoys mandated them. They wanted a city where the court could promenade in any weather. The court is gone, but the porticoes remain, and they give Turin an indoor-outdoor quality that no other Italian city has.

Egypt on the Po

The Museo Egizio — Turin's Egyptian Museum — is the largest collection of Egyptian antiquities outside Cairo. That sentence alone is strange enough to demand an explanation: why does a city in northwest Italy have thirty thousand Egyptian artifacts, including a complete tomb chapel from Thebes and a papyrus collection that Egyptologists travel from around the world to study?

The answer is one man. Bernardino Drovetti was born in Piedmont, became a French citizen, and served as Napoleon's consul general in Egypt from 1803 to 1829. In that quarter-century, Drovetti assembled an enormous personal collection of Egyptian antiquities — statues, sarcophagi, papyri, jewels — through a combination of excavation, purchase, and the kind of aggressive acquisition that was standard practice in the early nineteenth century and makes museum ethics boards wince today. When Drovetti needed to sell, King Carlo Felice of Sardinia bought the entire collection in 1824 for what turned out to be one of the great bargains in cultural history.

Turin did not set out to become a center of Egyptology. One man's obsession and one king's checkbook made it happen, and the collection has been growing ever since.

The museum was redesigned in 2015 by the architect Dante Ferretti, and the result is one of the best-presented archaeological museums anywhere. The Galleria dei Re — the Gallery of Kings — displays monumental statues in a dark corridor lit from below, and the effect is closer to standing in a temple than looking at objects in cases. You do not need to care about Egyptology to find it extraordinary.

The Factory on the Roof

In 1923, FIAT opened the Lingotto factory in southern Turin. It was, at the time, the largest car factory in the world: raw materials entered on the ground floor, moved upward through five floors of assembly, and finished cars rolled off the line onto a rooftop test track — a banked oval on the roof of the building where every car was driven at speed before leaving the factory. The building was designed by Giacomo Matte-Trucco, and when Le Corbusier saw it he called it "one of the most impressive sights in industry."

FIAT closed Lingotto in 1982, and the question of what to do with a five-story factory with a racetrack on its roof had no obvious answer. Renzo Piano provided one. He converted the building into a mixed-use complex with a concert hall, a hotel, a shopping center, and, on the roof where cars once raced, an art gallery — the Pinacoteca Giovanni e Marella Agnelli. The gallery is a glass-and-steel box that sits on the old test track like a landed spacecraft, and inside it holds a small, precise collection: Matisse, Picasso, Modigliani, Canaletto, and a few others, chosen by the Agnelli family (FIAT's founders) with the eye of people who bought art because they loved it, not because an advisor told them to.

FIAT defined Turin's identity for most of the twentieth century. The factory drew hundreds of thousands of workers from southern Italy in the 1950s and 1960s, transforming Turin from a Piedmontese court city into an industrial metropolis almost overnight. The social tensions of that transformation — southern workers in a northern city, factory labor in a place that thought of itself as refined — shaped Turin's character in ways that are still visible. The city has a blue-collar directness that Milan lacks and a cultural seriousness that Rome does not bother with.

Chocolate, Bicerin, and the Cafe as Institution

Turin's relationship with chocolate is not decorative. The city invented gianduja — the hazelnut-chocolate paste that Nutella is an industrial descendant of — in the early nineteenth century, when a Napoleonic trade blockade made pure cacao scarce and Piedmontese chocolatiers stretched their supply with the hazelnuts that grew abundantly in the hills south of the city. What started as a workaround became a regional flavor so specific that Piedmontese hazelnuts are now a protected designation of origin.

The cafes where this chocolate culture lives are not quaint relics. Caffè Al Bicerin, open since 1763 on Piazza della Consolata, is the birthplace of the bicerin — a layered drink of espresso, hot chocolate, and cream served in a small glass. The drink has been made in the same room, at the same marble counter, for over two hundred and sixty years. Caffè Mulassano, under the arcades of Piazza Castello, invented the tramezzino — Italy's triangular crustless sandwich — in 1926, and still serves them from behind the original Art Nouveau bar.

These cafes function the way Vienna's coffeehouses do: as public living rooms where the line between eating, drinking, reading, and thinking dissolves. Turin takes its cafe culture more seriously than any other Italian city, and the proof is that the cafes themselves have not been renovated into modern minimalism. The frescoes stay. The chandeliers stay. The tiny marble tables stay. The city decided, collectively and without discussion, that these rooms are worth keeping exactly as they are.

The Mole and the Shroud

The Mole Antonelliana is Turin's skyline — a spire that rises 167 meters and dominates every view of the city. It was designed in 1863 by Alessandro Antonelli as a synagogue for Turin's Jewish community, but Antonelli kept making it taller. And taller. The community ran out of money. The city took over. Antonelli kept building upward. When it was finished in 1889, it was the tallest unreinforced brick structure in the world, and it had no clear purpose. It was too tall and too strange to be a synagogue, too singular to be anything ordinary.

Today it houses the National Museum of Cinema, and the match is better than it has any right to be. The museum fills the Mole's cavernous interior with a vertical exhibition that spirals from the ground floor to the dome, and a glass elevator rises through the center of the open space to an observation deck near the top. The building's absurd proportions — a cathedral-sized void inside a needle-shaped tower — turn out to be perfect for displaying the history of cinema as a spectacle.

The Shroud is kept behind bulletproof glass in a climate-controlled case inside the Cathedral of Saint John the Baptist, and it is almost never displayed. You will see a photograph of it. That is the point.

The Shroud of Turin — the linen cloth bearing the faint image of a man that some believe wrapped the body of Christ — has been in Turin since 1578. Radiocarbon dating in 1988 placed the fabric in the thirteenth or fourteenth century. Believers dispute the methodology. The Vatican has never declared it authentic or inauthentic. What is not disputed is its effect on the city: the Shroud draws pilgrims, scholars, and the merely curious in a steady stream, and on the rare occasions it is displayed — the last was 2015, the next is not scheduled — millions come.

Superga and the Quiet Confidence

On the hill east of Turin stands the Basilica di Superga, a Baroque church built by Filippo Juvarra in 1731 on the spot where the Savoy duke Victor Amadeus II watched his army break the French siege of 1706. The basilica is magnificent — a domed central plan with a classical portico that commands a panoramic view of the city and the Alps beyond. The Savoy royal tombs are in the crypt.

But most Turinese who climb the hill are not thinking about the Savoys. On May 4, 1949, the plane carrying the entire Grande Torino football team — the dominant squad in Italian football, four-time consecutive champions, the backbone of the national team — crashed into the retaining wall of the basilica in fog. Thirty-one people died. The disaster destroyed not just a football club but a source of civic identity at a moment when Turin was rebuilding from the war. A plaque on the basilica wall marks the impact point. Torino FC has never returned to that level of dominance. The city has never forgotten.

This is the thread that runs through everything in Turin. The first capital. The factories. The chocolate. The cafes where unification was debated. The football team that was the best in Europe. Turin built all of it, and then history moved the spotlight to Rome and Milan, and Turin did not chase it. The city kept its arcades, kept its cafes, kept its museums, kept its grief for the Grande Torino, and settled into the quiet confidence of a place that knows exactly what it contributed and does not need you to acknowledge it. That composure — not flashy, not modest, just certain — is the thing you will feel in Turin before you can name it. It is the Italian city that built the country and then got back to work.
