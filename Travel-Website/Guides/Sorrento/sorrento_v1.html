Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 3 min · 🚕 2 min → Vallone dei Mulini
1. Vallone dei Mulini
↳ A gorge sealed off when 15th-century road works cut off drainage, trapping the medieval mills inside. Abandoned in 1866, the valley is now a tangle of ferns, moss and fig trees clinging to the ruins — visible only from the iron-rail viewpoint on Via Fuorimura.

🚶 5 min · 🚕 2 min → Sedile Dominova
2. Sedile Dominova
↳ One of two sedili — ancient civic meeting-houses of Neapolitan nobility — still standing in Sorrento. Built in 1319 by the western noble families, the open loggia has semicircular arches on piperno columns and a domed ceiling frescoed in the 18th century. Still actively used today as a cultural association.

3. Chiostro di San Francesco
↳ A 14th-century Franciscan cloister with interlaced arches on octagonal pillars and Byzantine capitals — every column slightly different. Draped in bougainvillea in summer, it serves as an open-air concert stage for classical music at dusk, one of the most photogenic spots in town.

4. Villa Comunale
↳ A clifftop public park from 1877, perched at the town's western edge above the sea. The terrace delivers the best unobstructed panorama in central Sorrento — Bay of Naples, Vesuvius and Capri on clear days. A lift connects the terrace to Marina Piccola below.

5. Marina Grande
↳ The older of Sorrento's two harbours — colourful fishermen's houses wedged between cliff and sea, wooden boats hauled up on the pebble beach. Sophia Loren filmed her first movies here in the 1950s. Lunch at one of the quayside restaurants before the steep walk or taxi back up.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 13 min · 🚕 5 min → Museo Correale di Terranova
1. Museo Correale di Terranova
↳ A 19th-century villa turned museum in terraced gardens overlooking the bay. The Correale brothers bequeathed their family home and collection — Neapolitan decorative arts, Capodimonte porcelain, Meissen figurines, Greek and Roman antiquities — to Sorrento in 1924. One of the finest small museums in southern Italy.
📖 Wikipedia

🚫 Closed Monday

2. Basilica di Sant'Antonino
↳ The oldest church in Sorrento, dedicated to the city's patron saint. The 11th-century basilica sits above Roman-era catacombs hung with whale bones — ex-votos left by sailors saved from storms through Sant'Antonino's intercession. The bones are still suspended in the catacombs below.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Sorrento Cathedral
↳ The cathedral rebuilt in the 15th century on ancient foundations, with a Baroque interior and elaborately inlaid wooden choir stalls — prime examples of the intarsia woodwork Sorrento is famous for. The bell tower's clock face repurposes Roman column capitals as its decorative frame.
📖 Wikipedia

🚶 3 min · 🚕 2 min → Piazza Tasso
4. Piazza Tasso
↳ The beating heart of Sorrento — a clifftop square named after the 16th-century poet Torquato Tasso, who was born here. Ringed by 19th-century hotels and caffè with deep terraces, it divides the old town from the modern corso and is the best vantage point for the evening passeggiata.

🍮 Local Tastes
Gnocchi alla Sorrentina
↳ Sorrento's signature pasta dish — potato gnocchi baked in a terracotta pot with San Marzano tomato sauce, basil and melted fior di latte; the charred edge is half the point.
📍 La Cantinaccia del Popolo · L'Antica Trattoria · O'Parrucchiano
Delizia al Limone
↳ A dome of sponge soaked in limoncello syrup and filled with lemon-cream mousse, invented in Sorrento in 1978 by pastry chef Carmine Marzuillo; the authentic version uses only Sorrento IGP lemons.
📍 Pasticceria Primavera · L'Antica Trattoria
Limoncello di Sorrento
↳ Made from sfusato amalfitano lemons grown on Sorrento Peninsula terraces — thicker-skinned, more aromatic and less bitter than varieties used elsewhere; served ice-cold in a frosted ceramic glass.
📍 Limonoro · Distilleria Quisisana
Provolone del Monaco DOP
↳ A semi-hard stretched-curd cheese aged 6–18 months in the hills above Sorrento, made from Agerolese cow milk; rich, slightly spicy, best sliced thick; the name comes from the cloaked cheese-sellers of Naples.
📍 Inn Bufalito · Via San Cesareo market
Torta Caprese
↳ A dense flourless chocolate-and-almond cake from nearby Capri — naturally flourless, with a moist truffle-like crumb; the better versions use ground Sicilian almonds and 70% dark chocolate.
📍 Pasticceria Primavera · Gelateria Davide

✨ Claude Inspiration

Sorrento has a problem that works in its favour: it is a stopping-off point, the place people stay on their way to Capri or the Amalfi coast, and so the energy faces outward — at the ferries, the tour-company windows, the departures board. The town rewards the opposite. The old centre is small enough to cross in fifteen minutes but dense enough that a wrong turn still surprises; the streets inland from Corso Italia empty out almost entirely by 10am when the day-trippers board their boats. The gorge of the mills is the centrepiece — there is nothing else quite like it in southern Italy, a ruined medieval industry the town built a road over and then simply forgot, leaving the ferns and the silence to take over. The walk down to Marina Grande in the late afternoon, when the fishing boats are back and the light is horizontal, is when the town feels most like itself rather than like a postcard. Take the path rather than the road; it deposits you among the coloured houses without the switchbacks.
