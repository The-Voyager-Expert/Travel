A cliff above the sea that the ancient world loved and never quite stopped visiting — and why this matters more than the lemons.

The cliff and what it means

Sorrento sits on a volcanic tuff cliff, 50 meters above the water, at the southern end of the Bay of Naples. The cliff is not incidental. It is the whole explanation for why the city exists where it does and why it looks the way it looks. You arrive in Piazza Tasso, the main square, and the town seems perfectly ordinary — a Italian piazza, some shops, the usual confusion of cars and pedestrians — and then you walk thirty meters to the edge and the ground simply stops. Below you is the sea. Across the water is Vesuvius, perfectly conical, trailing a faint smear of cloud at its summit. The island of Capri sits in the middle distance, close enough to look attainable and far enough to look mythological.

This view has been famous for a very long time. The ancient Romans built their vacation villas here — they called the southern shore of the bay Surrentum, and the location had the same appeal then that it has now: height, sea, the volcano visible but safely across the water. Augustus came. Nero came. The poet Torquato Tasso was born here in 1544. The Grand Tour brought every serious European traveler to this cliff in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. Goethe came in 1787 and wrote about the bay with a reverence that Germans of his generation had only for ancient things. Wagner composed here. Ibsen lived here for a year and wrote Peer Gynt while looking at this view.

Most of what Sorrento's visitors come for is still that view. The town has organized itself around delivering it: the terraced hotels built into the cliff face, the staircases down to the water, the boats that take you to Capri and Positano and the Blue Grotto. But the cliff itself is the original reason, and it's worth spending a morning just understanding the geography before you fill the day with activities. Walk to the Villa Comunale gardens at the cliff edge, find a bench, and spend thirty minutes with the bay. The volcano is 9 kilometers away. In the year 79 CE, the eruption that buried Pompeii and Herculaneum was visible from this bench for two days before the ash cloud reached this shore.

Vesuvius across the water

Pompeii is 30 kilometers away, accessible by the Circumvesuviana railway in about 35 minutes. You should go. Not because Pompeii is the most impressive archaeological site in Italy — it isn't, not quite, the forum and the amphitheater require imagination to fill in what the centuries have taken — but because standing in the streets of a city that was buried in a single afternoon gives you a different relationship with the landscape you've been looking at from the cliff.

The eruption of 79 CE was not a single explosion. It was an 18-hour event that began with a column of ash rising to 30 kilometers and showering pumice over the city, followed by a series of pyroclastic surges — superheated avalanches of gas and debris — that killed almost everyone who hadn't already fled. The people you see in the casts at Pompeii's Garden of the Fugitives were not struck down instantly. They sheltered. They waited. They miscalculated how long they had.

Pliny the Younger watched the eruption from Misenum, across the bay, and wrote the only surviving eyewitness account. He was 17 years old. His letters to Tacitus, written 25 years later, are still how we understand what happened.

From Sorrento's cliff, on a clear day, you can see the exact shape of the mountain that produced all of this. Vesuvius last erupted in 1944. It is not dormant; volcanologists call it quiescent. The cities around its base — Naples, Portici, Ercolano, Torre del Greco — are home to over a million people. The evacuation plan, updated regularly by Italian civil protection authorities, is called Piano Vesuvio. Everyone who lives in the shadow of the mountain knows this. Most of them have made peace with it in the way that people who live in beautiful places make peace with the cost of the beauty.

The Bay of Naples: the geography of mythology

You're in a part of the world where the ancient Greeks and Romans placed their mythology, and the placement was not random. The Bay of Naples sits at the boundary of what the ancient world considered the known and the unknown. To the south was the Amalfi Coast, and beyond it the open Mediterranean. The Oracle at Cumae — the Cumaean Sibyl — operated from caves just north of Cape Miseno, where the bay opens toward the west. Virgil set the entrance to the underworld at Lake Avernus, a volcanic crater lake just north of here that steams with sulfurous fumes and kills birds that fly over it (the ancients noticed this and drew the obvious conclusion).

The Phlegraean Fields — the Campi Flegrei, or Burning Fields — are the volcanic zone north of Naples, and they are geologically active in a way that no photograph quite prepares you for. Pozzuoli, on the western edge of Naples, has streets that have risen and fallen by several meters over historical time as the volcanic system beneath pressurizes and deflates. There are Roman market columns at the water's edge with waterline stains 5 meters up their shafts, evidence of centuries when the city was partly submerged. The ground in some parts of Pozzuoli is measurably warm underfoot.

Sorrento is at the southern edge of this landscape. The geology here is less violent, the volcanic rock more stable, but you are still very much in the place where the ancient world decided the boundaries of the human and the supernatural were thin. That feeling — hard to name, easy to feel — is part of what you're actually experiencing when you stand on the cliff and look north toward Vesuvius.

The Amalfi Coast: what to know before you go

The Amalfi Coast is one of the most beautiful drives in the world and also one of the most miserable driving experiences available to anyone who attempts it in July or August. The road — the SS163, built in the 1840s by the Bourbon King Ferdinand II — is barely two lanes wide and clings to cliffs with drops that will make your passenger stop speaking. In high season, this road carries an amount of tourist traffic that it was never designed to accommodate. The buses that serve it are full. The parking is nonexistent. Locals who have errands to run navigate this situation with a patience that amounts to a civic virtue.

The solution, which most visitors discover on day two after a traumatic bus journey, is the ferry. Boats run regularly from Sorrento to Positano, Amalfi, and Ravello throughout the season, and the experience of arriving in these towns by sea — as they were always meant to be approached — is completely different from the experience of approaching them by road. From the water, the coast does what it's supposed to do: the villages appear stacked vertically on the cliffs, the colors are impossible, the scale is right. Positano from the sea looks the way Positano looks in photographs. Positano from the coast road, where you've been wedged in a bus for 45 minutes, looks like a traffic problem.

Ravello is the exception. It sits 350 meters above the water and is reached from Amalfi by a steep winding road, and it has a quality that the cliff-face towns lack: quiet. The Villa Rufolo and the Villa Cimbrone have gardens that look down over the coast from a height that makes the whole panorama comprehensible at once. Wagner came to Ravello in 1880 and was so overwhelmed by the garden at Villa Rufolo that he set the second act of Parsifal there. Richard Strauss and Virginia Woolf also came. Ravello draws people who want to think about what they're seeing, which means it draws a different kind of visitor than the towns below.

The lemons and what they actually mean

The lemons of the Sorrento peninsula — the Femminello di Sorrento variety — are grown on terraced hillsides above the town and covered with nets that filter the light, producing fruit that is larger and milder and more fragrant than ordinary lemons. They are used in limoncello, in pastries, in pasta sauces, and in the fresh-squeezed lemonade sold from carts near the marina. The limoncello served at the end of a meal in a Sorrento restaurant is, if the restaurant makes its own, genuinely different from what you buy in a bottle — sweeter, more floral, less alcoholic-tasting.

The lemon terraces on the hillsides are also an argument for the agricultural history of this coast. The terracing is Roman in origin, extended by the Angevins, maintained through centuries by farmers who had no mechanical assistance and who did this work entirely by hand. The nets are a twentieth-century refinement, but the walls and the soil they retain are ancient. When you walk through the lemon groves above Sorrento — and you can, on the agricultural paths that wind between the terraces — you're walking through a landscape that has been cultivated continuously since the Roman period.

The best limoncello in Sorrento is made by a family that has been growing the lemons for four generations. The worst limoncello in Sorrento is sold in ceramic bottles shaped like Vesuvius to tourists who leave it in their luggage.

Skip the ceramic bottles. Buy a bottle from a producer, not a souvenir shop. Ask where it was made, not who bottles it. The lemons here are genuinely exceptional and the product made from them is genuinely good — but only if you buy the real version, which in Sorrento, as in most places, requires a small amount of effort that most visitors don't bother with.

The streets behind the square

Most visitors to Sorrento spend their time in Piazza Tasso and on the Corso Italia — the main shopping street — and at the cliff-edge terrace bars, which is a reasonable use of the view but a partial use of the town. The historic center, behind and below the corso, is a grid of narrow lanes lined with churches, artisan workshops, and ordinary shops that serve the people who actually live here. The Museo Correale di Terranova, in a palazzo at the eastern end of town, holds the best collection of Neapolitan decorative arts in the region: coral carvings, inlaid furniture, majolica, Chinese porcelain that arrived via the Bourbon court. It is almost never crowded.

Walk the Via San Cesareo in the late afternoon, when the cheese shops and the deli counters are doing business with the neighborhood. The provolone del Monaco — a semi-hard cheese made from the milk of a local cattle breed, aged in caves — is produced on the hills above Sorrento and sold here fresh. The bread shops still bake traditional Neapolitan bread in wood-fired ovens. These are not tourist operations. They are the infrastructure of a town that has been feeding itself for several thousand years and intends to keep doing so regardless of how many visitors show up to photograph the view from the cliff.

What you're really looking at

Go back to the cliff edge one more time before you leave — early morning is best, before the heat comes in and before the tour groups arrive in the piazza. The bay will be flat and bronze in the early light. Vesuvius will be clear. Capri will be close enough to make you want to go there, which you should. Here is the thing to carry with you: this bay has been continuously inhabited and continuously visited for at least three thousand years, by Greeks and Romans and Byzantines and Normans and Bourbons and Grand Tourists and now by you. Every one of them came for the same reason — the view, the light, the geological drama, the feeling that the world is organized at a scale you can actually comprehend from this cliff. The instinct you feel standing here is not modern sentiment and it's not tourism. It's the oldest response to this landscape there is. The ancient world built temples here because they felt it too. You're at the end of an unbroken line of visitors who stood in this exact spot and felt exactly what you're feeling, and the line goes back further than you can see.
