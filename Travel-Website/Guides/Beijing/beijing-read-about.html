The city that has been deciding what China means for eight hundred years — and where every dynasty built on top of the previous one, sometimes literally.

The city at the center of everything

Beijing is a planned city, and the plan has never really changed. Stand at the Gate of Heavenly Peace — Tiananmen — and look north through the Forbidden City. What you're seeing is a single axis, running north to south through the center of the city, on which the Chinese emperors aligned the entire imperial capital. The axis was not symbolic shorthand. It was a cosmological statement: the emperor was the Son of Heaven, his palace was the center of the known world, and the city was built around that fact the way planets organize themselves around a sun.

The axis still exists. It runs from Yongding Gate in the south, through the Temple of Heaven, through Tiananmen Square, through the Forbidden City, through Jingshan Park, all the way to the Bell and Drum Towers in the north — a straight line through nearly eight kilometers of city. The Communist Party, when they took Beijing in 1949, didn't disrupt the axis. They put Mao's portrait on Tiananmen Gate and his mausoleum in the square below it, which made the new ruler's face the first thing you see when you look south from the Forbidden City. The political logic was exactly the same as the imperial one. Only the vocabulary changed.

What the Forbidden City actually was

The Forbidden City is the largest surviving palace complex in the world — 178 acres, nearly a thousand buildings, constructed between 1406 and 1420 under the Yongle Emperor of the Ming dynasty. Twenty-four emperors lived and ruled here over five centuries. Most of them rarely left.

This confinement was the point. The emperor was the link between Heaven and earth, and his role was ceremonial as much as political: performing the rituals that kept the cosmos in order, receiving tribute from vassal states, issuing edicts that organized the empire. To be the Son of Heaven was to be simultaneously all-powerful and completely constrained. The Forbidden City is a machine for that paradox — a city within a city, sealed from the outside world, in which ultimate power expressed itself through elaborate protocol and almost total immobility.

The Forbidden City is not a palace. It is a cosmological model, built in brick and timber and red lacquer.

The Hall of Supreme Harmony, the largest building in the complex, is where the emperor appeared before his court on the most important occasions — the winter solstice, his birthday, military victories. The throne sits on a dais elevated by several steps, surrounded by gilded columns and clouds of incense. Officials prostrated themselves on the marble terrace outside. The physical experience of approaching this building — crossing the broad courtyards, ascending the terraces, feeling your own smallness against the scale of it — is still doing what it was designed to do six hundred years ago. You feel it even when you know exactly what it's doing.

Go early, before the tour groups arrive. Walk to the back of the complex — the Garden of Eternal Spring, the Palace of Tranquil Longevity — where things are smaller and quieter and the wear of actual habitation shows through. The imperial family lived here, in these side courts and private gardens. It feels different from the ceremonial front halls. It feels like somewhere people actually were.

The city Mao remade

When the People's Republic of China was proclaimed on October 1, 1949, Tiananmen Square did not look the way it does now. The square was smaller, hemmed in by buildings. The Gate of Heavenly Peace — Tiananmen — faced a maze of imperial-era government offices. Mao looked out from it and declared, to perhaps 300,000 people, that "the Chinese people have stood up."

Then he set about rebuilding the city to match the claim. Over the next decade, Tiananmen Square was expanded to its current vast dimensions — at roughly 440,000 square meters, one of the largest public squares in the world — by demolishing the buildings that surrounded it. The goal was a space that could hold a million people for mass rallies, which it did, repeatedly, during the Cultural Revolution. The square is not a public amenity. It is a stage.

The wider city transformation was equally radical. The medieval walls that had enclosed Beijing since the fifteenth century — a circuit nearly forty kilometers long, with watchtowers and gates and a moat — were demolished in the 1950s and 1960s to make way for the Second Ring Road. A few gate towers survived: Qianmen, southeast of the square, is the most famous. The walls themselves are gone. Urban planners argued about it at the time; the architectural historian Liang Sicheng, who wanted to preserve the walls and build the new government center outside them, lost the argument. He died in 1972, during the Cultural Revolution, having watched the city he loved dismantled systematically.

The hutongs: what's left of the old city

Beijing's hutongs — the narrow alleyways of single-story courtyard houses that once covered the city within the walls — are the surviving tissue of the Beijing that existed before the emperors, before the Communists, before anyone thought to impose a grand plan on the place. The word hutong is Mongolian, from the Yuan dynasty (1271–1368), when the Mongol rulers of China laid out a new capital on this site and created the alleyway grid that still, in places, underlies the modern city.

The courtyard house — siheyuan — is the basic unit of old Beijing life. Four low buildings arranged around a central courtyard, the family's rooms on the north side (the warmest in Beijing's bitter winters), the courtyard planted with a pomegranate tree or a persimmon, a millstone for grinding grain, a place for the dog. The buildings have no exterior windows — all light and air come from the courtyard. The world outside the gate is formal and public; the world inside is domestic and intimate. This is a fundamentally different relationship to privacy than the apartment tower, and it produces a different kind of neighborhood.

Most of the hutongs were demolished between the 1950s and the 2000s. The survivors — concentrated now around Nanluoguxiang, the Drum Tower, and the Shichahai lakes — have been converted into cafes, boutiques, and hostels to such a degree that the word "hutong" has become a tourism brand rather than a description of a place. But push off the main lane into the side alleys and you'll find courtyards where families have lived for generations, old men playing chess at folding tables, a grandmother hanging washing across the grey brick. That version of the hutong is still there. It is not going to be there forever.

The Wall at the edge of the world

The Great Wall is not one thing. It is a series of walls, fortifications, and watchtower systems built by different dynasties over fifteen centuries, running in rough parallel across the mountains north of Beijing. The section at Mutianyu, which the guide takes you to, was built primarily by the Ming dynasty in the sixteenth century — solid granite construction on steep ridge lines, designed to be visible from the valleys below and to move troops quickly along its top.

What the Wall was meant to stop is worth understanding. The northern frontier was the permanent problem of Chinese civilization — the boundary between the agricultural world of the Yellow River plain and the nomadic world of the steppe. For millennia, steppe nomads could move faster and fight better in open terrain than the settled Chinese armies; the Wall was a response to that asymmetry, a way of substituting stone and manpower for mobility. It worked intermittently and never completely. The Mongols under Genghis Khan and his successors didn't breach the Wall — they went around it, through treachery or simply by controlling enough of the terrain to make the Wall irrelevant. The Manchu who founded the Qing dynasty in 1644 were let through by a Ming general who opened the gates.

The Wall is not a border. It is the material record of two thousand years of anxiety about what lay to the north.

At Mutianyu, on a clear day, you can see the ridge lines extending in both directions until they disappear into the haze. What the builders faced — the terrain they had to work with, dragging granite blocks up slopes that would challenge a fit hiker today — is visible in the construction itself. The watchtowers are spaced so that signal fires could be relayed from one to the next, carrying a warning from the frontier to Beijing in hours. The system worked. What it couldn't stop was the thing it was never designed to stop: the slow accumulation of problems inside the Wall that eventually undid every dynasty that built it.

The Temple of Heaven and the emperor's burden

The Temple of Heaven sits in a large park in the southern part of Beijing, separated from the Forbidden City by about two kilometers. The park is where Beijing residents go in the mornings — for tai chi, for walking backwards (a folk remedy for back pain), for opera singing, for card games — and the contrast between the crowds in the park and the solitude of the actual temple complex is one of the better things in the city.

The Hall of Prayer for Good Harvests — the circular blue-roofed building that appears on every postcard — was where the emperor came each year at the winter solstice to pray for a good harvest on behalf of the empire. The ritual was performed in darkness and silence, by the emperor alone, after days of preparation including fasting and sexual abstinence. If the harvest failed, it meant the emperor had lost the Mandate of Heaven — the divine sanction that legitimized his rule. Bad weather was a political problem. The consequences could be catastrophic: harvest failure led to famine, famine led to peasant uprising, uprising led to dynasty change. China's history is largely a record of this cycle repeating.

The building itself is remarkable — three-tiered, circular, built entirely without nails, the blue tiles representing heaven, the proportions calibrated to the cycles of the lunar calendar. Stand in the center of the Echo Wall, the circular courtyard below the main hall, and whisper something. The wall curves the sound around the full circle back to you. The acoustic engineering is not accidental.

What to carry when you leave

Every city in China claims to be the real China. Beijing earns the claim differently than the others. It is the city that has continuously been used to define what China is — by the Mongol Yuan dynasty, by the Ming, by the Qing, by the Nationalists, by the Communists, by the current government. Each of them imposed their version of China on this city, built monuments to it, aligned their power along the old north-south axis. Each of them was eventually replaced.

What survives is layered. Underneath the Communist city is the Qing capital. Underneath the Qing is the Ming. Underneath the Ming is the Mongol grid. The hutong alleyways follow the Yuan-dynasty street plan from the thirteenth century; the Forbidden City was built to dominate a space that the Mongols prepared; the Temple of Heaven sits on a site used for imperial ritual since the early fifteenth century. Beijing does not have history the way other cities have history — as something that happened before the present. Beijing's history is its present, visible in the alignment of streets, in the proportions of public squares, in the decision about whose face appears on the gate.

Here is the thing to hold onto when you walk into the Forbidden City tomorrow: for five hundred years, the men who ruled the most populous empire in the world lived inside these walls and almost never went outside them. They understood the world through documents, through the testimony of officials, through the rituals they performed season after season in these courtyards. The Forbidden City was not a retreat from power. It was where power lived. When you understand that, the scale of the place stops being impressive and starts being clarifying.
