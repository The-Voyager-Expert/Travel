A city that has changed countries four times without moving an inch, where the border between France and Germany runs not through a river but through the kitchen, the language, and the architecture.

The city that kept changing countries

You will notice it the moment you step off the train. The station is French — grand, stone, imperial — but the street names on the buildings outside are set in a typeface that feels unmistakably German. The half-timbered houses lining the canals could be in Colmar or they could be in Freiburg. The bakeries sell both croissants and Bretzel. This is not a city that borrows from two cultures. This is a city that has been, constitutionally and legally, both.

Strasbourg was part of the Holy Roman Empire for centuries, a free imperial city governing itself in a loose German-speaking confederation. France annexed it in 1681 under Louis XIV. It stayed French through the Revolution, through Napoleon, through the building of the great civic squares you will walk through on Day 1. Then Germany took it back after the Franco-Prussian War in 1871. France reclaimed it in 1918. Germany seized it again in 1940. France took it back for the last time in 1944. Four changes of nationality in 73 years. Your great-grandmother, had she been born here in 1870, would have been German, then French, then German, then French again — without ever leaving her apartment on the Grand Île.

The Alsatian identity that grew out of this is not confusion. It is something harder and more interesting: a refusal to be reduced to either side. The dialect — Elsässisch — is Germanic in grammar but laced with French. The cuisine pulls from both traditions but belongs to neither. When you hear locals switch mid-sentence from French to Alsatian to German, you are hearing the sound of a place that decided its complexity was its identity, not a problem to be solved.

A cathedral that touched the sky

Notre-Dame de Strasbourg was the tallest building in the world from 1647 to 1874 — over two hundred years. Not the tallest church, not the tallest structure in Europe, but the tallest thing human beings had ever built, period. Its single spire rises 142 metres, and when you stand at its base on the Place de la Cathédrale and look up, the effect is still vertiginous. The pink Vosges sandstone catches the light differently at every hour — pale rose at dawn, burnt amber at sunset, almost violet under cloud.

The building took over four hundred years to complete, from 1015 to 1439, and the most famous thing about it is what is missing. The plan called for two spires, as at Chartres or Cologne. Only the north spire was ever finished. The south platform stands flat and open, 66 metres up, and you can climb to it via 332 steps. Whether the second spire was abandoned for lack of funds, structural concern, or simply because the builders moved on to other projects, nobody is entirely sure. But the asymmetry is the point now. One spire, slightly off-centre, gives the whole west facade an asymmetry that feels almost human — as if the building is acknowledging that perfection is less interesting than character.

Inside, the astronomical clock is a room-sized mechanism built in the 1840s on the bones of a 1574 original. It computes Easter, eclipses, and the precession of the equinoxes. Every day at 12:30, a parade of mechanical apostles crosses in front of a figure of Christ while a rooster crows three times. You will stand in a crowd of tourists watching a sixteenth-century idea of the cosmos tick forward, inside a thirteenth-century idea of heaven, and the layers of time will feel physical.

Strasbourg's cathedral stood as the tallest thing on earth for two centuries — and the most remarkable thing about it is the spire that was never built.

Little France and the sickness district

La Petite France is the part of Strasbourg that appears on every postcard: half-timbered houses leaning over narrow canals, geraniums in window boxes, weirs where the River Ill splits into multiple channels. It is genuinely beautiful, and it is packed with visitors for good reason. But the name tells a darker story.

This was the tannery and milling district in the medieval period — the part of town where the work was wet, smelly, and low-status. In the late fifteenth century, a hospice was established here to treat soldiers returning from the Italian Wars with syphilis. The Germans called syphilis "die Franzosenkrankheit" — the French disease. The district where the sick were quarantined became "Little France." The picturesque name you see on every tour brochure is, at root, a euphemism for a syphilis hospital.

None of this diminishes the beauty of the place. If anything, it adds a layer. The houses you see today, with their steep roofs and open attic galleries where hides were once hung to dry, are the physical remnant of a working-class district that the rest of the city preferred not to think about. The gentrification took about four hundred years, but here you are, taking photographs of the result.

Where France and Germany collide on the plate

Alsatian food makes no sense if you try to file it under French or German. It is its own thing, and the best way to understand it is to eat your way through it without trying to categorize.

Choucroute garnie is sauerkraut, yes, but braised in Riesling with juniper and served under a heap of smoked pork, sausages, and potatoes that would feel at home in Munich. Tarte flambée — or Flammekueche, because everything here has two names — is a paper-thin unleavened dough slicked with crème fraîche, fromage blanc, onions, and lardons, fired in a wood oven until the edges char. It is not pizza. It is not a quiche. It is Alsatian, and you eat it with your hands.

Kugelhopf is the brioche-like cake baked in a fluted mould, sometimes sweet with almonds and raisins, sometimes savoury with bacon and walnuts. Munster cheese comes from the Vosges and smells like a declaration of war but tastes creamy and complex. Baeckeoffe is a slow-cooked casserole of three meats and potatoes layered in a sealed clay pot, the kind of dish that was traditionally assembled on Saturday, left with the baker to cook overnight in his cooling oven, and collected after church on Sunday.

The wines are the final proof that Alsace is its own country. Alsatian Riesling is bone-dry, mineral, nothing like the sweet German Rieslings most people picture. Gewürztraminer is aromatic, lychee-scented, almost exotic — again, nothing like anything you will find across the Rhine or in the rest of France. You drink these in a winstub, the Alsatian equivalent of a wine bar: wood-panelled, checked tablecloths, a handwritten menu, and a proprietor who may or may not switch to Alsatian when speaking to the regulars.

The capital of a borderless idea

On Day 4 of your visit, you will walk through the Quartier Européen and pass the European Parliament, the Council of Europe, and the European Court of Human Rights. These are large, modern, glass-and-steel buildings set in parkland along the Ill, and they look like what they are: the administrative infrastructure of an idea.

The idea is that the borders which tore Strasbourg apart for centuries — the borders that made this city change nationality four times in a single lifetime — could be made irrelevant. The European institutions were placed here deliberately. A city that had been the prize in every Franco-German conflict since Louis XIV would become the seat of the institutions designed to make such conflicts structurally impossible. The irony is not accidental. It is the entire point.

A city fought over for centuries by France and Germany now hosts the institutions built to make borders irrelevant. The irony is the entire point.

You can visit the European Parliament when it is in session — the hemicycle where 720 members from 27 countries debate in 24 languages simultaneously. Whether you find this inspiring or bureaucratic probably depends on your politics, but the physical fact of it, happening in Strasbourg of all places, is worth sitting with for a moment.

The oldest Christmas market in France

If you happen to be here between late November and late December, you will walk into the Christkindelsmärik — the oldest Christmas market in France, running since 1570. The name is Alsatian, not French and not quite German, and the market itself predates every imitation you have ever seen in London, Chicago, or anywhere else that sets up wooden chalets and sells mulled wine in December.

The original market centres on Place Broglie, but it has expanded across the city — Place de la Cathédrale, Place Kléber (where the Great Christmas Tree stands), Place du Château, and several smaller squares. The smell is vin chaud, cinnamon, and grilling sausage. The stalls sell hand-blown glass ornaments from Meisenthal, pain d'épices, and bredele — the small Alsatian Christmas cookies that come in dozens of varieties and that every family makes from recipes they do not share.

Even if you are not here during the market season, you will see traces of it everywhere: the permanent Christmas shops on Rue du Maroquin, the ornament workshops, the gingerbread moulds in antique stores. Strasbourg calls itself the Capital of Christmas, which sounds like marketing but is closer to a historical claim. The tradition started here. Everything else is a copy.

The one idea to carry

Strasbourg is proof that borders are arbitrary lines drawn by people with armies, and that the places caught in between those lines often end up richer for it. This city did not choose to be French or German. It was chosen, repeatedly, by forces it could not control. And out of that choicelessness it built something that belongs to neither side: a dialect, a cuisine, an architecture, a stubbornness about its own identity that outlasted every flag that flew over the cathedral.

When you walk through La Petite France, eat a Flammekueche in a winstub, hear Alsatian spoken at the next table, and then pass the European Parliament on your way to dinner, you are moving through the physical evidence of an argument that took five hundred years to settle. The argument is this: a city can be claimed by everyone and owned by no one, and be better for it. Strasbourg does not resolve the tension between its French and German halves. It holds both, without apology, and calls the result Alsatian.
