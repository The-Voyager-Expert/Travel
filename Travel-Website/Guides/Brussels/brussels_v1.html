Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 6 min · 🚕 19 min → Grand-Place
1. Grand-Place
↳ The central square of Brussels and a UNESCO World Heritage Site, ringed by the Gothic Town Hall, the King's House and gilded baroque guildhalls. Victor Hugo called it the most beautiful square in Europe — come early for soft light on the facades, and again after dark when they are lit.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Grand-Place · Lower Town
🚶 4 min · 🚕 4 min → Manneken Pis
2. Manneken Pis
↳ A 55 cm bronze fountain statue of a boy urinating, cast in 1619 and the irreverent mascot of Brussels. He owns a wardrobe of more than a thousand costumes and is dressed several times a week — checking what he is wearing is half the fun of the visit.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~15 min
📍 Rue de l'Étuve 31 · Lower Town
🚶 11 min · 🚕 9 min → Sablon
3. Sablon
↳ The elegant hill quarter of the upper town, centered on the Grand Sablon square and the flamboyant Gothic Church of Our Lady of Victories. This is the chocolate heart of Brussels — Pierre Marcolini, Neuhaus and Wittamer all keep flagship shops here, and a weekend antiques market fills the square.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Place du Grand Sablon · Sablon
🚶 9 min · 🚕 4 min → Place Poelaert
🚶 4 min → Petit Sablon · 🚎 92 tram to Fort-Jaco → Poelaert · 🚶 1 min → Place Poelaert
4. Place Poelaert
↳ The esplanade beside the colossal Palace of Justice offers the best free panorama in Brussels — the whole lower town spreads out below, with the Atomium and Koekelberg basilica on the horizon. A glass elevator drops from the square into the Marolles, the city's old working-class quarter.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Place Poelaert · Marolles

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 15 min · 🚕 15 min → Mont des Arts
1. Mont des Arts
↳ A geometric formal garden stepping down between the royal quarter and the old town, framed by the Royal Library and the National Archives. From the upper terrace the Town Hall spire lines up perfectly over the flowerbeds — the classic Brussels postcard view, especially at sunset.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~30 min
📍 Mont des Arts · Royal Quarter
🚶 5 min · 🚕 4 min → Coudenberg Palace
2. Coudenberg Palace
↳ Beneath the Place Royale lies the buried palace of Charles V — streets, cellars and the great Aula Magna hall of one of Europe's grandest courts, destroyed by fire in 1731 and rediscovered by archaeologists. You wander an entire underground city block, then surface through the BELvue museum.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Parc du Cinquantenaire
↳ A grand French-style park laid out for the fiftieth anniversary of Belgian independence, crowned by a monumental triple triumphal arch topped with a bronze quadriga. The lawns and fountains make a natural picnic break at the edge of the European Quarter.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 25 min → Atomium
1. Atomium
↳ A 102-meter iron crystal magnified 165 billion times, built for the 1958 World's Fair and never taken down. Escalators and a lightning-fast elevator link the nine spheres — exhibitions inside, and from the top sphere a 360° panorama across Brussels and the green royal estate of Laeken.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Mini-Europe
↳ All of Europe at your feet: 350 hand-built 1:25 scale models of the continent's landmarks, from Big Ben to the Acropolis, set in a garden at the foot of the Atomium. Vesuvius erupts, the Berlin Wall falls, and the gondolas of Venice actually float — kitsch done with astonishing craft.

🍮 Local Tastes
Belgian Praline
↳ The filled chocolate bonbon was invented in Brussels in 1912 by Jean Neuhaus Jr., whose pharmacy-turned-confiserie still trades in the Galeries Royales Saint-Hubert. Buy by the piece, not the box.
📍 Neuhaus · Pierre Marcolini · Mary
Brussels Waffle
↳ Rectangular, deep-pocketed and feather-light — yeasted batter, crisp outside, hollow inside. Eaten with powdered sugar, not buried in toppings. Not the dense caramelized Liège kind.
📍 Maison Dandoy
Lambic & Gueuze
↳ Beer fermented by wild airborne yeasts found only in the Senne valley — sour, still and ancient. Gueuze blends young and old lambic into a sparkling bottle Brussels calls its champagne.
📍 Cantillon Brewery · Moeder Lambic
Speculoos
↳ The spiced shortcrust biscuit of the Low Countries — cinnamon, clove and candied brown sugar. Maison Dandoy has pressed it into carved wooden molds on this spot since 1829.
📍 Maison Dandoy
Moules-Frites
↳ Belgium's national dish — a black pot of mussels steamed in white wine and celery, with twice-fried frites on the side. In season from July to April, best in the old centre.
📍 Aux Armes de Bruxelles · Le Fin de Siècle

❗️ Heads Up
Atomium — Winter Maintenance Closure
↳ The Atomium closes for about three weeks of maintenance each winter, from mid-January to early February.
Workaround: Check the official Atomium opening calendar before any January or February visit.
Mini-Europe — Closed Early January to Mid-March
↳ The miniature park shuts for its annual winter break from early January to mid-March.
Workaround: Pair the Atomium with Mini-Europe only from mid-March to early January; book online.
Pedestrian Zone — Taxis Detour Around the Car-Free Centre
↳ The lower town is car-free: driving from the hotel to the Grand-Place takes 19 min vs a 6 min walk.
Workaround: Walk everywhere inside the old centre; save taxis for Laeken and the European Quarter.
🎩 The City That Buried Its River

Brussels is built on a river you will never see. The Senne, which gave the city its lambic-brewing wild yeasts and its medieval port, was vaulted over in the 1860s after cholera outbreaks — the grand boulevards between your hotel and the Bourse run directly on top of it. The fish market of Sainte-Catherine, steps from the OPO's door, kept selling fish for decades beside a quay with no water in it.

A quieter pattern from this research: the city's icons keep surviving their own obsolescence. The Atomium was meant to stand for six months in 1958; the Grand-Place was rebuilt in four years after the French army flattened it in 1695; the Coudenberg palace burned, was buried, and became the city's basement. Brussels doesn't preserve things so much as refuse to finish demolishing them — which might be the most Belgian form of love.
