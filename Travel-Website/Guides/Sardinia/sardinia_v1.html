1. Bastione di Saint Remy
↳ A monumental neoclassical bastion built atop the old Spanish fortifications in 1902. The Terrazza Umberto I offers a sweeping panorama over the city rooftops, the port, and the distant lagoons.

2. Cattedrale di Santa Maria
↳ Cagliari's cathedral dates to the 13th century and blends Romanesque, Gothic, and Baroque styles across eight centuries of rebuilding. Below the nave, the Crypt of Martyrs holds 179 carved niches with relics of early Christian saints.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Museo Archeologico Nazionale
↳ Sardinia's most important museum houses thousands of Nuragic bronzetti — miniature bronze figurines from the island's Bronze Age civilization found nowhere else. Four floors trace settlement from the Neolithic through the Phoenician, Roman, and Byzantine periods.
📖 Wikipedia

4. Anfiteatro Romano
↳ The largest Roman monument in Sardinia, carved directly into the limestone hillside in the 2nd century AD. It seated 10,000 spectators for gladiatorial contests. An elevated walkway crosses the arena with underground corridors visible below.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Anfiteatro Romano · 4.3⭐ · comune.cagliari.it

5. Orto Botanico
↳ A lush 5-hectare botanical garden built around a Roman-era cistern and Punic well. Over 2,000 plant species from the Mediterranean, tropical Africa, and Southeast Asia fill winding paths shaded by century-old ficus trees.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 15 min → Molentargius-Saline Park
1. Molentargius-Saline Park
↳ A 1,600-hectare wetland park right inside the city where thousands of pink flamingos breed year-round. Former salt flats now shelter herons, cormorants, and stilts across freshwater and saltwater basins.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Via La Palma · Quartucciu
🚶 20 min · 🚕 7 min → Poetto Beach
2. Poetto Beach
↳ An 8-kilometer stretch of fine white sand backed by dunes and lined with beach bars. The water is shallow and turquoise for hundreds of meters. Kite-surfers work the wind at the eastern end.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Lungomare Poetto
🚶 15 min · 🚕 5 min → Sella del Diavolo
3. Sella del Diavolo
↳ The dramatic limestone promontory that splits the Gulf of Angels in two. A marked trail climbs through Mediterranean scrub past Punic cisterns and a Spanish watchtower to a ridge with views reaching from the city skyline to the open sea.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
⚠️ Rocky trail — wear hiking shoes. No shade on the ridge in summer.
📍 Via Calamosca

4. Calamosca Beach
↳ A small sheltered cove tucked below the Sant'Elia promontory. Calm, crystalline water in a natural rock amphitheater — far quieter than Poetto and favored by locals. The reward after the Sella del Diavolo hike.
📖 Wikipedia

1. Nora Archaeological Site
↳ The oldest city in Sardinia — founded by the Phoenicians in the 8th century BC and expanded by Carthage and Rome. Mosaics, a Roman theatre, thermal baths, and a temple of Tanit line a peninsula jutting into turquoise water.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Area Archeologica di Nora · 4.5⭐ · fondazionepulacultura.it
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 8:30pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Strada Provinciale 71 · Pula
🚶 5 min → Spiaggia di Nora
2. Spiaggia di Nora
↳ A crescent of white sand framed by the ruins on one side and Torre del Coltellazzo on the other. The water is shallow, clear, and sheltered by the peninsula. Roman columns lie submerged just offshore.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Spiaggia di Nora · Pula
🚶 3 min → Chiesa di Sant'Efisio
3. Chiesa di Sant'Efisio
↳ A tiny Romanesque church built on the spot where Sant'Efisio was beheaded in 303 AD. Every May 1st, a four-day procession — the largest religious festival in Sardinia — walks 65 km from Cagliari to this site.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~20 min

🍮 Local Tastes
Culurgiones
↳ Hand-pinched ravioli shaped like ears of wheat, filled with potato, mint, and pecorino. Originally from Ogliastra in eastern Sardinia, now served island-wide with fresh tomato sauce.
📍 Trattoria Lilliicu · Sa Domu Sarda
Malloreddus alla Campidanese
↳ Small ridged semolina shells tossed in a slow-cooked sauce of pork sausage, tomato, and saffron — Cagliari's signature primo. The ridges trap the fennel-spiked ragù.
📍 Trattoria Lilliicu · Antica Hostaria
Seadas
↳ Giant fried ravioli filled with melted fresh pecorino and lemon zest, drizzled with warm Sardinian honey. Once a shepherd's main course, now the island's most iconic dessert.
📍 Dulcis Caffè · Sa Domu Sarda
Pane Carasau
↳ Paper-thin crispy flatbread at least 3,000 years old — baked for shepherds because it stays fresh for a year. Served plain, drizzled with olive oil, or layered into pane frattau with tomato and egg.
📍 ChiaroScuro · Antica Hostaria

🏺 The Island That Named Itself

The Stele of Nora — found at the archaeological site on Day 3 — carries the oldest known written reference to Sardinia: the word ŠRDN, carved in Phoenician script around the 9th century BC. The name may derive from the Shardana, one of the mysterious Sea Peoples who raided Egypt during the Bronze Age collapse. Whether the Shardana settled the island or the island gave its name to the raiders remains one of Mediterranean archaeology's unsolved puzzles. Either way, the same three consonants have echoed from the pharaohs' enemies to the island's modern name for over 3,000 years.

Cagliari's pink flamingos — the colony visible at Molentargius on Day 2 — are not seasonal visitors. They breed here year-round, making this one of the few European cities where flamingos are permanent urban residents. The salt pans that attracted them were in commercial operation until 1985; when industry left, the birds stayed.
