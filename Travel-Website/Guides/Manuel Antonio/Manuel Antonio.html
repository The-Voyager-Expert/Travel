Day 1

Manuel Antonio National Park

↳ The smallest national park in Costa Rica holds one of the highest concentrations of biodiversity on earth. Four ecosystems — tropical rainforest, mangrove, beach, and coral reef — share 1,983 hectares. The main trail leads from the park entrance through dense canopy to Playa Manuel Antonio, a wide white-sand beach backed by forest. Punta Catedral, the park's central peninsula, connects two beaches via a forested ridge walk with Pacific views. Wildlife along the main trail includes white-faced capuchin monkeys, two-toed sloths, scarlet macaws, and — if the season is right — the endangered Central American squirrel monkey. Arrive at opening; the park caps daily visitors at 2,500 and wildlife sightings are most active in the first two hours.

Day 2

🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 14 min → Quepos

Quepos

↳ The working port town 3.5 km north of Manuel Antonio, built on the back of a banana and tuna industry before tourism reshaped the economy. The downtown grid is compact and navigable on foot: a covered market, hardware shops, and sodas (local diners) serving gallo pinto and casado line the main streets. The seafront Paseo and the central square (Parque Central) are the social anchors. Unlike the resort strip, Quepos moves at a local pace and is worth an hour's walk for context on the region beyond the tourist corridor.

🚕 8 min → Isla Damas

Isla Damas

↳ A mangrove island in the Damas Estuary, reached by a guided boat tour from the dock near the estuary mouth. The tour moves by motorboat through a network of tidal channels fringed by red and black mangroves. Guides identify animals using the root system — American crocodiles resting on mudbanks, white ibis picking through shallows, green iguanas draping overhead branches, and night herons roosting in the canopy. The channel narrows toward the island interior, where the forest closes in and the boat slows to a near-stop. Departure times depend on the tide cycle; the tour runs best at mid-to-high tide when the channels are navigable to the deepest sections.

Day 3

🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 55 min → Don Lulo's Nauyaca

Nauyaca Waterfalls

↳ Two-tiered waterfalls on the Río Nauyaca, accessed from Don Lulo's private land south of Dominical. The upper falls drop 45 meters into a wide swimming pool; a lower cascade sits 200 meters downstream. Access is either by horseback or by truck shuttle — both depart from Don Lulo's entry point and cover 7 km of dirt track into the Fila Costeña mountains. The truck option is faster (45 min each way); horses take about 90 min but allow closer contact with the jungle. At the falls, a flat rocky area beside the pool makes a natural rest stop. The pool is deep enough to swim under the waterfall and clear enough to watch your feet on the bottom. The access road closes at 2:00pm, so entry by 9:00am is needed to have full time at the falls.

🍮 Local Tastes

Gallo Pinto — Black beans and rice fried together, Costa Rica's national breakfast. Served with scrambled eggs, fried sweet plantains, and natilla (sour cream). Found at every soda in Quepos and Manuel Antonio from early morning.

Casado — The standard Costa Rican lunch plate: rice, black beans, salad, and a protein (corvina, chicken, or pork), with fried sweet plantains on the side. Order it by name at any soda for an affordable full meal.

Ceviche Tico — Unlike Peruvian-style ceviche, the Costa Rican version uses corvina (sea bass) marinated in lime juice with onion, cilantro, and a touch of Worcestershire sauce. Served with crackers at casual beachside sodas near the park entrance.

Pipa Fría — Young coconut water sold ice-cold from roadside carts near the park entrance and along the main road. Vendors crack the coconut on site; the soft flesh is eaten with a spoon after finishing the water.

🚌 Getting Around

Taxis: Red SETENA-licensed taxis are the standard mode of transport between Manuel Antonio and Quepos. Metered within Quepos; fixed rates apply on the Manuel Antonio road — agree on the fare before departure. Available at the Quepos taxi stand and by phone dispatch.

Shared Shuttles: Interbus and Grayline operate shared-van shuttles connecting Manuel Antonio with San José (Tobías Bolaños and Juan Santamaría airports), La Fortuna/Arenal, and Monteverde. Book at least 24 hours in advance; pick-up from the hotel is included.

Local Bus: Transportes Morales runs a frequent public bus along the main road between Quepos and Manuel Antonio. Stops are unmarked but the route is straightforward; ask the driver and any local will confirm the stop.

No tram or metro serves the Quepos–Manuel Antonio corridor.

🚆 Train Stations Near Hotel

No train stations near the hotel. Costa Rica has no passenger rail service. Intercity travel uses long-distance buses from the Quepos bus terminal (🚕 12 min from hotel), served by Tracopa and Transportes Morales from San José.

✨ Claude Inspiration

🐒 The Squirrel Monkey's Second Chance

The Central American squirrel monkey — called mono tití locally — is one of the rarest primates in the world. Its entire remaining population lives in two isolated pockets of Costa Rica: Manuel Antonio National Park and the Osa Peninsula. At the time the park was created in 1972, the species had been pushed to the edge of local extinction by cattle ranching and deforestation that had cleared most of the Pacific lowland forest.

A mono tití troop moves fast and loud — 20 to 75 individuals traveling through the canopy at once, the group maintaining contact with high-pitched chirps. They're most active along the park's main trail in the first two hours after the 7:00am opening. Their size (about 30 cm, roughly the size of a large squirrel) makes them easy to overlook until you're directly under a troop. The best sign is the noise: when a group passes overhead, the canopy sounds briefly alive.

The park's creation was contested by landowners and did not happen cleanly. A small community was displaced from within what became the park boundary, a fact that still comes up in Costa Rican conservation discussions. The result — one of the most biodiverse patches of protected land per square kilometer on earth — reflects a tradeoff that is genuinely contested rather than simply heroic.
