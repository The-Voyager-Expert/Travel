Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 2 min · 🚕 2 min → Zócalo de Oaxaca
1. Zócalo de Oaxaca
↳ The main public square of Oaxaca, established at the city's 1529 founding and bordered by the Palacio de Gobierno and the 17th-century Cathedral. The plaza has served as gathering ground for political events, markets, and festivals for nearly five centuries.

2. Templo de Santo Domingo de Guzmán
↳ A Dominican church begun in 1572 with one of the most ornate Baroque interiors in the Americas. A gilded genealogical tree of the Guzmán family spreads across the choir ceiling vault; the nave is encrusted with more than 100 relief figures of saints and apostles.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Jardín Etnobotánico de Oaxaca
↳ A 2-hectare botanical garden inside the former Dominican convent of Santo Domingo, documenting the plant heritage of Oaxaca's 16 ecosystems. The collection spans wild and cultivated agave species, copal trees, cacti, and medicinal plants. Guided tours only — booked at the museum office.

4. Basílica de Nuestra Señora de la Soledad
↳ Oaxaca's most beloved pilgrimage church, built between 1682 and 1690 in local Baroque style with a facade carved from green cantera stone. The interior holds the image of the Virgen de la Soledad — patroness of the state — encased in a pearl-embroidered robe and topped with a silver crown studded with diamonds.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 25 min → Monte Albán
1. Monte Albán
↳ The Zapotec capital for 1,300 years, built on a mountain artificially leveled 400 meters above the Oaxaca Valley. The ceremonial core — the Grand Plaza, Building J observatory, the Ball Court, the North Platform, and the South Platform — served a city of 25,000 at its peak. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1987.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Museo Textil de Oaxaca
↳ A free textile museum in a restored 16th-century Dominican building, holding the largest textile collection in Mexico — over 10,500 pieces from Oaxaca's seven regions. Rotating exhibitions focus on weaving techniques, natural dyes, and the cultural significance of textiles in indigenous communities.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 15 min → Árbol del Tule
1. Árbol del Tule
↳ A Montezuma cypress in the churchyard of Santa María del Tule with the widest trunk circumference of any tree on earth — 58 meters around the base. Carbon-dating supports an age of at least 1,400 years. The trunk's furrowed surface reveals shapes locals have named: an elephant, a lion, a puma.
📖 Wikipedia

⏰ ~45 min

2. Zona Arqueológica de Mitla
↳ The Zapotec religious capital after Monte Albán's decline. Palace walls are faced with the most sophisticated stone mosaic work in pre-Columbian Mesoamerica — cut pieces forming 14 distinct interlocking geometric motifs, assembled without mortar. The name means "Place of the Dead" in Nahuatl.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Hierve el Agua
↳ Mineral-laden springs deposit calcium carbonate over millennia, building cliff cascades resembling frozen waterfalls. Two natural pools at the cliff edge look out over the Oaxacan valley. The name means the water boils — a reference to the visible carbonation, not temperature.

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 18 min → Zona Arqueológica de Atzompa
1. Zona Arqueológica de Atzompa
↳ A Zapotec hilltop city active between 300 BCE and 900 CE, 8 km northwest of the city center. The site includes restored palace complexes, a ball court, and a ceramic workshop area producing distinctive green-glazed pottery. Far less visited than Monte Albán.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Cerro de la Campaña · Santa María Atzompa
🚕 15 min → Ex-Convento de Santiago Apóstol · Cuilapam de Guerrero
2. Ex-Convento de Santiago Apóstol · Cuilapam de Guerrero
↳ A Dominican convent begun in 1555 and abandoned unfinished around 1570 when indigenous labor was withdrawn after a dispute with colonial authorities. The roofless basilica nave — 74 meters long — now stands open to the sky. Vicente Guerrero, first president of independent Mexico, was executed here in 1831.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Monday - Friday 9:00am - 5:00pm
🚫 Closed Saturday & Sunday
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Cuilapam de Guerrero
🚕 20 min → Museo de las Culturas de Oaxaca
3. Museo de las Culturas de Oaxaca
↳ The state's pre-eminent archaeology museum in the Ex-Convento de Santo Domingo. Its centerpiece is the Mixtec treasure from Monte Albán Tomb 7 — the largest pre-Columbian burial hoard ever found in the Americas, excavated in 1932: gold pectorals, jade masks, turquoise mosaics.

🌵 Oaxaca at the Crossroads

Oaxaca is where three of Mexico's most significant pre-Columbian civilizations — Zapotec, Mixtec, and later Aztec — overlapped in time and territory without merging. Monte Albán was Zapotec for over a millennium; by 700 CE it was abandoned and the Mixtec moved into the surrounding valleys. Mitla, which Day 3 visits, served as the Zapotec sacred city through all of this — then became a Mixtec religious center, then fell to the Spanish. The 16th-century Spanish built their cathedral directly atop the Zapotec temples at Mitla's central group. Three civilizations, one sacred site.

The Mixtec treasure from Tomb 7 at Monte Albán — on display at the Museo de las Culturas — was found in a tomb the Zapotec had built but the Mixtec reused as a burial chamber around 1350 CE. Alfonso Caso opened it in 1932 and found nine individuals interred with gold pectorals, jade masks, and turquoise mosaics. The volume and quality of the objects make it the largest pre-Columbian burial hoard ever found in the Americas — hiding inside a site that had already been partially excavated for decades.
