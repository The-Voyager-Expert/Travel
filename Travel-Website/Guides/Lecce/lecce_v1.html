↳ Second-century amphitheatre built for 25,000 spectators, discovered in 1901 beneath Piazza Sant'Oronzo — only a third is excavated, with the rest concealed under surrounding buildings.

🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 1h 20min → Rione Monti
1. Rione Monti
↳ UNESCO World Heritage district of over 1,000 limestone trulli with conical dry-stone roofs — the densest concentration of these unique dwellings, many painted with mystical symbols, dating from the 14th century and still inhabited today.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Rione Monti · Alberobello
🚶 5 min · 🚕 2 min → Trullo Sovrano
2. Trullo Sovrano
↳ The only two-story trullo in Alberobello, now a museum showing how families lived inside these beehive-shaped stone houses — built in the 18th century as a semi-religious meeting place, it preserves original furnishings and domestic tools across both floors.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Ostuni Old Town
↳ The Citta Bianca perched on three hills — a labyrinth of whitewashed alleys, arches, and steep staircases offering panoramic views over olive groves stretching to the Adriatic, inhabited since the Stone Age with the medieval core retaining its defensive layout.
📖 Wikipedia

1. Grotta della Poesia
↳ Natural rock pool formed where a limestone cave roof collapsed, revealing turquoise waters and Messapian inscriptions from the 2nd millennium BC carved into the surrounding walls — one of the most important prehistoric graffiti sites in the Mediterranean.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 7:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Roca Vecchia · Melendugno

2. Otranto Cathedral
↳ 11th-century cathedral sheltering Europe's largest medieval mosaic floor — a 16-meter Tree of Life created by monk Pantaleone between 1163 and 1165, depicting biblical scenes, zodiac signs, and Alexander the Great across the entire nave, transept, and apse.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Castello Aragonese
↳ 15th-century coastal fortress with massive cylindrical towers and a dry moat, built by the Aragonese after the 1480 Ottoman siege that killed 800 inhabitants — the ramparts offer sweeping views over the harbour and Adriatic, and the interior hosts rotating exhibitions.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 1:00pm · 3:00pm - 11:00pm

4. Chiesa di San Pietro
↳ One of southern Italy's oldest Byzantine churches, built between the 9th and 10th centuries with three naves separated by columns and rare frescoes depicting scenes from the lives of Christ, Saints Peter and Paul, and the Last Supper in distinct Byzantine style.

💡 Pietra Leccese

Every carved angel, every spiralling column, every grotesque face staring down from a Baroque facade in Lecce exists because of one geological accident: the local limestone is so soft when freshly quarried that masons could carve it with hand tools like butter, yet it hardens to golden durability once exposed to air. This is pietra leccese — a calcarenite formed from compressed shell fragments 20 million years ago. Without it, the city's architects would never have attempted the level of ornamental excess visible on Santa Croce's facade, where a single column might carry a dozen sculpted figures. The stone's warm honey colour deepens with centuries of sun, which is why the old town glows amber at golden hour while newer restorations read pale yellow. Every building in the centro storico is essentially carved from a single material — the entire city is a sculpture made from the same block.
