1. Country Music Hall of Fame and Museum
↳ The definitive shrine of American country music, holding the genre's largest collection of artifacts, instruments, costumes, and recordings. The Hall of Fame Rotunda honors the music's inducted legends; rotating galleries trace the sound from string bands to modern stars.

↳ The state's history museum, purpose-built in 2018 at the foot of Capitol Hill. Galleries run from prehistoric Tennessee through frontier settlement, the Civil War, and the civil rights era, with original artifacts, an interactive timeline gallery, and rotating exhibitions. Admission is free.

2. Tennessee State Capitol
↳ A Greek Revival landmark completed in 1859 and designed by William Strickland, who is buried within its walls. The hilltop building still houses the state legislature; free guided and self-guided tours pass the legislative chambers, the library, and grounds holding the tomb of President James K. Polk.

3. Frist Art Museum
↳ A non-collecting art museum in Nashville's grand 1934 Art Deco former main post office. With no permanent collection, the Frist mounts a constant rotation of major travelling exhibitions spanning ancient to contemporary art, alongside an interactive studio. The marble lobby is a landmark in itself.

4. Parthenon
↳ A full-scale replica of the Athenian Parthenon, built for Tennessee's 1897 Centennial Exposition and made permanent in concrete. Standing in Centennial Park, it is the only exact-size reproduction of the Greek temple in the world, complete with reconstructed pediment sculptures.

1. The Hermitage
↳ Andrew Jackson's plantation home, a National Historic Landmark on grounds the seventh president owned from 1804 until his death here in 1845. The restored 1830s Greek Revival mansion, gardens, Jackson's tomb, and exhibits on the enslaved community make it one of the best-preserved early presidential sites.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Cheekwood
↳ A 1930s Georgian-style mansion set in 55 acres of cultivated gardens on the western edge of Nashville. The limestone estate of the Cheek family now serves as a botanical garden and art museum, with formal gardens, a sculpture trail, period rooms, and a permanent collection of American and decorative art.

↳ A Greek Revival mansion of 1853 at the heart of what was once a 5,400-acre thoroughbred stud farm that shaped American horse racing bloodlines. Guided tours of the antebellum house and grounds cover the mansion, original outbuildings, and the lives of the enslaved people who ran the famed estate.

🍮 Local Tastes
Nashville Hot Chicken
↳ Cayenne-lacquered fried chicken, born in Nashville at Prince's after a scorned cook over-spiced a partner's plate. Now a city icon, served on white bread with pickles across heat levels from mild to fiery.
📍 Hattie B's Hot Chicken · Prince's Hot Chicken
Goo Goo Cluster
↳ Invented in Nashville in 1912 and billed as the first combination candy bar — caramel, marshmallow nougat, peanuts, and milk chocolate. A flagship downtown shop sells classic and premium handmade versions.
📍 Goo Goo Shop
Meat-and-Three
↳ A Southern cafeteria tradition strong in Nashville: one meat plus three sides from a daily-changing board of vegetables and comfort dishes. Order at the counter — a lunchtime ritual at long-running local rooms.
📍 Arnold's Country Kitchen · Swett's Restaurant

✨ The Circle Travels

The wooden circle on the Grand Ole Opry House stage was cut from the floor of the Ryman Auditorium when the Opry moved out in 1974. Stand in either building on this trip and you are looking at the same six feet of oak — sawed out of the Mother Church and carried across town so every performer would keep singing on the exact boards Hank Williams and Patsy Cline stood on. Day 1 and Day 3 quietly bookend that same piece of wood.

Nashville keeps building exact copies of things it admires. The Parthenon in Centennial Park is a full-scale concrete replica of the Athenian original — the only one in the world — raised for a fair in 1897 and never torn down. A century later the city did it again in spirit, replicating the Ryman's stage circle inside a brand-new arena. For a place nicknamed Music City, Nashville's deepest instinct may be preservation: copy the thing you love, exactly, so it can never be lost.
