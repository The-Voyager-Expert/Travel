Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 3 min · 🚕 2 min → Málaga Cathedral
1. Málaga Cathedral
↳ Known as La Manquita — the one-armed lady — for its unfinished south tower. This Renaissance cathedral took over 200 years to build and blends Gothic, Renaissance, and Baroque styles under soaring 40-meter vaults.
📖 Wikipedia

🚶 5 min · 🚕 3 min → Roman Theatre
2. Roman Theatre
↳ A 1st-century BC theatre built under Augustus, buried for centuries and rediscovered during mid-20th-century roadworks. The cavea, orchestra pit, and stage backdrop survive in remarkable condition at the foot of the Alcazaba.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Saturday 10:00am - 6:00pm · Sunday 10:00am - 4:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday

🚶 2 min · 🚕 2 min → Alcazaba
3. Alcazaba
↳ An 11th-century Moorish fortress-palace with layered defensive walls, horseshoe arches, tranquil courtyards with flowing water, and sweeping views over the port and city from its ramparts.
📖 Wikipedia
🎟️ Alcazaba + Gibralfaro Combo · 4.5⭐ · alcazabaygibralfaro.malaga.eu

📍 Calle Alcazabilla 2 · Centro
🚶 20 min · 🚕 8 min → Castillo de Gibralfaro
4. Castillo de Gibralfaro
↳ A 14th-century hilltop castle built by the Nasrid dynasty to protect the Alcazaba below. The walkable ramparts offer the best panoramic views in Málaga — the port, the bullring, the cathedral, and the coastline stretching toward Africa.
📖 Wikipedia

↳ Housed in the 16th-century Palacio de Buenavista, this museum holds over 200 works spanning Picasso's career — from early academic sketches to late Cubist ceramics — in the city where he was born.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 15 min → La Concepción Botanical Garden
1. La Concepción Botanical Garden
↳ A 19th-century subtropical paradise in the hills north of the city, with over 2,000 plant species including towering palms, centuries-old wisteria, bamboo groves, and a romantic English-landscape design created by a marquis and his wife.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Sunday 9:30am - 8:30pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 Camino del Jardín Botánico 3 · La Concepción
🚕 12 min → Basílica de Santa María de la Victoria
2. Basílica de Santa María de la Victoria
↳ Málaga's patron saint basilica, built on the site where the Catholic Monarchs camped during the siege. The interior houses a lavishly decorated Camarín and a crypt with painted ceiling depicting death and the afterlife.

3. Iglesia de Santiago
↳ The oldest church in Málaga, built in the years immediately after the Christian reconquest. Its Mudéjar bell tower is one of three surviving in the city, and the baptismal font inside is where Pablo Picasso was christened.
📖 Wikipedia

🎨 The Picasso Thread

There's a quiet thread that runs through this trip. On Day 2 you'll stand in the Palacio de Buenavista looking at Picasso's earliest works — sketches he made as a child in this city. On Day 3 you'll walk into Iglesia de Santiago and find the exact baptismal font where he was christened Pablo Diego José Francisco de Paula Juan Nepomuceno María de los Remedios Cipriano de la Santísima Trinidad Ruiz y Picasso, shortly after his birth. The museum and the church are five minutes apart. One holds the art; the other holds the beginning.

The Alcazaba's double-walled design — where you walk between two parallel fortified walls before ever reaching the interior — was a defensive innovation that the Moors brought from Damascus. When Ferdinand and Isabella finally took Málaga after one of the longest sieges of the Reconquista, it wasn't the walls they broke through. They starved the city for three months. The Basílica de la Victoria on Day 3 was built on the exact camp where Ferdinand pitched his tent during that siege — the same spot where he made a vow to the Virgin if the city fell.
