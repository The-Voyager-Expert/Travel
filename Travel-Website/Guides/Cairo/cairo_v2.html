1. Grand Egyptian Museum
↳ The world's largest single-civilization museum, housing over 100,000 artifacts including the complete Tutankhamun collection and the two solar boats of Khufu. Connected to the Giza plateau by an elevated walkway.

2. Giza Pyramid Complex
↳ UNESCO World Heritage Site containing the pyramids of Khufu, Khafre, and Menkaure — built during Egypt's Fourth Dynasty. The site includes the Great Sphinx, valley temples, and a workers' village; the only intact ancient Wonder of the World.

3. Saqqara
↳ The Step Pyramid of Djoser is the world's oldest large-scale stone structure. The necropolis also contains the Pyramid of Unas — home to the oldest corpus of religious texts — and the Serapeum underground chambers of the sacred Apis bulls.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 20 min → Cairo Citadel
1. Cairo Citadel
↳ A fortified Ayyubid complex built by Saladin on a spur of the Mokattam Hills, serving as Egypt's seat of government for nearly 700 years. Within the walls: the Mosque of Muhammad Ali with its Ottoman alabaster interior and panoramic views across the city.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1.5 hr
📍 Salah Salem Street · Mokattam Hills
🚶 7 min · 🚕 4 min → Mosque-Madrassa of Sultan Hassan
2. Mosque-Madrassa of Sultan Hassan
↳ A Mamluk monumental complex at Salah al-Din Square combining a mosque with four madrassas for the Sunni schools of jurisprudence. The twin minarets reach 84 meters — the entrance portal is one of the tallest in the Islamic world.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Salah al-Din Square · al-Khalifa
🚕 5 min → Al-Azhar Park
3. Al-Azhar Park
↳ A 30-hectare public park built by the Aga Khan Trust for Culture on a site used for centuries as a rubbish dump. The park incorporates a restored Ayyubid wall and elevated sightlines across the minarets and domes of the historic quarter.
📖 Wikipedia

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 15 min → Manial Palace
1. Manial Palace
↳ A royal residence built between 1899 and 1929 by Prince Mohammed Ali Tewfik on Rhoda Island. Five buildings in Islamic, Ottoman, Persian, and European styles are set around garden courtyards, with a private museum containing the prince's hunting trophies, porcelain, and royal regalia.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1.5 hr
📍 Rhoda Island · al-Manial
🚕 20 min → Mosque of Ibn Tulun
2. Mosque of Ibn Tulun
↳ Egypt's oldest surviving mosque in its original form, built between 876 and 879 CE by Ahmad ibn Tulun in Abbasid style. The vast courtyard is ringed by arcaded halls and a unique minaret with an external spiral staircase — a design found only here among Cairo's medieval monuments.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Ahmad Ibn Tulun Square · Sayyida Zaynab
🚶 2 min · 🚕 3 min → Gayer-Anderson Museum
3. Gayer-Anderson Museum
↳ Two adjoining 17th-century Mamluk houses connected by a rooftop bridge, furnished as lived-in rooms from Major Gayer-Anderson's 1930s posting in Cairo. The interiors hold Islamic decorative arts, Persian carpets, and mashrabiyya screens alongside a personal collection of Egyptian antiquities.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 4:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Ibn Tulun Square · Sayyida Zaynab
🚕 20 min → Cairo Tower
4. Cairo Tower
↳ A 187-meter reinforced-concrete tower on Gezira Island completed in 1961, designed by architect Naoum Shebib with a lattice pattern modelled on the pharaonic lotus plant. The observation deck at the top offers 360-degree views over the Nile, the Citadel, the Giza plateau, and greater Cairo.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 11:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Gezira Island · Zamalek

2. Citadel of Qaitbay
↳ A Mamluk defensive fortress built between 1477 and 1479 by Sultan Al-Ashraf Qaitbay on the exact promontory where the ancient Pharos lighthouse — one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World — once stood. The sea-facing towers and interior halls now house the National Maritime Museum of Egypt.

↳ A 150-acre royal landscape on the eastern Mediterranean coast commissioned by Khedive Abbas II in 1892 as his summer residence. The public grounds include a pine and palm forest, a private beach peninsula, the Al-Haramlik Palace, and a watchtower on the shoreline.

🍮 Local Tastes
Koshary
↳ Egypt's national street dish: layers of macaroni, rice, vermicelli, lentils, and chickpeas topped with garlicky tomato sauce, spiced vinegar, and fried onions.
📍 Koshary Abou Tarek · Bab al-Luq
Ta'amiya
↳ A version of falafel made from ground fava beans rather than chickpeas — fried crisp and served in flatbread with salad and tahini. Distinct from the chickpea falafel found elsewhere in the region.
📍 Felfela · Bab al-Luq
Feteer Meshaltet
↳ A flaky layered pastry prepared on a large griddle, served sweet with honey or jam or savory with cheese or minced meat. Vendors called feteer-makers stretch and fold the dough to order.
📍 Downtown street bakeries
Karkadai
↳ A deep-red hibiscus infusion served cold or hot at coffeehouses and street stalls throughout the city — tart and lightly sweetened, central to the traditional ahwa coffeehouse culture.
📍 El-Fishawi Café · al-Gamaliyya

🗼 A Tower Built to Insult the United States, in Full View of Its Embassy

The Cairo Tower exists because Gamal Abdel Nasser refused to be bought. A CIA operative reportedly handed Nasser a personal cash gift meant to steer him away from supporting African independence movements — an attempt at quiet influence Nasser found insulting enough to make public. Rather than pocket it, he redirected the entire sum to Egypt's government to fund a monumental tower, deliberately sited on Gezira Island so it would rise in plain view of the American Embassy across the Nile. Egyptians nicknamed it "Roosevelt's endowment" after the CIA officer involved; the phrase got garbled in translation into something considerably ruder once it reached Washington, and the tower's nickname among Americans stuck for decades as an inside joke about exactly who got the last laugh.

The rooftop where James Bond fought a henchman in The Spy Who Loved Me is a real, functioning museum. The Gayer-Anderson Museum is two 16th-century houses joined into one home by a British army doctor who spent years filling the rooms with furniture, textiles, and antiques collected across the region. The production shot Bond walking straight out of the Mosque of Ibn Tulun next door and into the "mansion of Fekkesh" — actually this house — before the rooftop chase scene. Decades later, both the mosque courtyard and the terrace look almost exactly as they did on camera.
