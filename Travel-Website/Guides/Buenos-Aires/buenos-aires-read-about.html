A city built by immigrants to be somewhere else — and why that restless, unresolved energy is the most interesting thing about it.

The city that wanted to be Paris

You'll feel it within an hour of arriving: Buenos Aires is the most European city in the Americas, and it knows this, and it has complicated feelings about it. The wide diagonal boulevards, the ornate apartment facades, the café culture where an espresso comes with a small glass of sparkling water and no one rushes you — all of it was consciously designed in the late nineteenth century to look like something it wasn't. The city wanted to be Paris. It got close enough that the comparison became unavoidable, and then uncomfortable, and then a kind of running joke that Porteños — Buenos Aires locals — tell on themselves before you can tell it on them.

The Avenida de Mayo, which connects the Casa Rosada to the Congreso Nacional, was laid out in 1894 by a city engineer who had just returned from Paris. The Palacio Barolo, which rises at its southern end, was designed in 1923 by an Italian architect obsessed with Dante's Divine Comedy — the building's height in meters matches the total canto count of the Commedia, and its three sections represent Inferno, Purgatory, and Paradise. That kind of extravagant, slightly unhinged ambition is very Buenos Aires: a city that builds its literary obsessions into its skyline and then plays it cool when you notice.

The Buenos Aires that exists today was built almost entirely between 1880 and 1930, which means it is a city that looks like the fever dream of an era that wanted very badly to arrive.

The wealth that paid for all of it came from beef and wheat. Argentina in those decades was one of the richest countries on earth — richer per capita than France or Germany at the turn of the century, genuinely competitive with the United States. The grand public buildings, the private palaces of the oligarchy, the European-style residential neighborhoods with their wrought iron balconies and limestone facades: all of it was funded by the pampa, the vast flat grassland that stretches west of the city like a green ocean to the Andes, producing grain and cattle on an industrial scale that the world had never seen.

The ships that made the city

Between 1880 and 1930, more than six million immigrants landed in Buenos Aires. They came mostly from Italy and Spain, but also from Eastern Europe, Syria, Lebanon, Germany, and dozens of other countries, and they arrived with almost nothing and had to decide, on the spot, what they were going to become.

The Italians landed in such numbers — more than two million in fifty years — that they didn't fully assimilate into Argentine Spanish so much as fuse with it. Lunfardo, the slang of Buenos Aires, was born in the port neighborhood of La Boca in the 1880s and is built largely from Italian dialect words that nobody in Italy uses anymore. When you hear Porteños speak Spanish, the cadence is unmistakably Italian — the rising intonation, the long vowels, the rhythm of the sentence — because for three generations, Italian was the language the city was actually thinking in while it spoke Spanish out loud.

La Boca, where the Riachuelo meets the Río de la Plata, was the first neighborhood most of those immigrants saw. Walk down the Caminito today — the tourist strip of colorful corrugated-iron houses that has become one of the city's most photographed spots — and you're standing in what was once a working waterfront neighborhood that smelled of chemicals from the nearby tanneries. The colors came from leftover ship paint: the cheapest possible material for painting the cheapest possible housing. The neighborhood has been curated since then, but the story it's telling is real.

Tango: what the city invented to talk about itself

Tango was born in the same port neighborhoods — La Boca, Barracas, San Telmo — in the 1880s, when waves of newly arrived immigrant men vastly outnumbered women and the dance halls provided a place to meet, to compete, and to rehearse an intimacy that had no other outlet. The music fused African candombe rhythms (from the city's earlier Black population, largely erased from the official history), Cuban habanera, and the milonga, a faster precursor form. The embrace of early tango was explicit and close — too close for polite society, which banned it in several Buenos Aires parishes around 1900 before it became internationally fashionable in Paris and returned home vindicated.

Carlos Gardel, the singer who became the voice of classic tango, is the most famous Argentine who ever lived. His face is on murals across the city. He died in a plane crash in Medellín in 1935 at the height of his fame, which sealed his mythic status permanently. His tomb in the Chacarita cemetery has a bronze statue with a cigarette perpetually burning in the hand — changed out by fans every week.

Tango is not a tourist attraction in Buenos Aires. It is still what the city does when it wants to talk about desire and loss without having to use words.

The milongas — the social tango dances that happen in community halls and rented ballrooms across the city several nights a week — are nothing like the show-tango you'll see in San Telmo tourist restaurants. They are serious, almost formal, conducted according to a complex etiquette of eye contact and slight nods that determines who dances with whom. People arrive, sit at tables along the wall, and wait. The invitation to dance — the cabeceo, a small tilt of the head toward someone across the room — is given and received or declined without a word spoken. If you go to a real milonga and sit at the edge, you will see something the city doesn't put in its brochures: what it looks like when people who've spent decades learning to move together actually dance.

The century that broke the country

Here is the thing about Argentina that most visitors sense but can't quite place: this is a country that was rich and is now not, and everyone over forty can feel the distance between those two states like a phantom limb.

The decline was not sudden. It started with the Great Depression, which wrecked the commodity-export economy. It accelerated with the first military coup in 1930, which ended Argentina's democratic experiment and inaugurated a century of violent oscillation between elected governments and military takeovers. Juan Perón came to power in 1946 and built a mass movement based on labor rights and social spending and a cult of personality centered on himself and, fatally, his wife Eva. Peronism remade Argentina's politics in ways the country is still arguing about — a religion, a ideology, a set of symbols that different Argentines interpret as entirely opposite things. The economic model Perón built eventually collapsed, as they all did, but the political identity it created proved indestructible.

Between 1976 and 1983 the military ran the country as a terror state, "disappearing" an estimated 30,000 people — kidnapping, torturing, and killing suspected leftists, sometimes dropping them still alive from planes into the Río de la Plata. The Plaza de Mayo, the main square in front of the Casa Rosada, is where the mothers of the disappeared began marching in 1977 with photographs of their children around their necks. They still march, every Thursday at 3:30 p.m. This is not history. You can watch it happen.

The economic crises kept coming — 1989, 2001, 2018, 2023 — until crisis ceased to be an event and became the ambient condition. By 2001, the government froze bank accounts to stop a run; middle-class Argentines who had spent their lives saving watched their savings locked away, then devalued, then converted at punitive rates. The cacerolazos — pot-banging protests in the streets — lasted for weeks. Five presidents resigned in ten days.

The Recoleta and the limits of amnesia

The Recoleta Cemetery is in the wealthiest neighborhood of the city, and it looks like a small city itself: marble mausoleums stacked together like a compressed version of Paris's most expensive arrondissement, with narrow alleys between them and an atmosphere somewhere between museum and gothic novel. The Duartes are here, the Alvears, the Anchorenas — the oligarchic families who built this city and ran it for a century.

Eva Perón is also here. Her tomb — covered in fresh flowers every day — is in a narrow passage near the back, guarded by a bronze plaque bearing her name. Evita grew up in poverty in a small town in the province, the illegitimate daughter of a landowner, and came to Buenos Aires at fifteen to become an actress. She became the most powerful woman in Argentine history, the voice of the descamisados — the shirtless ones, the poor — against the oligarchy whose dead are buried twenty meters from her grave. That proximity is not accidental. It is the whole Argentine argument compressed into a few square meters of marble.

What the city feels like now

The Buenos Aires you're walking through is a city that has been in some form of economic crisis for most of your lifetime, and it does not feel like a city in crisis. That is genuinely confusing until you spend a few days and start to understand the mechanism: Porteños are exceptionally good at maintaining the texture of a rich life on drastically reduced means. The cafés stay full. The bookstores — Buenos Aires has more bookstores per capita than almost any city on earth — stay open. The Sunday antique fair in San Telmo, the open-air art shows in Palermo, the asado grilling that happens every weekend in every park and backyard: none of this requires the peso to be stable.

What the economic pressure has produced is a city that is, by the standards of its peer capitals, extraordinarily affordable to visit — and also one where the tension between what was and what is runs just below the surface of every conversation. Argentines are among the most politically aware people you'll encounter anywhere. Everyone has a theory about what went wrong and who is responsible. These are not casual opinions. They are held the way other people hold religious beliefs, with the same intensity and the same immunity to evidence.

Walk through Palermo — the sprawling residential neighborhood of tree-lined streets and restaurants and independent boutiques that is, in many ways, the most livable part of the city — and you're in what used to be cattle auction grounds and is now the Buenos Aires that imagines itself as a South American Brooklyn. The wine bar next to the design studio next to the record shop: this is a city that keeps reinventing itself in the spaces left by the last collapse, building something new out of what the previous era abandoned.

The thing to carry with you

Before you go, here is the thing to carry with you through the city: Buenos Aires was built by people who arrived with nothing and built everything they wanted to be — in a borrowed European style, with Italian rhythms underneath the Spanish, tango invented to process feelings there were no words for, political passions so intense they survived every catastrophe. The city has been broken, more than once, by forces more powerful than it. It keeps coming back to the same identity: restless, literate, dramatic, convinced that something better is just around the corner, furious that it hasn't arrived yet.

Once you see it — in the Teatro Colón that the oligarchy built to prove the city belonged in the same conversation as Vienna, in the Thursday marchers in the Plaza de Mayo who haven't stopped since 1977, in the tango dancers at three in the morning negotiating an entire conversation with a tilt of the head — you see it everywhere. A city that has always been arguing with itself about what it should have been. Still arguing. Still dancing.
