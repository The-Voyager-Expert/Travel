The smallest capital in the European Union — built in a single generation by a military order, and still wearing it.

A city built for war

Valletta was not grown. It was decreed. In 1565, the Ottoman Empire sent a fleet of roughly 40,000 soldiers to take Malta — the rocky island at the center of the Mediterranean where a few hundred Knights of St. John had stationed themselves since being expelled from Rhodes in 1522. The siege lasted four months. When it ended, the Ottomans had retreated and the Knights had held, but the old capital of Mdina and the Knight's garrison town of Birgu were shattered. What remained was the determination to build something that would not fall again.

The new city rose on the Sciberras Peninsula — a bare limestone spur between two of the finest natural harbors in the Mediterranean — and it rose fast. The foundation stone was laid in 1566, one year after the Great Siege ended. Within a generation, Valletta was essentially complete: a grid of straight streets on a limestone ridge, lined with auberges for each langue of the Order, a co-cathedral, a hospital, and the grand palaces of an organization that was simultaneously a religious community, a naval power, and one of the wealthiest institutions in Europe. Pope Pius V, who had helped fund the rebuilding, called it a city built by gentlemen for gentlemen.

The planning came from Francesco Laparelli, a military architect sent by the Pope who had previously worked with Michelangelo at St. Peter's. Laparelli applied the principles of Renaissance ideal-city planning to a site that was essentially a fortress: the grid was oriented to maximize crosswinds in the intense Mediterranean summer, but also to channel defenders and fire corridors in case of another attack. Every detail had a tactical purpose. The result, accidentally, was one of the most beautiful urban plans in Europe.

The Knights and their money

The Knights of St. John were not ordinary monks. Founded in the twelfth century to care for sick pilgrims in Jerusalem, they had evolved — through the Crusades, through the loss of the Holy Land, through their bases in Cyprus, Rhodes, and finally Malta — into something closer to a sovereign state with a religious mission. They had a navy, a hospital, an intelligence network, and income from vast estates across France, Spain, Portugal, and Italy. The language groups within the Order — the langues — corresponded to these estates, and each langue had its own auberge in Valletta where its members lived and governed themselves.

The wealth showed. The interior of St. John's Co-Cathedral, begun in 1573 and completed in stages over the following century, is one of the most overwhelming Baroque rooms in existence. The floor is a continuous pavement of marble tombstones, each an individual work of carving celebrating a dead Knight. The side chapels are competitions in opulence, each langue determined to outdo the others with gilded ceilings, painted vaults, and marble altarpieces. The painter Mattia Preti spent twenty years working on the cathedral's barrel vault in the 1660s. Two Caravaggios hang in the oratory, including the artist's largest surviving canvas and his only signed work — he had fled to Malta in 1607 after killing a man in Rome, and produced the painting as an offering in exchange for admission to the Order.

The Knights spent two centuries turning a bare limestone peninsula into a demonstration of what organized wealth and absolute conviction could build.

The Grandmaster's Palace, begun in the 1570s, functioned as the government of a sovereign state for two hundred years. Its armoury still holds one of the largest collections of medieval and early modern weapons in Europe — suits of armour, pikes, muskets, and cannons accumulated across centuries of crusading and naval warfare. The hospital, the Sacred Infirmary, was for its time one of the most advanced medical institutions in Europe, with individual beds for each patient at a time when most European hospitals put multiple patients to a bed, and a silver service for the wealthy sick because the Knights believed good food aided recovery.

Napoleon's three days

The Knights' two centuries in Valletta ended abruptly and somewhat absurdly. In June 1798, Napoleon Bonaparte stopped at Malta on his way to Egypt and demanded provisioning for his fleet. The Order refused. Napoleon landed troops. Within two days, the Knights had surrendered a fortress that had held against the full force of the Ottoman Empire for four months. The explanation is partly that the Knights' military capacity had declined as their role became more administrative than martial; partly that many of their Maltese subjects had no great loyalty to a foreign military order; and partly that Napoleon's army of 30,000 simply overwhelmed any realistic defense.

Napoleon spent three days in Valletta. He ordered the churches stripped of their silver, the university reorganized, the Inquisitor's Palace repurposed, and slavery abolished — the Knights had maintained a slave population, mostly Muslim sailors captured at sea. Then he sailed for Egypt, leaving a garrison behind. Two years later, after a Maltese uprising and a British naval blockade had starved the French garrison into surrender, the British took control of Malta and kept it for the next 164 years.

British Malta

The British did not dramatically alter Valletta's layout — the Knights had built too well and too thoroughly — but they added the characteristic overlays of Victorian colonial administration: the law courts in what had been the Knights' auberge, the Anglican cathedral in a public square, the garrison church, the governor's palace. They also added the Barrakka Lift, the mechanical elevator connecting the upper city to the harbor below, which in its current form is electric but occupies the position of a fortification element. The cannon that fires from the Saluting Battery below the Upper Barrakka Gardens fires on a British schedule — noon and four o'clock — established when the harbor was full of Royal Navy ships that needed to know the time.

Malta's strategic importance in the Second World War exceeded even its importance in the sixteenth century. As the only Allied airbase between Gibraltar and Alexandria, it was the refueling and staging point for the North Africa campaign, and the Axis powers attempted to bomb it into submission from 1940 to 1942. Over 15,000 sorties were flown against Malta; at the peak in April 1942 the island was hit harder than London had been during the Blitz. The survival of Malta — achieved partly through the desperate sacrifice of three American aircraft carriers delivering planes, partly through sheer Maltese endurance — was considered so important that King George VI awarded the George Cross to the island as a whole in 1942. The cross appears on Malta's flag.

The Lascaris War Rooms, carved into the cliff below the Upper Barrakka Gardens, were the nerve center of the Allied Mediterranean command during this period. From these tunnels, officers tracked the convoys that were keeping the island alive and coordinated the air defenses that were shot down by bombs. In 1943, the rooms served as the command post for Operation Husky, the Allied invasion of Sicily — the beginning of the end of the war in Italy.

The smallest capital

Valletta became the capital of an independent Malta in 1964. It is the smallest national capital in the European Union — the city within the historic walls covers roughly 55 hectares, not much larger than a medium-sized university campus. The population of the walled city itself is only a few thousand, though the broader metropolitan area that surrounds it is substantially larger. Many of the historic buildings now serve as government offices, law courts, museums, and cultural institutions rather than residences, which gives the city a slightly formal air during business hours that relaxes in the evenings when restaurants and bars fill the narrow streets.

In 2018, Valletta was European Capital of Culture, which accelerated a renovation process that had been underway for several years. Buildings that had been deteriorating for decades were restored. The City Gate — destroyed in the Second World War and replaced by a grim 1960s structure — was rebuilt to a design by the Italian architect Renzo Piano, who also designed an open-air theater in the ruins of the Royal Opera House that was bombed in 1942 and never rebuilt. The theater occupies the roofless shell of the old building, with new metal seating on the original stone floor. It is an unusual decision — to leave a war ruin as a ruin and make it functional — and it says something about a city that has decided to carry its history rather than conceal it.

The prehistoric context is what most visitors underestimate. Malta's human history extends back to around 5900 BCE, and the island's temple-building civilization — which preceded the Pyramids of Giza and Stonehenge, and built the oldest freestanding stone structures in the world — left a legacy that the National Museum of Archaeology in Valletta holds in some depth. The Sleeping Lady, a small figurine found at the Ħal Saflieni Hypogeum dating to around 3000 BCE, is one of the most haunting objects in Mediterranean prehistory: a reclining female figure of unknown purpose, carved with a sophistication that the builders of the nearby megalithic temples brought to everything they made. The hypogeum itself, a subterranean necropolis carved into the rock south of Valletta, held the remains of approximately 7,000 individuals when it was discovered in 1902. Nobody knows what civilization built it, what language they spoke, or why it appears to have ended as suddenly as it began around 2500 BCE.

What remains

Valletta works as a city in a way that many monuments struggle to. The historic buildings are not museums sealed off from daily life — they are law courts, government offices, cafes, churches still holding Mass, auberges turned into restaurants where you eat surrounded by the architecture of a vanished sovereign military order. The street grid the Knights laid down in the 1560s is the street grid you walk today. The views from the bastions — the Grand Harbour to the south, Marsamxett Harbour to the north, the open sea beyond — are unchanged.

The city is compact enough to cover entirely on foot in a few hours, which means you develop an unusual intimacy with it. You walk past the same facades repeatedly and start to read the layers: the Knights' arched doorways, the British cast-iron balconies, the Maltese wooden balconies painted in deep reds and greens that are the most immediately recognizable visual signature of the city. You climb the same steep streets and emerge repeatedly onto the same bastions, each time looking out over a harbor that has been one of the most contested bodies of water in the world for two and a half thousand years.

The Knights built Valletta to last. It has.
