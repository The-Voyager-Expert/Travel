рҹҸқпёҸ From 1,000 People to a Global Port in a Generation

When Stamford Raffles arrived in 1819, Singapore was a sparsely populated swampy island at the southern tip of the Malay Peninsula вҖ” estimated population roughly 1,000 people, mostly Orang Laut sea nomads and a small Malay settlement. Raffles's single decision вҖ” to declare Singapore a free port with no tariffs on trade вҖ” changed everything. Within months, traders from across the region flooded in. Within five years, the population had grown to over 10,000. The strategic location at the junction of the Indian Ocean and South China Sea meant that once costs were eliminated, every merchant in the region had a reason to route through Singapore. The free port model has never really been abandoned; Singapore remains one of the world's lowest-tariff trading environments and consistently ranks as the world's busiest container port by throughput.

The Supertrees at Gardens by the Bay are not purely decorative. The vertical structures, rising up to 50 meters, are fitted with photovoltaic cells on their canopies that harvest solar energy вҖ” used to power the conservatories and the nightly light-and-sound show. The Supertrees also function as air intake and exhaust vents for the two cooled conservatories beneath them, drawing outside air through their root structures and expelling air from the conservatories through vents at the canopy level. The engineering is embedded in something that most visitors experience as a spectacle, which is a very Singaporean way of doing things: the functional hidden inside the impressive.
