Three low-lying islands in the western Caribbean that turned the absence of taxation into a national identity — and happen to sit on the edge of one of the deepest underwater cliffs in the hemisphere.

The islands Columbus couldn't name properly

When Columbus sailed past these islands in 1503, his crew saw the water churning with sea turtles — so many that the ships could barely navigate through them. He called the islands Las Tortugas. The name didn't stick. Later Spanish sailors, spotting the large iguanas on shore and mistaking them for alligators, renamed them Caimanas — from the Carib word for crocodile. So the Cayman Islands are named after an animal that has never lived there, honoring a confusion between lizards and crocodiles made by people who were passing through and never came back.

The turtles, meanwhile, were real. Green sea turtles nested on these beaches in numbers that are difficult to comprehend today. For two centuries after Columbus, the Caymans existed on European maps primarily as a provisioning stop — a place where passing ships could load up on fresh turtle meat. The crews of naval vessels, merchant ships, and pirate fleets all knew these islands for the same reason: you could pull in, flip a few hundred turtles onto their backs on the sand, and sail away with enough protein to last weeks. The turtles had no defense against this. By the mid-twentieth century, the wild nesting population had been reduced to almost nothing.

The turtle farm and what it means

The Cayman Turtle Centre in West Bay is the only facility of its kind in the world — a conservation farm that breeds green sea turtles, releases them into the wild, and also raises them commercially. This dual purpose makes it controversial in ways that are worth understanding. Animal welfare organizations have objected to the conditions. Marine biologists have debated whether captive-bred turtles released into the ocean meaningfully bolster wild populations. The Cayman government has maintained the operation for decades, arguing that farming turtles reduces the incentive to poach wild ones and that the release program has measurably increased nesting activity on local beaches.

What is not debatable is that the facility exists because these islands nearly destroyed the species that defined them. The Cayman Turtle Centre is, depending on your perspective, either a genuine act of ecological repair or an attempt to industrialize a relationship with wildlife that was already exploitative. You will form your own opinion when you visit. The turtles themselves — some of them enormous, gliding through the breeding pools with a slowness that reads as either serenity or resignation — do not appear to have one.

The wall

The Cayman Islands sit on the edge of the Cayman Trench, which drops to over 25,000 feet — the deepest point in the Caribbean Sea. What this means in practical terms is that the seafloor around Grand Cayman slopes gently outward from the beach for a short distance and then simply stops. The Cayman Wall is the point where the shallow reef shelf ends and the ocean floor falls away into open water. You can be swimming in 60 feet of visibility over a healthy coral reef and then, without much warning, find yourself looking down into a blue so deep it reads as black.

The wall is not a cliff you stand at the edge of. It is a cliff you float over, and the bottom is more than a mile below you.

This is one of the great wall dives in the world, and the reason serious divers have been coming to Grand Cayman since the 1950s. The visibility routinely exceeds 100 feet. The wall itself is covered in sponges and sea fans and black coral, and the drop-off hosts pelagic species — eagle rays, reef sharks, the occasional hammerhead — that you do not see on shallow reef dives. The north wall and the south wall offer different profiles, and dive operators will tell you which is better on a given day based on current and visibility. Both are extraordinary.

Stingray City, the famous sandbar in the North Sound where southern stingrays congregate in chest-deep water, is a completely different experience — shallow, warm, and designed for snorkelers and tourists rather than divers. The stingrays learned decades ago that fishing boats cleaning their catch in the sound meant free food, and they began congregating at the sandbar where the boats anchored. Tour operators figured out that if you brought tourists to the sandbar with buckets of squid, the stingrays would show up on schedule. They do. You stand in three feet of water and stingrays the size of coffee tables glide against your legs looking for a handout. It is surreal, and it is also entirely artificial — a wild animal behavior that has been shaped by human feeding into something that functions like a theme park attraction but takes place in the open ocean.

The money

The Cayman Islands have no income tax, no corporate tax, no capital gains tax, no payroll tax, and no withholding tax. This is not an oversight. It is the economic model. The islands are a British Overseas Territory with a population of around 70,000 people and more than 100,000 registered companies. There are more hedge funds domiciled here than in any other jurisdiction on earth. The law firms along George Town's waterfront manage structures of such complexity that explaining them to a non-specialist would take longer than your flight home.

How this happened is straightforward. In the 1960s, the Cayman Islands government — looking at a small territory with limited agricultural potential, no mineral resources, and a declining turtle fishery — decided to build an economy around financial services. They passed laws that made it exceptionally easy to incorporate, exceptionally private to bank, and exceptionally light on regulation. The global financial industry responded exactly the way you would expect.

The result is an island where the physical landscape and the economic landscape exist in a kind of surreal parallel. You drive past Seven Mile Beach, one of the most beautiful stretches of white sand in the Caribbean, and then turn inland to George Town, where glass office buildings house the registered addresses of entities that collectively hold trillions of dollars in assets. The contrast is not subtle. A bartender serving you a drink at sunset may work a second job at an accounting firm. The person next to you at the beach may be a fund administrator. The money is not visible in the way it is in Monaco or Dubai — there are no gold-plated buildings — but it is the reason the infrastructure works, the roads are paved, and the standard of living is among the highest in the Caribbean.

Seven Mile Beach and the problem of measurement

Seven Mile Beach is 5.5 miles long. Nobody is sure where the name came from. One theory holds that an early surveyor measured generously. Another suggests that the beach has physically shortened over the decades as development and storms have altered the coastline. A third possibility is that someone simply liked the sound of "Seven Mile Beach" better than "Five and a Half Mile Beach," which is fair.

Regardless of its length, it is one of the finest beaches in the Caribbean by any standard. The sand is white and fine-grained, the water is the particular shade of turquoise that travel photographers spend careers trying to capture accurately, and the beach faces west, which means the sunsets are directly in front of you. The resorts and condominiums that line it have been built to varying degrees of taste, but the beach itself is public along its entire length — a fact that the Cayman Islands government has defended against developer pressure for decades.

Hell and the postcard

In the district of West Bay, there is a small field of jagged black limestone formations called Hell. The rock has been eroded by rainfall and biological activity into shapes that look, if you are feeling generous, like a scorched and tortured landscape. A viewing platform lets you look out over it. Adjacent to the viewing platform is a gift shop and post office where you can send a postcard postmarked from Hell, Grand Cayman. "Wish you were here" is the standard message. The entire experience takes about ten minutes.

Hell is a minor geological curiosity that has been turned into one of the most visited attractions on Grand Cayman through sheer force of naming. If this rock formation were called "The Black Limestone Outcrop" it would appear in no guidebook. Instead, because someone at some point looked at it and said "this looks like Hell," it has a parking lot, a souvenir shop, and a dedicated postal cancellation stamp. This tells you something true about tourism: a good name is worth more than a good sight.

The British question

The Cayman Islands are a British Overseas Territory, which means the British Crown appoints a Governor, the legal system is based on English common law, and the currency — the Cayman Islands dollar — is pegged to the US dollar at a fixed rate. The Union Jack appears on the flag. The driving is on the left. The formalities of British governance are observed.

In practice, the relationship is more complicated than the constitutional structure suggests. The Cayman Islands have full internal self-governance. The elected Legislative Assembly controls domestic policy, taxation, and immigration. London retains authority over defense, foreign affairs, and — in theory — the power to impose legislation directly, a power it has used sparingly and controversially, most notably when it mandated public beneficial ownership registers for the overseas territories over Cayman's objections.

The Caymans chose to stay British at a moment when most of the Caribbean was choosing independence — and the reason was not sentiment.

When Jamaica gained independence in 1962, the Cayman Islands — which had been administered as a dependency of Jamaica — were offered the choice to go with Jamaica or remain under British sovereignty. They chose to stay. The decision was pragmatic rather than romantic. British sovereignty provided political stability, a credible legal system, and the kind of jurisdictional reputation that would later prove essential for building a financial services industry. Independence would have meant building all of that from scratch on three small islands with a population smaller than a mid-sized town.

The calculation has proven correct, at least economically. The Cayman Islands have the highest standard of living in the Caribbean and one of the lowest crime rates. Whether this constitutes a model for small-island governance or an artifact of a particular set of historical circumstances that cannot be replicated is a question that economists and political scientists continue to argue about. The residents, for the most part, seem content with the arrangement.
