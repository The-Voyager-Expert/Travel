A city that has been shaken apart and put back together more than once — and what that cycle made of the people who stayed.

The valley that explains everything

Santiago sits in a bowl. The Andes rise to the east — not rolling hills but actual jagged peaks, snow-capped most of the year, close enough that on a clear morning they look painted onto the sky behind the buildings. The Chilean Coast Range runs to the west. The Mapocho River cuts through the middle. This geography is not incidental backdrop; it is the operating condition of the city. It traps the smog (autumn and winter mornings can be opaque with it), concentrates the culture, and gives the place a dramatic frame that no other South American capital can match.

Most of the year the mountains are invisible. The valley fills with a brown haze that the locals call the smog without embarrassment or euphemism. Then a wind comes through, or it rains, and overnight Santiago becomes one of the most visually startling cities on earth. Santiaguinos talk about this with the proprietary pride of people who know they live somewhere that periodically reveals itself to be extraordinary. They are right to.

Look east from almost any high point in the city — from the top of Cerro San Cristóbal, from a rooftop bar in Lastarria, from the observation deck of the Gran Torre — and the Andes are either there or they're not. When they're there, they stop you. First-time visitors often forget what they were about to say.

Founded twice, on a floodplain

Pedro de Valdivia founded Santiago in 1541, on a small hill above a bend in the Mapocho, and the Mapuche burned it to the ground seven months later. He rebuilt it. This pattern — destruction, reconstruction, continuation — turns out to be the essential story of the city.

Valdivia chose the site strategically: the hill (the Huelén, now called Santa Lucía) gave a defensible position, and the river provided water and a natural barrier. What he couldn't know, or chose to ignore, was that the Mapocho was a glacier-fed river with a tendency to flood catastrophically. Santiago has been inundated so many times that the colonial city's original street level is now more than a meter below the current one. The Plaza de Armas, where Valdivia planted his founding cross, has been rebuilt so many times that virtually nothing above ground predates the nineteenth century.

The Spanish built a grid. The Mapuche resisted for three hundred years. Neither fully won, and the tension between those two facts runs through everything the city became.

The Mapuche were never conquered in the way the Inca were. The Spanish signed treaties with them, broke them, fought them, signed more treaties. The southern frontier — called the Araucanía — was not formally "pacified" until the 1880s, which means that Santiago was a colonial capital with an active frontier war running in its back country for three centuries. This is different from most colonial capitals, where indigenous resistance was crushed relatively quickly. It left a different imprint on the psychology of the place.

The hill that Pedro de Valdivia climbed

Cerro Santa Lucía — the small rocky hill in the middle of the city center — is where Valdivia stood in 1541 and decided this was the place. He called it Huelén, using the Mapuche name. The Spanish renamed it after their patron saint of the day. Now it's a tiered park of fountains and terrace gardens, with a fort at the top that mostly dates from the nineteenth century, and couples sitting on the steps at dusk.

You can walk up in fifteen minutes. At the top, there's a Chilean flag, a bronze relief of Valdivia's face, and a view east toward the Andes that, on a clear day, makes you understand immediately why he stopped walking. The city fans out below you in every direction — the dense grid of the center, the highways threading between neighborhoods, the smudge of residential districts extending to the foothills. Stand there long enough and Santiago starts to make sense as a shape.

Most visitors walk past Santa Lucía on the way somewhere else. Go up. It takes fifteen minutes and reorients the whole trip.

What September 11 means here

On September 11, 1973, the Chilean Air Force bombed the presidential palace. The coup that ended Salvador Allende's government and installed Augusto Pinochet's military dictatorship is not history the way, say, the War of the Pacific is history — distant, settled, taught in textbooks without emotional charge. It happened fifty years ago, but Chile is still, in many ways, having the argument about it.

La Moneda palace — the government building that was bombed that morning — is in the center of Santiago, between the Plaza de Armas and the Alameda. It's been restored, and the president's offices are still there. You can walk through the public courtyard. It looks correct, official, composed. There is a small open-air museum below the plaza in front of it, and a memorial. But the building's very normality is part of what makes it strange to stand in front of if you know the history — this is where Allende died, and this is what was rebuilt after.

The debate about what happened in 1973 is not settled. You will meet Chileans who lived through it who disagree about it in the same room.

The Museum of Memory and Human Rights, opened in 2010, is about a ten-minute walk from the city center. It documents the seventeen years of the dictatorship — the disappeared, the torture sites, the exile, the machinery of repression. It is not a comfortable museum. It shouldn't be. Go anyway. Santiago is a city in which the recent past is physically present — in the museum, in the stadium where prisoners were held, in the Villa Grimaldi peace park where a torture center once stood — and understanding that presence changes how you read everything else.

Barrio Italia and the city that wasn't planned

Santiago has one of those curious urban qualities: its most interesting neighborhoods are mostly the ones that weren't intentionally designed. Barrio Italia — the district of early-twentieth-century houses, antique dealers, coffee shops, and restaurants that has become the city's creative center — was an immigrant neighborhood, home to Italian workers who arrived in the late 1800s and built modest houses along irregular streets.

Walking through it now, the houses are recognizable: two-story, narrow lots, small courtyards behind iron gates, facades in the dusky pinks and yellows of faded terracotta. Many have been converted into restaurants or design shops, but not in a way that has stripped the neighborhood of its residential texture. People live here. There are kids on the sidewalks in the afternoon and old men reading newspapers in doorways. The gentrification that has hollowed out similar neighborhoods in other cities has been, so far, managed — probably because Santiago has kept developing outward and upward rather than concentrating all its energy into a single fashionable zone.

Lastarria, closer to the center, is the other neighborhood you'll keep returning to. Bohemian is a word that gets overused, but Lastarria genuinely is — bookshops, jazz bars, street market stalls on weekends, the city's serious contemporary art museum (the MAC) in the park at the edge. The restaurants there are among the best in the city, and the streets are narrow enough that they feel human-scaled in a way that Santiago's wider arteries do not.

The Andes are in the food

Chilean food is underrated in the way that all of South America's non-Argentine food tends to be underrated: the continent exports beef and ceviche, and everything else gets lumped into "also South American." This is a mistake.

The country runs for nearly 4,300 kilometers from the Atacama in the north to Patagonia in the south, and its food reflects that range. The seafood is extraordinary — the cold Humboldt Current that runs up the Pacific coast produces some of the best shellfish in the world, and a plate of machas (razor clams) baked with Parmesan, or a bowl of chupe de centolla (king crab stew), will recalibrate your expectations for what cold-water seafood can be. Empanadas de pino — baked, not fried, with beef, olives, and hard-boiled egg — are the everyday food of Santiago, available at every corner bakery and significantly better than they have any right to be.

The wine is easier to find internationally, but drinking it here, where the valleys that produced it are visible in the distance, is a different experience. The Maipo Valley starts essentially at Santiago's southern edge. A glass of Carmenère — the grape that disappeared from France after phylloxera and was found surviving, misidentified, in Chilean vineyards in 1994 — is the correct drink for a city that has had to rediscover itself more than once.

The one thing to carry with you

Before you board, here's what to keep in mind as you walk Santiago: this is a city that has been destroyed by the Mapuche, flooded by the Mapocho, shaken apart by earthquakes (the 1960 Valdivia earthquake, the largest ever recorded, was felt here), and fractured politically in ways that haven't fully healed. Every time, Santiago rebuilt — not because it had no other choice, but because the people who stayed had a specific attachment to this particular valley between these particular mountains.

That attachment is visible if you look for it. It's in the care taken with the neighborhood markets, in the seriousness of the café culture, in the way santiaguinos talk about the Andes on a clear day — not as scenery but as a presence that belongs to them. A city that has been through what Santiago has been through could have become provisional, or bitter, or simply efficient in the way that cities become when they've stopped believing in themselves. Santiago didn't. Once you see that, you see it in the parks, in the architecture, in the people sitting in Barrio Italia on a Sunday afternoon as if there's nowhere they'd rather be.

There probably isn't.
