Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 7 min · 🚕 5 min → Gyeongbokgung Palace
1. Gyeongbokgung Palace
↳ The largest of Seoul's five Joseon-era palaces, built in 1395 at the foot of Bugaksan. Lily-pond pavilions, gate towers, and ceremonial halls sprawl across a complex that once held 7700 rooms. The Changing of the Guard ceremony runs twice daily at the main Gwanghwamun Gate.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 6:00pm
🚫 Closed Tuesday
⏰ ~3 hrs
📍 161 Sajik-ro · Jongno-gu
🚶 15 min · 🚕 6 min → Bukchon Hanok Village
2. Bukchon Hanok Village
↳ Six hundred traditional Korean hanok houses from the Joseon Dynasty, tucked between Gyeongbokgung and Changdeokgung. The alleyways function as a living residential quarter while opening views of curved tiled roofs against the Seoul skyline — the most concentrated traditional streetscape remaining in the city.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 5:00pm
⏰ ~1.5 hrs
🆓 Free
⚠️ Private residences are occupied — noise violations carry fines.
📍 37 Gyedong-gil · Jongno-gu
🚶 20 min · 🚕 8 min → Jongmyo Shrine
3. Jongmyo Shrine
↳ Korea's oldest and best-preserved Confucian royal shrine, housing the spirit tablets of 19 Joseon Dynasty kings and 30 queens. The main hall Jeongjeon is the longest wooden structure in East Asia. On weekdays entry is by English guided tour only, running four times daily. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1995.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 6:00pm
🚫 Closed Tuesday
⏰ ~1.5 hrs
⚠️ Weekday visits by guided tour only. English tours at 10:00am · 12:00pm · 2:00pm · 4:00pm. Weekend entry is free-walk.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 20 min · 🚕 7 min → Changdeokgung Palace
1. Changdeokgung Palace & Secret Garden
↳ The UNESCO-listed Palace of Prospering Virtue, completed in 1412, is crowned by Huwon — the Secret Garden — an extraordinary 78-acre pleasure garden of ancient pavilions over lotus ponds and thousand-year-old trees. Entry to the garden is by guided tour only, with English tours four times daily.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:00am - 6:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~3 hrs
⚠️ Secret Garden English tours at 10:30am · 11:30am · 2:30pm · 3:30pm. Separate admission required for the garden in addition to the palace.
📍 99 Yulgok-ro · Jongno-gu
🚕 20 min → Seodaemun Prison History Hall
2. Seodaemun Prison History Hall
↳ Japan's colonial-era lockup, built in 1908 and used by successive authoritarian regimes until 1987. Independence fighters were imprisoned, tortured, and executed here. The preserved cell blocks, underground execution chamber, and permanent exhibition document Korea's most harrowing chapter with unflinching honesty.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:30am - 6:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~1.5 hrs
📍 251 Tongil-ro · Seodaemun-gu

🚕 20 min → N Seoul Tower
3. N Seoul Tower
↳ Seoul's defining communications tower stands at 480m elevation on Namsan Mountain, with a public observatory delivering a 360-degree panorama across one of Asia's largest cities. Plan for dusk — when the city transitions from sunlit sprawl to a neon sea, the view from Namsan becomes exceptional.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Sunday - Thursday 10:30am - 10:00pm · Friday - Saturday 10:00am - 11:00pm
⏰ ~2 hrs
⚠️ Cable car runs 10:00am - 11:00pm — separate fare. Walk-up trail also available via Namsan Park paths.

🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 20 min → War Memorial of Korea
1. War Memorial of Korea
↳ The world's largest war memorial complex, anchored by tanks, artillery, and warplanes in its forecourts. Inside, immersive galleries trace Korean military history through the Korean War, including the famous Brother Meets Brother bronze sculpture at the entrance — one of the most powerful public monuments in Asia.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 9:30am - 6:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~2.5 hrs

🚕 35 min → SEOUL SKY Observatory
2. SEOUL SKY Observatory
↳ The 5th tallest building on earth — 555m, 123 floors — houses the highest glass-floor observatory in the world at 478m, holding a Guinness World Record. The elevator ride at 600 metres per minute is a spectacle before you reach the top. The glass floor walkway and Sky Terrace deliver the most dramatic perch in Seoul.

Day 4
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 8 min · 🚕 5 min → Cheonggyecheon Stream
1. Cheonggyecheon Stream
↳ A 5.8km historic stream restored in 2005 by tearing out the elevated expressway that had buried it since the 1960s. The sunken greenway of stepping stones, waterfalls, and lit bridges threads through downtown Seoul's concrete canyons. Start at Cheonggye Plaza and stroll east to Dongdaemun.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1.5 hrs
📍 Cheonggye Plaza · Jung-gu
🚶 10 min · 🚕 5 min → Dongdaemun Design Plaza
2. Dongdaemun Design Plaza
↳ Zaha Hadid's visionary 2014 landmark — a sweeping aluminium-clad structure with zero straight lines or right angles flowing across an entire city block. The most striking piece of contemporary architecture in Seoul and the anchor of the city's design scene. The exterior grounds and public spaces are free.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 10:00am - 7:00pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~1.5 hrs
📍 281 Eulji-ro · Jung-gu
🚕 25 min → Han River Cruise · Yeouinaru
3. Han River Cruise
↳ E-Land's Hangang River cruise departs Yeouido, tracing 40 minutes through Seoul's skyline and twelve illuminated bridges. Best timed at dusk when N Seoul Tower glows against Namsan's ridge and the bridges light their spans across the wide river — a perspective impossible to achieve from land.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 11:30am - 9:00pm
⏰ ~1.5 hrs
⚠️ Take Line 5 subway to Yeouinaru Station · Exit 3, then walk to Han River Park dock. Tickets at the pier.

🌆 Claude Inspiration

Seoul does something almost no city achieves: it makes five centuries of history feel urgent. The Joseon palaces are not relics — they are the organizing logic of the city. Stand at Gwanghwamun Gate and look north at Gyeongbokgung against Bugaksan's ridge, then turn south toward the glass towers of Gangnam. That is the whole arc of Korean modernity in a single turn of the head.

What struck me most is the resilience embedded in the landscape itself. Cheonggyecheon was a highway overpass thirty years ago. Seodaemun was still a functioning prison within living memory. Bukchon's alleyways were nearly lost to redevelopment. Seoul saved each of these things — not from nostalgia, but from an insistence that where you come from shapes where you are going. Every city talks about that. Seoul actually did it.
