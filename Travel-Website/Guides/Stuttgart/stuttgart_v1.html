Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 7 min · 🚕 3 min → Schlossplatz
1. Schlossplatz
↳ Stuttgart's grand central square, framed by the horseshoe-shaped Baroque Neues Schloss that Duke Carl Eugen built to rival Versailles. Manicured lawns, fountains and the 30-metre Jubilee Column make it the city's open-air living room.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Schlossplatz · Mitte
🚶 3 min · 🚕 1 min → Landesmuseum Württemberg
2. Landesmuseum Württemberg
↳ The moated Renaissance Old Castle, with a three-tiered arcaded courtyard that is one of Germany's finest. Inside, the Landesmuseum Württemberg traces the region from the Celts and Romans to the crown jewels of the Württemberg dukes.
📖 Wikipedia

3. Stiftskirche
↳ Stuttgart's main Protestant church, a Gothic landmark with two mismatched towers rising over Schillerplatz. Inside stands a celebrated Renaissance row of carved stone statues of the Counts of Württemberg, rebuilt after wartime damage.
📖 Wikipedia

4. Staatsgalerie Stuttgart
↳ One of Germany's most loved art museums, paired with James Stirling's playful postmodern Neue Staatsgalerie of 1984. The collection runs from medieval altarpieces to Picasso, Monet and a famous trove of Oskar Schlemmer and modern masters.

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 11 min → Mercedes-Benz Museum
1. Mercedes-Benz Museum
↳ A spiralling double-helix building by UNStudio that walks you down through 135 years of the automobile — from Carl Benz's 1886 first car to Silver Arrow racers and the Popemobile. More than 160 vehicles across 16,500 square metres.
📖 Wikipedia

2. Porsche Museum
↳ A gleaming white monolith by Delugan Meissl that floats above three columns at the Porsche works in Zuffenhausen. Dozens of cars rotate through the hall — the 356, the 917 Le Mans winners, and the 911 in every generation.
📖 Wikipedia

🚕 26 min → Fernsehturm Stuttgart
3. Fernsehturm Stuttgart
↳ The world's first reinforced-concrete television tower, opened in 1956 and the model for TV towers everywhere since. Two observation decks and a café at 150 metres look out over the city bowl, the vineyards and the Swabian hills.
📖 Wikipedia

🍮 Local Tastes
Maultaschen
↳ Swabia's signature filled pasta pockets — pork, spinach and herbs in a thin dough — eaten in broth, pan-fried, or geschmelzt with onions. Said to have been invented to hide meat during Lent.
📍 Weinstube Kachelofen · Alte Kanzlei
Kässpätzle
↳ Soft hand-scraped egg noodles layered with mountain cheese and crisp fried onions — the Swabian comfort dish, found on every traditional menu in the city.
📍 Alte Kanzlei · Weinstube Kachelofen
Trollinger
↳ Württemberg's easy-drinking light red, grown on the steep terraced vineyards that ring Stuttgart itself. Locally it is the everyday Viertele poured by the quarter-litre in the city's wine taverns.
📍 Weinstube Kachelofen · Carls Brauhaus

⛲️ Day Trips by Train
Ludwigsburg · 15 min
↳ The Versailles of Swabia — one of Europe's largest surviving Baroque palaces, 452 rooms and the vast Blühendes Barock gardens. A 15-minute S-Bahn ride to the heart of town.
🎫 book at: Deutsche Bahn or Omio
Esslingen am Neckar · 15 min
↳ A medieval town of 200-plus half-timbered houses, cobbled lanes and Germany's oldest sparkling-wine cellar, nicknamed Little Venice for its canals. Fifteen minutes by S-Bahn line S1.
🎫 book at: Deutsche Bahn or Omio
Tübingen · 45 min
↳ A postcard university town on the Neckar — punts gliding past pastel houses, a hilltop Renaissance castle and one of Germany's prettiest old towns. A 45-minute regional train ride.
🎫 book at: Deutsche Bahn or Omio
Heidelberg · 45 min
↳ Germany's most-visited town after Munich — a ruined red-sandstone castle looming above the old town, a Baroque Marktplatz, and the country's oldest university founded in 1386. Direct ICE trains run in under 45 minutes.
🎫 book at: Deutsche Bahn or Omio
Baden-Baden · 1h
↳ Europe's grand thermal spa town — the Roman Caracalla Baths, the Belle Époque Kurhaus casino, and 18th-century colonnades set in the Black Forest foothills. Direct regional trains run hourly on the Mannheim–Basel mainline.
🎫 book at: Deutsche Bahn or Omio

❗️ Heads Up
❗️ City Museums — Closed Mondays
↳ The Mercedes-Benz and Porsche museums, the Staatsgalerie and the Landesmuseum all close on Mondays — a Monday museum day leaves you locked out.
Workaround: Build the museum day for any day Tuesday through Sunday.
🏎️ Notes from the Motor City

Stuttgart sits inside a steep green bowl locals call the Kessel — the kettle — and it is the only big German city ringed by working vineyards that climb right to the edge of downtown. The slopes you spot from the Fernsehturm are still planted with Trollinger, and the Viertele of red poured in the old wine taverns comes from grapes grown within sight of the office towers.

It is also, quite literally, where the car was invented — Gottlieb Daimler and Carl Benz both built their first vehicles here. That is why the two museums on Day 2 read less like corporate showrooms and more like the city's origin story; the double-helix Mercedes building even spirals like the twin strands of a history it is still writing.
