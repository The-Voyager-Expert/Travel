1. Jardim Botânico de Curitiba
↳ Ornamental greenhouse modeled on London's Crystal Palace, set in French-style gardens. The Art Nouveau glass structure shelters native Atlantic Forest plants; the grounds feature geometric flowerbeds and a botanical research center.

2. Ópera de Arame
↳ Concert hall enclosed in a transparent wire-mesh dome built inside a flooded limestone quarry. The structure appears to float above the water; the grounds and lake are free to explore independently. Performances are ticketed and held year-round.

3. Parque Tanguá
↳ Former basalt quarry transformed into a park with a tunnel cut through the rock face linking upper and lower levels. A waterfall, lake, pedestrian bridges, and elevated walkways reveal the geological strata of the old extraction site — industrial heritage at city-park scale, with no admission charge.

1. Museu Oscar Niemeyer
↳ Modernist museum completed in 2002, distinguished by the cantilevered Eye annex — an elliptical gallery elevated on a curved concrete column. Collections cover Niemeyer's architectural drawings, mid-century Brazilian design, and rotating contemporary art. Free on Wednesdays.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 1h 15min → Parque Estadual de Vila Velha
1. Parque Estadual de Vila Velha
↳ Sandstone pillars up to 23 metres tall sculpted by 300 million years of wind and rain into columns and arches across the flat Paraná plateau. Three circuits: the formation trail, the Furnas sinkholes descended by elevator, and the Lagoa Dourada lake loop. Ticket sales end at 3:00pm.
📖 Wikipedia

🏙️ The City That Planned Itself

Curitiba's reputation as a model of urban sustainability wasn't an accident — it grew from decades of deliberate investment in parks, rapid transit, and pedestrian corridors starting in the 1960s under mayor Jaime Lerner. The three parks in Day 1 are not decorative additions: they absorb former industrial quarries and underused land, turning infrastructure problems into public assets. Parque Tanguá was a basalt extraction site. The Ópera de Arame was a flooded limestone pit. Neither was scenic before the city decided to make it so.

The Oscar Niemeyer Museum follows the same logic into architecture — the Eye annex sits on a curved column because the original building footprint was already fixed and the only expansion direction was up and out. Form follows constraint, then transcends it. What stays after three days isn't any single stop but the cumulative effect: a city that decided what kind of place it wanted to be, and built toward it with unusual consistency across fifty years.
