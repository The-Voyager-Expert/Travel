The Brazilian city that became a case study in urban planning — not because it had money, but because it had an architect as mayor and no patience for waiting.

The city that planned itself

Curitiba's reputation precedes it in a way that is unusual for a Brazilian state capital. Urban planners from Bogotá to Seoul have studied it. The BRT system — the tube-station bus rapid transit that moves hundreds of thousands of people a day on dedicated lanes — was invented here and exported worldwide. The parks that ring the city were built not as amenities but as flood control, because Jaime Lerner, the architect who became mayor in 1971, understood that a park that absorbed floodwater was cheaper than a drainage canal and more useful the other 364 days of the year.

You should know this going in because it explains what you're looking at. Curitiba does not look like other Brazilian cities. It is cleaner, more orderly, more legible. The streets have a logic. The buses run. The parks are maintained. None of this happened by accident, and none of it happened because Curitiba is wealthier than its neighbors — it isn't, particularly. It happened because a small group of urbanists got control of the city government in the early 1970s and held it long enough to build a system.

Lerner's trick

Jaime Lerner was appointed mayor by the military government in 1971 — an irony that he and his admirers have never entirely resolved. He was thirty-three, trained as an architect at the Universidade Federal do Paraná, and he had a plan for Curitiba that the city's business establishment opposed because it involved closing the main commercial street to cars. So Lerner did it over a weekend. On a Friday night, city workers tore up the pavement on Rua XV de Novembro, laid cobblestones, planted trees, and installed benches. By Monday morning it was a pedestrian mall and there was nothing left to argue about. The merchants who had threatened lawsuits discovered that foot traffic increased their sales. The street is still pedestrian today.

This became Lerner's method: act fast, make it physical, let the result speak for itself. The BRT system followed the same logic. Curitiba couldn't afford a subway, so Lerner's team designed a surface system that behaved like one — dedicated lanes, pre-paid boarding at tube stations, articulated buses that loaded at platform height. The system carried passengers at subway capacity for a fraction of the cost, and the tube stations — those distinctive glass-and-steel cylinders you see along the avenues — became the city's most recognizable architectural feature.

Curitiba didn't invent good urban planning. It proved that good urban planning didn't require being rich — just being fast and stubborn enough to build it before anyone could say no.

Parks as infrastructure

The Jardim Botânico is the city's postcard image — a French-style greenhouse in an art nouveau iron-and-glass structure, set in formal gardens that give way to native Araucaria forest. It is genuinely beautiful, and it is also an engineered response to a flooding problem. The botanical garden sits in a low area that collected water during heavy rains. Building a park there solved the drainage and created a public space. The greenhouse was the architectural gesture that made the drainage project into a destination.

Parque Tanguá, in the northern part of the city, follows the same pattern. It occupies a former quarry — an industrial scar — that was converted into a terraced park with a lake, a waterfall, and a tunnel cut through the old quarry wall. The Ópera de Arame, the wire-frame opera house built from steel tubes and transparent polycarbonate, sits over a flooded quarry in the Pedreira Paulo Leminski park. Every major park in Curitiba was built on damaged or flood-prone land, and every one of them transformed a liability into an asset. This is Lerner's legacy distilled: the idea that a city's problems, if you look at them correctly, are also its opportunities.

The Araucaria pines that grow in these parks and throughout the city are the other thing you should notice. Araucaria angustifolia, the Paraná pine, is a prehistoric-looking conifer with a flat crown that grows naturally in the cool highlands of southern Brazil. Curitiba sits on a plateau at about 930 meters, high enough for the Araucaria to thrive, and the trees give the city a character that is unmistakably different from tropical Brazil. The light is different here — cooler, more European in quality — and the pines silhouetted against a gray winter sky look like nothing else on the continent.

The immigrant quarters

Curitiba's population is the most European in Brazil outside the deep south. Waves of immigrants — Polish, Ukrainian, German, Italian, Japanese — arrived in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries and settled in distinct neighborhoods that retain their character. Santa Felicidade, the Italian quarter in the western suburbs, is a strip of enormous restaurants — some seating over a thousand — that serve family-style Italian-Brazilian meals in a volume and at a pace that has no equivalent anywhere in Italy but makes perfect sense as a cultural expression of immigrant abundance.

The Bosque João Paulo II, a memorial park dedicated to Pope John Paul II's 1980 visit, preserves seven original Polish log houses from the immigrant settlements and sets them among Araucaria forest. The houses are small, dark, and beautifully constructed — the work of people who arrived in Paraná's highlands with woodworking skills and not much else. Walking through them, you get a sense of what southern Brazil looked like to a Polish farmer in 1880: dense forest, red earth, cool air, and a complete absence of anything familiar.

The Japanese immigration is visible in the Praça do Japão, a public square with a Japanese garden, torii gate, and cultural center, and in the food — Curitiba has excellent Japanese restaurants, which most visitors from outside Brazil do not expect. Brazil has the largest Japanese diaspora in the world, and Curitiba's share of it is large enough to sustain a food culture that goes well beyond sushi into regional Japanese cooking that has adapted to Brazilian ingredients over four generations.

The Museu Oscar Niemeyer

The MON — the Museu Oscar Niemeyer, which everyone calls the Eye Museum — is Curitiba's one building that belongs in every architecture guide. Niemeyer designed the original structure in 2002 at the age of 95, and the annex — the "eye," a massive sculptural form suspended on a single pillar over a reflecting pool — was added in the same year. It is quintessential late Niemeyer: a single bold shape, white concrete, curves defying gravity, a building that photographs better than almost any museum in the Americas.

Inside, the collection rotates between Brazilian and international contemporary art, and the eye itself functions as a gallery with a dramatic oculus window. The building is set in a park of Araucaria trees, which is the kind of juxtaposition that Curitiba does better than most cities — the futuristic and the primeval, the concrete curve and the prehistoric pine, sharing the same lawn.

Vila Velha and what lies beyond

An hour west of Curitiba, the sandstone formations at Vila Velha rise from the Campos Gerais plateau like a ruined city. The arenitos — pillars, arches, and balanced rocks sculpted by wind and rain over millions of years — look artificial from a distance, which is how they got their name: Vila Velha, Old Town. Up close, they are clearly geological, but the scale and the improbability of some of the shapes — a chalice balanced on a narrow stem, a camel, a sphinx — explain why indigenous peoples and early settlers both attributed them to human or supernatural origin.

Nearby, the Furnas — deep cylindrical sinkholes filled with green water — drop into the sandstone plateau with an abruptness that is genuinely startling. You walk across flat grassland and suddenly the ground opens into a vertical-walled lake sixty meters below. Guartelá Canyon, further west, is one of the longest sandstone canyons in Brazil, with the Iapó River running through a gorge of layered red and white stone. These landscapes are not well known outside Paraná, which is part of their appeal — you will not find crowds, and the geology is world-class.

Curitiba is the rare city where the urban planning is a tourist attraction — not because it's flashy, but because it works and you can see it working from the window of a bus.

The thing to carry

Curitiba's critics — and there are critics, especially among Brazilians who find the city's self-regard about its planning achievements tiresome — will tell you that the model has limits. The BRT system is overcrowded at peak hours. The favelas that ring the metropolitan area are not covered by the transit network that serves the center. The "ecological capital" branding oversells a city that still has air quality problems and sprawl on its periphery. All of this is true.

But stand at a tube station at rush hour and watch the system work — the bus arrives, the doors align with the platform, a hundred people board in seconds, the bus leaves — and you understand why delegations from cities around the world came here to learn. Curitiba proved that you could build a functioning transit system without billions in subway tunnels, that you could turn flood zones into parks, that you could close a street to cars over a weekend and make it stick. It proved that urban design was not a luxury but a tool, and that the tool could be wielded by a mid-sized city in a developing country with more ambition than money. That idea — that good design is not a function of wealth but of will — is the most exportable thing Curitiba ever produced, and it is still being exported.
