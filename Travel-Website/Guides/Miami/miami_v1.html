Day 1
🏨 From Hotel: 🚶 23 min · 🚕 8 min → Holocaust Memorial
1. Holocaust Memorial
↳ Kenneth Treister's Sculpture of Love and Anguish — a 42-foot bronze arm rising from a reflecting pool, ringed by nearly 100 figures climbing toward it. Opened in 1990, it is one of the most powerful public memorials in the United States and a quietly moving way to begin a South Beach day.

🚶 19 min · 🚕 5 min → Española Way
2. Española Way
↳ A two-block Mediterranean-revival promenade staged in 1925 as a historic Spanish village — pastel stucco façades, clay-tile roofs, and wrought-iron balconies. Pedestrianized since 2017, it is now an open-air strip of sidewalk cafés, tapas terraces, and live music, perfect for a slow wander and a bite.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~45 min
📍 Española Way · South Beach
🚶 13 min · 🚕 5 min → Art Deco District
3. Art Deco District
↳ The largest concentration of 1920s–30s Art Deco architecture in the world — some 800 protected buildings along Ocean Drive, Collins Avenue, and Washington Avenue. Tropical Deco mixes pastel façades, nautical curves, neon, and shade-casting eyebrows. Listed on the National Register since 1979.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 Ocean Drive · South Beach
🚶 23 min · 🚕 10 min → South Pointe Park
4. South Pointe Park
↳ The 17-acre green tip of South Beach, where Government Cut meets the Atlantic. A waterfront promenade, a wooden boardwalk over the last natural dune, and an unobstructed view of cruise ships threading the channel make this the best free vantage point on the island — ideal at golden hour.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 6:00am - 10:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr
📍 1 Washington Ave · South Beach

Day 2
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 25 min → Wynwood Walls
1. Wynwood Walls
↳ An open-air museum of large-scale street art founded in 2009 by developer Tony Goldman to turn Wynwood's blank warehouse walls into a curated outdoor gallery. Today it holds 40-plus murals and a dozen sculptures by international artists, the anchor of Miami's most colorful neighborhood.
📖 Wikipedia

🚕 17 min → Little Havana
2. Little Havana
↳ The cultural heart of Cuban Miami, centered on Calle Ocho — SW 8th Street. Cigar rollers, ventanita coffee windows, fruit stands, and salsa drift between the storefronts; at Domino Park elders slap tiles under the trees. A living streetscape best explored on foot, café con leche in hand.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Open 24/7
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 1444 SW 8th St · Little Havana
🚕 7 min → Vizcaya Museum and Gardens
3. Vizcaya Museum and Gardens
↳ An Italian Renaissance-style villa built in 1916 as the winter estate of industrialist James Deering, set on Biscayne Bay. Ten acres of formal gardens, fountains, and a stone barge breakwater surround a house furnished with European antiques. A National Historic Landmark and the most photographed garden in the city.

Day 3
🏨 From Hotel: 🚕 25 min → Bill Baggs Cape Florida State Park
1. Bill Baggs Cape Florida State Park
↳ A barrier island at the southern tip of Key Biscayne, anchored by the Cape Florida Lighthouse — built in 1825, torched by Seminole warriors in 1836, and now the oldest standing structure in Miami-Dade County. A path runs the length of the island to the lighthouse base and sweeping views over Biscayne Bay.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Daily 8:00am - 6:00pm
⏰ ~1 hr 30 min
📍 1200 S Crandon Blvd · Key Biscayne
🚕 25 min → Venetian Pool
2. Venetian Pool
↳ A 1923 public pool carved from a coral rock quarry in Coral Gables — cascading waterfalls, palm-shaded porticos, a Venetian bridge, and a spring-fed lagoon holding 820,000 gallons. One of the few public pools on the National Register of Historic Places.
📖 Wikipedia
🏛️ Tuesday - Friday 11:00am - 5:30pm · Saturday - Sunday 10:00am - 4:30pm
🚫 Closed Monday
⏰ ~1 hr

🚕 10 min → Fairchild Tropical Botanic Garden
3. Fairchild Tropical Botanic Garden
↳ An 83-acre garden in south Coral Gables with the largest palm collection in the continental United States — over 2,600 species across lakes, vine pergolas, a sunken garden, and a rain forest. Founded in 1938 by the plant explorer who introduced mangoes and nectarines to American agriculture.
📖 Wikipedia

4. Pérez Art Museum Miami
↳ Miami's modern and contemporary art museum, opened in 2013 in a Herzog & de Meuron building on the downtown waterfront. Hanging gardens by Patrick Blanc cascade from the overhanging roof; rotating exhibitions of international 20th- and 21st-century art fill the galleries above the Biscayne Bay terrace.

🌴 Postcards from the Magic City

Miami reads as one long argument between preservation and reinvention, and you can trace it in a single morning. Española Way and the Art Deco district only exist because Barbara Capitman threw herself in front of the wrecking ball in the late 1970s — the same instinct that, decades later, turned Wynwood's dead warehouse walls into the most-photographed art in the city. Two neighborhoods, two generations, the same refusal to let Miami be ordinary.

The water keeps showing up as the quiet headliner. South Pointe Park sits at Government Cut watching cruise ships thread the channel; Vizcaya was built facing Biscayne Bay with a stone barge moored offshore purely as a view. Bookend your two days at the water's edge — golden hour at South Pointe, late afternoon in Vizcaya's gardens — and the city's whole personality clicks into place.
