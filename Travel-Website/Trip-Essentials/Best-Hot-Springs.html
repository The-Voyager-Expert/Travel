Best Hot Springs

Iceland
🔵
Blue Lagoon
Grindavík, Iceland
A geothermal spa set in a black lava field on the Reykjanes Peninsula. The milky-blue water is rich in silica and sulfur, averaging 37–39 °C. The lagoon is man-made — fed by the outflow of the nearby Svartsengi geothermal power plant — but the surreal landscape and mineral-dense water are entirely natural.
🌡️ 37–39 °C💧 Silica & sulfur☀️ Year-round
Official Site Reykjavik Guide
🌊
Sky Lagoon
Reykjavik, Iceland
An oceanfront geothermal lagoon on the edge of Reykjavik with an infinity edge that blends into the North Atlantic. The 70-m-long pool holds seawater heated to 38–40 °C. A seven-step ritual includes cold plunge, sauna, fog room, and a sky scrub with sesame and almond oil.
🌡️ 38–40 °C🌅 Ocean views☀️ Year-round
Official Site Reykjavik Guide
🏔️
Landmannalaugar
Fjallabak, Iceland
A natural hot stream in the highland interior surrounded by rhyolite mountains streaked in red, green, yellow, and purple. Cold river water mixes with geothermal springs to create a swimmable temperature. The trailhead for the Laugavegur trek — one of the world's great multi-day hikes.
🌡️ 36–40 °C🥾 Highland access☀️ Jun–Sep only
Visit Iceland Reykjavik Guide
🌿
Mývatn Nature Baths
Mývatn, Iceland
A geothermal lagoon on the shores of Lake Mývatn in northeast Iceland — the north's answer to the Blue Lagoon but far less crowded. The milky-blue water is naturally heated to 36–40 °C and rich in minerals. Surrounded by volcanic craters, lava fields, and pseudocraters. Steam vents rise from the earth across the landscape.
🌡️ 36–40 °C💧 Mineral-rich☀️ Year-round
Official Site
🏊
Secret Lagoon (Gamla Laugin)
Flúðir, Iceland
The oldest swimming pool in Iceland, built in 1891. A natural hot spring pool surrounded by a mossy, steaming landscape with a small geyser erupting every few minutes at the pool's edge. The water holds at 38–40 °C year-round. Far less commercial than the Blue Lagoon — a simple, unadorned soak in a natural setting on the Golden Circle route.
🌡️ 38–40 °C🕯️ Since 1891☀️ Year-round
Official Site
🏔️
Seljavallalaug
South Iceland
A 25-m geothermally heated pool built into a mountainside in 1923 — one of the oldest pools in Iceland. Fed by a hot spring seeping through the hillside, mixing with cold stream water. No changing rooms, no staff, no fee — just a concrete pool tucked into a valley with moss-covered cliffs on three sides. A 20-minute walk from the road.
🌡️ 25–35 °C🆓 Free☀️ Year-round

Europe
🏛️
Széchenyi Thermal Bath
Budapest, Hungary
The largest medicinal bath complex in Europe, built in 1913 in neo-Baroque style. Eighteen pools (indoor and outdoor) fed by two thermal springs reaching 74–77 °C at the source. The iconic outdoor pool steams year-round — locals play chess on floating boards in the warm water even in winter.
🌡️ 27–38 °C (pools)🏛️ Neo-Baroque☀️ Year-round
Official Site Budapest Guide
🦁
Gellért Thermal Bath
Budapest, Hungary
An Art Nouveau thermal bath built in 1918 inside the Gellért Hotel, at the foot of Gellért Hill on the Danube. The main indoor pool is flanked by carved stone columns, mosaic walls, and a glass-domed ceiling. Thirteen pools total, including an outdoor wave pool. Fed by springs discovered in the 13th century — Hungarian kings bathed at this site.
🌡️ 26–40 °C🏛️ Art Nouveau☀️ Year-round
Official Site Budapest Guide
🌙
Rudas Thermal Bath
Budapest, Hungary
A 16th-century Ottoman bathhouse with an octagonal main pool under a domed stone ceiling pierced by star-shaped skylights. Built in 1550 during Turkish rule. The rooftop pool, added in 2014, offers panoramic views over the Danube and the city skyline. Night bathing on weekends with coloured lighting under the Ottoman dome.
🌡️ 28–42 °C🕯️ Ottoman (1550)☀️ Year-round
Official Site Budapest Guide

Bath Thermae Spa (Roman Baths)
Bath, England
The only naturally occurring hot spring in Britain, used since Celtic and Roman times. The spring delivers 1.17 million litres daily at 46 °C. The Roman Baths complex (museum only — no swimming) dates to 70 AD. The modern Thermae Bath Spa next door offers a rooftop pool overlooking Bath Abbey.
🌡️ 46 °C at source🕯️ 2,000+ years☀️ Year-round

Saturnia Hot Springs
Saturnia, Tuscany, Italy
Cascading thermal waterfalls flowing over natural travertine terraces in the Tuscan countryside. The sulfur-rich water emerges at 37.5 °C and tumbles down a series of pools called Cascate del Mulino. Free and open 24 hours — a wild thermal experience with no fences, tickets, or infrastructure.
🌡️ 37.5 °C🆓 Free & open 24h☀️ Year-round

Aqua Dome
Längenfeld, Ötztal, Austria
A futuristic thermal spa in the Ötztal Alps with three bowl-shaped outdoor pools elevated on pillars, reflecting the surrounding peaks. The water is drawn from a thermal spring at 1,865 m elevation. Pools range 34–36 °C. The architecture — glass, steel, and concrete bowls floating above the valley — is as striking as the setting.
🌡️ 34–36 °C🏔️ Alpine setting☀️ Year-round

Pamukkale Thermal Pools
Denizli, Turkey
Brilliant white travertine terraces formed by calcium-rich thermal water cascading down a hillside for thousands of years. The ancient Greco-Roman city of Hierapolis sits at the top. Visitors wade barefoot through shallow turquoise pools. The Antique Pool lets you swim among submerged Roman columns at 36 °C.
🌡️ 36 °C🏛️ UNESCO☀️ Year-round

Leukerbad Thermal Baths
Leukerbad, Valais, Switzerland
The largest thermal spa complex in the Alps, at 1,411 m elevation. Over 3.9 million litres of thermal water flow daily from 65 springs, feeding indoor and outdoor pools surrounded by 3,000-m peaks. The Romans bathed here; so did Goethe and Twain. The Gemmi Pass cliff face towers directly above the village.
🌡️ 28–43 °C🏔️ 1,411 m elevation☀️ Year-round
Official Site
🏛️
Friedrichsbad & Caracalla Spa
Baden-Baden, Germany
Two thermal spas fed by the same hot springs, used since Roman times. Friedrichsbad (1877) is a Renaissance-style bathhouse with a strict 17-step Roman-Irish bathing ritual — no swimwear. Caracalla Spa is the modern counterpart with indoor/outdoor pools and a grotto. Mark Twain wrote: "After ten minutes you forget time; after twenty, the world."
🌡️ 32–38 °C🕯️ Roman origins☀️ Year-round
Official Site

Karlovy Vary Hot Springs
Karlovy Vary, Czech Republic
A spa town built around 13 major hot springs and over 300 smaller ones. The Vřídlo (Sprudel) is the most powerful — shooting 12 m into the air at 72 °C. Visitors drink the mineral water from special porcelain sipping cups while strolling elegant colonnades. A spa destination since the 14th century, visited by Beethoven, Chopin, and Kafka.
🌡️ 30–72 °C🕯️ Since 1370☀️ Year-round

Bagno Vignoni
Val d'Orcia, Tuscany, Italy
A medieval village in the Val d'Orcia whose central piazza is a large open-air thermal pool instead of a square. The rectangular stone basin, built in the 16th century, steams year-round at 49 °C. Lorenzo de' Medici and St. Catherine of Siena bathed here. Below the village, the thermal water cascades freely into natural rock pools at Parco dei Mulini.
🌡️ 49 °C🕯️ Renaissance☀️ Year-round

Laugarvatn Fontana
Laugarvatn, Iceland
Geothermal baths built directly over a natural hot spring on the shore of Lake Laugarvatn, on the Golden Circle route. Steam rooms are heated by the geothermal spring bubbling up through the ground beneath the floor. Outdoor pools overlook the lake — in winter, visitors alternate between the hot water and a cold plunge in the icy lake.

Pozar Thermal Baths
Loutraki Aridaia, Greece
Natural hot springs in a forested gorge at the base of Mt. Voras in northern Greece. Thermal water at 37 °C flows from the mountainside into open-air stone pools surrounded by plane trees and waterfalls. An indoor hammam and outdoor cold-water cascades sit nearby. One of the most scenic thermal settings in Southern Europe.

Japan
♨️
Beppu Onsen
Beppu, Ōita, Japan
The highest-volume hot spring city in Japan and second in the world after Yellowstone — over 2,800 vents producing 130,000 tonnes of water daily. Eight distinct hot spring districts (Beppu Hattō) each with different mineral profiles. The "Hells" (Jigoku) are viewing springs too hot to enter, including a cobalt-blue pool at 98 °C.
🌡️ 40–98 °C♨️ 2,800+ vents☀️ Year-round

Jigokudani Monkey Park
Yamanouchi, Nagano, Japan
Wild Japanese macaques (snow monkeys) bathe in a natural hot spring surrounded by snow-covered forest at 850 m elevation. The only place in the world where wild monkeys soak in thermal water. The troop descends from the mountainside to warm up in winter — photographed sitting shoulder-deep in the steaming pool.
🌡️ 40 °C🐒 Wild snow monkeys☀️ Best Dec–Mar

Hakone Onsen
Hakone, Kanagawa, Japan
A volcanic hot spring resort area in the mountains southwest of Tokyo, known for open-air baths (rotenburo) with views of Mt. Fuji. Twenty distinct springs with varying mineral compositions — sulfur, sodium chloride, calcium. Many ryokan (traditional inns) offer private onsen rooms. The Ōwakudani valley steams with active sulfur vents.
🌡️ 35–45 °C🗻 Mt. Fuji views☀️ Year-round

Kusatsu Onsen
Kusatsu, Gunma, Japan
One of Japan's top three onsen towns. The Yubatake (hot water field) in the town centre is a massive wooden channel system where scalding spring water is cooled before distribution to bathhouses. Over 32,000 litres per minute flow through at 51–94 °C. The yumomi tradition — stirring the water with wooden paddles — is performed daily.
🌡️ 51–94 °C (source)♨️ 32,000 L/min☀️ Year-round

Dōgo Onsen
Matsuyama, Ehime, Japan
One of the oldest hot springs in Japan — referenced in texts from the 8th century and said to have been used for 3,000 years. The main bathhouse (Dōgo Onsen Honkan), built in 1894, is a three-storey wooden structure that inspired the bathhouse in Miyazaki's Spirited Away. Alkaline spring water at 42–51 °C with no sulfur smell.
🌡️ 42–51 °C🕯️ 3,000 years☀️ Year-round

Kinosaki Onsen
Toyooka, Hyōgo, Japan
A traditional onsen town with seven public bathhouses (sotoyu) along a willow-lined canal. Visitors wear yukata robes and geta sandals and stroll between baths, buying a pass for all seven. Each bathhouse has a distinct style — cave baths, rooftop baths, garden baths. The town has operated as a bathing destination since 720 AD.
🌡️ 37–42 °C🏮 7 public baths☀️ Year-round

Kurokawa Onsen
Minamioguni, Kumamoto, Japan
A secluded onsen village in a steep, forested river gorge on Kyushu. Over 20 ryokan each operate their own rotenburo (open-air baths) cut into the ravine walls, surrounded by forest canopy. A bath-hopping pass (nyūtō tegata) lets visitors try three different ryokan baths. The town deliberately limits signage and lighting to preserve its rural atmosphere.

Noboribetsu Onsen
Noboribetsu, Hokkaido, Japan
Hokkaido's most famous hot spring resort, powered by Jigokudani (Hell Valley) — a volcanic crater where steam, boiling water, and sulfur gases vent from dozens of fumaroles. Nine distinct mineral spring types emerge within a 500-m radius, each with different therapeutic properties. The sulfurous landscape is lunar — barren grey rock with yellow sulfur deposits and billowing steam.
🌡️ 45–90 °C🌋 9 spring types☀️ Year-round

Ibusuki Sand Baths
Ibusuki, Kagoshima, Japan
A beach where geothermally heated sand is shovelled over visitors as they lie in shallow trenches wearing yukata. The sand, heated to 50–55 °C by underground steam, presses down with a warm, heavy weight. A 10–15 minute burial is followed by a soak in an indoor onsen. The only place in the world where natural sand bathing is a daily public service.
🌡️ 50–55 °C (sand)🏖️ Sand burial☀️ Year-round

Polynesian Spa
Rotorua, New Zealand
A lakefront thermal spa fed by two natural springs — acidic Priest Spring and alkaline Rachel Spring — offering different mineral experiences in one complex. Twenty-eight pools terraced above Lake Rotorua. Named by Condé Nast as one of the world's top ten spas. Rotorua sits in the Taupō Volcanic Zone, one of the most active geothermal areas on Earth.
🌡️ 36–42 °C💧 Dual mineral springs☀️ Year-round

Hot Water Beach
Coromandel Peninsula, New Zealand
A beach where geothermal water seeps up through the sand at temperatures up to 64 °C. At low tide, visitors dig their own hot pool in the sand with a shovel and sit in it while waves crash metres away. The hot zone is a 10-m-wide stretch — miss it by a few steps and the sand is cold.
🌡️ Up to 64 °C🏖️ Dig-your-own pool☀️ Best at low tide

Kerosene Creek
Rotorua, New Zealand
A free, wild hot stream in the forest south of Rotorua where geothermal water flows into a bush-lined creek. A small waterfall drops into a natural pool large enough for a dozen people. The water holds at 36–40 °C. No facilities, no signs beyond a small carpark — just a short forest walk to a warm river in the woods.
🌡️ 36–40 °C🆓 Free☀️ Year-round

Surrounded by mountain beech forest and snow-capped peaks. Also offers hydroslides, a lazy river, and an aqua therapy pool. The main thermal complex in the South Island.

A geothermal wonderland with vivid, surreal hot springs — the Champagne Pool is 74 m across with bright orange mineral deposits ringing turquoise water at 75 °C. Grand Prismatic Spring
Yellowstone, Wyoming, United States
The largest hot spring in the United States and third-largest in the world — 110 m across. The vivid rings of orange, yellow, green, and blue are produced by heat-loving bacteria (thermophiles) living in different temperature bands. The centre reaches 87 °C. Best viewed from the overlook trail above, where the full colour gradient is visible.
🌡️ 87 °C (viewing only)📐 110 m across☀️ Apr–Nov
NPS Info Yellowstone Guide

Boiling River
Yellowstone, Wyoming, United States
A spot where a hot spring enters the Gardner River, mixing scalding geothermal water with the cold river to create a swimmable zone. Visitors sit in the river and adjust position to find their ideal temperature. One of the only places in Yellowstone where soaking in geothermal water is permitted. A short, flat trail leads to the confluence.
🌡️ 38–44 °C (mix zone)🏞️ River soaking☀️ Summer only
NPS Info Yellowstone Guide

Strawberry Park Hot Springs
Steamboat Springs, Colorado, United States
Natural stone-lined pools fed by mineral springs at 2,100 m elevation in the Routt National Forest. Water reaches 40 °C in the hottest pool, with cooler pools downstream. Surrounded by aspen and pine forest — in winter, snow blankets the surrounding hillsides while steam rises from the pools. Clothing-optional after dark.

Water temperatures range 35–45 °C, with cold plunge pools between. The design is deliberately minimalist — dark stone, red-painted wood, and steam.

A series of thermal pools and waterfalls heated naturally by the Arenal Volcano, set in a landscaped tropical garden. Water flows down the volcanic slope through channels, pools, and cascades ranging 27–39 °C. The hottest water is nearest the source — the volcano itself. Night bathing with the volcanic cone silhouetted above is the draw.

The water emerges at 33 °C from the volcanic ground and flows through a series of stone-lined basins connected by wooden walkways. Hierve el Agua
Oaxaca, Mexico
Petrified waterfalls — mineral-rich spring water has deposited calcium carbonate over millennia to form cliff faces that look like frozen cascades. Shallow infinity pools sit at the cliff edge, 2,500 m above sea level, overlooking the Oaxacan valley. The water is cool to lukewarm (24–26 °C) rather than hot, but the geological spectacle is unmatched.

Hot Springs National Park
Hot Springs, Arkansas, United States
The only national park centred on thermal springs. Forty-seven springs on Hot Springs Mountain produce 2.5 million litres daily at 62 °C. Bathhouse Row — eight ornate buildings from the 1880s–1920s — lines the main street; two still operate (Buckstaff and Quapaw). The Fordyce Bathhouse is now the park visitor centre, restored to its 1915 grandeur.
🌡️ 62 °C (source)🏛️ National Park☀️ Year-round

Glenwood Hot Springs
Glenwood Springs, Colorado, United States
The largest hot spring pool in the world — a 123-m-long mineral pool fed by the Yampah Spring at 51 °C. The big pool is cooled to 32–36 °C; a smaller therapy pool stays at 40 °C. Located at the confluence of the Colorado and Roaring Fork rivers, with the Rocky Mountains rising on both sides. In operation since 1888.
🌡️ 32–40 °C📐 123 m long☀️ Year-round

Chena Hot Springs
Fairbanks, Alaska, United States
A geothermal resort 100 km east of Fairbanks, open year-round. An outdoor rock lake stays at 41 °C even when air temperatures hit −40 °C. One of the best locations on Earth to soak in hot water while watching the northern lights — the resort sits under the aurora oval, and the surrounding wilderness has no light pollution.
🌡️ 41 °C🌌 Aurora viewing☀️ Year-round

Banff Upper Hot Springs
Banff, Alberta, Canada
A historic hot spring pool at 1,585 m on the shoulder of Sulphur Mountain in Banff National Park. The thermal water emerges at 47 °C from a fault line in the Canadian Rockies and is cooled to 37–40 °C in the pool. The discovery of these springs in 1883 led directly to the creation of Canada's first national park. Mountain views from the pool.
🌡️ 37–40 °C🏔️ 1,585 m☀️ Year-round

Liard River Hot Springs
British Columbia, Canada
Canada's second-largest hot spring, in the boreal wilderness of northern BC along the Alaska Highway. A boardwalk leads through swampy marshland to two large natural pools — Alpha (42 °C) and Beta (52 °C, cooled by a cold stream). The hot water supports a pocket of tropical-like vegetation — orchids and ferns — in subarctic forest. Moose frequently visit.

Ojo Caliente
Ojo Caliente, New Mexico, United States
One of the oldest health resorts in North America, in operation since 1868. The only hot spring in the world with four distinct mineral sources in one location — iron, soda, lithia, and arsenic (therapeutic dose). Each pool has a different mineral composition and colour. Set in the high desert of the Sangre de Cristo foothills at 2,000 m. Pueblo people used these springs for centuries.

Soufrière Sulphur Springs
Soufrière, Saint Lucia
The Caribbean's only drive-in volcano. A collapsed volcanic crater with bubbling mud pools and sulfurous vents between the Pitons. Nearby, mineral-rich thermal baths at 35–45 °C are fed by the volcano's runoff. Visitors coat themselves in volcanic mud, let it dry, and rinse in the warm mineral water. The setting — between two UNESCO-listed volcanic spires — is dramatic.

Hot springs at the base of Huangshan (Yellow Mountain), one of China's most celebrated landscapes. Beitou Hot Springs
Beitou, Taipei, Taiwan
A thermal valley in the hills north of Taipei where steam rises from a sulfurous creek running through the neighbourhood. Three spring types — white sulfur, green sulfur (rare, found at only two places on Earth), and iron-rich carbonate. The Beitou Hot Spring Museum is a 1913 Japanese-era public bathhouse. Dozens of hotels offer private tubs, and the Millennium Hot Spring is a free public outdoor pool.

Manikaran Hot Springs
Parvati Valley, Himachal Pradesh, India
Scalding hot springs in a narrow Himalayan valley at 1,760 m, sacred to both Hindus and Sikhs. The water emerges at 88–95 °C — hot enough to cook rice and eggs, which locals and pilgrims do at the source. A Sikh gurdwara sits directly over one spring, using the thermal water in its communal kitchen. Snow-capped peaks frame the valley on both sides.

One of Asia's largest jjimjilbang (Korean bathhouse complexes), spanning two floors inside the world's largest department store. Two natural hot springs — sodium chloride (seawater) and sodium bicarbonate (alkaline) — feed 22 themed baths including an outdoor Finnish-style pool. Thirteen themed sauna rooms range from ice rooms to Himalayan salt rooms to hinoki wood chambers.

Yangpachen Hot Springs
Yangpachen, Tibet, China
The highest-altitude developed hot spring in the world, at 4,300 m on the Tibetan Plateau, 87 km from Lhasa. Geysers and fumaroles dot the valley floor, and the geothermal field powers a small power plant. A basic outdoor pool offers soaking in spring water at 40–45 °C with views of snow-covered peaks. The thin air, vast plateau, and steaming earth create a stark, otherworldly setting.

Aguas Calientes (Colca Canyon)
Chivay, Arequipa, Peru
Natural thermal pools on the banks of the Colca River at 3,600 m, at the entrance to Colca Canyon — one of the deepest canyons in the world. Stone-lined pools at 36–40 °C overlook the canyon walls. Termas de Chillán
Nevados de Chillán, Chile
Volcanic hot springs at 1,850 m elevation on the slopes of the Nevados de Chillán volcano complex. Outdoor pools sit in a valley with views of snow-capped volcanic peaks. In winter, the resort doubles as a ski area — soak in 37–41 °C springs then ski the next run. Some of the highest naturally heated pools in the Chilean Andes.
🌡️ 37–41 °C⛷️ Ski + soak☀️ Year-round

Lençóis Thermal Springs
Caldas Novas, Goiás, Brazil
The largest hydrothermal complex in the world by volume of naturally heated water. The city of Caldas Novas sits on top of a massive underground aquifer heated by deep volcanic rock. Over 80 springs emerge at 37–57 °C. Multiple resort parks offer pools, lazy rivers, and water slides all fed by the natural hot water. The surrounding cerrado (savanna) landscape contrasts with the lush resort pools.
🌡️ 37–57 °C💧 Largest thermal aquifer☀️ Year-round

Lake Bogoria Hot Springs
Baringo County, Kenya
Over 200 hot springs and geysers line the western shore of Lake Bogoria in the Great Rift Valley. Some erupt as geysers shooting 5 m high. The alkaline lake is one of the primary feeding grounds for lesser flamingos — hundreds of thousands turn the shoreline pink while steam rises from the boiling vents behind them.
🌡️ Up to 100 °C🦩 Flamingo colony☀️ Year-round

Dallol Hydrothermal Fields
Danakil Depression, Ethiopia
The hottest inhabited place on Earth. Acidic hot springs push through salt flats to create neon-yellow, green, and orange mineral formations unlike anything else on the planet. Located 125 m below sea level. Water temperatures reach 108 °C. Not swimmable — the pools are sulfuric acid — but the landscape is extraterrestrial. Guided expedition only.
🌡️ Up to 108 °C⚠️ Viewing only🥾 Guided expedition

Mataranka Thermal Pool
Elsey National Park, Northern Territory, Australia
A crystal-clear thermal pool fed by underground springs at a constant 34 °C, surrounded by a canopy of palm trees in the Australian Outback. The water rises from a deep limestone aquifer and flows through the Roper River system. The setting — a pocket of tropical palms in otherwise dry savanna — creates a natural oasis.
🌡️ 34 °C🌴 Palm-fringed oasis☀️ Year-round

Peninsula Hot Springs
Mornington Peninsula, Victoria, Australia
Australia's first natural thermal mineral springs resort. Over 50 globally inspired bathing experiences — hilltop pools with 360-degree views, a Turkish hammam, cave pool, and a cold plunge amphitheatre. The water is drawn from a bore 637 m deep, emerging at 50 °C with high mineral content. An hour south of Melbourne.
🌡️ 37–43 °C (pools)♨️ 50+ bathing experiences☀️ Year-round
