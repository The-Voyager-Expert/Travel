Multi-Destination Trip Planning

Visiting two or three cities in one trip is often better value than visiting one — if you build the routing intelligently. A poorly structured multi-city itinerary adds hours of dead travel and unnecessary cost. A well-structured one lets geography carry you forward.

The Open-Jaw Ticket

A standard round-trip forces you to fly back to where you started — even if you've ended your trip 600 km away. An open-jaw ticket lets you fly into one city and out of another, with no backtracking.

Example: you fly Seattle → Lisbon (SEA → LIS) and return Madrid → Seattle (MAD → SEA), spending the middle days traveling south through Spain by train. The open-jaw costs roughly the same as a Lisbon round-trip, and you save two full travel days that would otherwise be spent reversing your route.

Open-jaw tickets are bookable through any standard flight search (Google Flights calls it "multi-city"; most airline sites support it). Book the inbound and outbound legs together as one itinerary — if either leg is on the same airline or alliance, fares are usually competitive with a direct round-trip.

Building a Flight Arc

The key principle for multi-destination routing: travel in one direction. Enter from the west, exit from the east — or enter in the north and exit in the south. When your travel arc has geographic logic, connecting legs are short and cheap. When it doesn't, you pay extra to backtrack.

Geographic chaining means picking cities in a natural sequence along a route. Lisbon → Seville → Barcelona → Madrid follows the Iberian coast north and east — each connection is a comfortable train or short flight, and the final city is your airport-exit point. The alternative (Lisbon → Barcelona → Seville → Madrid) scrambles the geography and requires backtracking.

Hub-and-spoke is a different model: fly into one major hub and take radial day trips or overnight trains from there. Tokyo as a base with day-trips to Kyoto, Nara, and Hakone is hub-and-spoke — no open-jaw needed, very low friction. Works best when the hub city anchors the trip and side cities are genuinely close.

Regional Hubs That Unlock a Region

Some cities carry excellent flight connectivity into a region and serve as natural launching points for multi-city arcs. Flying into a hub first — even if it's not your primary destination — often produces better routing and lower total fares than flying directly into a secondary city.

Western Europe
Paris · Lisbon · Madrid
Strong transatlantic connections; dense rail network radiates outward. Lisbon is particularly well-priced for US–Europe entry.
Northern Europe
Amsterdam · London · Frankfurt
Major hubs for Scandinavia, the Baltics, and Central Europe. AMS and FRA have exceptional frequency from North America.
Middle East
Dubai · Istanbul
Emirates and Turkish Airlines connect almost every region; layovers are often 4–8h and make viable stopovers in their own right.
Southeast Asia
Bangkok · Singapore · Kuala Lumpur
Budget airlines (AirAsia, Scoot, Lion Air) fan out from all three. Bangkok is usually the cheapest Asia entry from the US via a layover.
East Asia
Tokyo · Seoul
NRT and ICN are natural entry points for Japan–Korea arcs. Haneda (HND) works better for Tokyo-only trips given its central location.
Latin America
Bogotá · Lima · São Paulo
Avianca (BOG) and LATAM (LIM, GRU) hub here with dense regional networks. Bogotá reaches the Caribbean, Andes, and Amazon efficiently.

Adding a Rail Leg

On routes where a high-speed train covers the distance in under 3 hours, the train is almost always the better choice over a short flight: no airport check-in overhead, city-center to city-center, and often cheaper than a last-minute flight.

Key rail connections worth substituting for flights in the guide fleet:

  • Paris → Amsterdam — 3h20 on Eurostar/Thalys (city-center to city-center)
  • Tokyo → Kyoto — 2h15 on the Shinkansen Nozomi
  • Tokyo → Osaka — 2h30 on the Shinkansen (skip the Kyoto stop if time-limited)
  • Lisbon → Seville — ~3h by Alfa Pendular + Avant combination or direct bus
  • Barcelona → Madrid — 2h30 on the AVE high-speed
  • London → Paris — 2h15 on the Eurostar
  • Vienna → Budapest — 2h40 on the Railjet; Vienna → Prague — 4h on Railjet
  • Milan → Venice — 2h15 on the Frecciarossa

Managing Luggage Across Cities

Pack for the hardest leg. If any leg of your trip requires navigating a crowded market, a Shinkansen with no luggage storage, or a narrow medina street, that leg sets the size limit for your whole bag. Multi-city trips reward carry-on-only travel more than single-city trips.

Forwarding luggage. In Japan, takkyubin (luggage forwarding) services (TA-Q-BIN) are widely available at convenience stores: ship your bag from your Kyoto ryokan to your Osaka hotel for ¥1,000–2,000 and travel hands-free on the Shinkansen. Similar services exist in South Korea. In Europe, luggage-forwarding is less common but possible via Excess Baggage Company (UK) and Luggage Forward (international).

Left-luggage storage. Most major train stations offer coin lockers or staffed left-luggage counters. Useful on arrival day (before hotel check-in) or departure day (after check-out, before an evening train). Typical cost: €4–8/day in Europe, ¥600–900/day in Japan.

For more on what to pack and airline bag restrictions, see Travel Packing and Baggage Fees.

Worked Examples

Japan Arc · 7 nights
1 flight 2 rail legs Open-jaw
Fly INTO Tokyo (NRT/HND) · Return FROM Osaka (KIX)
  • Tokyo Fly in 3 nights — Shinjuku, Shibuya, Asakusa base
  • Kyoto 2h15 Shinkansen 2 nights — temples, Fushimi Inari, Arashiyama
  • Osaka → KIX 16 min train 2 nights — Dotonbori, Shinsekai; fly home from KIX
Tip: Book an open-jaw (NRT in / KIX out) as one itinerary — same price as a Tokyo round-trip with no backtracking. Get the JR Pass for unlimited Shinkansen travel before leaving home.
Iberian Peninsula Loop · 10 nights
1 flight 3 rail legs Open-jaw
Fly INTO Lisbon (LIS) · Return FROM Madrid (MAD)
  • Lisbon Fly in 3 nights — Alfama, Belém, Sintra day trip
  • Seville ~3h bus/rail 2 nights — Alcázar, tapas bar crawl, Flamenco
  • Barcelona 1h20 flight 3 nights — Gaudí, Gothic Quarter, La Boqueria
  • Madrid → MAD 2h30 AVE 2 nights — Prado, El Retiro; fly home from MAD
Tip: The Lisbon–Seville gap is the least seamless leg (no high-speed rail). Consider a Flixbus or a 1-stop train via Badajoz/Huelva (~3h30). The rest of the arc is fast rail throughout.
Southeast Asia Arc · 12 nights
4 flights Multi-city
All one-way budget flights — fly in and out of any city
  • Bangkok Fly in 3 nights — temples, street food, Chao Phraya
  • Chiang Mai 1h AirAsia ~$30 3 nights — Doi Inthanon, Sunday Walking Street
  • Hanoi 2h VietJet ~$40 3 nights — Old Quarter, Ha Long Bay day tour
  • Ho Chi Minh City 2h Vietnam Air 3 nights — Ben Thanh, Mekong Delta, Phú Quốc option
Tip: Budget airlines in Southeast Asia run 3–4× daily on these routes. Book each leg separately on Google Flights or directly with AirAsia/VietJet — total cost for the connecting flights is typically under $120. Enter Bangkok on a through-ticket from the US and book the regional hops independently.
Mediterranean Sampler · 9 nights
2 flights 1 ferry Open-jaw
Fly INTO Athens (ATH) · Return FROM Rome (FCO)
  • Athens Fly in 3 nights — Acropolis, Plaka, Monastiraki
  • Santorini ~8h ferry 2 nights — Oia sunset, caldera views
  • Rome 1h20 flight to FCO 4 nights — Colosseum, Vatican, Trastevere; fly home
Tip: The overnight Santorini ferry from Piraeus leaves around 9 pm and arrives at 5–6 am — book a cabin for a proper night's sleep. Flying Athens→Santorini is 45 min and cheaper in high season; the ferry is the better experience out of season.

How to Plan It — The Right Sequence

  1. Choose your entry and exit cities. Start with where you're flying in and out — these anchor everything else. Entry and exit can be the same city (round-trip) or different (open-jaw).
  2. Map a geographic arc between them. List the cities you want to visit and arrange them so travel moves in one consistent direction. Eliminate backtracking.
  3. Identify which connecting legs are rail and which are air. Any connection under ~3 hours by high-speed train: default to rail. Over that, or where no fast rail exists: budget airline or regional flight.
  4. Book the transatlantic or long-haul flights first. Get the open-jaw or multi-city international fare locked before anything else. These seats constrain your schedule; the regional connections are flexible.
  5. Book rail legs and regional flights. In Europe, book Eurail or point-to-point tickets (high-demand routes like Paris→Amsterdam and London→Paris sell out weeks in advance). In Asia, book Shinkansen reserved seats for travel at specific times.
  6. Set accommodation check-out to allow midday city transfers. Aim for a check-out at 11 am–noon and a check-in after 3 pm at the next city. This gives you a comfortable transfer window and a few hours to explore before settling in.
