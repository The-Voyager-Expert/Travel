Cards with foreign transaction fees — avoid abroad
3% FTF on every international purchase
Do not use internationally
These common cards charge 3% foreign transaction fees on every international purchase. On a $5,000 trip that's $150 in invisible charges:
  • Chase Freedom Unlimited — 3% FTF despite being a Chase card. Do not use abroad.
  • Chase Freedom Flex — 3% FTF. Do not use abroad.
  • Citi Double Cash — 3% FTF. Do not use abroad.
  • Citi Custom Cash — 3% FTF. Do not use abroad.
  • Most standard bank debit cards (Chase checking, BofA checking, Wells Fargo checking) — 1–3% FTF plus ATM fees. Use Schwab or Fidelity debit instead.
  • Most store co-branded cards (Target RedCard, Amazon Prime, etc.) — 3% FTF. Do not use internationally.
Always check your specific card's terms at the issuer website. FTF policies occasionally change.

Dynamic currency conversion (DCC)
The most common travel money trap
Always decline
When a foreign ATM or card terminal offers to charge you in USD rather than the local currency, it's applying its own exchange rate — typically 3–7% above the interbank rate, with extreme cases documented at 13–18%. This markup goes to the bank or merchant, not your card issuer.

At ATMs: When prompted "Proceed in USD or [local currency]?" — always choose local currency. Some ATMs in tourist areas default to USD and pre-check it; look carefully before confirming. If the machine forces conversion with no opt-out, cancel and use a different ATM.

At card terminals: If a terminal asks "Pay in USD?" or a salesperson offers "I'll charge you in dollars" — always decline. Select local currency.

Even Schwab does not reimburse DCC markup — it only reimburses the operator surcharge. DCC is a hidden markup on the exchange rate itself.
4-digit PIN requirements
International PIN compatibility
Most ATMs outside the US accept only 4-digit PINs. Many European and Asian ATMs have a maximum of 4 or 6 digits. If your PIN is longer than 6 digits, you will likely encounter problems. Most US bank PINs are 4 digits, which works everywhere.

If your PIN starts with 0, some older European ATMs may not accept it — the leading zero drops and the PIN becomes 3 digits. Contact your bank if this applies to you.

How to change PIN before travel: Visit a US branch in person (most secure method), or use a US ATM — insert card, select PIN Change from the main menu. Allow 24 hours to propagate. Do this before departure; changing a PIN while abroad can be complicated.

Credit card cash advance PINs: US credit cards often have no PIN assigned. If your credit card is also an ATM backup (not recommended — cash advances accrue interest immediately with no grace period), call your issuer 7–10 business days before departure to request a PIN by mail.
Currencies you can't take out of the country
Non-convertible currencies
Some currencies are non-convertible — they cannot be legally exchanged outside the country, or have no exchange value abroad. Do not bring these home.
  • Moroccan Dirham (MAD): Non-convertible. Cannot be exchanged outside Morocco. Spend down or convert back to a convertible currency before leaving.
  • Cambodian Riel (KHR): No exchange value outside Cambodia. USD is widely used there — withdraw/use USD instead.
  • Vietnamese Dong (VND): Non-convertible outside Vietnam. Spend down before departure.
  • Indonesian Rupiah (IDR): Technically convertible at major financial centers but rates outside Indonesia are very poor. Exchange in-country.
📋 ATM Tips
Choose local currency
Decline dynamic currency conversion
Always select local currency at ATMs and card terminals — never USD. Choosing USD activates dynamic currency conversion, which adds 3–7% above the real exchange rate. See Exchange section for full detail.
Use bank ATMs only
Avoid independent kiosks
Stick to ATMs attached to a physical bank branch. Independent kiosks in airports, tourist areas, and convenience stores charge higher fees and often apply DCC automatically. In Europe, avoid Euronet and Cardpoint machines specifically — they are notorious for high fees. Look for ATMs displaying the Visa, Plus, Mastercard, or Cirrus network logos.

Withdraw larger amounts less often
Minimize per-transaction fees
Flat ATM surcharges (Thailand's 220 THB, for example) make small frequent withdrawals expensive. Take out enough for 2–4 days at once. Keep excess in a hotel safe. Exception: in high-crime areas, carry only what you need for the day.
Notify your bank before travel
Prevent card freezes
Most banks monitor for unusual foreign activity and freeze cards automatically. Set a travel notice in your bank's app or call before departure. Carry two cards from different networks (ideally different issuers) — if one is frozen or lost, you have a backup. Save your bank's international emergency number in your phone before you leave.
ATM skimming — how to spot it
Card theft prevention
Skimming devices are most common in tourist areas and older freestanding ATMs.
  • Card slot: Wiggle it before inserting. Legitimate readers are firmly attached; a skimmer overlay feels loose or wobbly.
  • Keypad: Press the edges — a PIN capture overlay feels raised, spongier, or doesn't flush with the panel.
  • Hidden cameras: Look for unusual holes, bumps, or add-on pieces above the screen or on the side panels. Cover your hand over the keypad when entering your PIN regardless.
  • Shimmers: Newer ultra-thin devices go inside the chip slot — no external sign. If the card feels caught or inserts oddly, withdraw and report to the bank.
Prefer ATMs inside bank lobbies or secured vestibules. Best defense: use contactless/tap-to-pay for purchases and Schwab/Fidelity debit for cash so card data exposure is minimized.
If your card is swallowed by an ATM
What to do
  1. Do not leave the ATM — note the bank name, ATM ID number, address, and exact time.
  2. Call your card issuer's international emergency number immediately. Report the card captured and request a temporary freeze.
  3. If the ATM is at a bank branch during business hours, go inside immediately — a teller may be able to retrieve the card.
  4. Request an emergency card replacement or emergency cash transfer from your issuer.
  5. File a police report if you suspect tampering.
Have your issuer's international number saved in your phone before you travel — it's on the back of your card but you won't have the card if it's swallowed.

Japan Post Bank ATMs also accept foreign cards but may have limited hours and a ¥220 fee on some cards.

Withdraw cash at the airport Seven Bank ATM upon arrival and carry enough for the first day or two.
Thailand
Partial cash dependency · High ATM surcharge
Cards accepted at hotels, shopping malls, chain restaurants, 7-Eleven. Local markets, street food, tuk-tuks, and local transport are cash-only. ATM surcharge of 220 THB (~$6.30) applies at all major Thai banks per withdrawal. AEON Bank charges a lower 150 THB but has fewer locations.

Strategy: use Schwab or Fidelity debit to get the surcharge reimbursed. Withdraw larger amounts at AEON or another bank to amortize the flat fee. Thai Baht is fully convertible — you can exchange it back before leaving if needed.
Vietnam
High cash dependency
Vietnamese Dong (VND) is required for daily life. USD is accepted for some hotel bills and tour operators in major cities but is not a substitute for VND at street level. Best ATM options: VPBank (fee-free, up to 10M VND/transaction) and ACB (fee-free, up to 3M VND/transaction). Avoid TPBank (introduced ~3.3% fee June 2025).

VND is non-convertible outside Vietnam — spend down or convert back to USD at an in-country bank before leaving.
Indonesia
High cash dependency outside Bali tourist zones
Cards accepted at malls, international hotels, and upscale restaurants in Bali and Jakarta. Local warungs, transport, markets, and most small businesses are cash-only. Best ATMs: BCA and BNI — no foreign card surcharge. Avoid Mandiri ATMs (added Rp50,000 fee in February 2026). Withdraw from BCA or BNI at malls or in central areas.
Cambodia
Dual currency — USD widely accepted
USD is legal tender alongside Cambodian Riel (KHR) and is accepted everywhere — hotels, restaurants, shops, transport, and markets. Most ATMs dispense USD, not KHR. Change for amounts under $1 is often given in a mix of USD and riel (exchange rate: 1 USD = 4,000 KHR). Carrying USD cash is the simplest approach in Cambodia — you rarely need KHR specifically. ATM fees typically $2–$5/withdrawal.
India
Moderate-to-high cash dependency
Cards accepted at upscale hotels, international chains, and urban restaurants in major cities. Auto-rickshaws, street food, local shops, markets, smaller restaurants, rural areas, and temples require cash. Keep 2,000–5,000 INR in cash at all times. ATM fees from Indian banks (SBI, HDFC, ICICI): typically ₹150–₹200 per transaction for foreign cards.

UPI for foreigners: The "UPI One World" prepaid wallet allows tourists from select countries (G20 passport holders) to make QR-code payments without an Indian bank account. Requires a Safaricom or local SIM and setup at a partner location. Works well once set up but requires effort to activate. It is not the seamless UPI experience Indian residents have.
Morocco
Moderate cash dependency · Non-convertible currency
Cards accepted at larger hotels, upscale restaurants, and some shops in Marrakech, Fes, and Casablanca. Medinas (old cities), souks, local transport, street food, and small vendors require cash. ATMs are available in cities.

Critical: the Moroccan Dirham (MAD) is non-convertible outside Morocco and cannot be exchanged abroad. Do not exchange currency into dirhams before arriving. Budget conservatively since leftover dirhams are essentially worthless outside the country — you can exchange them back to euros or dollars at Moroccan airport banks before departure.
Middle East — UAE, Saudi, Jordan
Mostly cashless (UAE/Saudi) · Partial (Jordan)
UAE: ~84% of transactions are contactless. Cards (Visa, Mastercard) accepted almost universally in Dubai and Abu Dhabi — shops, restaurants, taxis, even small vendors in malls. Cash rarely needed except for very small street vendors.

Saudi Arabia: Highly cashless in cities. Mada (domestic debit network) is dominant; foreign Visa/Mastercard credit cards accepted at most merchants. Cash still used at smaller local shops and in rural areas.

Jordan: Cards accepted at hotels, upscale restaurants, and larger shops in Amman, Aqaba, and Petra. Local souks, taxis, and rural areas require Jordanian Dinar (JOD) cash. JOD is fully convertible — you can exchange it before or after arrival.
Western Europe
Mostly cashless · Contactless universal
Contactless payment (tap) accounts for ~80% of in-person card transactions across Europe, near 100% terminal capability. Contactless limits raised post-COVID (€50 in EU; £100 in UK). Apple Pay and Google Pay work on virtually all contactless terminals via Visa or Mastercard.

Cash rarely needed in UK, Netherlands, Nordics, and major German cities (though Germany historically prefers cash more than peers). Carry €10–30 in cash for small cafes, markets, and tips in France, Spain, and Italy. In smaller towns and rural areas across all countries, cash is more common.

Australia surcharge note: Some businesses add 0.5–2% for card payments — this is legal in Australia and disclosed at point of sale.
Eastern Europe
Mixed — cities cashless, towns cash-heavy
Poland: Cards widely accepted in cities. Blik (local mobile payment) is dominant among Polish consumers but not accessible to foreign tourists without a Polish bank account. Carry PLN cash for markets and smaller towns.

Czech Republic: Prague is very card-friendly. Outside cities, smaller shops and rural areas prefer Czech Koruna cash. Euro not accepted — always use CZK.

Hungary: Budapest: cards accepted at most restaurants and shops. Rural Hungary and local markets: cash in Hungarian Forint. Euro not accepted.

Romania: Cash remains common even in Bucharest for smaller venues. Outside cities, primarily cash. Keep Romanian Leu (RON) on hand.
Latin America — Argentina, Peru, Colombia
Partial cash dependency · Argentina situation volatile
Argentina: Following the unification of Argentina's exchange rates in early 2025, foreign Visa/Mastercard credit cards now receive approximately the street rate — a major change from prior years when the "blue dollar" parallel rate was the only practical option. As of early 2026, the official and parallel rates sit in the 1,430–1,460 ARS/USD range. Card use is now straightforward. ATM per-transaction limits from Argentine domestic ATMs can be very low (often 5,000–10,000 ARS) requiring multiple withdrawals. This is a rapidly changing situation — verify current exchange conditions before travel.

Peru: Cash dominant outside Lima. USD accepted at upscale hotels and some tourist venues; Peruvian Sol (PEN) required everywhere else. Markets, transport, local restaurants: cash only. ATMs available in Cusco and major cities; fewer in rural areas.

Colombia: Bogotá, Medellín, Cartagena: Visa/Mastercard at hotels and upscale restaurants. Local neighborhoods, street markets, transport, and tips: cash (Colombian Peso, COP).
Africa — Kenya, South Africa
Mixed by country
Kenya: M-Pesa mobile payments dominate. Foreign tourists can register for M-Pesa using a valid passport + Safaricom SIM (available at Nairobi airport) — after funding at any M-Pesa agent, you can pay via QR code at most local merchants. Cards accepted at Nairobi hotels and larger restaurants. Local markets, transport, and most daily spending require cash or M-Pesa.

South Africa: Cards (Visa, Mastercard) widely accepted in Cape Town, Johannesburg, and safari lodges. Contactless standard. Smaller businesses, township areas, and local markets prefer cash. Due to crime risk, avoid carrying large amounts of cash — use ATMs inside bank branches or shopping centers (not standalone outdoor machines).
Singapore, Australia, Canada, New Zealand
Highly cashless
Singapore: Cards and contactless accepted everywhere at modern establishments. Hawker centres (local food courts) are often cash-only — carry SGD 20–50 for meals. PayNow is used by locals but not accessible to foreign visitors without a Singapore bank account.

Australia: ~87% of transaction value is electronic. Contactless (tap) is universal. Cash rarely needed. Some businesses add a 0.5–2% surcharge for card payments — this is disclosed at the terminal before you confirm.

Canada: Interac debit is dominant among Canadians but foreign Visa/Mastercard cards work fine. Cards accepted almost universally. Cash rarely needed. Non-bank ATMs charge CAD $1.50–$3 fees.

New Zealand: 94% of transaction value is electronic. Cards and contactless accepted nearly everywhere including rural areas. Surcharges for credit cards are legal in NZ (often 1–2%) — debit cards typically surcharge-free.
Caribbean
Varies significantly by island
Resort properties and tourist infrastructure: cards universally accepted. Local restaurants, markets, transport, taxis, and tips: cash. USD is often accepted on many smaller islands (particularly islands with USD as the official or semi-official currency), but paying in local currency usually gets better value. ATM availability varies significantly — larger islands have plenty; smaller islands may have one or two machines with limited hours or frequent outages. Carry USD cash as a backup on any Caribbean trip.
