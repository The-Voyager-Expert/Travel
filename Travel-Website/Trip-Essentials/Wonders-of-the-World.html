Seven Wonders of the Ancient World
🔺
Great Pyramid of Giza
Giza, Egypt
The only surviving ancient wonder — a 4,500-year-old monument that held the record as the tallest man-made structure for over 3,800 years. Built as a tomb for Pharaoh Khufu using an estimated 2.3 million limestone blocks, each averaging 2.5 tonnes. Originally clad in polished white Tura limestone with a gold capstone.
📅 Built c. 2560 BC📐 146 m (original height)🏛️ UNESCO

Hanging Gardens of Babylon
Babylon, Iraq (disputed)
The most mysterious of the ancient wonders — ascending terraces of lush gardens irrigated by an elaborate system of screws or chain pumps, attributed to King Nebuchadnezzar II for his homesick wife. No definitive archaeological evidence has been found; some scholars believe they were actually in Nineveh. Their existence remains debated.

Statue of Zeus at Olympia
Olympia, Greece
A 12-m seated figure of Zeus crafted by Phidias (the sculptor behind the Parthenon's Athena) from ivory and gold-plated bronze. It filled the interior of the Temple of Zeus so completely that Strabo wrote if Zeus stood up, he would unroof the temple. Destroyed by fire in the 5th century AD. The workshop where it was built has been excavated.
📅 c. 435 BC📐 12 m tall🔥 Destroyed 5th c. AD
Wikipedia UNESCO
🏛️
Temple of Artemis at Ephesus
Selçuk, Turkey
A Greek temple dedicated to Artemis, rebuilt three times over 600 years. The final version (c. 323 BC) was four times the size of the Parthenon — 127 Ionic columns, each 18 m tall, on a platform 137 m long. Antipater of Sidon ranked it above every other wonder. Destroyed by Goths in 262 AD; a single re-erected column marks the site today.
📅 c. 550 BC (first build)📐 137 × 69 m🔥 Destroyed 262 AD

Mausoleum at Halicarnassus
Bodrum, Turkey
A tomb built for Mausolus, a satrap of the Persian Empire, by his grieving wife Artemisia II. Rising 45 m in three tiers — an Ionic colonnade atop a massive base, crowned by a pyramid and a chariot sculpture — it was so magnificent that "mausoleum" became the word for any grand tomb. Destroyed by earthquakes; fragments are in the British Museum.
📅 c. 353 BC📐 45 m tall💬 Origin of "mausoleum"

🗽
Colossus of Rhodes
Rhodes, Greece
A 33-m bronze statue of the sun god Helios erected to celebrate Rhodes' victory over Cyprus in 280 BC. One of the tallest statues of the ancient world, it stood for only 54 years before an earthquake snapped it at the knees. The ruins lay on the ground for over 800 years and were still considered a marvel by visitors.
📅 c. 280 BC📐 33 m tall💥 Earthquake 226 BC

🔦
Lighthouse of Alexandria
Alexandria, Egypt
A tower between 100 and 140 m tall on the island of Pharos — the first lighthouse in history and one of the tallest man-made structures for centuries. Its fire, amplified by a bronze mirror, was visible 50 km out to sea. Three earthquakes between 956 and 1323 AD progressively destroyed it. The Citadel of Qaitbay now stands on its foundation.
📅 c. 280 BC📐 100–140 m tall💥 Earthquakes 956–1323 AD

🏟️
Colosseum
Rome, Italy
The largest amphitheatre ever built — a 50,000-seat arena where gladiators, wild animals, and mock naval battles entertained Rome for nearly 500 years. Completed in 80 AD under Emperor Titus, its innovative system of 80 entrance arches could fill or empty the crowd in minutes. The hypogeum (underground network of tunnels and cages) is now open to visitors.
📅 Completed 80 AD📐 189 × 156 m🏛️ UNESCO

Great Wall of China
Northern China
A series of fortifications stretching over 21,000 km across northern China — built over 2,000 years by successive dynasties to protect against northern invasions. The most visited and best-preserved sections date from the Ming Dynasty (1368–1644). Watchtowers every few hundred metres served as signal stations using smoke and fire.
📅 7th century BC – 17th century AD📐 21,196 km total🏛️ UNESCO

Taj Mahal
Agra, India
A white marble mausoleum built by Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan for his wife Mumtaz Mahal, who died during childbirth. Twenty thousand artisans worked for 22 years, inlaying 28 types of precious and semi-precious stones into the marble in floral patterns. The dome rises 73 m, and the reflecting pools double its silhouette at dawn and dusk.
📅 Built 1632–1653📐 73 m dome height🏛️ UNESCO

Machu Picchu
Cusco Region, Peru
A 15th-century Inca citadel perched at 2,430 m on a narrow ridge between two peaks in the Andes. Built under Emperor Pachacuti around 1450 and abandoned a century later during the Spanish Conquest, it remained unknown to the outside world until Hiram Bingham's expedition in 1911. Over 150 buildings connected by 3,000 stone steps.
📅 Built c. 1450⛰️ 2,430 m altitude🏛️ UNESCO

Petra
Ma'an Governorate, Jordan
A Nabataean city carved directly into rose-red sandstone cliffs over 2,000 years ago. The approach through the Siq — a narrow 1.2-km gorge — reveals the Treasury (Al-Khazneh), a 40-m facade carved into the rock face. At its peak, Petra was home to 30,000 people and controlled the incense trade routes. Rediscovered by Swiss explorer Johann Ludwig Burckhardt in 1812.
📅 c. 312 BC📐 264 km² archaeological park🏛️ UNESCO

Christ the Redeemer
Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
A 30-m Art Deco statue of Jesus Christ atop Corcovado mountain at 710 m, arms outstretched 28 m wide over Rio de Janeiro. Designed by French sculptor Paul Landowski and built by Brazilian engineer Heitor da Silva Costa from reinforced concrete and soapstone. Completed in 1931 after nine years of construction. Struck by lightning regularly; the tips of the fingers and head have been replaced.
📅 Completed 1931📐 30 m tall + 8 m pedestal⛰️ 710 m elevation

Chichén Itzá
Yucatán, Mexico
A pre-Columbian Maya city dominated by the Temple of Kukulcán (El Castillo) — a 30-m stepped pyramid with 365 steps (one for each day of the year). During the spring and autumn equinoxes, the setting sun casts triangular shadows down the northern staircase that resemble a serpent descending. At its height (600–1200 AD) it was one of the largest Maya cities.
📅 c. 600–1200 AD📐 30 m pyramid🏛️ UNESCO

Honorary Wonders
🏛️
Parthenon
Athens, Greece
A Doric temple on the Athenian Acropolis dedicated to Athena, the city's patron goddess. Completed in 432 BC under Pericles, it is considered the pinnacle of Classical Greek architecture. Its 46 outer columns employ subtle curvature — no straight lines — to correct optical illusions. Once held a 12-m chryselephantine (gold and ivory) statue of Athena by Phidias.
📅 Completed 432 BC📐 69.5 × 30.9 m🏛️ UNESCO

Statue of Liberty
New York City, United States
A 46-m copper statue on Liberty Island — a gift from France to the United States, designed by Frédéric Auguste Bartholdi with an iron framework by Gustave Eiffel. Dedicated in 1886, it depicts the Roman goddess Libertas holding a torch and a tablet inscribed with the date of American independence. The crown's seven rays represent the seven continents and oceans.

Hagia Sophia
Istanbul, Turkey
A 6th-century cathedral that became a mosque, then a museum, then a mosque again — the architectural bridge between the Roman and Ottoman empires. Emperor Justinian I completed it in 537 AD with a dome 31 m in diameter that appeared to float on a ring of windows. For nearly 1,000 years it was the largest enclosed space in the world.
📅 Completed 537 AD📐 31 m dome diameter🏛️ UNESCO

Eiffel Tower
Paris, France
A 330-m wrought-iron lattice tower built as the entrance arch for the 1889 World's Fair — intended to stand for only 20 years. Gustave Eiffel's firm used 18,038 pieces of iron joined by 2.5 million rivets. Widely criticized as an eyesore during construction, it became the world's most-visited paid monument. Repainted every seven years using 60 tonnes of paint in three graduated shades.
📅 Completed 1889📐 330 m tall👥 7 M visitors/year

Angkor Wat
Siem Reap, Cambodia
The largest religious monument in the world — a 162.6-hectare temple complex built by King Suryavarman II as a Hindu temple dedicated to Vishnu, later converted to Buddhism. Its five lotus-bud towers symbolize Mount Meru, the home of the gods. The bas-relief gallery stretching 800 m depicts scenes from Hindu mythology and historical Khmer battles. Appears on Cambodia's national flag.
📅 Built 1113–1150📐 162.6 hectares🏛️ UNESCO

Stonehenge
Wiltshire, England
A prehistoric ring of standing stones erected in stages over 1,500 years on Salisbury Plain. The largest sarsen stones weigh 25 tonnes; the bluestones were transported 250 km from Wales by methods still debated. Aligned with the sunrise on the summer solstice and sunset on the winter solstice. Its purpose — temple, astronomical observatory, healing site — remains one of archaeology's great mysteries.
📅 c. 3000–1500 BC📐 25-tonne stones🏛️ UNESCO

New7Wonders of Nature (2011)
🌊
Iguazu Falls
Argentina / Brazil
A system of 275 cascades stretching nearly 3 km along the Iguazu River, straddling the Argentine-Brazilian border. The Devil's Throat — an 80 m-tall, U-shaped chasm where 14 falls converge — produces a permanent cloud of spray visible 30 km away. Greater in width than Niagara and taller than Victoria Falls, the system discharges 1,500 m³/s on average. Set inside subtropical Atlantic Forest teeming with toucans, coatis, and jaguars.

Ha Long Bay
Quang Ninh, Vietnam
Nearly 2,000 limestone karst islands and islets rising from emerald waters in the Gulf of Tonkin. Shaped by 500 million years of tectonic activity and erosion, the pillars are riddled with caves, grottoes, and hidden lagoons. Floating fishing villages have existed here for centuries. The name translates to "descending dragon" — legend says a dragon family spat jewels that turned into the islands to defend against invaders.
📐 1,969 islands🌊 1,553 km²🏛️ UNESCO

Jeju Island
South Korea
A volcanic island 130 km off the southern coast of Korea, formed by eruptions over 2 million years. Hallasan — South Korea's tallest peak at 1,950 m — anchors the island, surrounded by 360 parasitic cones called oreum. The Geomunoreum lava tube system, stretching 13 km, is among the finest on Earth. Jeju's haenyeo (sea women) free-dive without oxygen tanks to harvest seafood — a tradition spanning centuries, now a UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage.

Puerto Princesa Underground River
Palawan, Philippines
An 8.2 km navigable underground river flowing directly into the South China Sea — one of the longest in the world. The cave system features massive cathedral-like chambers up to 120 m wide and 60 m high, filled with stalactites, stalagmites, and rock formations shaped over 20 million years. The lower 4 km are tidally influenced, creating a rare mix of freshwater and saltwater ecosystems. Bats, swiftlets, and monitor lizards inhabit the cave and its surrounding old-growth forest.

Table Mountain
Cape Town, South Africa
A flat-topped sandstone massif rising 1,085 m above sea level, forming the backdrop to Cape Town. At roughly 600 million years old, its rocks predate complex life on Earth. The summit plateau stretches 3 km and is frequently covered by a thin layer of orographic cloud known as the "tablecloth." Home to over 1,470 plant species — more than the entire United Kingdom — many found nowhere else on Earth. A rotating cable car has carried visitors to the top since 1929.
📐 1,085 m🌿 1,470+ plant species🏛️ UNESCO (Cape Floral Region)

Komodo Island
East Nusa Tenggara, Indonesia
A rugged volcanic island in the Lesser Sunda chain, home to the Komodo dragon — the world's largest living lizard, reaching 3 m in length and 70 kg. Roughly 3,000 dragons roam Komodo and neighboring Rinca and Flores, hunting deer, water buffalo, and occasionally each other. The surrounding waters hold some of the richest marine biodiversity on Earth, with manta ray cleaning stations, pristine coral walls, and strong currents that attract pelagic species. The national park spans 1,733 km² of land and sea.
🦎 ~3,000 dragons📐 1,733 km² park🏛️ UNESCO

Amazon Rainforest
South America (9 countries)
The largest tropical rainforest on Earth — 5.5 million km² across nine countries, with roughly 60% in Brazil. The Amazon River discharges more water than the next seven largest rivers combined, accounting for about 20% of all freshwater entering the oceans. The forest contains an estimated 390 billion individual trees belonging to 16,000 species, and one in ten known species on Earth lives here. It produces roughly 6% of the world's oxygen and stores 150–200 billion tonnes of carbon.

Seven Natural Wonders of the World
🏜️
Grand Canyon
Arizona, United States
A 446 km-long gorge carved by the Colorado River over 5–6 million years, exposing nearly 2 billion years of geological history in its layered red rock walls. At its deepest the canyon drops 1,857 m; at its widest it stretches 29 km. The South Rim stands at 2,134 m elevation. The canyon's ecosystems range from desert scrub at the bottom to spruce-fir forest on the North Rim. Over 1,000 caves have been identified, with fewer than 350 surveyed.
📐 446 km long⬇️ 1,857 m deep🏛️ UNESCO

Great Barrier Reef
Queensland, Australia
The world's largest coral reef system — 2,300 km of interconnected reefs and 900 islands stretching along Australia's northeast coast. Composed of nearly 3,000 individual reef systems built by billions of tiny coral polyps over 500,000 years. Home to 1,500 fish species, 400 coral species, 4,000 mollusc species, and six of the world's seven marine turtle species. Visible from space. The reef faces significant bleaching events due to rising ocean temperatures.
📐 2,300 km🐠 1,500 fish species🏛️ UNESCO

Victoria Falls
Zambia / Zimbabwe
A 1,708 m-wide curtain of water plunging 108 m into the Batoka Gorge on the Zambezi River — the largest sheet of falling water on Earth by combined width and height. Known locally as Mosi-oa-Tunya ("The Smoke That Thunders"), the spray column rises 400 m and is visible 50 km away. During peak flood (February–May), 500 million litres per minute pour over the basalt lip. The falls form the border between Zambia and Zimbabwe, with viewing points on both sides.
📐 1,708 m wide⬇️ 108 m drop🏛️ UNESCO

🏔️
Mount Everest
Nepal / China (Tibet)
Earth's highest peak at 8,849 m above sea level, sitting on the border of Nepal and Tibet in the Himalayas. Formed by the collision of the Indian and Eurasian tectonic plates roughly 50 million years ago, the summit is marine limestone — the ocean floor pushed to the roof of the world. First summited by Tenzing Norgay and Edmund Hillary on 29 May 1953. Above 8,000 m lies the "death zone," where oxygen levels are one-third of sea level. Over 6,000 people have reached the summit; more than 300 have died trying.
📐 8,849 m🧗 First summit 1953🌍 Nepal / China border

Aurora Borealis
Arctic & subarctic regions
Curtains and arcs of green, pink, violet, and red light that dance across polar skies when charged particles from the solar wind collide with atmospheric gases along Earth's magnetic field lines. Oxygen produces green and red; nitrogen yields blue and purple. Visible within the "auroral oval" centered on the magnetic poles, most reliably between 65° and 72° latitude. Solar maximum cycles (roughly every 11 years) intensify displays, pushing visibility as far south as the northern United States. Best viewing: September–March, clear dark skies, minimal light pollution.

Paricutin
Michoacan, Mexico
A cinder cone volcano that erupted from a cornfield on 20 February 1943, growing to 336 m in its first year — the only volcano in recorded history whose birth and full lifecycle were witnessed by scientists from start to finish. Active for nine years until 1952, it buried the villages of Paricutin and San Juan Parangaricutiro under lava; the half-buried church tower of San Juan remains visible above the solidified flow. The final cone stands 424 m above the original field elevation.
📅 Born 1943📐 424 m cone🌋 Active 1943–1952

Harbor of Rio de Janeiro
Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
Guanabara Bay — a 412 km² natural harbor framed by granite peaks, tropical forest, and white sand beaches. Sugarloaf Mountain (396 m) and Corcovado (710 m, topped by Christ the Redeemer) define the entrance. Portuguese explorers who arrived on 1 January 1502 mistook the bay for a river mouth, naming it "Rio de Janeiro" (January River). The bay is studded with 130 islands and ringed by the city's iconic shoreline — Copacabana, Ipanema, and the forested slopes of Tijuca National Park.
