Best Architecture

Ancient & Classical
🏛️
Parthenon
Athens, Greece · Doric Temple
Temple dedicated to Athena on the Acropolis, built entirely of Pentelic marble. The 46 outer columns incorporate subtle optical refinements — a slight convex curvature in the stylobate and inward-leaning columns — to counteract visual distortion at this scale. The most influential building in Western architecture.
📐 Ictinus & Callicrates📅 Completed 432 BC
Wikipedia Athens Guide
🏟️
Colosseum
Rome, Italy · Roman Amphitheater
Largest amphitheater ever built, seating 50,000–80,000 spectators across four stories of arched travertine. Its system of 80 entrance arches allowed full evacuation in minutes. The elliptical plan and tiered seating set the template for every stadium built since.
📅 Completed 80 AD👥 ~7M visitors/year
Wikipedia Rome Guide
🏛️
Pantheon
Rome, Italy · Roman Concrete Dome
Unreinforced concrete dome spanning 43.3 meters — the largest of its kind for over 1,300 years. The oculus at the apex (8.7 m diameter) is the sole light source. The interior height equals the diameter, inscribing a perfect sphere within the rotunda.
📅 Completed ~125 AD👥 ~9M visitors/year
Wikipedia Rome Guide
🕌
Hagia Sophia
Istanbul, Turkey · Byzantine Basilica
Byzantine cathedral with a 31-meter dome that appears to float on a ring of 40 arched windows. Held the record as the world's largest enclosed space for nearly a thousand years. The pendentive construction — transitioning from a square base to a circular dome — was an engineering breakthrough that influenced all domed architecture that followed.
📐 Anthemius of Tralles & Isidore of Miletus📅 Completed 537 AD
Wikipedia Istanbul Guide
⛩️
Hōryū-ji
Nara, Japan · Buddhist Temple Complex
The oldest surviving wooden structure in the world. The five-story pagoda and main hall use an interlocking bracket system with no nails, allowing flexibility during earthquakes. The complex demonstrates Chinese Tang Dynasty architectural principles adapted to Japanese timber construction.
📅 Completed ~607 AD
Wikipedia Tokyo Guide
🏛️
Great Pyramid of Giza
Giza, Egypt · Ancient Monument
Built from approximately 2.3 million limestone blocks averaging 2.5 tons each, the pyramid stood 146.6 meters tall and remained the world's tallest structure for over 3,800 years. Its base is level to within 2.1 centimeters across 230 meters — a precision unmatched until the modern era.
📅 Completed ~2560 BC
Wikipedia Cairo Guide
🏯
Angkor Wat
Siem Reap, Cambodia · Khmer Temple
The largest religious monument ever built, covering 162.6 hectares. The central quincunx of towers represents Mount Meru. The temple is oriented to the west (unusual for Khmer temples) and its walls carry the longest continuous bas-relief in the world at nearly 600 meters.
📐 Suryavarman II📅 Completed ~1150 AD
Wikipedia
Gothic & Medieval
⛪
Notre-Dame de Paris
Paris, France · French Gothic
Among the first buildings to use flying buttresses, which allowed thinner walls and larger stained-glass windows than any previous structure. The cathedral's twin-tower west facade and three rose windows (the north rose at 13.1 meters diameter) set the standard for Gothic cathedral design across Europe. Reopened in December 2024 after a five-year restoration following the 2019 fire.
📅 Built 1163–1345👥 ~12M visitors/year (pre-fire)
Wikipedia Paris Guide
⛪
Chartres Cathedral
Chartres, France · High Gothic
Contains 176 stained-glass windows — the largest surviving collection of medieval stained glass, including the iconic "Chartres blue" windows from the 12th and 13th centuries. The nave vault reaches 37 meters, and the mismatched spires (one Romanesque, one Flamboyant Gothic) record two distinct building campaigns.
📅 Built 1194–1220
Wikipedia
⛪
Cologne Cathedral
Cologne, Germany · High Gothic
Twin spires rise 157 meters — the tallest twin-spired church in the world. Construction began in 1248 but halted in 1473; work resumed in 1842 using the original medieval plans, and the building was completed in 1880. The facade is the largest of any church, at over 7,000 square meters.
📅 Built 1248–1880👥 ~6M visitors/year
Wikipedia
⛪
Duomo di Milano
Milan, Italy · Late Gothic
The largest church in Italy and third-largest in the world, built entirely in Candoglia marble. The exterior carries 3,400 statues and 135 spires. Took nearly six centuries to complete (1386–1965). The rooftop terraces allow visitors to walk among the forest of Gothic pinnacles.
📅 Built 1386–1965👥 ~6M visitors/year
Wikipedia Milan Guide
⛪
Westminster Abbey
London, United Kingdom · English Gothic
Coronation church of the English and British monarchs since 1066. The nave is the tallest Gothic nave in England at 31 meters. Henry VII's Lady Chapel (1503–1519) features one of the finest fan-vaulted ceilings ever built, with elaborate pendant bosses that appear to hang unsupported.
📅 Built 1245–1517👥 ~2.5M visitors/year
Wikipedia London Guide
⛪
Sainte-Chapelle
Paris, France · Rayonnant Gothic
Royal chapel built to house Christ's Crown of Thorns. The upper chapel is essentially a glass box — 15 stained-glass windows rising 15 meters with minimal stone support between them. Considered the highest achievement of Rayonnant Gothic, where structural walls are almost entirely replaced by glass.
📅 Completed 1248
Wikipedia Paris Guide
🕌
Alhambra
Granada, Spain · Moorish / Nasrid
Fortified palace complex of the Nasrid dynasty, built on a plateau overlooking Granada. The Court of the Lions features 124 marble columns and a muqarnas (honeycomb) ceiling in the Hall of the Abencerrajes with over 5,000 individual cells. The intricate stucco carvings and geometric tilework represent the peak of Islamic architecture in Europe.
📅 Built 1238–1358👥 ~2.7M visitors/year
Wikipedia
Renaissance & Baroque
⛪
Florence Cathedral (Brunelleschi's Dome)
Florence, Italy · Early Renaissance
Filippo Brunelleschi's dome (1420–1436) spans 45 meters without centering or buttresses — an engineering feat thought impossible at the time. He invented a double-shell construction and herringbone brick pattern that made the dome self-supporting during construction. It remains the largest masonry dome ever built.
📐 Filippo Brunelleschi📅 Dome completed 1436
Wikipedia Florence Guide
⛪
St. Peter's Basilica
Vatican City · Renaissance / Baroque
The largest church in the world by interior area (15,160 square meters). Michelangelo redesigned the dome, which rises 136 meters from floor to cross. Bernini's elliptical colonnade (284 columns) wraps the piazza. The building took 120 years and the work of Bramante, Raphael, Michelangelo, Maderno, and Bernini.
📐 Bramante, Michelangelo, Bernini📅 Built 1506–1626
Wikipedia Rome Guide
🏰
Palace of Versailles
Versailles, France · French Baroque
Louis XIV transformed a hunting lodge into a 700-room palace with 2,300 rooms and 67 hectares of formal gardens. The Hall of Mirrors (73 meters long, 357 mirrors) is the defining interior of French Baroque. The palace set the model for royal residences across Europe for the next century.
📐 Louis Le Vau, Jules Hardouin-Mansart📅 Expanded 1661–1710
Wikipedia
🕌
Taj Mahal
Agra, India · Mughal
Mausoleum built in white Makrana marble by Shah Jahan for his wife Mumtaz Mahal. The central dome rises 73 meters, flanked by four minarets that lean slightly outward to protect the tomb in case of collapse. The inlaid pietra dura work uses 28 types of precious and semi-precious stones from across Asia. Took 22 years and 20,000 artisans to complete.
📐 Ustad Ahmad Lahori📅 Completed 1653
Wikipedia
🏯
Himeji Castle
Himeji, Japan · Japanese Feudal
The finest surviving example of Japanese castle architecture, with 83 buildings and a complex network of defensive walls, gates, and baileys. The six-story main keep appears to have five stories from the outside and rises 46.4 meters. Its white plastered walls earned it the name "White Heron Castle." One of the first UNESCO World Heritage Sites in Japan.
📅 Current form completed 1609
Wikipedia
⛪
St. Basil's Cathedral
Moscow, Russia · Russian Orthodox
Nine chapels grouped around a central tower, each topped with a uniquely shaped and colored onion dome. Commissioned by Ivan the Terrible to commemorate the capture of Kazan. The seemingly chaotic exterior follows a precise geometric plan — eight chapels arranged symmetrically around the ninth central tower.
📐 Postnik Yakovlev📅 Completed 1561
Wikipedia
🏛️
Villa Rotonda
Vicenza, Italy · Palladian
Andrea Palladio's symmetrical villa with identical temple-front porticoes on all four facades — the first domestic building designed this way. The centralized plan with a domed central hall influenced centuries of residential architecture, from Thomas Jefferson's Monticello to the White House.
📐 Andrea Palladio📅 Completed 1592
Wikipedia
Art Nouveau & Art Deco
⛪
Sagrada Família
Barcelona, Spain · Art Nouveau / Organic
Antoni Gaudí's unfinished basilica, under construction since 1882. The interior columns branch like trees to distribute structural loads without flying buttresses. The 18 planned towers represent the twelve Apostles, four Evangelists, the Virgin Mary, and Jesus Christ. Scheduled for completion in the 2030s — 150 years after construction began.
📐 Antoni Gaudí📅 Under construction since 1882
Wikipedia Barcelona Guide
🏠
Casa Batlló
Barcelona, Spain · Art Nouveau
Gaudí's renovation of an existing building on Passeig de Gràcia. The facade uses broken ceramic tiles (trencadís) and bone-shaped columns, with a roof ridge resembling a dragon's spine. No straight lines or right angles anywhere in the structure — every surface curves, undulates, or spirals.
📐 Antoni Gaudí📅 Completed 1906
Wikipedia Barcelona Guide
🎵
Palau de la Música Catalana
Barcelona, Spain · Catalan Modernisme
Concert hall by Lluís Domènech i Montaner, built almost entirely of glass and steel frame with ceramic-clad structure. The centerpiece is an inverted stained-glass dome skylight that floods the hall with natural light. The only concert hall in Europe lit entirely by natural light during the day.
📐 Lluís Domènech i Montaner📅 Completed 1908
Wikipedia Barcelona Guide
🏙️
Chrysler Building
New York, United States · Art Deco
319-meter skyscraper with a stainless-steel crown of seven radiating arches and triangular windows. Briefly the world's tallest building (1930). The lobby features African marble walls and a 29.7 x 33.5 meter ceiling mural by Edward Trumbull. Widely considered the finest Art Deco skyscraper ever built.
📐 William Van Alen📅 Completed 1930
Wikipedia New York Guide
🏙️
Empire State Building
New York, United States · Art Deco
443-meter tower (including antenna) built in just 410 days — an average of four-and-a-half floors per week. Its steel frame was revolutionary for its speed of construction. The stepped-back massing (driven by New York's 1916 Zoning Resolution) created the iconic setback silhouette that defined the Manhattan skyline for decades.
📐 Shreve, Lamb & Harmon📅 Completed 1931👥 ~2.5M visitors/year
Wikipedia New York Guide
🏠
Casa Milà (La Pedrera)
Barcelona, Spain · Art Nouveau
Gaudí's last secular work before devoting himself entirely to the Sagrada Família. A self-supporting stone facade with no load-bearing walls — among the first buildings to use a free-plan open floor. The rooftop terrace features surreal warrior-shaped chimneys and ventilation towers clad in broken tiles.
📐 Antoni Gaudí📅 Completed 1912
Wikipedia Barcelona Guide
🏛️
Flatiron Building
New York, United States · Beaux-Arts
22-story triangular tower built on a wedge-shaped lot where Broadway crosses Fifth Avenue. The steel-frame structure tapers to just 2 meters at its north point. One of New York's earliest skyscrapers, its unusual footprint and ornate terra-cotta facade made it an instant icon when it opened.
📐 Daniel Burnham📅 Completed 1902
Wikipedia New York Guide
Modern (1920–1980)
🏠
Fallingwater
Mill Run, Pennsylvania, United States · Organic Modern
Frank Lloyd Wright's house built over a waterfall using cantilevered reinforced-concrete balconies. The horizontal planes extend directly over Bear Run creek, integrating the building into the rock ledges. Named the "best all-time work of American architecture" by the American Institute of Architects in 1991.
📐 Frank Lloyd Wright📅 Completed 1939
Wikipedia
🎭
Sydney Opera House
Sydney, Australia · Expressionist Modern
Jørn Utzon's interlocking shell roofs sit on Bennelong Point in Sydney Harbour. The shells are all cut from a single sphere of 75-meter radius, which allowed prefabrication of the 1,056,000+ Swedish-made tiles. The project took 16 years and went 1,357% over budget, but became Australia's most recognizable building.
📐 Jørn Utzon📅 Completed 1973👥 ~10.9M visitors/year
Wikipedia Sydney Guide
🏛️
Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum
New York, United States · Organic Modern
Frank Lloyd Wright's inverted ziggurat on Fifth Avenue. Visitors take an elevator to the top and walk down a continuous spiral ramp — 400 meters of gallery space with no rooms or corridors. The reinforced-concrete shell was Wright's only major New York building, completed six months after his death.
📐 Frank Lloyd Wright📅 Completed 1959
Wikipedia New York Guide
🔲
Centre Pompidou
Paris, France · High-Tech
Renzo Piano and Richard Rogers turned the building inside out — all structural elements, mechanical systems, and circulation are on the exterior. Color-coded pipes (blue for air, green for water, yellow for electricity) and external escalator tubes in glass free the interior for uninterrupted gallery space.
📐 Renzo Piano & Richard Rogers📅 Completed 1977👥 ~3M visitors/year
Wikipedia Paris Guide
🏠
Farnsworth House
Plano, Illinois, United States · International Style
Mies van der Rohe's glass pavilion — a single room elevated 1.5 meters above a floodplain on eight steel columns. Floor-to-ceiling glass on all four sides dissolves the boundary between interior and landscape. Considered the purest expression of the International Style's "less is more" philosophy.
📐 Ludwig Mies van der Rohe📅 Completed 1951
Wikipedia
⛪
Cathedral of Brasília
Brasília, Brazil · Modernist
Oscar Niemeyer's hyperboloid structure made of 16 concrete columns weighing 90 tons each, curving together and apart to form a crown shape. The congregation enters through a dark tunnel and emerges into a light-filled interior under a stained-glass ceiling — a deliberate sequence from darkness to illumination.
📐 Oscar Niemeyer📅 Completed 1970
Wikipedia
✈️
TWA Flight Center
New York, United States · Neo-Futurist
Eero Saarinen's terminal at JFK Airport features a thin-shell concrete roof in the shape of a bird in flight — four intersecting vaults supported by only four Y-shaped columns. Every interior surface curves: counters, railings, and even the departure boards. Now a hotel preserving the original 1962 interiors.
📐 Eero Saarinen📅 Completed 1962
Wikipedia New York Guide
🏛️
National Congress of Brazil
Brasília, Brazil · Modernist
Oscar Niemeyer's twin towers flanked by two domes — one convex (Senate) and one concave (Chamber of Deputies) — sitting on a vast horizontal platform. The composition is the centerpiece of Brasília's Monumental Axis, a planned capital city designed entirely from scratch by Niemeyer and urban planner Lúcio Costa.
📐 Oscar Niemeyer📅 Completed 1960
Wikipedia
🏛️
Salk Institute
La Jolla, California, United States · Brutalist
Louis Kahn's research campus for Jonas Salk, built in poured-in-place concrete and teak. Two symmetrical laboratory wings frame a travertine courtyard with a central water channel aligned to the setting sun on the equinox. The interplay of raw concrete, water, and unobstructed sky is among the most photographed architectural compositions in America.
📐 Louis Kahn📅 Completed 1965
Wikipedia La Jolla Guide
Contemporary (1980–present)
🌀
Guggenheim Museum Bilbao
Bilbao, Spain · Deconstructivist
Frank Gehry's titanium-clad museum transformed a declining industrial port city into a global cultural destination — a phenomenon now called the "Bilbao effect." The curving, ship-like forms were among the first buildings designed with CATIA aerospace software. The 32,500 titanium panels are each unique and only 0.38 mm thick.
📐 Frank Gehry📅 Completed 1997👥 ~1.2M visitors/year
Wikipedia
🏙️
Burj Khalifa
Dubai, United Arab Emirates · Neo-Futurist
The tallest structure ever built at 828 meters (163 floors). The Y-shaped floor plan maximizes views while the setback profile reduces wind loads — the tower changes cross-section 26 times as it rises. The exterior cladding system of 26,000 glass panels was designed to withstand Dubai's extreme summer temperatures.
📐 Adrian Smith (SOM)📅 Completed 2010👥 ~1.9M visitors/year
Wikipedia Dubai Guide
🏟️
Beijing National Stadium (Bird's Nest)
Beijing, China · Parametric
Built for the 2008 Olympics, the stadium's lattice of interwoven steel beams (42,000 tons of steel) forms a self-supporting structure with no internal columns. The "bird's nest" appearance comes from the random-looking but precisely engineered criss-crossing of steel members that conceal the retractable roof supports.
📐 Herzog & de Meuron with Ai Weiwei📅 Completed 2008
Wikipedia Beijing Guide
🏛️
Louvre Abu Dhabi
Abu Dhabi, United Arab Emirates · Contemporary
Jean Nouvel's museum sits on Saadiyat Island under a 180-meter-diameter dome — a lattice of eight layers of steel and aluminum stars that filters sunlight into a "rain of light" pattern inspired by overlapping palm fronds. The dome weighs 7,500 tons (more than the Eiffel Tower) and appears to float above the 55 white buildings beneath it.
📐 Jean Nouvel📅 Completed 2017
Wikipedia
📺
CCTV Headquarters
Beijing, China · Deconstructivist
Rem Koolhaas's 234-meter tower forms a continuous loop — two leaning towers joined at the top and bottom by angular bridges, creating a three-dimensional cranked form. The diagonal bracing pattern on the facade makes the structural forces visible. The cantilevered overhang extends 75 meters with no support below.
📐 Rem Koolhaas / OMA📅 Completed 2012
Wikipedia Beijing Guide
🌊
Fondation Louis Vuitton
Paris, France · Deconstructivist
Frank Gehry's art museum in the Bois de Boulogne uses twelve glass "sails" mounted on a reinforced-concrete "iceberg" base. The 3,600 glass panels are each unique in shape and curvature. The building was designed to evoke a sailing ship and changes appearance dramatically with the light and weather.
📐 Frank Gehry📅 Completed 2014
Wikipedia Paris Guide
🏙️
The Shard
London, United Kingdom · Neo-Futurist
Renzo Piano's 310-meter glass spire is the tallest building in Western Europe. The tapered form — eight sloping glass facades that don't meet at the top, leaving open gaps — was designed to reflect the London sky and disappear into it. The irregular glass planes fracture light differently depending on the weather and time of day.
📐 Renzo Piano📅 Completed 2012
Wikipedia London Guide
🏛️
Heydar Aliyev Center
Baku, Azerbaijan · Parametric
Zaha Hadid's cultural center uses a continuous, flowing surface that folds from the ground into the building's walls and roof with no sharp transitions. The fluid form required a space-frame structure with over 12,000 unique panels. No two points on the exterior share the same curvature — every section is geometrically distinct.
📐 Zaha Hadid📅 Completed 2012
Wikipedia
🌿
Bosco Verticale
Milan, Italy · Green Architecture
Two residential towers (111 m and 76 m) with facades hosting 900 trees, 5,000 shrubs, and 11,000 ground cover plants — the equivalent of 2 hectares of forest distributed vertically. The vegetation filters dust particles, absorbs CO₂, and moderates temperatures, reducing energy consumption by up to 30%. Won the International Highrise Award in 2014.
📐 Stefano Boeri📅 Completed 2014
Wikipedia Milan Guide
🍎
Apple Park
Cupertino, California, United States · High-Tech
Norman Foster's ring-shaped campus spans 260,000 square meters and is clad in the largest panels of curved glass ever fabricated. The 1.6-kilometer circumference building encloses a 12-hectare courtyard with 9,000 drought-resistant trees. Powered entirely by renewable energy, with one of the largest on-site solar installations in the world.
📐 Foster + Partners📅 Completed 2017
Wikipedia
🌉
Marina Bay Sands
Singapore · Contemporary
Three 55-story towers support a 340-meter SkyPark at the top — a cantilevered platform extending 67 meters beyond the north tower. The 150-meter infinity pool on the 57th floor is the world's highest. The complex redefined Singapore's skyline and became the city-state's most photographed structure.
📐 Moshe Safdie📅 Completed 2010
Wikipedia Singapore Guide
🏛️
Museum of the Future
Dubai, United Arab Emirates · Parametric
Torus-shaped stainless-steel structure with a hollow center, clad in 1,024 fire-engineered composite panels. Arabic calligraphy windows — poetry by Sheikh Mohammed bin Rashid — are cut into the facade and serve as both decoration and natural light sources. The diagrid exoskeleton eliminates the need for internal columns.
📐 Killa Design (Shaun Killa)📅 Completed 2022
Wikipedia Dubai Guide
🏙️
Taipei 101
Taipei, Taiwan · Postmodern
508-meter tower designed to withstand typhoon winds and earthquakes using a 730-ton tuned mass damper — the largest in any building — suspended between the 87th and 92nd floors. The eight-story modules stacked like pagoda sections reference the number eight, which represents prosperity in Chinese culture. The world's tallest building from 2004 to 2010.
📐 C.Y. Lee📅 Completed 2004
Wikipedia Taipei Guide
🌀
Gardens by the Bay (Supertree Grove)
Singapore · Biophilic Architecture
Eighteen tree-shaped vertical gardens standing 25–50 meters tall, constructed from steel and reinforced concrete with living facades of over 162,900 plants. Eleven of the supertrees have solar photovoltaic systems that generate electricity for lighting. The 128-meter OCBC Skyway connects two of the tallest supertrees at the canopy level.
📐 Grant Associates & Wilkinson Eyre📅 Completed 2012
Wikipedia Singapore Guide
