United Kingdom
🌳
Royal Botanic Gardens, Kew
London, England
A UNESCO World Heritage Site spanning 132 hectares in southwest London. Home to the world's largest collection of living plants — over 50,000 species. The Victorian-era Palm House (1848) is an iron-and-glass cathedral filled with tropical rainforest plants. The Temperate House, reopened in 2018, is the world's largest surviving Victorian glasshouse. The Treetop Walkway rises 18 m above the forest canopy.
📐 132 hectares🌿 50,000+ species☀️ Year-round

Eden Project
Cornwall, England
Two massive geodesic biomes built inside a reclaimed china clay quarry. The Rainforest Biome — the world's largest indoor rainforest — reaches 55 m tall and 240 m long, holding tropical plants from Southeast Asia, West Africa, and South America. The Mediterranean Biome houses olive groves, citrus trees, and vineyards. The outdoor gardens cover 13 hectares of temperate plantings.
📐 13 hectares (outdoor)🌡️ Two climate biomes☀️ Year-round

🌹
Sissinghurst Castle Garden
Cranbrook, Kent, England
Created in the 1930s by writer Vita Sackville-West and diplomat Harold Nicolson around the ruins of an Elizabethan manor. The garden is divided into a series of outdoor rooms, each with its own colour scheme and planting theme. The White Garden — planted entirely in white, silver, and green — is among the most imitated garden designs in the world. The brick Tudor tower at the centre served as Sackville-West's writing room.
📐 5 hectares🌹 Famous White Garden☀️ Best Apr–Oct

🏛️
Stourhead
Wiltshire, England
An 18th-century landscape garden considered one of the finest examples of the English landscape movement. A walking circuit around a central lake passes classical temples, a grotto, a Gothic cottage, and a replica of the Pantheon — composed like a series of landscape paintings. Henry Hoare II designed the garden in the 1740s as a journey through classical mythology. The autumn foliage is among England's most photographed.
📐 1,072 hectares (estate)🕯️ Est. 1740s☀️ Best May–Oct

Royal Botanic Garden Edinburgh
Edinburgh, Scotland
Founded in 1670 as a physic garden, now spanning 28 hectares on Inverleith Row with views across to Edinburgh Castle. The Rock Garden, built in 1871, holds over 5,000 alpine plants from every mountain range on Earth. The Victorian-era glasshouses include a towering Tropical Palm House and ten interconnected climate zones. The Chinese Hillside is the largest collection of Chinese wild plants outside China.
📐 28 hectares🕯️ Est. 1670☀️ Year-round (free)

France
👑
Gardens of Versailles
Versailles, France
The defining masterpiece of French formal garden design, created by André Le Nôtre for Louis XIV beginning in 1661. The gardens cover 800 hectares with 300,000 trees, 210,000 flowers, and 55 fountains. The Grand Canal extends 1.6 km on a central axis. During the Grandes Eaux Musicales (Apr–Oct weekends), the fountains perform to Baroque music — the same spectacle Louis XIV staged for visiting dignitaries.
📐 800 hectares⛲ 55 fountains☀️ Best Apr–Oct
Official Site Paris Guide
🎨
Monet's Garden
Giverny, France
The house and garden where Claude Monet lived from 1883 until his death in 1926 — the living subject of his most famous paintings. The Clos Normand is a 1-hectare flower garden with arches of climbing roses, nasturtiums, and dahlias. The Water Garden, inspired by Japanese prints Monet collected, holds the Japanese bridge and water lily pond he painted obsessively for 30 years. The garden is planted to bloom continuously from April through October.
📐 2.5 hectares🎨 Water lily pond☀️ Apr–Oct only
Official Site Paris Guide
🌿
Jardin des Plantes
Paris, France
The main botanical garden of France, founded in 1626 as a royal medicinal herb garden. Twenty-three hectares in the 5th arrondissement with an alpine garden holding 2,000 mountain species, a rose garden, and the Grande Galerie de l'Évolution (natural history museum). The four 19th-century greenhouses range from tropical to Australian and Mexican desert environments. Free entry to the gardens.
📐 23 hectares🕯️ Est. 1626☀️ Year-round (free)
Official Site Paris Guide
Netherlands
🌷
Keukenhof
Lisse, Netherlands
The world's largest flower garden, open only eight weeks each spring (mid-March to mid-May). Seven million bulbs are planted by hand each autumn — tulips, hyacinths, daffodils, and muscari in sweeping colour blocks across 32 hectares of parkland. The garden changes completely every year with new designs and varieties. Indoor pavilions display hundreds of named tulip cultivars. Surrounded by commercial bulb fields that stretch to the horizon.
📐 32 hectares🌷 7 million bulbs☀️ Mid-Mar to mid-May only
Official Site Amsterdam Guide
Italy
⛲
Villa d'Este
Tivoli, Italy
A UNESCO World Heritage Renaissance garden built in 1560 by Cardinal Ippolito II d'Este on a steep hillside outside Rome. The terraced grounds hold over 500 fountains fed entirely by gravity from the Aniene River — no pumps. The Fountain of the Organ plays music using water pressure alone. The Avenue of the Hundred Fountains lines a 130-m walkway with mossy stone spouts. The garden set the standard for European water gardens for three centuries.
📐 4.5 hectares⛲ 500+ fountains☀️ Year-round
Official Site Rome Guide
🏛️
Boboli Gardens
Florence, Italy
A 16th-century Medici garden behind the Pitti Palace, covering 4.5 hectares of terraced hillside above Florence. The garden served as a model for many European royal gardens, including Versailles. A central axis rises from the amphitheatre (where early operas were staged) to a Neptune fountain and the Cavaliere Garden with panoramic city views. Ancient Roman and Renaissance sculptures line the gravel paths under a canopy of holm oaks and Italian cypresses.
📐 4.5 hectares🕯️ Est. 1550s☀️ Year-round
Official Site Florence Guide
🌊
Villa Rufolo & Villa Cimbrone
Ravello, Amalfi Coast, Italy
Two clifftop gardens perched 350 m above the Tyrrhenian Sea. Villa Rufolo (13th century) has Moorish-influenced terraces overlooking the coast — Wagner composed parts of Parsifal here in 1880, and summer concerts are held on a stage cantilevered over the cliff. Villa Cimbrone's Terrace of Infinity, lined with 18th-century marble busts, looks straight down the Amalfi coastline. Gore Vidal called it the most beautiful view in the world.
🏔️ 350 m above sea🎵 Wagner connection☀️ Year-round

🌴
Isola Bella Gardens
Lake Maggiore, Italy
A Baroque garden built on a small island in Lake Maggiore by the Borromeo family, beginning in 1632. Ten terraces rise in a pyramid shape from the lake, planted with citrus trees, camellias, and exotic species. White peacocks roam the grounds. The garden was designed to make the island resemble a ship, with the terraces as the stern and the palazzo as the bow. The microclimate supports plants that shouldn't survive this far north.
📐 Island garden🕯️ Est. 1632☀️ Mar–Oct

Spain
🕌
Generalife Gardens
Granada, Spain
The summer palace and gardens of the Nasrid sultans, adjacent to the Alhambra. Built in the 13th–14th centuries as a retreat, the garden centres on the Patio de la Acequia — a long reflecting pool flanked by arching water jets, flowerbeds, and colonnaded walkways. Water is central to every space, channelled through narrow stone rills, fountains, and cascading staircases. The oldest surviving Moorish garden in Spain and a UNESCO World Heritage Site.
🕯️ 13th–14th century💧 Moorish water garden☀️ Year-round

🌵
Real Alcázar Gardens
Seville, Spain
Palace gardens spanning a thousand years of design, from Moorish courtyards to Renaissance parterres. The Mercury Pool, flanked by grottos and galleries, dates to the 12th century. The Galera Garden holds orange and lemon groves. The hedge maze was laid out in the 18th century. Used as a filming location for Game of Thrones (the Water Gardens of Dorne). Peacocks wander the paths.
📐 7 hectares🕯️ 1,000+ years☀️ Year-round

Sanssouci Park
Potsdam, Germany
Frederick the Great's summer retreat, built from 1745, set in a 300-hectare UNESCO World Heritage park. Six vineyard terraces descend from the Rococo palace to a fountain basin. The park holds multiple palaces, a Chinese tea house, an Italian-style Roman bath complex, and the Orangery Palace modelled on Italian Renaissance villas. Avenues of plane trees radiate through manicured lawns and woodland.
📐 300 hectares🕯️ Est. 1745☀️ Year-round

🦌
Schönbrunn Palace Gardens
Vienna, Austria
The gardens of the Habsburg summer residence, redesigned in 1765 by Empress Maria Theresa. The Great Parterre stretches 300 m from the palace to the Neptune Fountain and up to the Gloriette — a triumphal arch on the hilltop offering a panoramic view over Vienna. The Privy Garden holds intricate Baroque parterres. The palm house (1882) is the largest glass structure in Europe after Kew. The oldest zoo in the world (1752) sits within the grounds.
📐 160 hectares🕯️ Est. 1765☀️ Year-round (free)

Morocco
💙
Jardin Majorelle
Marrakech, Morocco
Created by French painter Jacques Majorelle over 40 years beginning in 1923, later restored and owned by Yves Saint Laurent and Pierre Bergé. The garden is defined by its signature cobalt blue — Majorelle Blue — painted on walls, planters, and the Art Deco studio. Over 300 species of plants from five continents, including 15 species of bamboo, giant cacti, and towering Washingtonia palms. The Berber Museum and YSL Museum sit inside the grounds.
📐 1 hectare🎨 Majorelle Blue☀️ Year-round

Japan
🍁
Kenroku-en
Kanazawa, Japan
One of Japan's Three Great Gardens, developed over two centuries by the Maeda lords of Kaga Domain beginning in the 1620s. The name means "garden of six sublimities" — spaciousness, seclusion, artifice, antiquity, water, and panoramas. The Kotoji stone lantern on the edge of Kasumigaike Pond is one of the most photographed objects in Japan. In winter, rope supports (yukitsuri) are tied to tree branches in conical shapes to prevent snow damage — a visual signature of the garden.
📐 11.4 hectares🕯️ Est. 1620s☀️ Year-round
Official Site
🌸
Shinjuku Gyoen
Tokyo, Japan
A 58-hectare national garden in central Tokyo that blends three distinct styles: a French formal garden with rows of plane trees, an English landscape garden with broad lawns, and a traditional Japanese garden with ponds, stone lanterns, and wooden bridges. Originally the private estate of the Naitō feudal clan, then an imperial garden, opened to the public in 1949. One of Tokyo's premier cherry blossom viewing sites, with over 1,000 trees of 68 varieties.
📐 58 hectares🌸 1,000+ cherry trees☀️ Year-round
Official Site Tokyo Guide
🪨
Ryōan-ji
Kyoto, Japan
The most famous Zen rock garden in the world. Fifteen stones arranged in five groups on a rectangle of raked white gravel, 25 m by 10 m. The composition is designed so that from any seated viewing position on the veranda, only fourteen of the fifteen stones are visible — the fifteenth is always hidden. Built around 1500, the garden's meaning remains deliberately unexplained. The surrounding temple grounds hold a moss garden and a stone water basin inscribed with a Zen riddle.
📐 25 × 10 m (rock garden)🪨 15 stones on gravel☀️ Year-round
Official Site Kyoto Guide
🌿
Adachi Museum of Art Garden
Yasugi, Shimane, Japan
A 16,500 m² garden designed as a living painting, viewed through large windows that frame each scene like a scroll. Ranked the best Japanese garden in the world for over 20 consecutive years by the Journal of Japanese Gardening. Six distinct garden areas include a dry landscape garden, a moss garden, and a white gravel and pine garden. The mountains in the background are incorporated as borrowed scenery (shakkei). Visitors view the garden only through the museum — you cannot walk in it.
📐 16,500 m²🏆 #1 Japanese garden (21+ years)☀️ Year-round

China
🏯
Humble Administrator's Garden
Suzhou, China
The largest and most famous of Suzhou's classical gardens, first built in 1509 during the Ming Dynasty. A UNESCO World Heritage Site spanning 5.2 hectares, dominated by water — ponds, streams, and pools occupy nearly two-thirds of the garden. Zigzag bridges, lattice-windowed corridors, and moon gates frame carefully composed views of rockeries, lotus pools, and pavilions. Each window opening reveals a different picture-like scene as you walk.
📐 5.2 hectares🕯️ Est. 1509☀️ Year-round

🐉
Yu Garden (Yuyuan)
Shanghai, China
A Ming Dynasty classical garden in Shanghai's Old City, built in 1559 by Pan Yunduan for his father. Forty scenic points are compressed into 2 hectares using the Suzhou technique of borrowed scenery, winding paths, and strategically placed rockeries to make a small space feel vast. The Exquisite Jade Rock (Yù Línglóng) — a 3.3-m porous Taihu stone — is one of the most prized garden rocks in China. Dragon walls topped with undulating scaled dragons separate the garden's six sections.
📐 2 hectares🕯️ Est. 1559☀️ Year-round
Official Site Shanghai Guide
Southeast Asia
🌳
Gardens by the Bay
Singapore
A 101-hectare waterfront park that redefined what a botanical garden can be. Eighteen Supertrees — vertical gardens standing 25–50 m tall — are connected by an aerial walkway and perform a light-and-sound show nightly. The Cloud Forest conservatory holds the world's tallest indoor waterfall at 35 m, surrounded by a cloud mountain planted with orchids, pitcher plants, and ferns. The Flower Dome, the world's largest column-free glass greenhouse, replicates a Mediterranean and semi-arid climate.
📐 101 hectares🌳 18 Supertrees☀️ Year-round
Official Site Singapore Guide
🌺
Singapore Botanic Gardens
Singapore
Singapore's only UNESCO World Heritage Site, established in 1859. The 82-hectare garden is home to the National Orchid Garden — the world's largest orchid display with over 1,000 species and 2,000 hybrids across 3 hectares. The Rainforest, a 6-hectare tract of primary tropical forest, is older than the garden itself. The Evolution Garden traces 3.5 billion years of plant life. A free public park used daily by joggers, families, and wedding photographers.
📐 82 hectares🌺 1,000+ orchid species☀️ Year-round (free)
Official Site Singapore Guide
India
🕌
Shalimar Bagh
Srinagar, Kashmir, India
A Mughal garden built in 1619 by Emperor Jahangir on the eastern shore of Dal Lake. Three terraces descend toward the lake, each with a central water channel, stone pavilions, and 410 fountains. The highest terrace was reserved for the emperor and his court — public access was limited to the lowest. Chinar trees (oriental plane trees) planted in the 17th century still shade the garden. The water channels flow continuously from a mountain spring behind the garden.
📐 12.4 hectares🕯️ Est. 1619☀️ Best Apr–Oct

South Africa
🌿
Kirstenbosch National Botanical Garden
Cape Town, South Africa
Set against the eastern slopes of Table Mountain, Kirstenbosch is one of the great botanical gardens of the world and the first to be devoted entirely to indigenous flora. The 528-hectare estate (36 hectares cultivated) protects over 7,000 plant species from southern Africa's six biomes. The Centenary Tree Canopy Walkway (Boomslang) curves through and above the treetops. The fynbos collection — a biome found nowhere else on Earth — is a living catalogue of the Cape Floral Kingdom, a UNESCO site.
📐 528 hectares🌿 7,000+ species☀️ Year-round
Official Site
Canada
🌷
Butchart Gardens
Brentwood Bay, British Columbia, Canada
Created in 1904 by Jennie Butchart in a depleted limestone quarry on Vancouver Island. The Sunken Garden — the original quarry — is the centrepiece: 15 m deep, filled with flowering beds, meandering paths, and a lake. Five distinct gardens cover 22 hectares: the Sunken, Rose, Japanese, Italian, and Mediterranean. Over 900 bedding plant varieties are maintained, with seasonal replanting ensuring continuous bloom from March through October. Summer Saturday nights feature fireworks synchronised to music.
📐 22 hectares🌹 900+ varieties☀️ Year-round (best Mar–Oct)
Official Site Victoria Guide
🌿
Montreal Botanical Garden
Montreal, Canada
One of the world's largest botanical gardens, with 22,000 plant species across 75 hectares. Thirty thematic gardens include a Chinese Garden (the largest outside China, designed by architects from Shanghai), a Japanese Garden with a tea pavilion, and a First Nations Garden documenting indigenous uses of 300 plant species. The annual Gardens of Light festival (Sep–Oct) illuminates the Chinese and Japanese gardens with hundreds of handmade silk lanterns.
📐 75 hectares🌿 22,000 species☀️ Year-round
Official Site Montreal Guide
United States
⛲
Longwood Gardens
Kennett Square, Pennsylvania
A 436-hectare horticultural showplace created by industrialist Pierre S. du Pont beginning in 1906. The Main Fountain Garden holds 1,719 jets choreographed to music and coloured light — modelled on the fountains du Pont admired at Versailles and the Villa d'Este. The 1.6-hectare conservatory contains 20 indoor gardens, including an orchid house, a ballroom-sized Exhibition Hall rotated five times yearly, and a Children's Garden. The outdoor Meadow Garden covers 86 acres of native wildflower plantings.
📐 436 hectares⛲ 1,719 fountain jets☀️ Year-round
Official Site Philadelphia Guide
🌺
Huntington Library & Botanical Gardens
San Marino, California
A 49-hectare estate with 16 themed gardens built by railroad magnate Henry Huntington, opened to the public in 1928. The Desert Garden — one of the world's largest outdoor collections of cacti and succulents — holds over 5,000 species across 4 hectares. The Chinese Garden (Liu Fang Yuan, Garden of Flowing Fragrance) is the largest classical Chinese garden in the United States. The Japanese Garden, established in 1912, features a moon bridge, Zen court, and bonsai collection.
📐 49 hectares🌵 5,000+ desert species☀️ Year-round
Official Site Pasadena Guide
🌸
Brooklyn Botanic Garden
Brooklyn, New York
A 21-hectare urban garden founded in 1910 on a former ash dump. The Japanese Hill-and-Pond Garden (1915) was the first Japanese-inspired public garden in the United States. The Cranford Rose Garden holds over 1,000 rose varieties. The Cherry Esplanade is one of the finest cherry blossom viewing sites outside Japan, with 200 trees of 42 cultivars — the annual Sakura Matsuri festival draws 70,000 visitors in a weekend. The Steinhardt Conservatory holds desert, tropical, and warm-temperate pavilions.
📐 21 hectares🌸 200+ cherry trees☀️ Year-round
Official Site New York Guide
🍁
Portland Japanese Garden
Portland, Oregon
Widely considered the most authentic Japanese garden outside Japan, set on 5.5 hectares in Portland's West Hills above the city. Eight garden styles include a strolling pond garden, a sand and stone garden, a natural garden, and a flat garden viewed from a veranda. The Cultural Village, designed by Kengo Kuma, opened in 2017 with a tea café, gallery, and garden house. Former Japanese ambassador Nobuo Matsunaga called it "the most beautiful and authentic Japanese garden in the world outside of Japan."
📐 5.5 hectares🍁 8 garden styles☀️ Year-round

🌳
Chicago Botanic Garden
Glencoe, Illinois
A 156-hectare living museum on nine islands connected by bridges, managed by the Chicago Horticultural Society since 1972. Twenty-seven distinct gardens include a Japanese Garden on three islands, a sensory garden, an English walled garden, and a 6-hectare prairie restoration. The Evening Island holds a carillon of 48 bells. Over 2.7 million plants across 9,700 taxa. Free admission — one of the most visited public gardens in the United States.
📐 156 hectares🏝️ 9 islands☀️ Year-round (free)
Official Site Chicago Guide
South America
🌿
Jardim Botânico
Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
A 137-hectare garden at the foot of Corcovado mountain, founded in 1808 by King João VI of Portugal. The avenue of imperial palms — 134 royal palms planted in a double row for 750 m — is the garden's signature. Over 6,500 species, including the world's largest collection of bromeliads (1,700+ species). The garden preserves a remnant of the Atlantic Forest biome, one of the most endangered ecosystems on Earth, with toucans and marmosets visible in the canopy.
📐 137 hectares🕯️ Est. 1808☀️ Year-round
Official Site Rio Guide
🌵
Jardín Botánico de Bogotá
Bogotá, Colombia
The largest botanical garden in Colombia, covering 19.5 hectares at 2,600 m elevation. Specialises in high-altitude Andean and páramo ecosystems — the collection includes 5,000 species of flora from Colombia's five natural regions. The tropical greenhouse, cryptogamic garden (mosses and ferns), and the rose garden are the main sections. An important conservation centre for Colombia's cloud forest and alpine grassland species. Free admission.
📐 19.5 hectares🏔️ 2,600 m elevation☀️ Year-round (free)

Australia
🌳
Royal Botanic Garden Sydney
Sydney, Australia
A 30-hectare garden on the eastern shore of Sydney Harbour, established in 1816 — the oldest scientific institution in Australia. The harbourside path offers unobstructed views of the Opera House and Harbour Bridge. Collections include a rare and threatened species garden, a fern gully, and a palm grove with over 200 species. The Calyx glasshouse hosts rotating large-scale horticultural exhibitions. Flying foxes (fruit bats) roost in the fig trees by the thousands.
📐 30 hectares🕯️ Est. 1816☀️ Year-round (free)
Official Site Sydney Guide
🌿
Royal Botanic Gardens Melbourne
Melbourne, Australia
A 38-hectare garden on the south bank of the Yarra River, established in 1846. Holds over 8,500 plant species, with particular strengths in Australian native flora, cycads, and succulents. The Ornamental Lake, framed by weeping willows and black swans, is the centrepiece. The Ian Potter Foundation Children's Garden and the Guilfoyle's Volcano — a 19th-century reservoir converted into a water-conservation display garden — are within the grounds. Free entry, open year-round from dawn to dusk.
📐 38 hectares🌿 8,500+ species☀️ Year-round (free)
Official Site Melbourne Guide
