Best Cathedrals

Europe
Notre-Dame de Paris
Paris, France
French Gothic masterpiece on the Île de la Cité, begun in 1163 and completed around 1345. Flying buttresses, twin 69-m towers, the south rose window (13 m across, 84 panes of 13th-century glass), and a forest of oak timbers in the roof. Severely damaged by fire in April 2019; reopened in December 2024 after a five-year restoration that rebuilt the spire and vaulting.
📅 1163–1345🏛️ UNESCO👥 12 M visitors/year (pre-fire)
Paris Guide Official Site
Sagrada Família
Barcelona, Spain
Antoni Gaudí's unfinished basilica — under construction since 1882 and expected to complete around 2026. Eighteen towers (the tallest reaching 172.5 m), organic columns that branch like trees, and stained glass that casts rainbow light across the nave. Gaudí devoted 43 years of his life to the project and is buried in the crypt.
📅 1882–present🏛️ UNESCO👥 4.5 M visitors/year
Barcelona Guide Official Site
Duomo di Milano
Milan, Italy
The largest church in Italy and the third-largest in the world. Nearly 600 years to complete (1386–1965), with 3,400 statues, 135 spires, and a rooftop terrace where visitors walk among the Gothic pinnacles. The gilded Madonnina statue atop the main spire (108.5 m) has watched over Milan since 1774.
📅 1386–1965📐 11,700 m²🗿 3,400 statues
Milan Guide Official Site
Florence Cathedral (Santa Maria del Fiore)
Florence, Italy
Brunelleschi's dome — the largest brick dome ever constructed (45.5 m diameter), completed in 1436 without a supporting wooden framework. The polychrome marble facade in white, green, and pink, Giotto's 85-m campanile, and Vasari's Last Judgment fresco inside the cupola (3,600 m²) define the Florence skyline.
📅 1296–1436🏛️ UNESCO📐 45.5 m dome diameter
Florence Guide Official Site
St. Peter's Basilica
Vatican City
The largest church in the world by interior area (15,160 m²). Michelangelo designed the dome (42 m diameter, 136 m high), Bernini the colonnade of 284 columns in St. Peter's Square. Inside: Michelangelo's Pietà, Bernini's bronze baldachin (29 m tall), and the tombs of 91 popes. Construction spanned 1506–1626.
📅 1506–1626🏛️ UNESCO📐 15,160 m² interior
Rome Guide Official Site
Seville Cathedral
Seville, Spain
The largest Gothic cathedral in the world, built on the site of a 12th-century Almohad mosque. The Giralda bell tower (104.5 m) was originally the mosque's minaret — ramps instead of stairs let the muezzin ride a horse to the top. Inside: Christopher Columbus's tomb, the altarpiece of gold-leafed carved wood (the largest in Christendom), and 80 chapels.
📅 1401–1506🏛️ UNESCO📐 Largest Gothic cathedral
Seville Guide Official Site
Westminster Abbey
London, England
The coronation church of English and British monarchs since 1066 — every sovereign crowned here for nearly 1,000 years. Gothic architecture spanning the 13th to 16th centuries. Burial place of 17 monarchs, plus Newton, Darwin, Dickens, and Handel in Poets' Corner. Henry VII's Lady Chapel has the finest fan vaulting in England.
📅 1245–1517🏛️ UNESCO👑 Royal coronations since 1066
London Guide Official Site
St. Paul's Cathedral
London, England
Christopher Wren's masterpiece — the English Baroque cathedral built after the Great Fire of 1666 destroyed its medieval predecessor. The dome (111 m high) was the tallest structure in London for 250 years. The Whispering Gallery inside the dome carries a whisper across 34 m of curved wall. Churchill's funeral was held here in 1965.
📅 1675–1710📐 111 m dome height🎤 Whispering Gallery
London Guide Official Site
Cologne Cathedral (Kölner Dom)
Cologne, Germany
High Gothic twin-spired cathedral that took 632 years to build (1248–1880). The 157-m twin spires made it the world's tallest building upon completion. Houses the Shrine of the Three Kings — a gold reliquary said to contain the remains of the Biblical Magi. Over 500 steps to the viewing platform at the base of the south tower.
📅 1248–1880🏛️ UNESCO📐 157 m spires
Official Site
Hagia Sophia
Istanbul, Turkey
Byzantine cathedral for 916 years, Ottoman mosque for 481 years, museum for 86 years, and mosque again since 2020. The 55-m-high dome (31 m diameter) was the largest enclosed space in the world for nearly a thousand years. Gold mosaics of Christ and the Virgin Mary survive alongside Islamic calligraphy discs. Completed in just five years by Emperor Justinian in 537 AD.
📅 532–537 AD🏛️ UNESCO📐 55 m dome height
Istanbul Guide Official Site
St. Stephen's Cathedral (Stephansdom)
Vienna, Austria
Vienna's Gothic landmark since the 14th century — its 136-m south tower (Steffl) dominates the skyline and offers views to the Carpathians. The chevron-patterned glazed tile roof (230,000 tiles) depicting the Habsburg double eagle is one of the most recognizable rooftops in Europe. Catacombs below hold 10,000 burials and Habsburg organs in urns.
📅 1137–1433📐 136 m tower🪦 Catacombs below
Vienna Guide Official Site
St. Vitus Cathedral
Prague, Czech Republic
Gothic cathedral within Prague Castle — seat of the Archbishop and burial place of Bohemian kings and Holy Roman Emperors. Construction began in 1344 but the nave was not completed until 1929. Mucha's Art Nouveau stained-glass window, the silver tomb of St. John of Nepomuk (2 tonnes of silver), and the Crown Chamber housing the Bohemian Crown Jewels.
📅 1344–1929🏛️ UNESCO (Prague Castle)👑 Bohemian Crown Jewels
Prague Guide Official Site
Strasbourg Cathedral
Strasbourg, France
A single-spired Gothic cathedral of red Vosges sandstone — the tallest building in the world from 1647 to 1874 at 142 m. Victor Hugo called it a "gigantic and delicate marvel." The astronomical clock (1843) stages an automaton parade at solar noon daily. The lace-like western facade has been described as the finest stone carving in Gothic architecture.
📅 1015–1439🏛️ UNESCO (Grande Île)📐 142 m spire
Strasbourg Guide Official Site
Chartres Cathedral
Chartres, France
The most complete surviving example of High Gothic architecture and home to the finest medieval stained glass in the world — 176 windows covering 2,600 m² of glass, most from the 12th and 13th centuries. The mismatched spires (one Romanesque, one Flamboyant Gothic) and the labyrinth in the nave floor (12.9 m diameter) are iconic.
📅 1194–1220🏛️ UNESCO🪟 176 stained-glass windows
Official Site
Siena Cathedral (Duomo di Siena)
Siena, Italy
A Romanesque-Gothic cathedral with a striking black-and-white striped marble exterior that mirrors Siena's coat of arms. The inlaid marble floor (56 panels of biblical and allegorical scenes) is one of the most elaborate in Italy — uncovered only a few weeks each year. Nicola Pisano's pulpit (1268), Bernini's chapel, and the Piccolomini Library with Pinturicchio frescoes inside.
📅 1215–1263🏛️ UNESCO (Siena)🖤🤍 Black-and-white marble
Siena Guide Official Site
St. Mark's Basilica
Venice, Italy
A Byzantine-Romanesque cathedral blanketed in 8,500 m² of gold mosaics — the "Church of Gold." Built to house the relics of St. Mark stolen from Alexandria in 828 AD. Five domes, the bronze Horses of Saint Mark (looted from Constantinople in 1204), and an opulent Pala d'Oro altarpiece of gold, enamel, and 1,927 gems behind the altar.
📅 1063–1094🏛️ UNESCO✨ 8,500 m² gold mosaics
Venice Guide Official Site
Salzburg Cathedral
Salzburg, Austria
An early Baroque cathedral with an imposing marble facade flanked by twin towers (79 m). The first cathedral north of the Alps modelled on Italian Baroque prototypes. Mozart was baptized here in 1756 and later served as organist. The 71-m dome, bronze baptismal font (1321), and a crypt with excavated foundations of the original 774 AD church.
📅 1614–1628🏛️ UNESCO (Old Town)🎵 Mozart's baptism
Salzburg Guide Official Site
Matthias Church
Budapest, Hungary
A Gothic church on Castle Hill with a diamond-patterned Zsolnay ceramic tile roof that glitters over the Danube. Originally built in 1255, converted to a mosque under Ottoman rule (1541–1686), then restored in neo-Gothic style by Frigyes Schulek. Coronation church of the last two Hungarian kings. The interior is covered in elaborate painted geometric patterns.
📅 1255 (rebuilt 1874–1896)🏛️ UNESCO (Budapest)👑 Coronation church
Budapest Guide Official Site
St. Giles' Cathedral
Edinburgh, Scotland
The High Kirk of Edinburgh on the Royal Mile — the historic centre of Scottish Presbyterianism and where John Knox preached the Reformation. The crown steeple (49 m) has been an Edinburgh landmark since 1500. The Thistle Chapel (1911), one of the most ornate Gothic Revival interiors in Britain, seats only 16 — the knights of the Order of the Thistle.
📅 12th century (present form 1385)👑 Order of the Thistle⛪ Crown steeple
Edinburgh Guide Official Site
Christ Church Cathedral
Dublin, Ireland
Dublin's oldest medieval building — founded by the Norse King Sitriuc in 1030 and rebuilt in stone by Strongbow in 1172. The 12th-century crypt is the largest in Ireland and the oldest surviving structure in Dublin. A medieval bridge connects the cathedral to Dublinia, an interactive museum of Viking and medieval Dublin.
📅 Founded 1030📐 Largest crypt in Ireland🛡️ Viking origins
Dublin Guide Official Site
Helsinki Cathedral
Helsinki, Finland
A gleaming white neoclassical cathedral crowning Senate Square — the symbol of Helsinki since 1852. Carl Ludvig Engel designed the green-domed Greek cross plan with Corinthian columns modelled on St. Petersburg's architecture. The stark white interior is intentionally austere in the Lutheran tradition. Zinc statues of the Twelve Apostles line the rooftop.
📅 1830–1852📐 62 m dome🤍 Neoclassical icon
Helsinki Guide Official Site
Pisa Cathedral
Pisa, Italy
A Romanesque cathedral of white marble in the Piazza dei Miracoli — often overshadowed by its famous leaning campanile next door. Built to celebrate Pisa's 1063 naval victory over the Saracens. Giovanni Pisano's pulpit (1302–1310), a gilded coffered ceiling, and Byzantine-influenced mosaics in the apse. The bronze doors by Bonanno Pisano (1180) survive on the south transept.
📅 1063–1092🏛️ UNESCO🗿 Pisano pulpit
Pisa Guide Official Site
Basilica of Notre-Dame de Fourvière
Lyon, France
A lavish 19th-century basilica perched on Fourvière hill above Lyon's Old Town. Built after the Franco-Prussian War as a vow of gratitude. The exterior mixes Byzantine and Romanesque elements with four octagonal towers; the interior is covered floor-to-ceiling in gold mosaics and polychrome marble. A lower crypt church sits beneath it.
📅 1872–1884🏛️ UNESCO (Old Lyon)⛰️ Hilltop landmark
Lyon Guide Official Site
Naples Cathedral (Duomo di Napoli)
Naples, Italy
A Gothic cathedral built over a 4th-century early Christian basilica — three times a year the dried blood of San Gennaro (patron saint of Naples) is said to liquefy in a glass vial. Failure to liquefy is considered an omen of disaster. The Chapel of the Treasure of San Gennaro contains more silver and gold than any other chapel in the world.
📅 1272–1315🩸 Blood miracle 3×/year💰 Richest chapel in the world
Naples Guide Museum
Church of Our Saviour (Vor Frelsers Kirke)
Copenhagen, Denmark
A Baroque church famous for its external spiral staircase that winds 400 steps around the spire to a golden globe at 90 m — the staircase narrows as you climb and the final steps are on the outside with nothing above you but sky. The carillon of 48 bells plays every hour. An ornate Baroque interior with a three-story pipe organ (1698).
📅 1682–1696📐 90 m spiral spire🔔 48-bell carillon
Copenhagen Guide Visitor Info
St. Basil's Cathedral
Moscow, Russia
Nine brightly painted onion domes — each a different shape and colour — clustered on Red Square. Built by Ivan the Terrible in 1555–1561 to celebrate the capture of Kazan. The swirling domes make it look like one building, but it is actually nine separate chapels connected by corridors and staircases. A persistent legend says Ivan blinded the architects to prevent them from replicating it.
📅 1555–1561🏛️ UNESCO⛪ 9 chapels in one
Official Site
Westerkerk
Amsterdam, Netherlands
The largest Protestant church in the Netherlands, built in Dutch Renaissance style (1620–1631). The 85-m tower (Westertoren) is topped with a blue imperial crown granted by Maximilian I of Austria and is the tallest church tower in Amsterdam. Rembrandt is buried somewhere in the church in an unmarked pauper's grave. Anne Frank could see the tower from her hiding place.
📅 1620–1631📐 85 m tower🎨 Rembrandt's burial
Amsterdam Guide Official Site
Cathedral of Saint Domnius
Split, Croatia
The oldest cathedral building in the world still in use — originally Emperor Diocletian's mausoleum (305 AD), converted to a Christian cathedral in the 7th century. The octagonal structure with its 57-m Romanesque bell tower sits within the ruins of Diocletian's Palace. The irony: Diocletian was one of Christianity's greatest persecutors.
📅 305 AD (cathedral 7th c.)🏛️ UNESCO📐 Oldest cathedral building in use
Split Guide Official Site
Clérigos Tower (Torre dos Clérigos)
Porto, Portugal
A 76-m Baroque bell tower — the tallest structure in Porto and a landmark visible from nearly everywhere in the city. Part of the Igreja dos Clérigos designed by Nicolau Nasoni in 1754. Climb 240 steps for panoramic views over the Douro River and the terracotta rooftops. The church's elliptical nave was among the first in Portugal.
📅 1735–1763📐 76 m / 240 steps🌇 Best views in Porto
Porto Guide Official Site
Cathedral of St. Michael and St. Gudula
Brussels, Belgium
A Brabantine Gothic cathedral on the Treurenberg hill between the Upper and Lower Town. Built over 300 years (1226–1519), its twin towers (64 m each) frame a facade compared to Notre-Dame de Paris. Stained glass by Bernard van Orley (1537), a Baroque pulpit of carved oak (1699), and the royal weddings and coronations of Belgian monarchs held here.
📅 1226–1519📐 Twin 64 m towers👑 Belgian royal church
Brussels Guide Official Site
Hallgrímskirkja
Reykjavik, Iceland
A 74.5-m expressionist church — the tallest structure in Reykjavik and visible across the city. Designed by Guðjón Samúelsson in 1937 and built over 41 years. The columnar basalt facade mimics Iceland's volcanic landscape. A 15-m pipe organ with 5,275 pipes fills the austere concrete interior. An observation deck in the tower offers views to Snæfellsjökull glacier.
📅 1945–1986📐 74.5 m tall🌋 Basalt-inspired
Reykjavik Guide Official Site
Sacré-Cœur
Paris, France
A Romano-Byzantine basilica of white travertine crowning Montmartre — the highest point in Paris. Built as a national penance after the Franco-Prussian War (1875–1914). The 83-m dome offers a 30-km panorama across the city. The apse mosaic of Christ in Majesty (475 m²) is one of the largest in the world. Perpetual adoration of the Blessed Sacrament has continued uninterrupted since 1885.
📅 1875–1914📐 83 m dome👥 11 M visitors/year
Paris Guide Official Site
Canterbury Cathedral
Canterbury, England
The Mother Church of Anglicanism and seat of the Archbishop of Canterbury. Thomas Becket was murdered here in 1170, making it medieval Europe's foremost pilgrimage site — the destination of Chaucer's Canterbury Tales. Norman crypt (1070s), Perpendicular Gothic nave, Bell Harry Tower (72 m), and the earliest Gothic choir in England (1175).
📅 597 AD (rebuilt 1070–1498)🏛️ UNESCO⚔️ Becket's martyrdom 1170
Official Site
York Minster
York, England
The largest Gothic cathedral in Northern Europe — 158 m long with the widest Gothic nave in England. The Great East Window (1405–1408) is the largest expanse of medieval stained glass in the world (the size of a tennis court). Twin western towers (60 m) and a central tower (71 m). Foundations reveal Roman ruins and a Norman cathedral beneath the current 13th-century structure.
📅 1220–1472📐 158 m long🪟 Largest medieval window
Official Site
Reims Cathedral
Reims, France
The coronation cathedral of the French kings — 33 monarchs were crowned here from Louis VIII in 1223 to Charles X in 1825. High Gothic architecture with 2,303 exterior statues (more than any other cathedral), including the famous Smiling Angel. Marc Chagall designed three stained-glass windows (1974) in the apse. Heavily shelled in WWI and meticulously restored.
📅 1211–1275🏛️ UNESCO👑 33 royal coronations
Official Site
Amiens Cathedral
Amiens, France
The tallest complete cathedral in France (42.3 m nave vault) and the largest by interior volume — twice the size of Notre-Dame de Paris. Built in just 50 years giving it unmatched Gothic stylistic unity. The western facade holds 3,000+ carved figures in the "Bible in stone." A summer light show projects the original medieval polychrome colours onto the facade.
📅 1220–1270🏛️ UNESCO📐 42.3 m nave (tallest in France)
Official Site
Santiago de Compostela Cathedral
Santiago de Compostela, Spain
The destination of the Camino de Santiago pilgrimage — walked by over 400,000 pilgrims per year from all corners of Europe. Built over the alleged tomb of the Apostle James. A Romanesque interior behind a Baroque Obradoiro facade (1738). The Botafumeiro — a 1.5-m, 80-kg silver censer — swings across the transept on a pulley system at special Masses.
📅 1075–1211🏛️ UNESCO🚶 400K+ pilgrims/year
Official Site
Burgos Cathedral
Burgos, Spain
A masterpiece of Spanish Gothic — the first cathedral in Spain to receive UNESCO status on its own merit. Twin openwork spires (88 m) inspired by Cologne, a lantern dome over the crossing (the Cimborrio) that illuminates the tomb of El Cid and his wife Jimena below. The Golden Staircase by Diego de Siloé (1519) bridges a 10-m height difference between street and cloister.
📅 1221–1567🏛️ UNESCO⚔️ El Cid's tomb
Official Site
Duomo di Orvieto
Orvieto, Italy
A Romanesque-Gothic cathedral with what many consider the finest facade in Italy — a 52-m-wide wall of gold mosaics, bas-reliefs, and polychrome marble begun in 1310 by Lorenzo Maitani. Inside: Luca Signorelli's Last Judgment frescoes (1499–1504) in the Chapel of San Brizio — a direct influence on Michelangelo's Sistine Chapel ceiling. Perched on a volcanic tufa cliff above the Umbrian plains.
📅 1290–1591🎨 Signorelli frescoes⛰️ Tufa cliff setting
Official Site
Alexander Nevsky Cathedral
Sofia, Bulgaria
A Neo-Byzantine cathedral and the largest in the Balkans — built to honour the 200,000 Russian soldiers who died liberating Bulgaria from Ottoman rule in 1877–78. The central gilt dome rises 45 m; the bell tower reaches 53 m with a 12-tonne bell. The interior holds 270+ murals, Italian marble, Brazilian onyx, and alabaster from the Alps. Capacity for 10,000.
📅 1882–1912📐 Seats 10,000🔔 12-tonne bell
Official Site
Middle East
Church of the Holy Sepulchre
Jerusalem, Israel
The holiest site in Christianity — built over the rock of Golgotha where Jesus was crucified and the tomb where he was buried and resurrected. The current structure dates to Crusader reconstruction (1149) after earlier versions were destroyed. Six Christian denominations share custody under a fragile status quo. The Edicule shrine over the tomb was restored in 2016–2017.
📅 326 AD (rebuilt 1149)✝️ Holiest Christian site⛪ 6 denominations share it

Basilica of the Nativity
Bethlehem, Palestine
Built over the cave where Jesus is believed to have been born — one of the oldest continuously operating churches in the world. The original basilica was built by Emperor Constantine in 327 AD; the current structure dates largely to Justinian's 6th-century reconstruction. A 14-pointed silver star marks the exact spot of the birth in the Grotto beneath the high altar.
📅 327 AD (rebuilt 565)🏛️ UNESCO⭐ Silver star marks birthplace
Bethlehem Info
Americas
St. Patrick's Cathedral
New York, United States
A Neo-Gothic cathedral at Fifth Avenue and 50th Street — dwarfed by Rockefeller Center but still the largest decorated Gothic-style Catholic cathedral in North America. Twin 100-m spires, a 7,855-pipe organ, Charles Connick's Great Rose Window (8 m), and the bronze Atlas sculpture across the street. Over 5 million visitors per year.
📅 1858–1878📐 100 m spires👥 5.5 M visitors/year
New York Guide Official Site
Washington National Cathedral
Washington DC, United States
The sixth-largest cathedral in the world — a Neo-Gothic structure built entirely of Indiana limestone without a steel frame. Construction ran from 1907 to 1990. A piece of Moon rock is embedded in one stained-glass window. Gargoyles include a Darth Vader grotesque. State funerals for Eisenhower, Reagan, Ford, George H.W. Bush, and McCain were held here.
📅 1907–1990📐 157 m long, 91 m tower🌙 Moon rock window
Washington DC Guide Official Site
Notre-Dame Basilica of Montreal
Montreal, Canada
A Neo-Gothic basilica whose interior explodes with colour — deep blue ceiling with gold stars, polychrome wood carvings, and a sanctuary lit by thousands of candles. The 7,000-pipe Casavant organ is one of the largest in North America. Céline Dion was married here in 1994. The AURA light show transforms the interior nightly.
📅 1824–1829🎵 7,000-pipe organ🌟 AURA light show
Montreal Guide Official Site
Metropolitan Cathedral of Mexico City
Mexico City, Mexico
The largest and oldest cathedral in the Americas — built atop the ruins of the Aztec Templo Mayor on the Zócalo square. Construction spanned 240 years (1573–1813), layering Renaissance, Baroque, and Neoclassical styles. The Altar of the Kings is a gilded Churrigueresque wall of carved saints 25 m high. The building is visibly sinking into the soft lakebed beneath it.
📅 1573–1813📐 Largest in the Americas🏛️ Aztec foundations
Official Site
Cathedral of Lima
Lima, Peru
A cathedral on the Plaza Mayor founded by Francisco Pizarro in 1535 — Pizarro's mummified remains are displayed in a glass coffin in a chapel by the entrance. Rebuilt multiple times after earthquakes. The current facade is a restrained Baroque with twin towers. A museum of religious art in the sacristy and catacombs beneath the nave.
📅 Founded 1535🏛️ UNESCO (Historic Lima)⚰️ Pizarro's remains
Lima Guide Official Site
São Paulo Cathedral (Catedral da Sé)
São Paulo, Brazil
A massive Neo-Gothic cathedral that seats 8,000 — the fourth-largest in the world by volume. Its 92-m twin spires and a 30-m Renaissance-style dome anchor the Praça da Sé in downtown São Paulo. The 10,000-pipe Italian organ is one of the largest in Latin America. A crypt below holds the remains of indigenous chief Tibiriçá and other figures from São Paulo's founding.
📅 1913–1967📐 Seats 8,000🎵 10,000-pipe organ
São Paulo Guide Official Site
Las Lajas Sanctuary
Ipiales, Colombia
A Gothic Revival basilica built on a 50-m bridge spanning a gorge of the Guáitara River — one of the most dramatic church settings in the world. Built between 1916 and 1949 on the site where a miraculous image of the Virgin appeared on the canyon wall in 1754. The church is literally built into the rock face, with the natural stone forming the wall behind the altar.
📅 1916–1949🌉 50 m bridge over gorge⛰️ Canyon setting
Official Site
Cathedral of Brasília
Brasília, Brazil
Oscar Niemeyer's hyperboloid masterpiece — 16 curved concrete columns (each weighing 90 tonnes) reach skyward like hands in prayer, supporting a stained-glass ceiling that floods the underground nave with coloured light. The congregation descends through a dark tunnel before emerging into the luminous interior. Three aluminium angels suspended from the ceiling. A UNESCO-listed icon of modernist architecture.
📅 1958–1970🏛️ UNESCO (Brasília)📐 16 curved columns
Official Site
Metropolitan Cathedral of Rio de Janeiro
Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
A brutalist concrete cone inspired by Mayan pyramids — 96 m in diameter and 75 m tall, seating 20,000. Four enormous stained-glass windows (64 m tall) stretch floor-to-ceiling in a cross pattern, visible from outside at night. Built to replace the old colonial cathedral when Rio was still capital. The stark modernist interior is a deliberate break from ornate Catholic tradition.
📅 1964–1979📐 Seats 20,000🪟 64 m stained glass

Africa
Koutoubia Mosque
Marrakech, Morocco
The largest mosque in Marrakech and the model for the Giralda in Seville and the Hassan Tower in Rabat. The 77-m minaret — built of local red sandstone and visible across the medina — is the defining landmark of the city. Non-Muslims cannot enter, but the exterior and surrounding gardens are a constant focal point. The minaret's lantern holds three golden orbs said to be made from the jewellery of Yacoub el-Mansour's wife.
📅 1147–1199📐 77 m minaret🕌 Model for Seville's Giralda
Marrakech Guide
Asia
Sultan Ahmed Mosque (Blue Mosque)
Istanbul, Turkey
A 17th-century Ottoman imperial mosque directly opposite Hagia Sophia — the only mosque in Istanbul with six minarets (a seventh was added to Mecca's Grand Mosque to restore its primacy). The interior is lined with over 20,000 handmade İznik blue tiles that give it its nickname. The cascading domes and half-domes create one of the most iconic silhouettes in Islamic architecture.
📅 1609–1616🏛️ UNESCO🔵 20,000+ İznik tiles
Istanbul Guide Official Site
Meiji Shrine (Meiji Jingū)
Tokyo, Japan
A Shinto shrine dedicated to Emperor Meiji and Empress Shōken, set within a 70-hectare forest of 120,000 trees donated from across Japan. The massive torii gate at the entrance is made from a 1,500-year-old cypress. Destroyed in WWII air raids and rebuilt in 1958. Japan's most visited shrine — over 3 million during the New Year's first three days alone.
📅 1920 (rebuilt 1958)🌲 70-hectare forest👥 30 M visitors/year
Tokyo Guide Official Site
Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion)
Kyoto, Japan
A three-story Zen Buddhist temple covered entirely in gold leaf, reflected in a mirror-calm pond. Originally a shogun's retirement villa (1397), converted to a Rinzai Zen temple after his death. Burned down by a mentally disturbed monk in 1950 (the subject of Mishima's novel The Temple of the Golden Pavilion) and rebuilt in 1955 with even more gold.
📅 1397 (rebuilt 1955)🏛️ UNESCO✨ Gold leaf exterior
Kyoto Guide Official Site
Myeongdong Cathedral
Seoul, South Korea
The first brick Gothic church in Korea and the symbolic heart of Korean Catholicism. Built in 1898 when Catholicism was still persecuted. The red-and-grey brick exterior with a 45-m bell tower stands in contrast to the neon shopping district surrounding it. During the 1980s democracy movement, Myeongdong Cathedral served as a sanctuary for student protesters.
📅 1892–1898📐 First Gothic church in Korea🕊️ Democracy symbol
Seoul Guide Official Site
St. Andrew's Cathedral
Singapore
A white Neo-Gothic cathedral in the heart of Singapore's Civic District — the largest cathedral in Southeast Asia. Built by Indian convict labourers in 1856–1861 using Madras chunam plaster (shell lime, egg white, sugar, and coconut husk) that gives the walls their brilliant white finish. The spire (63 m) is a landmark against Marina Bay's skyline.
📅 1856–1861📐 Largest in Southeast Asia🤍 Madras chunam walls
Singapore Guide Official Site
Sheikh Zayed Grand Mosque
Abu Dhabi, United Arab Emirates
One of the largest mosques in the world — 82 domes, over 1,000 columns, and capacity for 41,000 worshippers. The main prayer hall holds the world's largest hand-knotted carpet (5,627 m², woven by 1,200 artisans) and one of the largest chandeliers (10 m diameter, 12 tonnes of Swarovski crystal). White Macedonian marble covers the exterior and interior.
📅 1996–2007📐 82 domes, 1,000+ columns👥 41,000 capacity
Abu Dhabi Guide Official Site
Oceania
St. Mary's Cathedral
Sydney, Australia
An English Gothic Revival sandstone cathedral overlooking Hyde Park — the seat of the Catholic Archbishop of Sydney. Built over 100 years (1821–1928), the twin southern spires were finally completed in 2000 for the new millennium. The terrazzo floor in the crypt depicts the Creation story in Celtic and Art Deco style — one of the finest examples of ecclesiastical terrazzo in the world.
